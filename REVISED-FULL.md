# BLOOD AND ICE — BOOK ONE: THE WILD BOY (Revised)

---
# Chapter 11: Marks and Meaning

I take the skins back out at the dark, after the fire, when the high stone is mine and the Kala has folded down into her own still.

I have carried them up from the wreck a moon now, rolled in a sleeve of fat-soaked hide against the wet, and I have not opened them. There was no place in me to open them. The shore gave me only its own laws, and the laws of the shore do not read marks. But up here among the Vorth a thing has loosened. The sounds are coming back, one stolen at a time, laid in the gap behind my teeth — *low, high, come,* the gentling, the question-sound — and where the sounds come the rest stirs. The body says, before the head ever does: *now. Try them now.*

So I unroll the skins on the bare dark stone in the red light.

---

They will not speak.

The marks lie in their rows, ordered as nothing in the green is ordered, line under line, and the body knows past all doubt that they are made to mean. There is a way of taking the rows in through the eye and having them come out the other side as the thing a man wanted to say. I had that way once. I am sure of it the same as I am sure of the name behind the teeth. The reading is gone like the name is gone. I strain at it, going in through the only door there is, and there is no floor.

But the skin shows me a thing past the marks. It shows me a place.

Most of the rows I cannot crack — the small busy lines, line on line. But here and there the dead hand laid down other things, larger, standing apart, and these the body half-takes. A shape that is the curve of a coast, the bend of a reach, the bar of black mud. I have walked that bend. The dead man drew my green — the channels and the high stone — scratched small on the skin where they are scratched large on my soles. Where his lines bend I know the bend, because the feet walked it.

And among the shapes, driven into the dead skin in dark deliberate line: the eye. Ringed, open, looking. And through it, crossing it, the thorn.

The disc at my chest goes warm against the breastbone. I lay the worked metal beside the drawn skin and they are the same mark — the true round, the true line, the thing on my chest and the thing the dead man scratched. One thing, made twice, by men, before the green. The drowned-deep place hums: *I know this. This is mine.*

The dead man's lines do not stop at the bar. They run inland, up channels I have never followed, into the deep green under the high country — and where they end the hand drew the eye again, biggest of all, and around it a shape I take for stone laid on stone, climbing, a made hill. I know the place. Before the Kala took me in, I followed a fat-tailed thing up a channel and came out under the canopy into a clearing that was not a clearing. Stone laid on stone, gone green and slick, climbing in steps into the dark of the canopy — a hill made and not grown. Channels cut in the old stone, running down, step to step, all the long way to a basin at the foot. And on every face of the climbing stone, carved deep, worn but never gone: the eye. Ringed. Open. The thorn through it.

I did not go to the top. The body turned from it, went still all over, the hide standing up along my arms, and took me back down fast. Now I know why. The channels were for a thing to run down. Dark soaked into the stone past washing. The dead men knew — they could read what I cannot, and they went up the channels toward the made hill, and the green laid them down before they got there.

I want to know what they were reaching for. The wanting has a tooth in it now.

---

The seer comes to me at the marks.

It is old past the others, the body sunk in on itself, the dark hide gone ash and near-white. It moves the way the oldest things move, slow and without sound, so that I look up from the rows and it is simply there. The milk-pale eyes already on me.

It does not look at me first. It looks at the skins. The young ones look at the skins and make the dismissing sound — dark scratches on dead hide, no meat, no warm. The seer does not dismiss. It bends its great near-white head down over the rows, following the made-thing back to the hand that made it. It knows the rows are made. It knows they are a thing that is read. It is the only one of them that knows the difference.

Then it lays its finger on the disc at my chest. It follows the ring with the old pale fingertip, and then the thorn, and makes the long low sound — the one for a thing that comes from before and means. The same sound it lays on the squared dead.

Then it does the thing it did at the loop, and worse. It lifts the finger off the disc and lays it against the side of my throat, where the blood goes, where the great pulse runs up under the skin — and holds it there. Light. The way I lay my finger along a worn line in the mud, reading the thing that passes under.

I hold still under it. The beast comes up a little at the touch on the throat — the old place, the place the hand closes — but this is not the closing hand. I let the beast settle. I let the seer read the blood. And the seer goes very still. Its milk-pale eyes change — the change of a thing turning toward something it did not look for and now half-sees.

It feels something in the blood.

I do not know what it feels. But I know my blood is one of the wrong things. The green that could not eat me. The water that knots every belly but mine. The fever-air I sleep in and wake whole from. The swarms that ring me and never land. The wrongness is in the blood. The seer feels it go under the finger and the milk-pale eyes change.

It looks from the throat to the disc. From the blood to the eye-and-thorn. And it lays them side by side, two halves of one strangeness — the mark that is mine from before, and the blood the green cannot kill — and bows the great near-white head over both, the long meaning-sound coming up low out of the sunk old chest.

I strain to turn it over. The head has no shape for it. But the seer has found a thing I cannot find in myself and bowed to the meaning it cannot reach. I bow with it. We are two now, both reaching, neither able. It is more than the bones gave. More than the green ever gave. To be felt for and found and named *kept* by the oldest eye on the high stone — it fills the near edge of the want. Not the whole of it. The whole of it wants the reading, the name out of the gap, the word for what bled down the channels of the made hill. That hunger does not close. But the seer turns. And the seer finds. And I lean into the finding and am, for this low, fed.

---

The rains break.

One dark the rain thins. The next it stops between two breaths, and I wake in the kept warm against the Kala and do not know what woke me, and then I know. It is the quiet. For the first time in the long drowned season, the rain has gone off the high stones and left them dripping and steaming, and through the broken roof of the green the lights come back, far and small and many.

I do not count them. That is the thing I learn, lying in the steam with the lights come back. I have stopped counting them. For the first time in all my counting there is no between.

The tribe pours down off the high stones into the steaming green to take what the lifted rains have left. And this low I do not go down behind them. A big one waits at the head of the run — the heavy old male with the torn ear, the one that stood a whole season at the lip of the rot-thickets and went up empty because the soft ground would not hold him. He has seen the man-shape go out light over the skin of the rot where his own weight goes through, read the run, bring back the fat-meat beast. And this low he waits with his small dark patient eyes, and when I come he makes the asking-sound, the rising one, *where, where* — and tips his great head toward the thicket where the ground will not hold him.

He is asking the weakest thing on the high stones to read him the country his strength cannot cross.

I go ahead of him. Down the bank, out onto the skin of the rot where I am light and he is not. I find the run with my soles and he comes behind me set in my track, foot for foot. I am the eye and he is the hand. I find the thing and he closes on it, the great arm doing in one blow what my whole weight could not. We bring it up between us, his kill on my reading.

I am not trailing the strong anymore. The strong is set in my track.

---

The bold small one brings me a frog.

It has been mine for a moon now — the small dark quick one that rides my chest and lays the world's names on me one at a time. It comes up the line of my torn voice quicker than it goes to any big one, and when the half-grown ones cuff it, it runs to me. Puts the man-shape between it and the cuffing. And the half-grown one reads it and turns away.

This low it comes rocking up the rock with a thing held proud in the curled small hands and lays it at the fire-edge — a fat green frog-thing, alive, blinking, the best thing it owns, brought to the man-shape to put on the fire.

I look at the frog-thing. The frog-thing looks at me. The small one looks between us, glad, waiting for me to be glad.

And a thing comes up in me that has no name in the green and no use — it comes up my chest and out the torn throat in a bark, broken and ugly and *good,* and the small one startles back, and then makes it too, learning it off me, the two of us barking the wrong glad sound at each other over a frog while the fat thing hops free into the dark and neither of us moves to stop it. A thing a small one brought up out of me with a frog. Light. Not hunger and not the deep leaning-out. A third thing.

---

The Kala lays me with the squared dead.

She comes off the high clean shelf in the steaming dark and takes my hand in her great dark hand, and turns and goes up, and does not let my hand go. I go up after her onto the clean ground where the dead are faced to the sun — the place no foot of mine has gone, the keeper's ground. I brace for the arm. It does not come.

She kneels at a clear place among the squared dead and takes my two hands in hers and sets them on a still small body and shows them, slow, how to square a gone thing — how to fold the limbs straight, how to turn the face up, how the hands go flat and order the small clean shape of it into the line of the kept. My hands under her hands. The first thing I have done with my hands on this ground, and it is this.

When it is squared she moves my hands to a bare clear place beside it, an empty squared place at the sun-struck edge, and lays her great dark hand flat on it, and then my hand on it beside hers, and makes the meaning-sound again. The one for the kept.

She is showing me my place among the laid-down. She is telling me, in the only tongue there is between us, that when the gone-low comes for me, this is where I go. Squared and faced with the kept. She has laid the man-shape into the deepest order the Vorth have — the one that says *this was ours, this we keep even gone* — and there is a place in it shaped to me.

To be given a place among the kept dead is to be given a place among the living that does not pour away when the hand opens. It is the un-borrowed stone. The first ground I have stood on since before the black that is not lent.

---

Gull-Marr comes up onto the clean shelf.

The gentling goes out of the high stones. The small ones feel it first, the bold one slipping off my shoulder and away. And the king climbs onto the high clean shelf and stops over the Kala and the man-shape, and looks down at us, and his flat eyes do not warm.

The Kala does not move her hand off mine. She lifts her head to the king and holds his flat eyes, and does not make the keeping-sound. She only holds his look with her hand flat on my hand on the squared place, the whole still vast bulk of her unmoving between the king and the thing she has laid among her dead.

The seer comes up on the king's far side, lifts one long dark finger, and points it — not at me, at the eye bound at my chest — and makes the long meaning-sound.

The king looks from the mark to the cut place to the Kala's hand on my hand. And he does not lay the brand on me. He does not turn me off the stone. He makes a sound — low, short, flat, the teeth out of it. The thing a strong one lays on a thing it has weighed and will let stand. Not chosen. Not warmed-to. Allowed.

The king has let it stand. Three of them — the seer that marked me, the Kala that set herself between me and the law, the king whose word is the law. I am laid into the order of the tribe at every height there is.

---

I lie in the warm that dark with the rains gone and the lights come back through the broken green, and I do not count them.

The bold small one is heaped against my chest, half asleep, learning the still-sound after the Kala and me. The Kala folds down beside us, the great warm silver-shot bulk, and lays the arm over me and makes the still-sound — *be still, you are held, nothing comes.* I make it back, low and torn, two falls, scraping over the gap where the three teeth are gone. And the small one makes it after us both, and the three of us throw the one soft sound back and forth in the steaming dark, around and around, and no part of it closes like a hand.

The far mouth is full.

The belly-hunger I have filled a thousand times; it empties with the next low and you fill it again. But the deeper one — the hunger that has leaned out of me toward a thing to turn and see me and make me worth a sound, since before the black, since the mouth that made sounds at me in the dark went down into the deep water — that one is full. Not the near edge. The whole drowned-deep of it, where the lost name lies and the buzzing turns over in its sleep.

I have a name behind the teeth that no one has spoken. I have the brand beside it. And now a third thing lies in the warm dark with them: *this is filled, and the name for it is here, this, mine, kept.*

I would do it again. I do not know yet what it will cost me to have learned it — that the home, once given, is a thing that can be taken, all at once, by a hand reaching in across the water. I only know, this low, that the leaning-out is over.

I am held. Nothing comes.

# Chapter 18: Smoke on the Water

There are things on the water that sit it wrong.

---

I am up on the seaward stack in the green half-light of the low, where my green runs out and goes to bare wet rock and the channel opens past the last of the mangrove into the wide flat water and the haze. I come up here before a hunt to read the tide — the set of the swell, the dark roads under it where the fat river-pig runs the shallows. The water lies past the pinch the way it always lies, hot and pale and old under the standing haze.

And there is a thing on it that has no business being there.

One shape. Long, dark, sitting the swell the way a thing sits it that has lost the fight with the water and is being taken where the water wants. But it holds a line — not the dumb hold of a drift-log pushed flat, the lean hold of a thing that has a will in it still, pointed, even now. Even dying.

Marrow is the one who stills first. The curled hand working slow against his chest. I read the water before I let his stillness in.

The shape is a boat. The word comes up out of me from far down, from under all the green I packed over everything before the green, and it comes up whole. *Boat.* The whole skin draws tight. The small hairs lifting along the arm. The gut clenching to a fist. The body knows the word before the head can turn it. The body wore the word.

And the boat is wrong. It wallows. The oars that work are out of time, the hull sits too low. There are not enough oars for the size of it. I read it the way I read a wounded thing — the listing, the slowness, the desperation of a body that feels the ground going under it. The boat is running from something.

---

They beach the thing on my spit. Not where the dead expedition died — further along, past the old ribs in the mud. They run the boat in on desperation and luck, and the keel grinds up the sand, and the dark uprights come over the sides.

Not many. Fewer than the boat was built for. Some move wrong — stiff, favoring, one with an arm bound in torn cloth. They are hurt. Not the green-hurt — the hurt of things that have been hit. There is a smell off the boat I know from the far-down place: the iron-smell of someone else's blood in the seams of the deck. They have been in a fight and come away broken.

One of them — big, slow-moving, the one the others turn to when the work stops — puts a hand on the hull and leans there, breathing. And the green closes around them. The fever-air, the seep. I do not sicken. Every other human who has walked onto this shore has gone down inside twenty lows. They do not know it yet. The big one reads the green wall and sees cover. He does not see the teeth in it.

The body says: *they are going to die here.*

---

I lie above them on the bone-spit lookout and I read them the way I read a herd.

The big one first. He moves through the camp the way Gull-Marr moved through the tribe — slow, certain, touching things as he passes. A shoulder here. A hand on a back there. Not the hand that owns. The hand that holds together. He stops at the one with the bound arm and crouches and unwraps the cloth and his hands are gentle on the wound. Then the tired settles — the tired of a thing that has held something together too long.

The thick-armed one is the law behind the holding. He carries an axe like a thing that belongs on his hip. The body reads him: he is not the strength — he is the fear of the strength, the held blow. And under it, something I did not expect: he is afraid. Not of the green. Of the big one's face when the dying goes too far.

The young one is the runner. The climber. All quick joints, no weight in the settling. He is the age I was when the green took me. The big one puts a hand on the back of the young one's neck and holds it there a breath. Not the brand-hand. Something else.

And the far mouth opens so wide it hurts. I have seen this. Not the shape — the thing under the shape. The holding-together. The shoulder-laid and the hand on the back of the neck that means *you are mine and I am yours and the thing that is coming for us has to come through that first.* I have seen it in the Kala's arm. I have seen it in the old male standing foot-for-foot at my side. It is the thing the tribe gave me. And here it is on two legs, made of the same shape that made the hold and the brand — and it is not the brand. It is the other thing.

A woman reads the sky — she stands at the tide-line with her face up, reading the wind. She has a heading that does not match the big one's. They argue, short and sharp, and the argument is the argument of two minds that trust each other enough to be wrong at each other.

A steady-handed woman whose hands go to wounds the way my hands go to a kill. A big gentle one who cooks and makes sounds nobody laughs at, and the not-laughing has affection in it.

---

The green takes them fast.

The first low: the coughing one. The wet tearing, the chest drowning. The steady-handed woman's hands on him, the orders, the professional set. Before the dark the big one goes in to read what her hands find, and when he comes out his face has changed. The coughing one dies in the night. They cover him with stones.

The second low: a sting in the shallows. The steady-handed woman cuts the puncture, packs it with paste from wrong leaves — she does not know this green. The leg goes the color of the rot. She shakes her head.

A third goes into the green to cut limbs and comes out with a bite that swells and turns hot. She cuts the arm off at the elbow with the thick one's axe. He lives one low past the cutting and dies in the dark.

The thick-armed one stands over the dying and his face goes flat and he turns away and walks to the tree line and stands with his back to the camp. The young one follows — not touching, not speaking, just there — and after a while the thick-armed one puts his hand on the young one's head, brief, rough, and they come back to the fire.

---

By the third low the big one stands at the tide-line and reads the green wall and the thinning pack at his back. He is counting days. The sky-reader stands beside him and points at the green — inland, away from the death-shore. He shakes his head. She points again, sharper, her voice going up. The argument goes. And the big one puts both hands flat on the rail of the boat and looks at them — his own hands — and nods. Once. The nod of a thing that has drawn a new line because the old one was killing everyone behind it.

They break camp. They go inland, toward my green, toward the high stones.

I follow them through the canopy. The far mouth leans at every step they take into the deeper shade. They do not know what lives in the high green. They do not know about the Vorth.

Marrow follows me. Behind us the bone-spit holds its dead and the green comes down over the camp. Ahead of us the hurt pack walks deeper into my island with the holding-center at its front and the sky-reader behind him and the young one in the branches above. And I count the lows they have left against the thing they are walking toward, and the number is going down.

# Chapter 20: The Score

They find the high stones.

---

I knew they would. The sky-reader had the heading and the young one had the instinct, and the green thins around the high stones the way it thins nowhere else on the island — the canopy opening, the air moving, the light coming down in sheets through the break. It is the first good ground the hurt pack has walked since they beached. I watch them feel it. The big one stops at the tree line where the green opens and puts his hands on his hips and reads the cleared ground and the standing stones, and the breath that comes off him is the breath of a thing that has been drowning and found a pocket of air.

They do not see the tribe. The tribe sees them.

I am in the canopy above the high stones when the pack breaks the tree line. I am already too late to stop what is coming, because the Vorth were watching before I was. The old male with the torn ear has the lookout today — he is up on the far stack, the great scarred body turned toward the new smell on the wind, and I read him from a hundred body-lengths: the head up, the nostrils wide, the flat old eyes fixed on the tree line where the two-legged pack is emerging.

He has never seen a human up close. The tribe has the smell of the old dead expedition on the far shore, the smell that means *wrong-shape, danger,* and they have the memory of me — the first two-legged thing they took in, the soft wrong thing that became the king. But these are not me. These are the pack the smell came from. The old male reads them and the reading lands in the old place: *threat.*

I go down from the canopy fast. Not fast enough.

---

The Vorth send three.

Three prime-blood warriors, moving through the green toward the tree line the way the Hesh moved through the green — low, fast, the great bodies poured through the undergrowth without sound. They have the hand-axes. They have the killing weight.

The hurt pack does not see them. The big one is at the edge of the clearing, reading the standing stones with the face of a thing that has found something it does not understand. The sky-reader is beside him. The thick-armed one is at the back of the pack, the axe on his hip, his face still flat, still afraid — but not of the green now. Of the clearing. Of the open ground. A thing that has been hunted reads open ground as the place the arrow finds you.

He is right.

The first Vorth comes out of the green at the thick-armed one. Not a charge — a taking. The great gray-silver arm sweeps low, the hand-axe aimed at the knee, and the thick one goes down hard, the axe on his hip clanging off the stone, and the sound he makes is the sound of a thing that has been hit by something it cannot read. The second Vorth is at the sky-reader, and the third is flanking the pack to cut the retreat, and the green has gone from cleared ground to killing ground in the space of a breath.

But the thick-armed one is not done.

He gets the axe up from the stone. Not the pretty swing of a trained fighter — the ugly, short, desperate swing of a thing that is on its back with a Vorth warrior on top of it and one chance to stay alive. The axe catches the Vorth in the shoulder, and the Vorth makes a sound I have never heard one of the tribe make — surprise, pain, the rage of a thing that expected the two-legged prey to fold — and the thick one rolls, bleeding, and gets his feet under him.

The big one moves. He does not freeze. The body notes it — the way the holding-center reads the attack and does not go to pieces, the way his hands find the short iron at his belt and he puts himself between the sky-reader and the second Vorth. He will not win this. No human on this island wins a close fight with a prime Vorth warrior. But he puts his body in the way with the same set his face had when the dying went bad on the spit: *I drew the line, and the thing that is coming has to come through the line first.*

The Vorth sweeps the short iron aside and bowls the big one over. The sky-reader goes down under the back-swing.

And then I am there.

---

I come out of the canopy onto the killing ground between the tribe and the pack, and the sound I make is the sound I have not made since the challenge — the rasp, the cough with the growl riding its back, the full deep-chest killing-sound the still place holds back. I do not hold it back this low. I let the whole of it out, and it goes across the high stones the way the thunder goes, and every Vorth on the ground goes still.

The three warriors read me. The king. The crown. The thing on the lip that the strength walked off of and left standing. They do not know why the king is between them and the two-legged prey, and the not-knowing stops them. The old male on the far stack is up, the scarred head turned, reading the thing on the ground that is the king standing in front of the threat. The seer will be reading it too, from wherever the seer keeps its watch. The whole high-stone tribe has stopped to read the thing I am doing, and I do not have a word for it, and I am doing it anyway.

I put myself between the Vorth and the pack.

The first warrior — the one the thick-armed one axed in the shoulder — reads my front and backs off. Low. The head down. Not the submission of a thing that has lost, the *reading* of a thing that knows the king is the king and the king has said *stop.* The second warrior is slower. It has the sky-reader on the ground and the big one is trying to get up between them, and I step into the Vorth's front the way I stepped into Gull-Marr's front — low, inside the reach, reading where it stands.

I make the still-sound. The gentling. The Kala's sound, the one that says *be still, nothing comes.* Not to the pack. To the warrior. The way the Kala made it over me when the fever-fog took me under. The warrior reads it. The great dark eyes reading the king who holds the line the Kala taught — *nothing comes* — and the warrior backs off the sky-reader, slow, the way a thing backs off a kill it has been told to leave.

The third warrior flanking the retreat goes still. Reads the other two. Reads me. Turns and goes back into the green, silent, the way the tribe came.

I stand on the killing ground between the tribe and the pack and the breath goes through me like a tide.

---

The hurt pack lies on the stone. The thick-armed one has the axe up, bleeding from the knee the Vorth swept, his face white, the flat gone to a thing past flat — past fear, into the place where the body stops reading and starts dying. But the axe is up. The thick one is the kind of thing that holds the axe up inside the dying.

The big one is on his feet. He got up between the sky-reader and the Vorth and stayed up. His face has blood on it — hers or his, I cannot tell — and he is reading me. Reading the thing that came out of the canopy and stopped the killing. The thing that stands between him and the great gray-silver warriors the way he stood between the sky-reader and them.

His eyes are wide. Not with fear. With the thing that comes after fear, when the fear reads something it does not have a shape for and the body goes still to let the head catch up.

The young one is in the tree above. He went up when the Vorth came — the climbing instinct, the runner's fast answer to the ground going bad — and he is pressed to a branch with his face white and his hands dug into the bark and he is looking down at the thing on the stone that is shaped like him and is not him and is standing between the pack and the monsters.

The steady-handed woman has the sky-reader. She is on the ground beside her, hands moving, reading the wound where the Vorth's back-swing caught the head. The sky-reader is breathing. The blood is scalp-blood — the kind that looks worse than it is, the kind that runs and runs from a cut that is not deep. She will live.

I stand on the stone and let the big one read me.

He sees the scars. He sees the body that does not match the island — too light, too scarred, too wrong for the green that made it. He sees the disc at my chest, the Eye-and-Thorn bound in worked metal, and his eyes change. The change is small and the body catches it because the body catches everything: the pupil widening, the breath holding, the thing that happens in a face when it sees something it has seen before — not this, not here, but the *shape* of this, the shape of the disc, the shape of the eye.

He has seen the Eye-and-Thorn before.

I do not have time to press at this. The Vorth are in the green, reading, and the tribe is on the high stones, reading, and I am standing on the killing ground between them with a pack of hurt two-legged things that the green is eating and the tribe is hunting, and the count of things I have to hold is going up faster than I can hold them.

I turn to the big one. I put my front to him. I do the thing the body does when it has no words — I set the jaw, I turn toward the green wall, I turn back to him, and the meaning is the only meaning I have: *not here. Not this ground. This ground is taken.*

He reads it. I do not know how — the body says he reads it, the way the old male read the wrist lifted on the bank, the way the tribe reads everything, body to body, the meaning under the sound. He reads the king on the killing ground telling him *go* and he does not understand it and he understands it.

He gets the pack up.

---

They go.

They go bleeding and hurt and looking back at the thing on the stone that is shaped like them and turned the monsters aside, and the thick-armed one goes with the axe up the whole way, the knee dragging, and the young one drops from the tree and is at the big one's side with his hand on the big one's arm, and the steady-handed woman has the sky-reader's arm over her shoulder and the blood in her hair, and they go back the way they came, toward the green, toward the shore.

I watch them off the high stones. The Vorth watch them from the green. Nobody follows. I have said *stop* and the stop holds, the way it held when the seer's finger came down on the lip, the way it holds for the king.

But the big one looked back. At the clearing. At the high stones. At the thing that stood between his pack and the killing. And the look was not the look of a thing that is leaving.

It was the look of a thing that has found the thing it was looking for.

---

I go up to the high stones when the pack is gone.

The seer is there. The pale finger is up before I reach the seat, and it does not point at the green where the pack went. It points at me. At the blood on my arms where the Vorth's reach scraped the skin coming through — already closing, the edges pulling shut, the wrong-fast closing the seer has read and wondered over.

The meaning-sound is low and has the question folded into it, and the question is not *why did you stand between.* The seer has read that from the body. The question is older.

*What are you.*

I do not have the word. The seer does not have the word. The finger lifts off my chest and the seer looks at me long and folds down, and we keep the wondering on our separate shelves, the way we have always kept it.

But the thing that set its jaw in me when the big one looked back at the high stones is harder than the wondering. The body reads it plain, the way the body reads everything, before the head can shape it.

He will come back. The big one with the holding-center and the face that changed when he saw the Eye-and-Thorn. He will come back, and not for shelter, and not for water. He has seen the thing that keeps the monsters back. He has seen the value of it. And the body reads, with the bone-sure knowledge that has kept me alive on this island since the first low, that the thing the big one wants is not the thing the big one will say he wants, and the difference between the two is the thing I will have to read before he comes.

The tribe settles. The Kala folds down beside me on the seat and the keeping arm comes over and the still-sound falls, and I lean into it, and the leaning is full and the leaning is not enough, and outside the high stones the green holds a hurt pack that is walking back toward the shore with the thing they found on the high stones behind them and the value of it in front of their eyes.

The count has started. I can feel it in the set of my own jaw, the way Marrow feels it in mine. The lows between now and the thing the water has not shown me yet are numbered, and the number is going down.

# Chapter 21: The Sigil

The pack goes back to the spit. I follow.

---

Not close. Not the way I followed the Hesh, poured into the green at killing distance, reading every footfall. Far. The body distance of a thing that is watching its own ground and not hunting. The distance that lets me read the direction without being read back.

They go back the way they came — bleeding, hurt, the thick-armed one dragging the knee, the sky-reader on the steady-handed woman's shoulder, the young one flanking through the canopy. The big one at the front, reading the green the way I read it: badly. He does not have the feet for it. He has the instinct — the heading-instinct, the thing that says *this way, not that way* — but he reads with eyes, and the eyes miss what the feet know. He walks them through a rot-patch. He walks them past a stand of the spine-rooted thing that opens at dark. He does not see the channel-slit where the Mure wait at the turn of the water. But the green does not take any of them on the walk back. The green is slow. The green has time.

I hang behind them at three hundred body-lengths and I do not know what I am doing.

The body knows. The body has known since the big one's face changed when he saw the Eye-and-Thorn. The body has been counting since then, the way it counts the tide and the lows and the breaths of the thing it watches. The count says: *he is going back to the spit. He is going to make a plan. The plan has the disc in it.*

The head says: *go up to the crown. Go up to the Kala. You have a home. You have a seat the strength walked off of and left you on. The hurt pack is dying on the shore and the green will finish it and the thing the big one saw on your chest will go into the mud with the rest of them. Let it go. Let the green have them. You have everything you need on the high stones.*

The far mouth says: *you do not.*

---

I go back to the high stones at dark.

The Kala is awake. She makes the still-sound into the dark air of the low — *be still, you are held, nothing comes* — and the sound finds me at the edge of the seat where I sit with my front to the seaward stack and my jaw set on the thing I cannot shape. She folds down beside me. The keeping arm comes over. The great dark eye reads my front the way it has read me since the fever-fog — slow, deep, the body-knowledge of a thing that has held me since I was the soft wrong thing on lent ground. She reads the set of the jaw. She reads the front turned to the water. She makes the still-sound again, and this time the *nothing comes* has a question in it, the way the seer's meaning-sound has a question in it — *what is it. What has come.*

I put my hand on the Kala's arm. The great warm skin. The hide that has known the green and the rot and the killing and the keeping for longer than I have been alive. I lean into the arm and the leaning is full and the leaning is not enough and the two things are the same thing and I cannot set one down.

She does not know about the pack on the spit. She does not know about the big one's face when he saw the Eye-and-Thorn. She does not know that the count has started in me, the low-count, the tide-count, the thing that says: the water brought something to this island and the something is going to take something back.

I hold the name under my tongue. *Rook.* The one un-stolen thing. And I hold the disc at my chest, the worked metal, the eye looking out. And I hold the brand on the back of my neck, the old owning-weight, the hand that closed and never let go.

Three things. The name no one can catch. The eye the big one recognized. The brand the big one does not know is there.

The count says: these three things are going to collide. And the ground under all three of them is the thing I cannot read.

---

Two lows I stay on the high stones.

Two lows of the crown and the Kala and the bold heart warm on my shoulder and the tribe easy under the new law. Two lows of the seer watching me from the far edge, the pale finger lifting and falling, the meaning-sound with its question folded in. Two lows of Marrow at my flank, reading the set of my jaw, the turn toward the water, the counting that goes on under the skin where he cannot reach.

He tries once. The second low, in the half-light. He comes to where I sit at the edge of the seat and he puts his curled hand on my knee and leaves it there. Not the nudge. Not the warning. The hand. The whole of the small wrong body saying the thing it has said since the den in the rot: *I am here. I am here. I am here.*

I put my hand over his. The curled fingers under my palm. The bones of a thing the tribe would have cast out, that I named and kept and set standing. The nearest thing to my own kind the green ever gave me.

And the far mouth says, low and plain: *not your kind. Not the shape of the hole.*

Marrow reads it. He reads the leaning in me that leans past him, past the crown, past everything the green gave me, toward the dying pack on the spit. His hand closes under mine. The curled fingers pressing into the palm. And his face does the thing it does when the reading has landed and the landing is bad: the high shoulder drops, and the eyes go to the green behind us, the way back, and the small wrong body folds in on itself like a thing bracing for the blow that comes from the direction you cannot face.

---

On the third low I go down to the spit.

I go alone. Marrow does not come. I leave him on the high stone with the Kala's arm over him and the bold heart at his shoulder, and I go down through my green the way I went down the first time — fast, silent, the body in the killing-distance place, reading every footfall. But the killing-distance is not what takes me. The thing that takes me is the leaning-distance, the thing that leans toward the fire-smell on the wind.

The pack has rebuilt. Not on the death-ground — further up the spit, where the sand is dry and the air moves and the seep is thinner. The sky-reader found better ground. The steady-handed woman has the wounded under a hide-tent and the cook has the fire going and the count of the pack has held. No one has died since the high stones.

I lie above the camp in the green wall and I read them.

The big one and the sky-reader sit at the fire with a skin of hide stretched between them, and the sky-reader is drawing on it with a stick of charcoal — lines, shapes, the coast, the channels. She is mapping. I have done this in my head a thousand lows — the shape of the green, the course of the water, the seat of every den and every kill-ground. She is doing it on the hide because she does not have the feet for it and the head needs the map the feet cannot make. She is good. The lines are right. The shape of the coast is the shape I carry in my soles.

The big one is not watching the map. He is watching the steady-handed woman across the fire, and she is looking at something in her hands — a piece of worked metal, small, dark with age. She turns it in the firelight. And I see the eye.

Ringed. Open. The thorn through it.

Not the disc at my chest — a different piece, smaller, flatter, a token or a stamp, the same mark cut into a different thing. The steady-handed woman turns it and the firelight runs over the eye and the thorn and I taste the iron in my mouth and the drowned-deep place hums.

They have one. They have always had one. The Eye-and-Thorn is not a dead man's mark scratched on a skin and a disc on a dead island. It is a *living* mark. It belongs to something they know — something the big one recognized when he saw it on my chest, something the steady-handed woman holds in her hands and turns and does not flinch at.

The big one says something. Low. The steady-handed woman looks up. She says something back — short, certain, the sound of a thing that has been asked a question and has the answer and does not like it. She holds the token out to the big one. He takes it. Turns it. His face does the thing it did on the high stones — the widening, the breath-hold, the shape-recognition.

Then he looks at the green wall where I am. Not at me — he does not have me. But at the green. At the wall of it. And his mouth makes a sound I cannot read at this distance but the body reads the shape of: *there.* The thing that had the disc. The thing on the high stones. The thing that keeps the monsters back.

The sky-reader looks up from her map. She says something. The big one shakes his head. She says it again — sharper, the argument-shape, the two-trusting-minds-being-wrong-at-each-other. He shakes his head again. But the head-shake is not *no.* The head-shake is *not yet.* The not-yet of a thing that has read the ground and made a plan and knows the plan needs time.

They argue. The thick-armed one comes to the fire and stands behind the big one and listens and his face is the flat face, the held-blow face, and he says one short sound and the big one looks up at him and the two of them hold a look that has weight in it — the weight of two things that have done hard things together and are deciding whether the next hard thing is worth it.

The big one nods. The thick-armed one nods. The sky-reader puts the charcoal down. The argument is over and the argument has been won by the thing the big one saw on the high stones and the thing the steady-handed woman holds in her hands, and the value of both of these is the thing they have agreed on.

I press at the drowned-deep place. The humming. The sigil at my chest warm against the breastbone. *I know this. This is mine.* And the *this* is wider now — not just the disc, not just the eye, but the pack that carries the same mark. The pack that has the same mark and saw it on my chest and is making a plan around it.

---

I go back to the high stones.

I go up to the seat and the Kala is there and Marrow is there and the seer is there, and none of them have the word for the thing I have read on the spit, and I do not have the word either.

But the body has the shape of it. And the shape is: a line drawn from the big one's face when he saw the eye to the steady-handed woman's hands on the token to the argument at the fire to the nod at the end. A line that runs through all of them and lands on the thing at my chest and the thing under the crown.

They are going to come back. Not for the green. Not for the water. Not for the high stones. For the thing that keeps the monsters back. The thing with the disc. *Me.*

And the body reads the second thing, the thing under the first thing, the thing the head does not want to hold: the far mouth leans at it. The far mouth leans at being wanted by the shape of the hole, even when the wanting is the wanting of a thing that takes, because the being-wanted is the shape the hole was cut from, and the hole does not read the difference between being wanted and being kept. The hole reads *mine* and leans.

I hold the name under my tongue. I hold the disc at my chest. I hold the crown behind my teeth.

---

Voss-Marr comes to the far edge of the high stone in the half-light.

He does not come close. He has not come close since the challenge — since the day I leapt his birthright and did not take his neck and the not-taking set between us like a stone neither of us can lift. He keeps the far edge. He keeps the not-looking. He keeps the silence that is louder than anything he could put on the air.

But this low he comes to the far edge and he stands there, and the prime blood in him reads the way I sit on the seat with my front to the water and my jaw set on the thing I cannot say, and the air off him is the thing it has been since the challenge — hard, flat, still. The held blow that went back down into him and set.

He reads me. I let him read. There is no hiding the set of the jaw, the turn toward the water, the count running under the skin. The king is leaning at a thing that is not the tribe, and the rival who was promised the seat reads the leaning the way he reads everything — with the body, with the blood, with the bone-knowledge of a thing that was raised to wear the crown and had it taken by a thing softer than every thing he was ever told he stands above.

He makes a sound. One. Short. Not a word I can read — the shape of it is older than the sounds I have stolen, older than the keeping-sounds, older than the asking-sounds. It is the sound the Vorth make when they have read the ground and the ground is bad. The warning-sound that is not a warning but a *reading aloud.* The sound that says: *I see what you are doing. I see where it goes. I will not be the thing that follows you there.*

I turn to him. The first time I have turned to him since the challenge. His face is the flat face — the prime-blood face, the heir's face, the face of a thing that was promised the seat and the green and the keeping and watches a softer thing throw it all away for a shape on the water.

And the body reads him. Reads the thing under the flat. Not the anger — the anger is the surface, the anger is the easy reading. The thing under the anger is harder. It is the thing I did not let myself read on the challenge day because the reading would have cost me the crown. It is this: he is afraid. Not of the boats. Not of the pack on the spit. He is afraid of the leaning. He has read the leaning in me the way Marrow reads it — the thing that pulls the king toward the water, toward the shape of the hole, toward the thing the green cannot close — and he knows where it goes. He knows because he was raised to wear the crown and the crown's first law is *the tribe is the thing* and the thing on the water is not the tribe and the king who leans at the not-tribe is the king who loses the tribe.

He is not wrong.

The far mouth leans at the water and the near part of me knows — has known since the boats — that the leaning is the thing that will undo everything the high stones gave me. The crown, the Kala, the bold heart, Marrow. The leaning is the hand reaching in across the water, and the hand does not give back what it takes.

Voss-Marr reads the leaning in me and stands at the far edge with the prime blood gone quiet and the flat face saying the thing the held blow has been saying since the challenge: *I will not follow you into this. The tribe is the thing. You are forgetting the thing. And the forgetting will cost us all.*

He turns. He goes to the sleeping-shelf with the prime blood. He does not look back.

And the body holds the reading the way it holds everything Voss-Marr gives it — set down beside the crown, beside the challenge, beside the neck I did not take. The rival who saw it coming. The one who will not come when the water brings the thing it brings, and whose not-coming will be the thing I earned the day I leapt his birthright and left him whole.

Because the thing the water will bring is the shape of the hole. And the shape of the hole is the only shape the green could not close. And Voss-Marr knows it, and stands at the far edge of the tribe, and will not cross the distance between us to warn me again.

The count runs under the skin. The number is going down.

# Chapter 22: The Night Raid

They come for the Kala.

---

I do not read it in time.

The count said they would come for me — for the thing with the disc, the thing that keeps the monsters back. The count was wrong. The count was the count of a thing that leans at its own wanting and reads the world through the wanting. The count did not account for the big one reading the high stones with the eyes of a thing that has hunted value in every port and every sea, and the value the big one read was not the two-legged thing with the disc.

It was the Vorth.

A single great-ape Vorth — silvered, vast, the hide worth a lord's ransom in the fighting pits or the collector's cages of the old cities. The thing the sky-reader drew on her map. The thing the steady-handed woman priced in her head when she saw the warriors on the killing ground. The big one read the high stones the way I read a herd — counting, learning where it bunches thick, where it thins — and what he saw was not a tribe. What he saw was *stock.*

And the Kala is the slowest. The Kala is the vastest. The Kala folds down at the edge of the high stones in the dark, the keeping-arm over whatever is nearest, the great slow tide of her breathing the long even breathing of a thing that has been the mother of the tribe for longer than I have been alive and has forgotten how to be afraid.

They come in the dark before the low. Four of them. The big one is not there — the big one draws the plan and stays at the spit, because the big one is the holding-center and the holding-center does not walk into the thing that will break the center if it goes wrong. The thick-armed one leads. The young one is the runner. Two others I have not read close — hard-faced, quick-handed, the kind that does the taking.

They have nets. Rope nets, weighted at the edges, the weave tight enough to hold a thrashing thing. They have the short iron. They have a plan — the sky-reader's plan, drawn on the map, the way in and the way out through the green where the canopy thins at the landward edge of the high stones.

They come through my green in the dark and I do not hear them.

I hear everything in my green. Every footfall, every breath, every shift of weight in the canopy. But the thick-armed one has learned something in the lows on the spit. He moves the way a thing moves that has been taught by the green what the green kills — careful, placed, the weight on the flat of the foot, the breath held and let through the nose. Not silent the way I am silent. Silent enough. Silent enough for a four-thing raid in the dark before the low when the tribe is sleeping and the king is on the seaward stack reading the water for the thing the king fears is coming for him.

The king was wrong. The king was reading the wrong thing.

---

Marrow hears them.

Not the thick-armed one — Marrow does not hear the thick-armed one in time either. Marrow hears the Kala. The low sound she makes when the net goes over her — not the killing-sound, not the challenge. The *surprise* sound, the sound of a vast slow thing that has not been threatened in a thousand lows and does not have the sound for it ready in the great chest. A sound like the earth moving. The Kala comes up off the stone under the net and the weighted edges drag and the thick-armed one is there with the short iron and the rope, and the rope goes around the great wrists, and the Kala makes the sound again — louder now, the surprise going to something else, the thing under the surprise, the thing that says *I am taken* — and the sound goes across the high stones like a wave and hits me on the seaward stack and I am off the rock before the sound has finished.

Marrow is ahead of me. He has heard it first and he is already running — the rocking crooked gait, the wrong body at full speed, the high shoulder pumping, the curled hand closed to a fist. He is running for the Kala the way he runs for me when I have been too long in the dark, and he does not know what he is running toward and he runs.

He gets there first. He gets to the edge of the high stone where the Kala is being taken and he puts his small wrong body between the Kala and the thick-armed one and he makes the only sound he has — not the still-sound, not the name I gave him, the *other* sound, the sound I have never heard him make, the sound that comes out of a thing the order cast out when the thing it loves is being taken. It is small. It is torn. It is the sound of a body putting itself in front of a weight that will crush it because the body behind the weight is the body that kept it alive.

The thick-armed one does not hesitate. He backhands Marrow out of the way.

The small wrong body leaves the stone. Not far — a body-length, two — and hits the rock and rolls and the curled hand scrapes across the stone and the sound Marrow makes is the sound of a thing hitting ground wrong, the air going out of it, and he does not get up.

I am there.

---

The thing that comes out of the canopy onto the high stone is not the king.

I know this the way I know the tide — from the outside, from the body that watches itself do a thing the head has not agreed to. The king set the jaw and read the ground and counted the lows. The thing on the high stone does not count. The thing on the high stone is the thing the still place holds back, the thing under the crown, the thing that has wanted to be the hand since the hand was first laid on the back of the neck.

The Red comes up.

Not the omen-pulse the seer reads, not the slow warm wrongness under the blood. This is the *other* thing — the thing I have never let all the way up, the thing the second mouth feeds on, the thing I held back on the king's seat when the flat eye was on me and the wanting was bright and hot. The thing I did not feed.

I feed it.

The sound that comes out of me is not a human sound. It is not the rasp, not the cough, not the falls. It is the sound the Hesh made when it came through the green on the moved line, the sound the old male made when the rot-tooth went bad and the fever took the brain — the deep-chest killing-sound of a thing that has come all the way out from under the still place and is not holding any part of itself back.

The young one is the first to see it. He is in the canopy above the raid, the runner's place, and he looks down and the face he makes is the face of a thing that has been afraid of the green since it beached and has just found out the green was right. His mouth opens. No sound comes out. The body locks. The branch he is on sways.

The thick-armed one turns from Marrow's body on the stone and reads me and the axe comes up — fast, trained, the muscle-knowledge of a thing that has fought in tight spaces — and I go through him.

Not around. Not under. *Through.*

The speed is wrong. The speed is the thing the Red brings up from the place under the blood, the thing that is not muscle-speed and not wind-speed but the speed of a body that is being run by something older than the body. I take the thick-armed one's axe-arm at the wrist and the wrist bends the wrong way and the axe drops, and the other two are turning, the rope and the net in their hands, and I am at the first one before the net is off the Kala and the short iron in my hand is the short iron out of his hand and he goes down.

The Kala is making the sound. The deep-chest sound, the *I am taken* sound, and the rope is on her wrists and the net is on her back and she is pulling — the vast slow strength of her pulling against the rope — and the rope is holding because the rope was made for things that are strong and the maker of the rope knew what strong was.

I go for the rope. The second hard-faced one puts himself between me and the Kala and he has a blade — shorter than the short iron, curved, the kind that opens things — and he is fast and he is trained and he is not ready for the thing I am this low. The blade opens my arm — I feel it, the hot bright pain of a thing that cuts, the blood coming — and the wound does not slow me because the Red is up and the Red does not read the body's pain the way the body reads it. The cut is deep and the blood runs and the edges are already pulling shut when I take him by the throat and put him on the ground.

The young one drops from the canopy. He drops behind me, between me and the edge of the stone, and he has a blade — small, shaking, the hand that holds it not a fighter's hand — and he is making a sound, a word, the same word over and over, the body reads the shape of it even past the Red: *please, please, please.* His face is white and wet and the blade shakes and he is fourteen lows old maybe, maybe younger, and his eyes are the eyes of a thing that has just found out that the world has things in it that cannot be read and cannot be fought and do not stop.

The Red says: *take him.* The Red says: *the throat, the wrist, the thigh, the thing that is between you and the Kala, break it.* The Red is the thing under the blood that has no word and no law and no crown, and it runs the body like the hand ran the body in the hold — owning, certain, the grip that says *you are a thing and the thing is mine.*

I look at the young one. The blade. The shaking hand. The eyes.

And the still place — the thing behind the Red, the thing that held the line on the king's seat, the thing that did not feed the second mouth on Gull-Marr when the neck was there — the still place comes up behind the Red and reads the young one and reads the blade and reads the age of the thing in front of me and says one word, low, the way the Kala says *be still*:

*No.*

I turn from the young one. I go to the Kala.

---

The rope is thick. The weave is tight. My hands are bloody and the short iron is in my grip and I saw at the rope and the rope gives, strand by strand, and the Kala pulls and the net shifts and the great dark eye finds me over the shoulder and reads me — reads the Red still up, reads the blood on the arm where the cut is already closed, reads the thing her boy has become — and the still-sound comes out of the great chest. *Be still. Be still. Nothing comes.* Not to me. To the Red. The mother talking the beast out of the boy the way she talked the fever out of him on the bank.

But there are more of them. Not four — more. The big one was at the spit, but the big one is not at the spit. The big one is at the edge of the green with the rest of the pack — the sky-reader, the steady-handed woman, the cook, the others — and they have the Kala's trail marked and the rope and the chain and the plan, and the plan was never four raiders. The plan was four raiders and then the rest.

They come out of the green with the chains.

I fight. The Red runs the body and the body does things I cannot count later — the short iron and the teeth and the weight thrown low and sideways, the law of pulling the ground from under the strength. I break the first chain. I break the second. The thick-armed one is on me with the bad wrist and the weight and I put him down again, hard, and he does not get up this time, and the Kala is pulling the net and the net is half-gone and the rope is going and—

The third chain goes around my arm. The fourth goes around the Kala's neck.

And the nets come from behind — not the raid pack, the *rest*, the ones I did not see because the Red had me reading the four in front — and the nets come over the Kala from the landward side, weighted, layered, and the great body goes down under the weight of them, slow, the way the tide goes down, and the still-sound dies half-made in the great chest.

I go down on top of her.

Not the fighting-down — the *holding.* The body curling around the Kala's neck where the chain is, the arms going around the great head, the front pressed to the hide where the gentling-sound lives. Shielding. The thing the Kala did for me on the bank when the fever took me under — the body over the body, the keeping-arm over the thing that is kept. I do it with the chains on my arm and the blood on my front and the Red still up under the skin, and I will not let go.

The big one stands at the edge of the stone and reads the thing on the ground. The boy curled around the chained beast, the arms around the great head, the body making the shape of the keeping-arm the Kala taught him. And the thing on his face is not the face of a thing that has taken a score.

It is the face of a thing that has seen something it did not expect to see and cannot stop seeing.

He makes a sound. Low. The others stop. The nets settle.

And the small one comes.

Marrow. Off the stone where the thick-armed one hit him. Not dead — the body reads him through the Red's fading edge, the chest going, the curled hand twitching against the rock. He is up. Wrong — the high shoulder riding wrong, the gait listing, blood on the face where the rock took the skin. But up. And coming. Not away — *toward.* The small wrong body hauling itself across the stone toward the thing that chained him and the thing he loves, and the sound it makes is the sound from before — not the still-sound, not the challenge, the *other* sound, the one I have never heard him make before this night and now twice in it. The sound of a thing that will not be left behind.
A hard-faced one moves to stop him. The big one makes a sound — short, sharp. *Let it come.* The big one has read the boy on the ground curled around the beast, and the small one crawling toward the boy, and the thing the boy did when the small one was hit — the Red, the killing-speed, the going-through. The big one calculates: the small one is leverage. The thing that makes the boy comply. The thing that makes the killing-machine go still when the chain goes on.
Marrow reaches me. He puts the curled hand on my arm — the arm with the chain, the arm that is around the Kala's head — and he folds down against my side, and the small wrong body is warm against the great warm body, and we are three things chained on the high stone in the dark. The boy and the runt and the beast, curled together, the one around the other around the other.
The bold heart comes last. The small fierce thing that rode Marrow's shoulder — it drops from the canopy where it fled when the nets came, and it lands on Marrow's back and grips the wrong-made spine with its claws and makes the sound it makes when it is afraid — high, sharp, the sound of a thing that has nowhere else to go. It grips. It does not leave.

The big one reads the three of us on the stone. The boy, the runt, the beast, the bold heart. His face does the thing it did when he saw the disc on the high stones — the widening, the breath-hold. He is seeing something he did not expect to see, and the seeing is changing something behind his eyes I do not have a word for.

The Red goes down slow under the still place. The still place comes up. The name is there under the tongue — *Rook* — and the brand is there on the neck, and the disc is there at the chest, and the Kala is there under the chains, breathing, alive, and Marrow is there against my side, breathing, alive, and the bold heart grips the wrong-made spine with its claws and will not let go. And the gentling-sound comes one more time, muffled by the net, aimed at the boy who held on.

*Be still. Nothing comes.*

Something has come. And I am counting the breaths between what has come and what comes next, and the number is not going down.

It is gone.

# Chapter 23: Caged

They put us in the dark of the boat and the dark smells like the hold.

---

Not this hold. Not the one that took me. This one is shorter, lower, the wood newer, the stink different — tar and salt and the blood of the pack the night raid cost them. But the body does not know *different.* The body knows *hold.* The body wears the word the way it wore the word on the beach when the boats came: the skin crawling at the neck, the gut clenched, the breath going short and shallow the way it went short and shallow in the other hold, in the dark, with the boy warm against my side.

The Kala is beside me. Chained at the neck to a ring in the hull, the great body folded into the space that is too small for it, the silver-gray hide dulling already in the wrong air. Marrow is against my other side — not chained, too small for the chains they had, but caged in a thing of iron bars that sits on the hull floor like a trap for a small animal. He is still. The high shoulder rides wrong — the rock-hit shifted something that was already shifted, and the curled hand is curled tighter than it has ever been, the fingers locked. The bold heart grips his hair and does not move. It has not made a sound since the hold closed over us.

The Kala makes the gentling-sound — low, falling, *be still, you are held* — and the sound is wrong in this space. The space eats it. The wood and the iron eat the gentling and give back the creak of the hull and the slap of the water against the planks and the sound of the pack moving above.

She makes it anyway. The way the Kala has always made it — because the making is the thing, not the hearing. Because the mother does not stop the gentling when the thing she is gentling cannot hear it.

And the mind goes back. Up through the hull, across the water, over the green to the high stones. The seat. The king's seat where the Kala folded down every dark with the keeping-arm over whatever was nearest. The seat is empty now. The tribe is on it without her — the seer with the pale finger pointing at nothing, the old male with the torn ear standing at the lip where the Kala used to lie, and Voss-Marr at the far edge. Voss-Marr, who read the leaning, who made the one warning sound, who said *the tribe is the thing* and watched the king forget it. He is on the high stones now with the tribe that has no mother and no king and no keeping-arm, and the far edge is his, and the silence between us is the silence of a thing he warned and I did not listen and the not-listening is what chained the Kala in the hull of this boat. The tribe is up there. The green is up there. The seat is empty. And the Kala is here, in the dark, making the gentling-sound at a boy who brought the hand down on all of them by leaning at the shape of the hole.

She makes the sound and I lean into the great warm side and the leaning is full and the leaning is not enough.

---

I do not eat.

The first low they lower a thing — a pot, a bucket, the dried-meat and the hard bread the pack eats — through the slat in the deck. It lands on the hull floor between me and the Kala and sits there. The Kala does not eat it. She has never eaten the pack's food. She does not know it as food. And I — I press my front to the hull and I do not touch it.

I am not refusing. The body is refusing. The body that ate the green raw and lived — the body that ate the rot-pig and the river-fish and the Hesh's liver and the fat of a thousand things the green killed — that body looks at the dried meat and the hard bread and does not open. The throat closes. The gut turns. The body knows *hold-food* from the first hold, the food they pushed through the slat in the other dark, the food the boy warm against my side ate and I did not because the body wore the word *owned* and the owned body does not eat the food the owner pushes through the slat. The owned body says: *I will not be the thing that eats what you give it and lives.*

The body has not forgotten. Twenty thousand lows on the island and the body has not forgotten the hold-food rule. It sits on the hull floor between me and the Kala and it sits and it sits.

---

They are above me. The pack.

I hear them through the deck-planks — the step and the voice, the step and the voice, the endless handling of things that only the upright pack does. They are working the boat. The sky-reader has the heading and the big one has the helm and the crew has the oars — fewer oars than they beached with, fewer crew, the night raid cost them — and the boat moves. The water changes under the hull. The channel-swell goes to the open-sea roll, and the hull creaks, and the Kala braces against the too-small space, and the gentling-sound comes less often.

Above me: the big one's voice. Low, steady, the holding-center holding. A call and a response — he calls, the crew responds, the boat adjusts. The rhythm of a thing that has done this a thousand times, the muscle-knowledge of the water, the way my feet have the muscle-knowledge of the green. He is good at it. The body reads it through the deck-planks and the body knows: this thing is good at the water the way I am good at the green.

And the thick-armed one. I hear him too — the heavy step, the short words, the command-shape. His wrist is bad. I broke it. The body knows the break — the angle, the give, the sound of the thing going wrong under the hand. He carries the bad wrist against his chest the way Marrow carries the curled hand. But he works. The body reads the working through the planks — no lighter for the break, no shorter, the thing that does the work anyway because the work is the work and the wrist is the wrist and the two do not speak to each other.

And the young one. Quick feet on the deck. The runner, the climber, the thing that goes up the mast and reads the horizon. I hear him go up — the scrape of the feet on the wood, the small sounds he makes with his mouth, the word he throws down to the sky-reader. He is alive. I did not take his throat when the Red said *take it.* He does not know how close it was. The body knows. The body holds the *how close it was* the way it holds the name and the brand and the cut on the arm that closed too fast.

---

The second low the food comes down again. New food — the cook's work, the smell of it different from the dried meat. Warm. Salted. Fish-stew, the kind the cook makes from whatever the catch gives him.

It sits on the hull floor. The Kala does not read it as food. I press my front to the wood.

But this low it is closer. The body notes it without agreeing to it. The stew-smell is in the hold-air, warm, salted, and the gut that has eaten the green raw and lived has a thing to say about the stew-smell that is not *no.* The gut says: *this is not the hold-food. This is the cook-food. This is the food the pack eats at the fire, the food the big one shares out, the food the holding-center portions fair.* The gut knows the difference because the body spent five lows reading the pack from the green wall and the reading included the food and the food was *fair.*

The body does not eat. But the body notes the difference, and the noting is a crack, and the crack is the shape of the thing the pack does without knowing it does it: the thing that holds together.

---

The third low. The fevers come for the pack.

I hear it through the deck. The cough — the wet tearing sound I heard on the spit, the chest-sound, the thing that means the green has gotten in. One of them first. Then two. Then the steady-handed woman's voice, sharp, giving orders, the command-shape of a thing that has held death back with her hands a hundred times and is trying again. She works above me. I hear her boots on the planks and the sound of her voice laying the orders down, and the orders have the set they had on the spit — professional, unsentimental, the hands going to the wound because the wound is there and the hands are the hands.

The big one's voice is quieter this low. The holding-center still holding, but the holding has the sound of a thing held too long. He is losing them again. The green got the pack on the spit and the green is getting the pack on the water, and the water does not care, and the boat goes on.

And I do not sicken. I lie in the hold with the Kala chained beside me and the air is the hold-air and the hold-air has the fever-seep in it — I can read it, the sweet wet rot-breath, the same air that killed the dead expedition, the same air that killed the three on the spit — and it does not touch me. The body lies in it and breathes it and the fever does not come. The wounds from the night raid — the cut on the arm where the blade opened me, the scrapes, the deep bruise where the chain hit — close and heal in the dark. The Kala's wounds do not close. The Kala dulls in the wrong air.

They notice. Not the big one — the steady-handed woman. On the fourth low the slat in the deck lifts and her face is there in the square of light, looking down at me, and she has the steady hands on the edge of the slat and her face is the face I read from the green wall: professional, unsentimental, the eyes going to the wound because the wound is there.

She reads the arm. The cut is closed. Four lows old, closed, the scar pink and clean. Her eyes widen. Not the fear-widen. The *reading*-widen. The thing a body does when it sees a thing it has studied and the thing is not doing what the study says it should do. She looks at the arm and looks at me and her mouth shapes a word I do not catch, and the slat closes, and her boots go quick across the deck to where the big one stands.

I hear them through the planks. Her voice. Low, urgent, the argument-shape. His voice. Lower. The question-shape. Her voice again — certain, the sound of a thing that has read the evidence and has the answer. His silence. The silence of a thing calculating the value of a boy whose wounds close in four lows in a hold full of fever-air.

The slat does not open again that low.

---

The fifth low.

The food comes down. The cook's stew. Warm. Salted. And beside the pot, a thing I have not seen — a strip of dried fruit. Small. Placed with care, the way a thing is placed that is *for* something. Not the pack's portion. A separate thing. A thing someone added.

I do not know who. The body does not ask. But the strip of fruit is sweet — the first sweet thing in my mouth since the green, since the fat-figs at the canopy edge, since the low the Kala first let me eat from her hand. The sweetness lands on the tongue and the body opens for it the way the body opens for water after the thirst, and the crack widens.

Above me, at the edge of the deck where the slat is, quick feet. The young one's feet. He was the one who placed it. The runner. The thing that climbs. The thing whose throat I did not take when the Red said *take it.* He has placed a strip of dried fruit beside the pot and gone, and the going was not brave, and the going was not bold. The going was the shape of a thing that has been hungry and knows what hungry is and sees a thing in a cage and puts food near it because the food is the only thing the hands can do.

I eat the fruit. The body opens for it and takes it and the sweetness lies on the tongue like a word I have not said in a thousand lows. And the crack in the body — the crack that is the shape of the thing the pack does when it holds together — goes deeper.

The Kala makes the gentling-sound in the dark. I press against the Kala. Marrow presses against me. The three of us in the too-small space — the great warm hide and the small wrong body and the bold heart gripping the hair, and the leaning is full and the leaning is not enough and the two things are the same thing and I cannot set one down.

---

The sixth low the big one comes below.

He ducks through the hatch and stands in the hold and reads me. Reads the Kala. Reads the chains and the food-pot — empty now, I have eaten — and the arm where the cut was, the scar pink and clean, six lows old and healed like a month should have passed.

He does not come close. He stands at the hatch and lets his eyes work and his face does the thing it does — the calculating, the weighing. But there is another thing in the face. The thing I read on the high stone when he put himself between the sky-reader and the Vorth. The thing I read when he put the hand on the young one's neck.

He crouches. Slow. The way you crouch by a thing that is hurt and might bolt. He is ten body-lengths from me. The Kala between us, chained, the great dark eye tracking him.

He says a sound. A word. I read the shape of it off his mouth and the body goes still.

It is not a word I know. But the shape of it — the shape the mouth makes — the body has the shape from before the green, from the warm hollow where the name was made. The shape of a word said to a thing that is small. The shape of the mouth that made my name over me before the ship.

Not the name. Not *Rook.* The shape of the *kind* of word. The kind a thing says to a small thing it is not going to hurt.

I do not know the word. I do not have the road from head to mouth that would throw it back. But the body reads the shape and the body holds still, and the big one reads the holding-still and his face does the thing it did on the high stone when he saw the disc.

He goes back up the hatch. The light closes. And the hold is dark again with the Kala's breathing and the creak of the hull and the water slap and the sound of the pack above, fewer now, the fevers taking hold.

I lean against the Kala. Marrow's curled hand finds my knee in the dark. The bold heart settles against his neck. The gentling-sound comes.

And the kind-sound the big one made lies in me beside the fruit-sweetness, beside the word-shape. But I do not let it in. Not the crack. The crack is Fenn's — the fruit, the small secret thing placed by a thing that was hungry and knows what hungry is. The crack is earned. The big one's kind-sound is not earned. The big one chained me. The big one's crew hit Marrow. The big one took the Kala off the high stones and put her in the hold where the air is killing her. The kind-sound does not erase the chain.

But the body holds it. Beside the fruit. Beside the name. And the body does not throw it back, because the body does not throw back things that are given without the brand-grip, and the kind-sound was given without the brand-grip, and the not-throwing-back is a thing I do not have a word for.

The crack holds the fruit. The kind-sound lies beside it, outside the crack, waiting. And the body reads the thing the body always reads: that the world is not the green. That the things in the world do not divide into the tribe and the threat. That the big one is both — the chain and the kind-sound, the taking and the holding — and the body does not know how to hold both in the one skin without one of them going through the wall the other one built.

# Chapter 24: The Crossing

The pack dies by ones.

---

I count them through the deck. Not with ears — with the thing the body does when it has lain in a hold long enough to read the whole boat through the planks. Every step is a different foot. Every voice is a different shape. And when a foot stops stepping and a voice stops throwing, the body notes it the way the body notes a print going into the mud and not coming back.

The fevers take three more before the wind changes.

Three. The coughing one first — a young one, I think, from the quickness of the step that stopped. Then the other hard-faced one from the raid, the one I put on the ground by the throat, the one whose blade opened my arm. He dies of the fever, not the throat. The fever does not care what I did to him. The fever takes the body the fever finds. Then a third I have not read close — a body that stepped heavy and spoke low and is now silent.

The steady-handed woman works. I hear her boots. She is everywhere above — moving between the sick, the voice laying orders, the hands on the wounds that are not wounds but the body rotting from the inside. She cannot stop it. The body knows this from the sounds — the set in her voice that the big one's face had on the spit when the dying went bad. The holding-on-to-a-thing-you-cannot-hold set.

The cook works too. He boils the water. He makes the stew. He carries it to the sick, and the sounds he makes at them are the joke-sounds, the low bad sounds no one laughs at, and the not-laughing has affection in it, and the affection is the thing the dying hear last.

Until the low the heavy step stops.

Not the cook — one of the others. A body I have not read close, a crew-thing that stepped heavy and spoke low and hauled the rope without complaint. The fever took him the way it takes them — fast, the chest-sound, the drowning-from-the-inside. The cook is the one who carries the water to him. The cook's heavy step goes to where the crew-thing lies, and the joke-sounds come — low, bad, the sounds the cook makes at the sick — and this low the crew-thing does not answer them.

The young one goes to the crew-thing. The body hears it — the quick feet crossing the deck, the small sound the young one makes. A sound that is just the air going out of a body that has lost a thing it stood beside every low. The young one sits with the crew-thing. The cook stands over them both, and the joke-sounds are gone, and the big burned hands hang at his sides, and the cook's face has the thing the big one's face had on the spit when the dying went bad.

The crew-thing goes in the dark. The steady-handed woman wraps him in the sail-cloth and they slide him over the side, and the water takes him the way the water takes everything.

The pack that was thirty is seven.

The sky-reader has the heading. Her voice is up in the rigging with the young one, calling the wind, reading the water, the two of them doing the thing the body recognizes from the green — the heading-instinct, the thing that goes forward because forward is the only direction that has a shape. She does not sicken either. The body reads this: she is the kind that does not sicken easy. Some are. The green takes the easy ones first.

And the thick-armed one works with the bad wrist. No lighter. No shorter. The body hears him through the planks — the oar, the rope, the heavy step, the short command. He does not speak to me. He does not come below. He works the boat with the broken wrist and the flat face and the thing under the flat face that I read on the high stone when he stood over the big one calculating.

He is afraid. Not of the green. Not of the dying. Of the thing the big one is becoming — the thing the big one becomes when he reads the caged boy below whose wounds close in days. The thick-armed one reads the calculating and the calculating has a shape and the shape is *the debt* and the thick one is afraid of where the debt leads.

I do not know the debt. I do not have the word for it. But the body reads the fear off the thick one's step through the planks, and the fear is the shape of a thing that knows the holding-center is about to draw a new line.

---

The Kala dulls.

Low by low the great hide loses the silver. The gray goes flat. The skin that shone in the green half-light — that caught the canopy-dark and held it, alive, the living pelt of the thing that kept me — goes dead. The wrong air. The wrong water. The wrong food they push through the slat, which she does not eat, which sits on the hull floor and rots.

I try. The body tries. I take the fish-stew and I hold it to the great mouth and the mouth closes. I take the water and I pour it along the lip and the lip does not open. The Kala makes the gentling-sound less and less. The breathing slows. The great dark eye — the eye that read me on the bank, that read me on the king's seat, that read me in the dark of the hold every low since they chained us — the eye dims.

I press my front to the great side. The warmth is there. The tide of the breathing is there — in, out, in, out, slower, the long pauses between that mean a thing is going down into the place where the body does not come back from. I count the breaths. The way I counted the lows on the island. The way I counted the steps between the den and the kill-ground. The body counts because the counting is the thing the body does when it cannot do anything else.

And the Red stirs.

Not the killing-Red. Not the thing that came up on the high stone and went through the thick-armed one like water through a channel. The *other* thing. The thing the seer's finger found on the breastbone. The thing under the blood that is older than the crown. The thing that pulsed once in the pit of the fever-fog and once on the king's seat and once in the hold of this boat in the dark. The thing that has no word.

It stirs when the Kala's breathing goes long. It stirs the way a thing stirs that has been asleep and hears a sound it knows. And the warmth comes up — not the body-warmth, the *blood*-warmth, the deep-down warmth that closes the wounds too fast and rings the swarms off by a hand's width. It comes up and it goes into the front I press against the Kala's side, and the Kala's breathing — for a breath, for two — steadies.

Then it goes down again. The warmth goes down. And the Kala's breathing goes long again. And the body reads the thing the seer read with the pale finger: the warmth is there, and it is real, and it is mine, and I do not know how to make it stay.

---

The steady-handed woman comes below on the fifth low.

Not the big one — her. She ducks through the hatch with the steady hands carrying things — a blade, a pot of the paste she makes, a strip of clean cloth. Professional. The hands going to the work because the work is there. She does not look at the Kala. She looks at me. At my arm.

The cut. The blade-wound from the night raid — the one the hard-faced one opened from elbow to wrist. She reads it from three body-lengths away, and her face does the thing her face does: the reading-thing, the professional-thing, the hands-going-to-the-wound thing. Except the wound is not a wound anymore. Five lows old, closed, the scar pink and clean, the edges pulled shut in a way no wound she has ever seen pulls shut in five lows.

She crouches. She takes my arm. I let her take it — the body reads the professional in her hands, the way the hands go to the wound the way my hands go to a kill, automatic, sure. She turns the arm. Her fingers find the scar. She presses. The pressure is clinical — not gentle, not rough, the pressure of a thing that is reading the body through the fingertips the way I read the ground through the soles.

Her face changes.

Not the fear-change. The *fascination*-change. The change of a thing that has spent its life closing wounds and has never seen one close like this. She looks at the scar and she looks at me and her mouth makes a sound — a word, low, thrown up at the hatch. The big one's face appears in the hatch-light. She shows him the arm. The five-low scar that should be a five-week wound. The big one reads it. His face does the calculating-thing.

They make sounds at each other — low, fast, the argument-shape, the two-minds-being-wrong-at-each-other. She makes the case. Her hands on the scar, her voice certain, the voice of a thing that has read the evidence and has the answer. He listens. He calculates. The steady-handed woman's hands go from my arm to the other wounds — the scrapes, the bruises, the deep-tissue damage from the chains — and every one of them is days ahead of where it should be. She reads them the way she reads the fevers above, and the reading is the same: the body is doing something she cannot explain and she does not like things she cannot explain.

She goes above. The argument continues on the deck — her voice and the big one's voice, low, the shape of a thing being weighed. The thick-armed one's voice joins — short, flat, the thing he says when the answer is simple and the answer is *throw it overboard.* The big one overrules him. *The captain's word.*

The steady-handed woman does not come below again. But the food changes. The next low the pot comes down with the strip of dried fruit beside it — placed with care, the professional-care of a thing that has read a body and is adjusting the feeding. She has not softened toward me. She has *priced* me. The body reads the difference and the difference is the difference between the Kala's gentling and the big one's holding: one is warmth, the other is value. Both keep you fed. Only one keeps you warm.

---

The big one comes below every low now.

Not to the Kala. To me. He crouches at the hatch and he makes sounds at me — words, low, steady, the holding-center voice, the voice the big one uses on the pack when the pack is going bad. He does not reach for me. He does not come close. He crouches at the hatch and he talks the way the Kala makes the gentling-sound — because the making is the thing, not the hearing. Because the holding-center does not stop holding when the thing it is holding cannot hear it.

I do not know the words. I have not had the road from head to mouth since the green took it. But the body reads the shape of the sounds the way it reads the shape of the Vorth sounds, the way it reads everything — body to body, the meaning under the word. And the meaning under the big one's words is the same meaning the hand on the young one's neck carried: *you are mine and I am yours and the thing that is coming for us has to come through that first.*

Not the brand-meaning. The other one. The one I saw in the camp on the spit. The one I leaned at through the green wall.

The crack widens.

---

On the ninth low the wind dies and the boat sits on the flat water and does not move. The heat comes down. The air in the hold goes thick and wet and the fever-seep thickens with it. Another one dies above — a quick foot-stop, a silence, the cook's voice, low, the joke-shape that no one laughs at. The cook is still standing. The cook does not sicken. The big burned hands keep the pot on the fire and the stew in the buckets, and the not-sickening of the cook is the thing the pack does not name but reads — the cook is the cook, the fire is the fire, and the fire does not go out because the cook does not go out.

The big one comes below. This low he comes all the way in. He does not crouch at the hatch. He stands in the hold, five body-lengths away, and reads the Kala on the floor and the boy against her side. The Kala's breathing is bad. The pauses between breaths are long enough to hold a whole word in. The big one reads this. His face does the calculating thing.

Then he crouches beside the Kala. Not me — *her.* He puts his hand on the great hide the way the steady-handed woman puts her hands on a wound — careful, reading. The Kala's eye tracks him. The big dark eye in the dulling hide, reading the thing that chained her, reading the thing that took her off the high stones, reading the two-legged shape that means *hand* and *hold* and *owned.*

He takes his hand off. He looks at me. And his face does a thing I have not seen on him — it cracks. Not the holding-center crack, the professional crack, the calculating crack. The *other* crack. The crack of a thing that has done a thing it thought was right and is reading the cost of the thing on the body of the thing it took it from.

He stands. He goes to the hatch. He stops. He turns back.

He undoes the chain on my arm.

Not the Kala's chain. Mine. The iron falls off the wrist and the wrist is free and the blood goes back into the hand and the hand is mine again. He did not have to do this. The body reads the cost of it — the big one has given the caged thing a weapon, and the caged thing broke his enforcer's wrist and put two of his crew on the ground, and the giving is a risk the holding-center should not take.

He takes it. He looks at me. And the sound he makes is low and steady and it has the shape of a word I almost — *almost* — have the road to catch.

Then he goes up the hatch and the light closes and I am free in the hold with the Kala chained beside me, and the free hand goes to the Kala's side and the warmth comes up again — the blood-warmth, the deep-down thing — and I hold it there against the great dulling hide, and the Kala's breathing steadies, and the steadying is a breath longer than the last one, and the breath after that, and I count every one.

The crossing is not over. But the count has changed. It is not going down to zero. It is going — almost, barely, by the width of a breath — back up.

# Chapter 25: Property Again

We come to the place the boats go.

---

The water changes first. The flat open swell that has been under the hull for lows I have lost count of goes shallow, goes green, goes thick with the smell of land. Not my land. Not the green-smell, the rot-sweet, the canopy-dark. A different land-smell. Cold stone. Smoke that is not wood-smoke. The iron-tang of a thing done to metal that has no name in my green.

The Kala reads it through the hull. The great body stirs — not the gentling-stir, the *reading*-stir. She knows the wrong-smell. She knows this is not the place the green made her. The breathing that steadied on the crossing goes uneven again, and I press against her side and the blood-warmth comes up and the breathing holds, barely, barely.

They take us up through the hatch.

The light hurts. Lows of the hold-dark and the deck-slant-light through the slat, and now the full open sky comes down and the eyes go shut and the body flinches. The hands on me are not the thick-armed one's hands. Strange hands. Many hands. The body reads them — quick, professional, the hands of things that have done this before, the lifting and the chaining and the moving of property from one place to another. Not the pack's hands. Not the holding-center's hands. The hands of the place.

I open my eyes.

---

The place is stone.

Not the green stone of my island — the high stone, the canopy stone, the living rock the green grows down over. Cut stone. Stone laid on stone by hands, the way the made hill was laid on stone under the canopy, only this is not a ruin the green is pulling down. This is a thing that is alive. Stone laid on stone, climbing, the made hill built up instead of overgrown — walls, and towers, and the flat faces of cut rock with the eye cut into them. The Eye-and-Thorn. Ringed, open, looking out of every flat face over the water the way it looked out of the hundred stone faces under the canopy.

Only the canopy is gone. The green is gone. The air is cold and carries no seep, no rot-breath. The sky is pale and the sun is small and the water that slaps the stone at the base of the made-hill is not warm.

The Kala comes up through the hatch on a sling and the hands of the place take her and lower her to the dock and the great body on the stone is the wrong-est thing I have seen since the first human walked out of the boat onto my spit. A thing of the green lying on cut stone in the cold air with chains on her neck, and the silver hide — what is left of it — going dead in the wind that has no warmth in it.

And the pack is there.

The big one stands at the edge of the dock with the sky-reader and the steady-handed woman and the cook and the young one and the thick-armed one and what is left of the crew — and the pack that was thirty when it beached is eight, maybe nine, and the eight are holding together the way they have always held together, shoulder to shoulder, the big one at the center. But their faces are wrong. The calculating is done. The thing they were calculating toward is here, and it is not the thing they thought it was.

A shape waits for them on the dock.

Tall. Narrow. Dressed in a thing of dark cloth that moves in the cold wind like something alive, the folds deep, the hands folded inside the sleeves the way a thing folds its hands when it wants you to know the hands are not working hands. The face is narrow too — long-jawed, clean-shaved, the skin the color of old parchment left in the wet. The eyes are the thing you read. They are not the big one's eyes — not the holding-center eyes, the eyes that hold a pack together. They are not the thick-armed one's eyes — not the flat eyes, the held-blow eyes. They are the eyes of a thing that has never held anything together and never thrown a blow and never needed to, because the thing the eyes belong to owns the ground everything stands on, and the owning is enough.

The patron reads the big one the way the big one read the high stones: weighing, pricing. But the weighing is different. The big one weighs the way a hunter weighs — reading the thing, learning it, going by what the body shows. The patron weighs the way a scale weighs — the thing on one side, a number on the other, and the number is the only reading that matters.

The eyes go to the Kala on the dock. They widen. Not surprise — *appreciation.* The kind a body shows when a thing is worth more than the body expected. The eyes run over the great dulling hide, the vast body, the silver that is left under the wrong-air damage, and the body reads the pricing happening behind them — the number going up, the number that means something in a place I do not know, in a currency I have never seen.

The eyes go to me.

And the eyes stop.

The narrow face changes. Not the fear-change, not the surprise-change. The *recognition*-change — and this recognition is deeper than the big one's. The big one saw the disc and *recognized* it. The patron sees the disc and *knows* it. The way the seer knows the eye-and-thorn. The way I know the run in the mud. The professional knowing of a thing that has spent its life reading the mark and has just found it walking on two legs in front of it.

The patron steps forward. The hands come out of the sleeves — long, pale, the fingers narrow, the hands of a thing that has never gripped an oar or an axe or a rope. The fingers go to the disc at my chest and the patron turns it the way the steady-handed woman turned the token on the spit — the firelight running over the eye, over the thorn — and the sound the patron makes is low and measured and has the shape of a word I do not know but the body reads the shape: *found.* The shape of a thing that has been looking for something and has found it and the finding has the weight of a long, patient search coming to an end.

The big one watches the patron's fingers on the disc. His face does a thing — the fast calculating, the ground-reading, the thing he does when the ground changes under the pack. He is reading the patron's recognition and the recognition is telling him something he did not know, and the something is changing the shape of the deal he came here to make.

And the body reads a thing the big one does not read, because the body reads it from the outside and the big one is inside it. The token the steady-handed woman held on the spit — the small flat Eye-and-Thorn she turned in the firelight — and the disc at my chest, and the eye on every wall of the made-hill, and the patron's fingers on the disc with the professional knowing of a thing that has been reading this mark its whole life. *They are all the same mark.* The token the crew carried. The disc the dead man scratched on the skin. The eye cut into the hundred faces of the made hill under the canopy. And the patron's mark. The same eye, the same thorn, the same knowing — the patron's knowing deeper than the big one's, deeper than the crew's, the knowing of a thing that sits at the center of the mark the way the seer sits at the center of the tribe.

The big one brought the crew here because the patron was the debt. But the patron is not just the debt. The patron is the *eye.* The patron is the thing the eye belongs to. And the big one did not know this when he drew the line, and the not-knowing is the thing that is killing the pack on the dock stone right now, because the line the big one drew was drawn around a thing that owns the ground the line is drawn on.

The body reads it. The body holds it the way it holds everything — beside the name, beside the brand, beside the crown. The patron is the eye. The eye owns the ground. And the boy with the disc is standing on the ground the eye owns.

The patron makes a sound at the big one. Low, measured, the voice of a thing that measures everything including its own voice. The big one answers — short, direct, the holding-center voice, the voice that draws lines. The sound goes back and forth. The argument-shape. But the argument is not the argument of two equals. The argument is the argument of a thing that drew a line and a thing that owns the ground the line is drawn on.

The patron shakes its head. Slow. The way Gull-Marr shook his head when the tribe read the new king. Not *no.* The head-shake of a thing that has already decided and is letting you see the decision after it is made.

And the hands come.

---

Not the pack's hands. The hands of the place. Things in iron helms with the short iron at their hips, a dozen of them, surrounding the pack on the dock. Not threatening — *positioning.* The way the Vorth warriors positioned around the challenge ground. The way the tribe positioned around the king. The positioning that says: *we have read the ground you are standing on, and the ground is ours.*

The big one reads it. His face does the thing — the fast calculating, the ground-reading. He puts himself between the helms and his crew the way he put himself between the Vorth and the sky-reader. His mouth opens.

The patron makes one sound. Short. The sound a thing makes when it is done with the argument it never agreed to have.

And the helms take the boat.

Not the pack. The *boat.* The thing the pack lived on, the thing the sky-reader read the wind off of, the thing the young one climbed and the cook fired and the thick-armed one worked with the bad wrist. The helms go aboard and the pack's things come off and the boat is no longer the pack's boat. The boat belongs to the place now.

The big one stands on the dock with his crew behind him and his face empty.

The patron makes another sound. The helms move. Not to the boat — to the *pack.* Hands on the big one's arms. Hands on the sky-reader. Hands on the steady-handed woman, the cook, the young one. The thick-armed one goes for the axe and three helms are on him and the axe comes off the hip and clatters on the dock stone and the thick one goes to his knees with a helm's fist in his hair and the bad wrist bent behind him.

And the sound the big one makes is not a command. Not a protest. It is the sound of a thing breaking — the holding-center cracking open, the line he drew around his pack dissolving on the dock stone under the hands of the thing he brought them to.

He brought them here. The body reads it through the cold air, through the chains, through the iron-helm hands. He brought them here because the patron was the debt, and the debt was the line he drew around his pack to keep them alive, and he thought the line would hold. He thought paying the debt would set them free. He gave the patron the caged boy and the silver beast and the disc and the Eye-and-Thorn, and he thought the giving would clear the line.

The patron never intended to clear the line. The patron intended to take everything.

---

They chain us in a cart. Me, the Kala, and the pack.

The pack that took me is chained beside the thing it took. The big one's wrists in the iron beside my wrists in the iron. The sky-reader's bound hands beside the steady-handed woman's bound hands. The cook and the young one and the thick-armed one with the bad wrist and the rest of the thinned crew, all of them in the cart, all of them chained, all of them reading the same thing I am reading: the city going up above us as the cart climbs, the made-hill of cut stone, the eye on every flat face looking out at the water, and the cold.

The thick-armed one catches my eye across the cart. His face is the flat face. The held-blow face. But the blow is not held this low — the blow has landed. The helm's fist split his lip and the blood is on his chin and the bad wrist is swollen dark and the flat face reads: *I told him. I told him this is where the debt leads.*

He is not talking to me. He is talking to the big one. And the big one is not reading him. The big one is sitting in the cart with his wrists in the iron and his face empty, reading the thing he has done to the pack he held together, and the reading is the worst thing I have seen a face do since the boy warm against my side went still in the first hold.

I know this face. I know it from the inside. It is the face of a thing that held something and let the something go — not by choice, by the ground going under it. The face the Kala would make if the keeping-arm came off. The face Marrow would make if the still-sound went out of him and the thing he kept was gone.

The young one is beside the big one. Small. Quick. The runner's body folded in on itself in the chain. His face is wet. He is not making a sound. He is fourteen lows old, maybe younger, and he is chained in a cart beside the holding-center that broke, and the breaking is the first thing he has read that the climbing and the running cannot outrun.

The steady-handed woman has her arm around the sky-reader. The two of them sitting in the chains the way the Kala and I sat in the hold — body beside body, the holding-together made of meat and bone in the absence of everything else. The cook sits with his burned hands in his lap and his face has no joke in it.

And the Kala lies in the cart behind us all, chained at the neck, the great body on the boards, the breathing that I counted on the crossing going long again. The blood-warmth is in my hands but my hands are chained and I cannot reach her. I press my back against the boards and the warmth goes out of my shoulders and into the wood between us, and it is not enough.

The cart climbs. The city rises. The eye looks out of every face of cut stone. And the pack that came to my island to take is taken, and the boy they took is chained beside them, and the beast they took is dying behind them, and none of us are free, and the green is far away, and the cold has no seep in it and no swarms in it and no canopy in it, and the body reads the plain thing under all the rest: *this is the place the boats go. This is where the hand lives. This is where the brand was made.*

The brand on my neck. The word *owned.* The thing the big one is learning now, on the dock stone, in the cart. The thing the Kala has never known until the chain went on. The thing the young one is reading for the first time with the wet face and the silent mouth.

*You are a thing and the thing is mine.*

The cart climbs into the city and the city closes over us like the green closes over the dead.

# Chapter 26: The Hold

Under the city there is a hole, and in the hole they put us.

---

The cart goes down. A ramp cut into the stone, going under the made-hill, the walls closing in, the sky going to a slit and the slit going to nothing. The air changes. Not the green-seep, not the rot-breath. The stone-breath — cold, wet, the smell of things that have lived and died in the dark. The cut-stone walls sweat. The chains rattle on the boards.

The hole opens.

It is not a hold. It is not a pit. It is a thing between — a vast low space under the stone, the ceiling lost in the dark above, the floor cut flat and draining to a channel in the center where the water runs slow and brown. Cells line the walls — iron bars, stone backs, the kind of cage that is not made to open again. Things are in them. Bodies. Some moving, some not. The body reads them the way it reads everything: by smell first, by sound, by the weight of the breathing. Some are human. Some are not.

The Kala goes into a cell at the far end. The cell is too small for her. The great body folds into it and the chains go to the wall and the cell door closes and the sound it makes is the sound of a thing that does not open again.

I go into the cell beside her. The iron between us — bars, thick as fingers, the cold iron that does not warm in the hand. I can see her through them. The great dark eye, tracking me. The gentling-sound, faint, the last of it, coming through the bars.

The pack goes into the cells across the channel. The big one, the sky-reader, the steady-handed woman — each in a cell, each in chains. The cook, the young one, the thick-armed one — the iron closes over them. The crew that is left, split among the cells, the pack broken into pieces by the iron.

And the patron stands at the center of the hold, on the walkway over the channel, and reads us the way a thing reads livestock. The narrow face going from cell to cell. Pricing.

---

The pricing takes a low. Maybe two. The body loses the count in the dark.

The patron goes cell to cell with the things in iron helms. The narrow face reads the bodies. Reads the muscle. Reads the scars. Reads the teeth — one of the helms pulls the cook's mouth open and reads the teeth, the way I read the teeth of a kill to place the age. The pricing is not kind. It is not cruel. It is *accurate.*

The patron stands on the walkway over the channel and watches. Not helping — watching. The narrow face reads the pricing the way it reads everything: measuring, measuring, the number going up or down behind the pale eyes. The patron's eyes come back to me. To the disc. Every low the eyes come back — the pull of the eye on the thing that owns the mark, the gravity of the search ended. The patron prices me differently than the others. Not higher or lower — *differently.* The others are priced as muscle, as skill, as use. I am priced as a thing. A thing whose value is not in what the body can do but in what the body *is.*

The body reads this and the body goes cold. The reading-cold. The cold of a thing that has been priced before, in a hold, by a hand. The patron is pricing me the way the hand priced me. Not as muscle. As property. The deepest price. The one that says: *this is mine, and the mine is forever.*

The sky-reader is priced first. The patron reads her face and her hands and makes a sound — short, the sound of a thing placed. Navigator. The helms take her out of the cell and she does not look back at the big one. She looks at the patron. Holds the pale eyes with her own — the sky-reader's eyes, the eyes that read the wind and the water and the heading — and the look is not defiance. It is *measurement.* She is pricing the patron the way the patron priced her. And the patron reads the look and his face does a thing I have not seen on it: the narrow face shifts. A fraction. The patron does not enjoy being measured. The sky-reader is led away and the big one watches her go and his jaw works but he does not make a sound.

The steady-handed woman is priced with her. The patron reads the surgeon's hands and the pricing changes — the recognition, the number going up. The hands that close wounds are more valuable than the hands that carry. The steady-handed woman looks back through the bars at the cook and her face says the thing her hands say when they are on a wound: *I will come back.* She will not come back. The body knows it. The cook knows it. He makes a sound at her — the joke-shape, low, the last joke — and the corner of her mouth moves, and that is enough, and she is gone.

The cook is priced next. The helms read the burned hands, the big body, the age. The patron shakes its head. Not valuable enough to move. The cook goes back to the wall. He sits against the stone with his burned hands in his lap and his face has the stillness of a thing that has been priced and found ordinary. Then he does a thing I do not expect: he makes the joke-sound. Low. Bad. At himself. The sound a thing makes when the world has read it and found it wanting and the only answer is the wrong sound that no one laughs at. And across the channel, in his cell, the big one laughs. One short sound, torn out of the holding-center like a thing that escaped before the jaw could close on it. The cook hears it. The burned hands open once — the shrug, the *what can you do* — and close again. The two of them hold the sound between them across the iron, the joke and the laugh, the thing that survives the pricing because the pricing does not know what to do with it.

The thick-armed one is priced high. The helms read the muscle and the scarred face and the flat eyes and the patron makes the appreciation-sound. The thick one is taken out of the cell. He does not go quietly. He goes the way a thing goes that has decided the going is the last ground it will take — slow, the weight on the flat of the foot, every step read. The helms push him. He does not move faster. He sets the pace and the helms set theirs and the two speeds do not match and the thing in the corridor is a thing the patron's helms do not know how to read: a body that is not fighting and not obeying. The flat face goes through the hold door and the door closes and the heavy step goes down the stone and is gone.

The young one is priced last. The patron reads the runner's body — the joints, the quick-twitch, the light frame — and makes the placed-sound. Runner. Carrier. The young one's face is wet. He is not making a sound. He is fourteen lows old, maybe younger, and he is being led away into the dark of the under-city. He looks at the big one. The big one looks at him. And the big one makes the sound — the kind-sound, the word-shape, the thing the mouth makes at a small thing it is not going to hurt. The same sound he made in the hold of the boat. The same sound he made on the high stones when he put the hand on the young one's neck. The last sound the holding-center makes. The young one hears it and his face does the thing a face does when it breaks, and he is led away, and the sound hangs in the cell after him like a thing the iron cannot hold.

---

The big one is left in his cell.

Not priced. Not taken. Left. The body reads it through the bars — the patron reading the big one the way it read the cook, the slow weighing, the eyes going to the muscle and the scars and the age. But the big one is not priced as muscle. The big one is priced as *crew.* The helms know what he was. The captain. The holding-center. The thing that held the pack together and is sitting in a cell with the pack gone, and the pack gone is the thing the big one was, and without it he is a body in a cage, and the body in a cage is not worth as much as the body that held the pack.

The patron leaves. The helms follow. The hold goes dark — not all-the-way dark, the brown-dark, the wet-dark, the dark that has a little light in it from the torch-gaps in the walls. The kind of dark the body can read in.

I press against the bars between my cell and the Kala's. The iron is cold. The blood-warmth goes into it and the iron takes it and does not give it back. Through the bars I can reach the great side — barely, the fingertips on the dull hide. The breathing is bad. Long pauses. The gentling-sound has stopped.

Across the channel the big one sits against the wall of his cell. His wrists are raw where the iron has worked them. His face is the empty face — not the calculating face, not the holding face. The face of a thing that held and lost and is sitting with the losing.

He reads me through the bars across the channel. The small scarred thing in the next cage. The thing that broke his enforcer's wrist and put two of his crew on the ground. The thing whose wounds close in days. The thing he chained and took and brought here to pay a debt that was never going to clear.

And his mouth moves. A sound. Low. The same shape as the sound he made in the hold of the boat — the word-shape that is not a word I know, the kind-shape, the shape the mouth makes at a small thing it is not going to hurt.

I do not know the word. But the body reads the shape. And the body holds still.

And the crack — the crack the fruit opened, the crack the word-sound opened, the crack that holds the thing the pack does when it holds together — the crack goes deeper. Because the big one is making the kind-sound through the bars of a cage he is in too. The holding-center holding the line he drew even now, even in the cell, even with the pack gone and the debt a lie and the boy and the beast he took chained beside him in the dark. He is still holding. He does not know how to stop.

And the far mouth — the hole that leans toward the shape of the thing the hand can be when the hand is not owning — the far mouth reads the holding-center through the bars and leans.

And this low — this low the crack opens.

Not the fruit-crack. Not Fenn's crack. The *other* crack, the one that has been waiting outside the wall since the big one made the kind-sound in the hold of the boat. The big one is in a cage. The big one is priced and left and sitting with the losing. The big one is no longer the thing that chained me — the big one is the thing the patron chained. The chain on his wrists is the same iron as the chain on mine. The cell around him is the same stone as the cell around me. And the kind-sound he made in the boat — the word-shape, the mouth that makes a sound at a small thing it is not going to hurt — that sound was not the brand. It was never the brand. And the body reads this now because the body reads it from the *outside*, from the cage across the channel, from the holding-center broken open on the cell floor, and the reading is: he is in here with me. The big one who took me is in here with me. And the thing under his holding is the same thing under the Kala's keeping-arm, and it always was, and I would not let it in because the chain was on and the Kala was dying and the taking was the only reading.

The crack opens. The kind-sound goes through the wall and lies beside the fruit-sweetness, and the crack holds both, and the thing the crack holds is the thing the pack does when it holds together — and the pack is not a pack anymore, the pack is broken and priced and scattered through the under-city, and the holding-together is the only thing left of it, and it is in the cage across from me making the kind-sound at the thing it took because the making is the thing and the holding does not stop.

The green could not close this hole. The crown could not close it. The Kala could not close it. Nothing closes it but the shape of a thing that holds when holding has no point, and the shape is in the cage across the channel, and the hole leans at it.

I press my fingers through the bars against the Kala's side. The blood-warmth. The breathing. In, out, the long pause, in again. I count. The big one counts something of his own in the dark across the channel — the crew he had, the crew he lost, the thing he held and the ground that went under it.

And we sit in the under-city in our cages, the boy and the beast and the holding-center, and above us the eye looks out of every face of cut stone, and the green is a thousand lows and a sea away, and the cold has no seep in it, and the only warm thing is the blood under my skin that I cannot give away fast enough to keep the Kala breathing, and the count goes on.

The count goes on.

# Chapter 27: The Pit

They come for us on the second low in the hold.

---

Things in iron, different from the dock-helms — heavier, the faces covered, the short iron longer. Pit-guards. The body reads them the way it reads everything: the weight of the step, the set of the shoulders, the professional blankness of things that move property and do not name it. They are not cruel. Cruelty is a thing a face does. These faces do nothing. They are the hands of the place, and the hands do the work.

They open the Kala's cell first.

The chain comes off the wall. The Kala does not stand. The great body lies on the stone and the breathing goes in and out and the dark eye reads the open door and does not move toward it. The pit-guards go in with poles — long iron poles with hooks at the end — and they put the hooks in the chain and they drag the Kala out of the cell and across the stone floor, and the sound the great hide makes on the cut stone is the sound of a thing being dragged that cannot be dragged gently.

The Kala makes a sound. Not the gentling-sound. Not the *I am taken* sound. A new one. Low, deep, from the chest, the sound a thing makes when the last of the ground it knows goes under it. The sound goes through the iron bars and through the hold and through me, and the Red comes up.

Not all the way. The pressing Red — the thing that pushes at the still place, that says *break the bars, break the hands, take the Kala and go.* The still place holds. The still place says: *not here. The bars are iron and the guards are many and the Kala is chained and the place is stone under stone. Wait. Read the ground.*

I hold the Red down. I press my front to the bars and the Kala goes past on the stone floor, the great silver-dulled hide dragging, the chain on the neck, the eye finding me as she goes past and holding me — holding me the way she held me on the bank, on the king's seat, in the hold of the boat — and the eye says the thing the gentling-sound says: *be still. Nothing comes.* Said to me. By the thing being dragged away on the stone. The mother gentling the boy from the floor of a cage.

The eye goes past. The Kala goes down. A shaft in the floor at the far end of the hold — a vertical cut in the stone, a thing that goes down, the brown-dark opening under the great body as the pit-guards work the chain and the Kala descends. The sound of the chain on the stone. The sound of the breathing going further away. The sound of the gentling-sound, fainter, fainter, gone.

---

They come for me.

The cell opens. The chain comes off the wall — not off my wrists, off the wall. The wrists stay ironed. The hands go to the arms, professional, the lifting-and-moving hands, and I go out of the cell and into the hold and the body reads the hold the way it reads every new ground: exits, threats, the shape of the space.

One exit. The ramp the cart came down. Three pit-guards. The shaft in the floor. The body reads the shaft: a thing that goes down into the stone, a vertical hole, the kind of hole the island had in the limestone where the water goes underground and does not come back.

They take me to the shaft.

A cage. An iron cage on a chain, the size of a body, the kind of thing that goes down into a hole and does not come back up. The pit-guards put me in it. The door closes. The chain goes taut. And the cage descends.

---

The shaft goes down a long way.

The stone slides past — cut stone, then rough stone, then stone that is not stone but rock, the raw rock under the made-hill, the bones of the earth the city was built on. The air goes colder. The wet goes thicker. The brown-dark goes to the black-dark, the dark that does not have a torch-gap in it, the dark that is the dark all the way down.

And then the dark opens.

The cage comes out of the shaft into a space that is vast. The body reads vast before the eyes confirm it — the way the sound changes, the way the air moves, the way the echo goes out and does not come back for a count that means the ceiling is high and the walls are far. The torch-light comes in — not from torch-gaps but from iron baskets hung on chains, the fire burning low and red in them, the light going down into a space that is deeper than it is wide.

The pit.

I smell it before the light gives it to me. Old blood. Not the green-blood, not the fresh-hot kill-blood of the island. Old blood, soaked into stone, layered, the smell of a thing that has been bleeding for years in the same place and never been washed. And under the blood-smell, the smell of things caged — the close packed reek of bodies held where they cannot move, the piss and the sour breath and the open sores of the kept. The smell of the hold. The smell of the first hole. The body wears it again now in one breath, and the skin on the back of the neck crawls and tries to get away from a hand that is not there.

The cage descends past levels. Tiers. The body counts them — one, two, three — each one a ledge of cut stone with cells on it, the cells opening off the shaft the way the cells in the hold opened off the channel. Bodies in the cells. Things in the cells. Some moving, some not. Some human, some not. The body reads them in the torch-light the way it reads a herd — the alive ones and the dying ones and the dead ones, the professional count, the thing the body does when it enters new ground.

The cage stops at a level. The door opens. The pit-guards take me out and the chain goes to a ring in the wall and I am on a ledge of cut stone in a shaft in the earth, and the Kala is three levels up.

I know this because I can hear her.

Not the gentling-sound. The breathing. The great chest going in and out through the stone, the vibration of a thing too low for the ear, felt in the bone. Three levels of cut stone between us and the sound still comes through, the way water comes through the limestone channels under the green. I press my front to the rock wall and the breathing comes through it, barely. In. Out. The long pause. In again.

She is alive. Three levels up. Dying. And I am down here.

---

They put me in a cell.

Stone on three sides and iron on the fourth. Smaller than the hold-cell — meaner, the sleeping-place of a thing that is taken out and put back. The floor is dark with old blood. The walls are scored with the marks of things that clawed the stone and did not stay quietly in it.

I have been here before.

Not this cell. The other one. The first one. The dark of the hold, the wet wood sweating, the boy warm against my side, the hand closing on the back of the neck. The body knows the shape of the hole the way the feet know the shape of the ground. The body has been in a hole its whole life — the hold, the island, the crown, the boat, the under-city, and now this. The body wears the word *owned* the way it wore it the first low, and the word has never come off, and the crown did not take it off, and the Kala's arm did not take it off, and the king's seat did not take it off. The word was always there. Under the name. Under the crown. Under the keeping-arm. The brand on the neck and the word *mine* in the hand that closed and never let go.

I sit in the cell on the level three down from the Kala and the body reads the ground. The pit-guards. The shift-changes. The sound of the cage-chain. The water in the channel. The stone in the walls. The things in the cells — the breathing, the silence, the bodies that move and the bodies that do not. The dark.

And above — far above, up through the levels, up through the shaft, up through the stone — the patron. The narrow face. The pale eyes. The body reads the patron the way it reads the weather: the thing that does not come down into the dark but shapes everything in it. The patron does not visit the pit. The patron does not need to. The patron owns the ground the pit is cut into, and the owning reaches down through every level, and every chain in every cell is the patron's chain, and every body in every cage is the patron's body. The patron priced me differently on the dock and priced me differently in the hold and the pricing is still happening, up there in the cut-stone city, in a room I cannot see, in a currency I cannot read. The disc. The eye. The blood. The patron is calculating the value of the thing that does not die, and the calculation has not finished, and the not-finishing is the thing that keeps me alive. The patron will not let me die until the calculation is done. The pit is the place where the reading happens. The patron is the eye at the top of the shaft, looking down, and the boy at the bottom is the thing the eye has been searching for, and the finding is the thing the patron will not let go of.

The patron is the brand, made vast. The brand was one hand on one neck. The patron is the hand that owns the ground the neck stands on.

And the body says, the way it has said everything since the first low on the island:

*Count.*

---

And then — from somewhere in the dark of the pit, from a cell I cannot place, from a level I have not mapped — a sound comes up. Small. Torn. Hoarse, half breath. A sound I know. A sound I have heard in the green and on the high stones and in the hold of the boat. A sound I made and laid on a thing on purpose, the human way, the one word I gave to the runt the tribe cast out.

The still-sound. Marrow's sound. Given back to me now across the dark of the pit.

I go to the bars. The sound comes from two cells down on the same level — close enough to hear, close enough to reach if the bars were not between us. Marrow. Here. The runt the tribe cast out, the thing I named and kept, here in the pit with me.

And beside his breathing — smaller, quicker, the rapid heart-beat sound of a thing too small for the dark — the bold heart. The fierce bright thing that rode Marrow's shoulder on the high stones. It is here too. In the dark, in a cage, making the small high sound it makes when it is afraid. The sound I have not heard since the green. The sound that was the first warm thing to come to me after the wreck, the first thing that climbed onto the boy on the beach and said *alive, alive, you are alive.* It is here. Afraid. And Marrow is making the still-sound at it the way I made the still-sound at him in the den in the rot — low, torn, two falls, the gentling folded in. The runt gentling the bold heart in the dark of a pit cell the way I gentled him. The thing passed forward. The sound I gave him becoming the sound he gives.

He makes the sound again. Torn, hoarse, the still-sound scraped through a throat that has been screaming or choking or both. And the sound says what it has always said, what it said in the den in the rot when I laid it on him the first time:

*I am here. I am here. I am here.*

I make it back. The rasp, the cough, the still-sound given through the bars into the dark. Two falls. Scraping over the gap where the three teeth are gone. And the sound goes across the dark of the pit and finds him.

The breathing two cells down changes. The held breath let go. The settling of a thing that heard the sound and read it and lay back inside it.

---

The big one is on the level above me.

I do not know this until the dark gives me his breathing — the heavy, steady breathing of the holding-center, gone flat the way Marrow's still-sound goes flat, a thing chosen as the thing the body does when the body cannot do anything else. One level up. Across the shaft. In a cell the same size as mine.

He does not speak. I do not speak. The shaft-gap between levels carries the breathing and nothing else. But the body reads him through the stone the way the body reads the Kala three levels up — the vibration, the weight, the settling of a thing in a cage. He is there. The holding-center is there, in the same pit, in the same iron, at the same depth.

I think about the high stones. About the king's seat and the keeping-arm and the old male with the torn ear standing foot-for-foot at my side. About the challenge, when I leapt the birthright and did not take the neck. About Grist climbing to the king who counts what the strong waste. About the Torn One setting her feet. About Stub on the half-leg. About the long even sound with the gladness folded in.

Gone. All of it. A sea away and a shaft away and a cage away. And the crown is a thing I held behind my teeth in the dark, and the teeth are broken in three places, and the dark does not care about crowns.

But the body cares. The body holds the crown the way it holds the name — not because the crown is useful in the pit, but because the crown is a thing the body *was.* The body was the soft wrong thing on lent ground and it became the king of the wild things, and the becoming is a thing no cage and no chain and no pit takes, because the becoming is *inside.* The ground did not lend the crown. The body took it. And the body does not give back what it has taken.

---

I kneel in the dark and lay the squared stones in the still place, faced and even, the way the Kala taught me on the clean ground among the dead.

The name my mother gave me. *Rook.* Mine. The one un-stolen thing, held behind the teeth.

The three brands, ground small — the first hand, the slaver-captain's brand, the patron's brand, the thing the big one's code became on the dock stone. Kept to hand back across the water.

The Kala. The great dark eye going out, turned to me with the gentling unbroken. The price. Set faced in the still place where it will not pour away. The one who carried me. The one who woke the name.

And the new thing — the thing that is not the crown and not the name and not the brand. The thing the crack holds. The fruit-sweetness and the kind-sound and the thick-armed one's holding-center call through the bars. The thing the pack did when it held together. The thing that sits in cages in the dark and makes the sound at the thing it took because the making is the thing, not the hearing. The thing that is in the cage across the shaft and the cage two cells down and the cage three levels up. The thing the green could not close and the crown could not close and the Kala could not close. The thing that is not the tribe and not the pack and not the brand. The thing that is just the holding, the holding that does not stop when the holding has no point.

I set it faced in the still place with the rest. And the still place holds.

---

And the Red stirs.

Not the killing-Red. The other thing. The thing the seer's finger found on the breastbone on the high stones. The deep-down thing. The pulse. The warm. The thing that closed the wounds too fast and kept the fevers off and held the swarms at a hand's width and steadied the Kala's breathing on the crossing when I pressed my front to her side. The thing that has no word and no law and no crown.

It pulses. In the pit-dark. In the stone-and-iron cage. In the under-city of the place the boats go. It pulses the way it pulsed in the fever-fog on the bank when the Kala found me. The way it pulsed on the king's seat when the seer's finger found the place under the breastbone.

It pulses and the warmth comes up — not the body-warmth, the *blood*-warmth, the deep-down thing — and the warmth goes through the floor. Through the stone. Through the bars. Into the dark of the pit where the things in the cells read it and go still. Marrow's breathing changes two cells down. The big one's breathing changes on the level above. Every body in the dark reads the thing coming up through the stone, and the reading is the same reading the seer had with the pale finger on the blood:

*What is this.*

I do not know. I do not have the word. The head reaches for it and the thing slides back under, the way the name slid back under for a thousand lows, the way the word *boat* slid back under on the beach. It is an omen and a recognition and nothing I can swing. It does not pour into the empty places and fill them. The boy's strength is still the boy's strength — not enough, not yet, not for the iron and the stone and the three levels between here and the Kala.

But the pulse says a thing the pulse does not say in words. The pulse says: *this is in you.* The way the name is in you. The way the crown is in you. The thing the green could not kill and the fevers could not touch and the hold could not break. The thing that has been there since before the green, since before the brand, since before the name — older than all of them, deeper than all of them, the thing under the blood that the seer bowed to and could not read.

And the body knows the shape of it. And the shape is: *up.*

Three levels up. The Kala. Dying or dead. I do not know which. But the pulse says *up* and the count says *up* and the crown says *up* and the still place says *up* and the name under the tongue says *up* and every ground I have ever taken says *up.*

I do not look up at the rim. You do not spend the eye on ground that is already gone. I spend the eye on the floor the foot is in. The floor is dark with old blood, soaked deep with every caught thing they ever brought here, and I am standing in it, and Marrow is two cells down, and the big one is on the level above, and the Kala is three levels up, and the pulse is in the blood, and the name is behind the teeth, and the crown is in the body, and the still place holds.

One low the hand will open. It opened once on the moving floor and dropped me into the warm black water, and the water carried me to the shore, and the shore made me. It will open again. And the thing it opens on will not be the small wrong boy it closed on.

I rise in the blood of the pit. The dark does not care. The iron does not care. The pit-guards do not care. The patron three levels up in the cut-stone city does not care.

But the body cares. And the body counts. And the body does not give back what it has taken.

I am still counting. And now I am not the only thing in here counting back.
