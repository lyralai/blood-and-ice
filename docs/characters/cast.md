# Cast Bible — Book One: *Wild Boy*

Story-bible entries for the Book-1 supporting cast. One entry per character: **what they are**, **role/function**, **arc in Book 1**, and **Book-2 thread**.

For the protagonist, see `protagonist.md` (Rook — feral human boy, fever-immune wrong-blood, the dormant Red). Rook is not re-described here; every entry below is calibrated against him.

**Canon anchors (locked):** tropical death-island Saqarro; monster-peoples Vorth (great-ape brutes; Rook's adoptive tribe), Mure (deep-one tide-dwellers), Skell (carrion scavengers); "the Red" = the dormant blood-power (kept UNSOLVED — three contradictory theories: fed god / bloodline taint / stolen slain-god's blood); "the Bloodless / the Cold" = its bloodless husk-making inverse (cold = absence of blood, NOT winter); corruption ladder Red → Beast (Mure) → Husk (Bloodless); the Eye-and-Thorn sigil; Rook's true name hidden behind three missing teeth. "Low" = the in-world day-unit; "the high stones" = the Vorth crown-ground.

---

## The Kala

**What she is.** An aging she-Vorth, gray-silver at the muzzle and along the ridged back, past her physical strength. She is not near the king; she sits to the side of the high stones with the squared dead and tends them — *the one who keeps.* "Kala" is the keeping/mothering sound, not a personal name; it becomes the name Rook holds her by.

**Role/function.** The Tarzan-mother and the heart of the belonging engine. She fills the hole no kill and no water ever filled — Rook's "far mouth," the wanting-to-be-known. Her keeping is set in deliberate opposition to *the hand* (the slaver's owning grip): she holds the small one to keep it, never to own it, and the body knows the difference before the head does. She is also the seed of the trap: she is Rook's most legible attachment, which is exactly what makes her the Part-Five vulnerability.

**Arc in Book 1.** She lost a small one of her own (laid faced among the squared dead); the hole in her is shaped like a thing taken. She takes the burning, fever-stricken Rook off the channel-stones because the tribe *witnessed* the green try to kill him and fail — his wrongness reads as omen/kin (ch08). She is his first home: gentling-sound, slow vast heartbeat, the arm folded over him in the fever-dark. Through Book 1 the bond is deepened with concrete shared acts (tending the dead small one; her learning the noose-trick from his hands in ch10) and one friction — her keep-him-safe against his climb toward the crown. In Part Five the slavers stake her out alive as bait, knowing the wild king protects his own. Rook reads the trap and comes anyway. The slavers put the iron in her *live*, and hold him to watch, then kill her once he is netted — she was never the cargo; he is (ch26). Her death is his **Eo**: the forging wound, the great dark eye going out turned to him with the gentling unbroken.

**Book-2 thread.** Dead — but load-bearing as absence. Killing her strips Rook's chief human anchor: per the magic design, the fewer the anchors, the louder the Red's rage. Her death is the weight on the line that turns the "far mouth" from a hole he feels into a pull he *aims* — the engine of his climb out of the pits. Her gentling-sound is the keeping-shape Rook made of his own; it echoes in the pulse that lights faintly in his blood at the pit bottom (ch28). She is the standard he measures the cost of becoming-the-Red against.

## Marrow

**What he is.** A runt-Vorth, nearly culled at birth; survives on wit, as Rook does. One shoulder rides high and uneven; one hand is curled and will not open; a crooked, rocking gait — and then he is across the rock before you read the gait, quicker than the big ones. His tribe-noise name is a dismissal. **Rook renames him "Marrow" in the human tongue** — the rich thing the strong crack the bone for and leave behind, the leavings — his first *deliberate* word-claim (ch10), practice for freeing his own buried name.

**Role/function.** The Brother (the Sevro/Caenis). Rook's shadow, his eyes and conscience, and the first of the Pack. The single most important populating move in the book: he gives the one-consciousness narration a peer to be a *person* toward, and lets in the rare beats of uncomplicated joy and boy-humor. From Part Four on, the loyal warning voice — read off his body, never thesis-dialogue: high shoulder dropped, curled hand working, eyes pulled off the slavers — the monster more human-loyal than the humans.

**Arc in Book 1.** The tribe means to drive him out to the Skell as leavings (ch10). Rook saves him with a *trick*, not strength: together they rig the noose/deadfall and take the burrow-thing the strong hunters never could — Marrow baits and sets the trip with his crooked fingers; Rook reads the run. Small + smart beats big + strong; it produces the book's purest joy beat (the two wrong-made things laughing on the mud). From there Marrow is his crew's core through the rise and the crown. When the slavers spring the trap he is netted in the same surge — worth nothing to them, taken for the trouble it would have cost to leave him — and does not break. Carried across the water as fodder.

**Book-2 thread.** The pit-band seed. The slavers carried two and knew the worth of only one; they threw the runt in **as meat for the pit, a thing to be killed against the prize** — and he came out the far side still himself, still quick, still Rook's. The far-wall "hungry thing" of ch28 *is* Marrow; the closing line — "I am not the only thing in here counting back" — is him. He gives back across the pit-dark the still-sound Rook made of his own mouth: proof the thing Rook *made* crossed the water intact where the home did not. Book 2 opens not-alone; Marrow is the first brick of the underclass uprising in the blood-city.

## Voss-Marr

**What he is.** Gull-Marr's blood, strongest of Rook's generation, raised to inherit the high stones — everything Rook is not: prime-blooded, sure, the ripe thing off the branch. The Rival (the Cassius/au Bellona).

**Role/function.** The nemesis born from a Book-1 mercy. Held throughout as *read-as-weather* (the held blow, the not-looking, the air gone hard and flat), never thesis-dialogue. He is the structural cost of Rook's defining choice not to feed the Red — to shame rather than kill.

**Arc in Book 1.** The Tublat-tormentor: contempt for the soft, wrong-blood human come up on lent ground (ch13). Forced to fight side by side against the Hesh, he gives Rook grudging respect, almost brotherhood (ch14–15). Then Rook challenges **Gull-Marr over Voss's head** and takes the crown Voss was raised to inherit — and **shames-not-kills** him (the Kala's kinship lesson), leaving him whole and meaning nothing by it (ch17). Voss takes the mercy as the deepest insult: *I bled beside you and you stole what was mine, and you will not even fight me for it.* The held blow goes back down into him and sets. In Part Five his faction hangs back at the trap; the slavers came up the green for the human king and leave with him, and Voss is left **holding the broken tribe** — the wrong way, not the clean by-the-neck win he wanted. It will sit in him and rot. He is already counting.

**Book-2 thread.** The nemesis who won't bow. Voss has seized the Vorth and holds the fractured island; a war is born from a Book-1 mercy. When Rook returns to re-win the wild things who turned on him, Voss is the one who refuses — the rival who must be beaten again, now as a king, not a runt.

## The Pack (the Howlers)

**What they are.** The discarded who gather to the king who values what the strong waste — broken things the tribe threw away. They come to Rook on the king's-seat day (ch17) on the side no strong thing watches, because the leavings read the order first; it is the thing that nearly killed them. The truest of all of them, because they alone know what the order does to a thing it does not value. Nucleus of Book 2's army.

### Grist
**What he is.** A spared Skell — gray scuttling carrion-kin, the grind-marks of his own people still on him. Rook read him off the squaring-ground and would not let the order grind him off the bone. ("Grist" rhymes Marrow: what the strong grind off the bone — the leavings.)
**Function/arc.** First of the Pack to climb to the king who counts what the strong waste; reads the new stone faster than the big ones. Dramatizes the otherwise set-dressing Skell. **Book-2 thread:** a bridge to the captive Skell in the pit-city; the first sign Rook's crown crosses species lines toward "unite the bled."

### The Torn One
**What she is.** An outcast Vorth she-warrior; a long old wrong healed crooked down one side, driven to the edges. The order put her outside the order.
**Function/arc.** She does not bow — she is not made to bow; she sets her feet and turns out to the green like a thing meaning to hold a line (ch17). The Pack's spine and martial weight. **Book-2 thread:** a war-leader of the gathered-discarded; the loyal blade who survives where the home did not.

### Stub
**What he is.** A broken castoff on a half-leg the order would have let the green finish; the strong stopped reading him long ago, nothing left in him to take.
**Function/arc.** Comes up the rock last and slow; looking up is all he has to give, and he gives it (ch17). The felt anchor of the "lows" — the scale of the boy-to-king span. **Book-2 thread:** the measure of what Rook's rule is *for* — the thing the order discards, kept.

## Gull-Marr

**What he is.** The monster-king of the Vorth (the Kerchak analog / the summit) — the heavy, flat-eyed bulk that turns any Vorth off a rock without thought and has let no challenge stand. Voss-Marr's blood.

**Role/function.** The apex of the strength-law and the ceiling Rook must pass to become king. He embodies the old law: the strong are weighed, felled, and done.

**Arc in Book 1.** Marks the soft, wrong human as a threat from the day he comes up the rock (ch13). At the challenge he comes in the rush — the law of the strong, fast and sure — and Rook does not meet the strength; he reads where it stands and pulls the ground from under it, drops him with a law rather than a blow (ch17). Rook does **not** take the neck or lay the brand; he takes the standing — stands on the seat of Gull-Marr's own weighing. The tribe turns. Gull-Marr weighs the gone ground and **lets it stand**, then walks alone into the steaming green with his life. The first law of the new stone, laid down before Rook knows he is laying it: the strength let live.

**Book-2 thread.** Walked off into the green, alive — a deliberately open door. A deposed king loose on the island is a piece left on the board: a possible reclaimer, a possible ally against Voss, or a thread Book 2 may leave dormant. His exile is the precedent for Rook's "shame, don't kill" rule and the seed of Voss's grievance.

## The Seer

**What she is.** The oldest Vorth, the great body sunk in on itself, the eyes gone milk-pale at the edges but still moving, still reading — the Vorth elder who *senses something old in the blood.* (The "old-blood" people who half-know the Eye-and-Thorn.)

**Role/function.** The mentor-who-senses; the mystery-deepener who never solves. She reads Rook the way Rook reads the green — taking the whole shape before going down into it. She lays a gray finger on the Eye-and-Thorn and *knows the mark* (ch08); she sets the sigil beside the loop-and-fire cunning as two halves of one strangeness (ch10); and on the king's-seat night she lifts the finger off the sigil and lays it flat on Rook's chest, over the warm blood — reading not the standing, not the crown, but the *thing in the blood* that closes his wounds too fast (ch17). She lays a wondering *means* over it — a means with a question folded in — and never finishes it.

**Arc in Book 1.** The recurring, escalating non-answer. Every approach brings Rook one reach closer to what he is and gives him no word for it; she has the shape and not the name, and strains at the same wall from the elder's side. She is the in-world proof that his blood *means something* — the read that rhymes, by design, with the slaver-captain's read of the sigil (ch21), so the reader assembles the blood-economy shape with no explainer.

**Book-2 thread.** Left on the island when Rook is taken — a knowledge-keeper Book 2 may return to once memory and the Red detonate in the pits and the riddle she could only sense gets its (still-contested) answer. Her three-way ambiguity (fed god / taint / stolen blood) is held open; she is the island-side voice of the mystery the pit-city will fail to settle.

## Yara

**What she is.** A young human captive among the slavers, near Rook's own count of lows, root-rope hair, the thin sour under-smell of a body that has known near hunger. Owned but kept apart — their coerced **translator**, the one who carries meaning across between owner and owned. (Named in narration as "Yara" — the falling-open shape of the sound she lays on the dark; Rook names her in his own head before he has a word for her.)

**Role/function.** The subverted Jane / the D'Arnot role: **not a damsel — a mirror.** The owned thing on the killing ground, the one who breaks away from the pack onto the bare edge — Rook's own lifelong shape, reflected. She fills the **road** Rook lost: the easy human speech that lays a name clean on the air for another head to catch and throw back. She teaches him human words (naming snails, weed, the tides, the camp-sounds with teeth in them) without knowing she is teaching a person — and she *reads the Eye-and-Thorn*, drawing it on the wet air and laying a clean name on it she will not give him (ch20). Romance is a **deferred ember**, not consummated: she is the mirror, not the home; the Kala is the home.

**Arc in Book 1.** She slips the slaver camp low after low to talk to the moving green, thinking it a beast or a haunt; Rook steals her words one stone at a low, building back across the gap in his teeth the road torn out of him before the green. She knows the sigil's shape but not who wears it; he wears it but cannot say it — two who know the shape on all the drowning world, neither able to close the gap. The plan calls for **one real two-way contact beat before the trap** — an exchange where she *knows* she is talking to a person and chooses to anyway (the seed of the Book-2 relationship). The road she lays him runs, in the end, straight to the net.

**Book-2 thread.** A live, deferred thread. In the hold she is also taken toward the pits — freed or lost is the open Book-2 seam (the plan flags both the naming seam and her fate as deliberately unresolved). She is the deferred ember: the Book-2 relationship and, as the only head that carries a clean name for the sigil, a key to the heritage the pits detonate.

## Captain Drevan Sool

**What he is.** The slaver captain — "the patient one," "the leader." Master of a slaving expedition that lands on Saqarro to harvest monsters and curiosities and walks, unknowing, into the death every other human dies of. The green-killer who came up Rook's island and did not die of it (yet).

**Role/function.** The human antagonist and the hand made flesh — the owning grip Rook wore through the slave-dark, returned. His defining trait is *patience*: he learns which way Rook bolts, and so learns the one thing under the body that does not bolt, slide, or break. He is a **blood-trader**, not a random villain: he reacts to the Eye-and-Thorn and the gap behind Rook's teeth with the same *you are something* that the seer and Yara show — greed and a withheld fear. The slavers are the trap-springers; Sool is the mind that bait-staked the Kala.

**Arc in Book 1.** He stakes the Kala out alive to draw the wild king, reads the trap-walk, and takes Rook netted — putting the iron in her *live* and holding Rook to watch, because she was never the cargo: **Rook is** (ch26). He brands Rook with the hold-word (mine, be still, come, you are a thing), which falls off the face that will not take it. Across the water he begins to die of the fever Rook is immune to — the rot going down into the furrow Rook opened in his face — the dramatic-irony B-plot paying off: he carries the cure across the sea in chains and dies of the disease it would burn out. He hands Rook to the pit-masters, selling the *reading* along with the body: *this one does not die — watch.*

**Book-2 thread.** Likely dying or dead of Saqarro's fever by the pit-city — but the **trade** he represents outlives him. He is the reader who first priced Rook's blood; his sale plugs Rook into the blood-economy that has hunted the line for generations. The withheld truth behind his greed-and-fear (carriers draw the Cold down on everyone near them) is a Book-2 detonation. He rhymes the prologue's doomed greed: the man who reaches for the cargo and is taken.

## The Hesh

**What it is.** The island's apex predator/monster — the largest, most dangerous thing on Saqarro, scaled monstrous above even the Vorth king. (Not a person of the monster-peoples; a beast-tier threat — the Tarzan-Sabor analog, elevated.) Reads, like every strong thing, by the law of the rush: the sure thing comes fast and forgets the ground.

**Role/function.** The external trial that forges Rook's place and his band — the apex he must face to earn standing and to bind Voss-Marr to him (briefly). Mechanically, the proving-ground for Rook's method: defeat by intelligence + his wrongness (pain/venom tolerance, the wounds that close too fast) rather than strength.

**Arc in Book 1.** It threatens the tribe; Rook must face it (ch14). The long hunt drives it off / brings it down through cunning and wrong-blood toughness — fought side by side with Voss-Marr and the old male with the torn ear, the moment of grudging near-brotherhood before the crown breaks it (ch14–15). Over its going-under Rook lays the law he will keep through the king: *I do not feed the second mouth; I do not know what I become if I feed it.* It is during/after this hunt that the seer first reads the too-fast healing on his forearms.

**Book-2 thread.** Largely closed as a Book-1 trial — but it seeds the design's central dread by contrast: the Hesh is a *clean* monster (a beast that kills by strength), set against the corruption ladder where the real horror is becoming the Red → Beast (Mure) → Husk (Bloodless). It is the measure that makes the later, internal monstrosity legible: the apex Rook can beat by wit is nothing beside the thing he could *become*.
