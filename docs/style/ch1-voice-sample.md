# Chapter 1: Black Shore

The sea spits me onto black stone and takes three teeth as payment.

My face is split from cheekbone to jaw. My left hand won't close. Blood leaves from three places I can count and one I can't, and the one I can't is the one that scares me, because a wound you can't find is deeper than you can reach.

I am fourteen. I have been fourteen for six days. The ship is gone. I don't know where I am. I know the sea tried to keep me, and the fact that I'm breathing feels less like survival and more like a mistake the water hasn't corrected yet.

Three bodies share the beach.

One has no head. One has no legs below the knee. The third is a hand and part of an arm, still wearing a copper ring gone green from salt. I try to pull it free. My fingers slip. The sea slaps my wrist against the rock.

I go still.

Still is what you learn first when you're small and the world isn't. Still is what saves you when a hand is raised and you don't know if it's coming down. I've been still for most of my fourteen years, and it hasn't saved me yet, but I do it anyway because it's the only prayer I know.

The cold comes through the stone.

Not weather-cold. Something else. The rock holds cold the way a dead man holds weight — completely, without intention. It's been in the stone since before the stone was stone, and it presses up through my spine into my teeth with the patience of something that has all the time there is.

I'm going to die here.

The thought is small and clean, and part of me is relieved. I've been running from death for three days in open water. Stopping feels like the first kind thing the world has offered me.

Then something moves under my skin.

Not muscle. Not cold. Something that doesn't belong to the body it's living in. It uncurls in my chest — a fist opening around an ember it's been hiding — and warmth pushes outward into my fingers, my teeth, the soles of my feet. I can't stop it. The warmth eats the cold the way fire eats wood, and I am the wood, and the fire doesn't care.

But the warmth feels like being held.

Like arms around me. Like the space behind my mother's ribs where I fit when the world was too loud. And I lean into it — I *lean into it* — because I'm fourteen and freezing and when warmth comes you don't ask it for its name.

That's the first terrible thing I do on this island. Not the worst. But the first.

I choose comfort without asking where it comes from. And the thing that gives it to me lets me choose, which means it knows something I don't, and anything that benefits from your ignorance will never be the one to warn you.

The warmth settles. Coils. Tucks itself back into whatever space it lives in behind my lungs. It leaves a taste — copper, and under the copper, something that doesn't have a flavor so much as an age. Something that tastes the way the stone feels. Old. Patient. Not from here.

I push that thought away because it's too big for me, and the thing inside me lets me push it away, and that should tell me everything I need to know, but I'm fourteen and I don't speak the language of warnings yet.

I crawl to the nearest body.

He was large before the sea opened him. The waves washed his face clean, which makes him look peaceful. The dead should not look peaceful. Peace is what the living sell each other to make dying easier to watch.

I search him. Belt. Pouch. Wet wool. No blade. No food.

I find dried fish wrapped in oilskin, tied against his skin under his shirt like a charm. The smell fills my mouth so fast my jaw aches. I eat too fast and throw half onto the stones.

Then I eat that too.

The shame sits in my throat like a bone. But the thing inside me doesn't care about shame. It's warm and full and quiet, and that quietness bothers me more than anything it's done so far, because hunger that finishes eating and goes to sleep is hunger that will wake up again.

A sound above the beach.

Not waves. Not gulls. A dry knocking. Bone on bone.

I look up.

The cliff rises in black shelves slick with bird filth. At the top, something hangs from a pole. Antlers. Hollowed. Strung with teeth — not the teeth of any animal I can name. Some are small and human-regular. Some are larger, ridged, dark with age, from mouths I can't fit inside any creature I know.

They knock together in the wind, and I understand with a certainty below thought that the sound is not the wind. The teeth are talking to each other.

Below the antlers, a shape.

Human. Fur hood. Spear, point down.

The thing inside me stirs. Not alarm. *Interest*. The attention of something that was bored and isn't anymore. I feel it shift its weight toward the shape on the cliff the way a dog shifts before it lunges.

I pick up a stone.

The figure laughs or speaks. The wind shreds it.

I show my teeth.

The shape lifts the spear. Not to throw. To point inland.

More shapes appear. Three. Four. They come down the cliff path placed and careful, the way people walk who were born on rock.

The first reaches the beach. The hood falls back.

She's older than I expected. Broad face weathered like driftwood. Gray braid thick as rope. Her cheeks carry old scars — not fighting scars, which are messy. These are symmetrical, deliberate, cut to follow the cheekbone on both sides. A language written in flesh. She holds the spear the way you hold something that's part of your arm.

She looks at the bodies. At the fish oil on my mouth. At the wound under my ribs that should have killed me.

The wound has stopped bleeding. Wounds don't stop bleeding like that — not in cold, not in a boy who's been in water for three days. She sees it. Her eyes change — not fear, not surprise. The look of someone finding a word they only knew by sound and seeing it written for the first time.

She smiles. Not kind. Not cruel. The smile of a woman who's found a tool and wants to know if it still cuts.

She speaks. I don't know the words. I know the shape — question, warning, claim.

She's claiming me.

The thing inside my chest purrs.

And the antlers above us knock — bone against bone against bone — and the woman can hear what they're saying, and whatever it is makes her smile wider.

I tighten my grip on the stone, and three thoughts sit in me at once:

I am being claimed by a woman with a spear who sees something in my wound that I don't understand.

The thing inside me is not afraid of her. It *recognizes* her.

And the teeth on the antlers are still talking, and I don't know what they're saying, but they're saying it about me.
