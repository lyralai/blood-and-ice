# Voice Guide

This project needs an original voice: brutal first-person lyricism with political clarity and cosmic dread.

Do not imitate any named author's sentence patterns. Use the following internal rules instead.

## Point Of View

Primary POV is first-person Rook.

Early Rook does not explain the world like a scholar. He notices:

- hunger
- exits
- hands
- wounds
- weather
- who is allowed to speak
- who gets fed
- who watches without blinking

He understands politics physically before he understands it abstractly.

## Sentence Rhythm

Use short sentences for danger.

Use longer sentences when dread accumulates or when Rook is trying to understand a social system that has already trapped him.

Avoid constant one-line intensity. If every sentence screams, none of them do.

## Imagery Bank

Preferred imagery:

- black stone
- seal fat
- cold iron taste of blood
- bone dust
- salt
- antler wind
- wet fur
- old smoke
- hunger as a second mouth
- names as things held in the teeth
- ice as memory
- blood as witness

Use sparingly:

- crowns
- thrones
- destiny language
- abstract "darkness"
- generic rage

Avoid:

- borrowed lore terms
- modern idioms
- self-aware genre commentary
- endless repetition of the same symbolic phrase

## Rook's Voice By Stage

### Stage 1: Wild Boy

Rook's voice is immediate and suspicious.

Example direction:

> The beach had three bodies. Two gulls. One knife. I counted the knife first.

He should not sound like a polished adult reflecting from comfort.

### Stage 2: Captive

Rook begins mapping social power.

Example direction:

> The woman with antlers did not raise her voice. That made the men listen harder. I hated her for teaching me that.

### Stage 3: Pit-Survivor

Rook becomes aware that witnesses are building a version of him.

Example direction:

> They did not look at my face. They looked at the blood drying on me, as if it had spoken better than I had.

### Stage 4: Young Leader

Rook sees the difference between fear and obedience.

Example direction:

> Fear moves people fast. Hunger moves them longer. Law moves them when you are not there to bare your teeth.

## The Wound-Guest Voice

The Wound-Guest should not begin as a chatty inner demon.

Early:

- pressure
- heat
- laughter
- images
- instinctive certainty

Middle:

- repeated impulses
- almost-words
- memory that is not Rook's

Late:

- short phrases
- bargaining without human morality
- strategic hunger

Rule: the more articulate it becomes, the more worried the reader should be.

## Violence

Violence should be clear, consequential, and character-revealing.

Do not write gore as decoration. A violent scene should change at least one of:

- Rook's body
- Rook's reputation
- a political balance
- a relationship
- the Wound-Guest's strength
- a tribe's interpretation of events

## Cannibalism

Khar Vane cannibalism must be treated as culture, law, sacrament, logistics, and horror. It cannot be random edginess.

Use rules:

- who may be eaten
- who may not
- what parts mean
- when the rite is public
- how shame attaches
- how scarcity changes practice

The horror should come partly from how coherent the practice is to them.

## Politics

Every faction needs material motives:

- food
- marriage
- boats
- ritual authority
- old names
- winter stores
- revenge
- legitimacy

No faction exists only to admire or oppose Rook.

## Revision Standard

A scene is worth keeping if it does at least two jobs:

- advances plot
- reveals character
- clarifies world
- changes political state
- develops the magic cost
- deepens voice

If a scene only says "the monster is scary" again, cut it or make it do real work.
