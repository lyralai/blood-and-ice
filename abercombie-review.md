# BLOOD AND ICE: THE WILD BOY
## A Review by Joe Abercrombie

Right then. Someone's sent me a manuscript about a feral boy raised by giant apes on a fever island who ends up chained in a slave pit, and they want me to read it in the voice of the man who wrote about a torturer having a wonderful time and a one-handed warlord being the most reasonable person in the room. Fine. Let's have a look.

I'll say this upfront: this book does a thing that shouldn't work, and it does it for a long time, and somehow you don't want it to stop. That's craft. That's not luck. That's someone who knows what they're about.

---

**THE VOICE**

Here's the dangerous bit. The narrator has no language. I mean that literally — he doesn't have words. He thinks in body. He thinks in smell and footprint and the weight of a thing's breathing. Every sensation comes through the flesh first and the mind never, and I mean *never*, catches up in time to make it tidy.

And this is where most writers would muck it up within five pages. They'd get cute with it. They'd start reaching for metaphors the narrator hasn't earned. They'd explain the gimmick to you, and then explain it again, and then you'd be reading a clever exercise instead of a story.

This doesn't do that. Or not often enough to matter.

The voice works because it's *consistent* and because it's *specific*. This boy doesn't think "I felt afraid." He thinks the hide goes up on his arms. He doesn't think "I felt love." He thinks the far mouth is full. Every emotional beat is routed through the body, and the body is always honest even when the head is lying. The invention of a vocabulary — the Kala, Marrow, the bold heart, the far mouth, the Red, the still place — gives you a purchase on a consciousness that would otherwise be pure abstraction. The named things become real. The unnamed things become powerful. The name he holds behind his teeth — *Rook* — lands harder than any dramatic reveal I've written in twenty years because you've spent the whole manuscript understanding what it costs him to have a word he can't even say.

Does the gimmick wear thin? Somewhere around the crossing — chapters 23 and 24 — it gets close. The hold of the boat is deliberately claustrophobic and the prose gets claustrophobic with it, and there were stretches where I wanted the lens to pull back. Not much. A breath. But then the Kala's breathing goes long and the boy puts his hands against the bars and you're back in it, and the thin spot is forgotten.

**THE VIOLENCE**

The night raid is the set piece, and it's properly done. What I respect about it — and I don't say that lightly, I'm very particular about screen violence — is that the Red isn't fun. It's not the "cool" moment where the hero goes beast mode and you're meant to pump your fist. It's terrifying. The boy goes through the thick-armed one like weather, and the prose makes you understand that what's happening is something being *unleashed*, not something being *chosen.* The still place saying *no* to the Red when the young one is on the ground with the shaking blade — that's the moment. That's where the violence earns itself. Because the scene isn't about the damage the boy can do. It's about the damage he *doesn't* do, and why, and what that costs him.

The pit, the brand, the chains — the darkness here isn't edgy. It's structural. The book isn't grimdark because it thinks suffering is cool. It's grimdark because it understands that the hand on the back of the neck never really lets go, and the whole story is about a boy trying to find a place where the hand isn't. That's earned.

**THE BAD GUYS**

Here's where it gets interesting, because the "bad guys" are the most layered characters in the book, and that's a risk that pays off.

The big one — Hark, the captain — is the best piece of character work here. He's the holding-center. He holds things together. He also chains a boy and steals a gorilla. And the book never lets you off the hook with him. You read the crew dying on the spit and you feel the weight of what he's carrying. You read the kind-sound he makes in the hold of the boat and you feel the crack in the boy's wall. The moment on the dock, when the patron takes everything and Hark's face goes empty — that's a villain becoming a character. That's the "shit, he's one of us" moment, and it's devastating because you've been reading him as the threat for fifteen chapters and suddenly he's in a cage next to you.

Dren, the enforcer — he's simpler. The held blow. The flat face. But the book does something smart with him: it makes him *right.* He reads the leaning before anyone else. He reads where the debt leads. He's the Cassandra with an axe, and when he's standing on the dock with the bad wrist and the split lip looking at Hark like *I told you* — that lands.

The patron is more of a force than a character. Narrow face, pale eyes, measuring. The book knows this — it compares him to weather. He works as the vastness of the system the boy is up against, the brand made institutional. But he's thinner than Hark. He's the eye on the wall. He doesn't need to be deep. He needs to be inevitable, and he is.

**THE EMOTION**

This is where I'd put the book down if it didn't work, because the whole thing rests on whether you believe the boy and the Kala. And you believe it. You believe it from the moment she puts the keeping-arm over him and you believe it harder when she shows him his place among the dead.

The frog scene. The frog scene is the one I'd point at and say "this writer knows what they're doing." A small ape brings the boy a frog, and the boy makes a sound he's never made before — a laugh, but he doesn't know that — and the two of them sit there barking at each other over a frog that hops away, and it's the most human moment in the book and neither of them is human. That's the trick, isn't it? Making the animal moments more human than the human ones.

The pit ending. The boy kneeling in the dark, laying out his dead — metaphorically, the way the Kala taught him — and the pulse coming up through the stone. And then Marrow's voice from two cells down. *I am here. I am here. I am here.* And you realize you've been holding your breath for thirty pages.

It hits. It hits hard. It earns it.

**THE PACING**

Mostly it moves. The island chapters — 11, 18, 20, 21 — build beautifully. Chapter 11 is the slow one, the settling-in chapter, and it earns its length because it's building the world the raid will destroy. The approach of the hurt pack onto the high stones, the Vorth attack, the boy standing between — this is propulsive, muscular writing.

The boat chapters (23-24) are where it catches. Deliberately, I think — the hold is a hold, it's supposed to suffocate — but there were places where the counting-of-breaths and the reading-through-the-planks could have been tighter. The pit chapter (27) recovers beautifully and the ending is all forward momentum.

**THE HONEST VERDICT**

Would I blurb it? Aye. I'd blurb the hell out of it. Because it does the thing I care about most: it makes you feel something for a character who can't tell you what he's feeling, and it does it without tricks and without sentimentality, and when the darkness comes it comes because the world is dark, not because the author is trying to impress you.

It's not perfect. The patron needs flesh. The boat could lose ten percent. The voice occasionally reaches for a construction that's too clean for a mind that doesn't have language. But these are small things.

The one-line pitch: *Tarzan written by someone who understands that the real jungle is the one inside you, and the real cage is the one they put on your neck before you're old enough to know it's there.*

Read it. It'll hurt. You'll be glad it did.
