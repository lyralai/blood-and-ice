# The Weight of the Word: On *Blood and Ice — Book One: The Wild Boy*

### A review by Ursula K. Le Guin

---

There is a sentence I have written elsewhere — that a writer is a person who cares about words. I want to put that sentence at the door of this book, because *Blood and Ice* is a book that cares about words with a ferocity I do not often encounter in fantasy, and because the caring is its greatest achievement and its greatest risk.

**LANGUAGE.** The narrator's voice is the book's engine and its argument. It is a made language — English stripped to the bone, rebuilt from the body outward, a grammar of gut and footfall and scent. There are no ordinary sentences here. Every perception comes through a mind that does not possess the word for perception. The narrator reads the world the way a literate person reads a page: track in mud, set of jaw, change of wind. Everything is a text. Everything is *read.* This is the voice's triumph and, occasionally, its tic.

The triumph is genuine. When the narrator lies among the apes and hears the still-sound — *"be still, you are held, nothing comes"* — the repetition does not weary; it accrues. The phrase becomes liturgical, a prayer the body teaches the mouth. The passage where the bold small one brings him a frog, and the narrator barks "the wrong glad sound" — that is poetry. Not the decorative poetry of fine prose, but the structural poetry of a mind discovering joy and having no shelf to put it on. The frog hops away. Neither of them stops it. That is the scene. It is one of the truest passages I have read in a fantasy novel in years.

The tic is the hyphenated compound: *meaning-sound, holding-center, keeping-arm, gentling-sound.* These are the narrator's only architecture, and for long stretches they carry the weight beautifully. But they also become a rhythm the prose leans on, and in the later chapters — particularly the pit sequences — the compounds begin to do the work that syntax should do. The voice risks becoming not a genuine foreign tongue but a style, a manner of speaking that signals "primal" without always achieving it. The author should trust the voice enough to let it, occasionally, simply *say* a thing. Even this narrator, perhaps especially this narrator, should be allowed a plain sentence now and then. The body that reads everything does not always need a compound to say what it sees.

**THE OTHER.** This is where the book most succeeds and where I want to press the author hardest. The boundary between human and animal is the book's central imaginative act, and it is handled with a seriousness that honors both sides of the line. The Vorth are not Disney gorillas. They are not cozy. They have law and violence and hierarchy, and the narrator's place among them is earned through the body — through the reading of track and posture and the political arithmetic of the challenge ground. The old male with the torn ear setting his feet in the narrator's track is a masterpiece of worldbuilding: two creatures of different species finding a practical interdependence that becomes, without sentimentality, a bond.

What troubles me is the inverse of this achievement. The narrator is, by every signal the text offers, human. He heals wrong-fast. The swarms do not land on him. The seer feels something in his blood. He carries a sigil connected to a civilization of cut stone. These are the markers of the Chosen One, the Prophesied Boy, the special child — and they are the least interesting thing about him. Every time the book reaches toward the supernatural explanation of his difference, it dims the much more radical thing it is doing: telling a story about a creature who is neither fully ape nor fully human and who builds a self in the gap. The blood-warmth, the healing — these are not bad in themselves, but they nudge the narrator toward a destiny narrative, and destiny narratives are the great cliché of fantasy. I would rather the wrongness in his blood remain unexplained — a mystery the seer bows to and cannot read — than become, in later books, the reason he is Important. Importance is the enemy of interest.

**POWER AND OWNERSHIP.** Here the book finds its moral spine. The passage from the island to the slave-pit city is a passage from one kind of ownership to another, and the narrator's body knows this before his head does. The brand on his neck — "the thing the big one is learning now, on the dock stone, in the cart" — is the book's central metaphor and its central reality. Property is not an abstraction here. It is a hand on the back of the neck. It is a chain on a neck that has known only the keeping-arm. The book does not flinch from the realization that the narrator has been *owned* his entire life — by the slavers who branded him, by the island that claimed him, by the tribe that admitted him, by the pirates who took him. The question the pit chapters ask — is there a self that is not property? — is the only question worth asking in a fantasy that takes slavery seriously.

And the book does take it seriously. The scene where the patron prices the crew on the dock — reading teeth, reading muscle, the narrow face going from body to body with the accuracy of a scale — is as cold and as precise as anything in the literature of the trade. The narrator recognizes it because the body remembers. This is not metaphorical slavery, the kind fantasy uses for cheap stakes. This is the thing itself.

**THE WOMEN.** This is where I must be direct.

The Kala is the book's heart. She is not a function. She is a mother, and the book lets her be one without apology — vast, slow, fierce, dying in the wrong air, making the gentling-sound through the bars of a cage on the stone floor. The narrator's love for her is the book's emotional truth, and it is earned in every chapter. I believe in the Kala completely.

Lira — the sky-reader — is less fully present but sharply drawn. She argues. She has a heading. She prices the patron with her eyes while the patron prices her. I wanted more of her. The book gives her the map and the wind and the argument and then takes her away, and the taking feels premature. She is a mind the book cannot afford to lose.

Sable — the steady-handed woman, the surgeon — is where the trouble is. She is defined entirely by her function: the hands that go to wounds. She has no interiority the narrator can read, and since the narrator cannot read her, the book does not give her to us. She is competent. She is valuable. She is, in the patron's economy, "more valuable than the hands that carry." But the book accepts the patron's reading. Sable is her hands. The book does not push past the function to the woman, and this is a failure the narrator's limited perspective excuses but the author should not. Give Sable a scene the narrator cannot witness. Give her a voice that is not professional. The surgeon who prices the boy's healing body "differently" is a woman standing at the edge of a moral decision, and the book leaves her on that edge. That is not enough.

**WHAT IS MISSING.** The book needs humor that is not the cook's — though the cook's humor, and the big one's laugh across the iron, is one of the book's grace notes. It needs more of the interior life of the crew, more of the pack as minds rather than bodies. And it needs, urgently, a second perspective — not to replace the narrator's voice, which is the book's great achievement, but to *frame* it. The narrator reads the world through the body. We need, somewhere, a chapter read through the head. The sky-reader's map. The surgeon's journal. A voice that does not read with its feet.

But these are the criticisms of a reader who has been moved. *Blood and Ice* does what fantasy at its best ought to do: it makes the familiar strange, it gives the body its full dignity as an organ of knowing, and it asks questions about ownership and freedom that are not decorative. The narrator's voice, mannered as it sometimes becomes, is a real literary creation — not a trick but a sustained act of imagination. The boy in the pit, counting in the dark, is a character I will not forget.

The book's last line says *I am still counting.* That is right. That is exactly right. The counting is the self that cannot be owned.
