# A Craft Review — In the Voice of Brandon Sanderson

**On reading *Blood and Ice: Book One — The Wild Boy* (Revised Chapters)**

---

Okay. So. Let me talk about this book.

I'm going to do what I do — which is try to be honest, try to be specific, and try to treat this like a conversation between working writers. Because that's what this is. This manuscript is doing something genuinely ambitious, and I want to respect that by being precise about where it succeeds and where it doesn't.

## 1. PROMISES — What Does This Book Promise?

Here's the thing I teach my students first: every book makes a promise in its opening chapters. The promise is a contract. It says: *this is the kind of story you are reading, and this is what I will deliver.*

What does *Blood and Ice* promise? It promises a story about a boy who was property, who became a king, and who will be property again — and the promise is that the *becoming* in the middle is the thing that changes how the second owning lands. That's a spectacular promise. That's a promise with teeth.

The island chapters (11, 18, 20–22) promise something very specific: a feral child's integration into a non-human society, told from inside a consciousness that has no access to standard English. The pirate and pit chapters (25–27) promise the payoff — the collision between the world that made him and the world that made the world that made him. And the Eye-and-Thorn, threaded through all of it, promises a mystery that connects the personal (who is this boy?) to the cosmological (what is the Red?).

Does it deliver? Mostly, yes. The pit landing works because the book earned it. Every piece of the ending — the brand, the name, the crown, the Kala's breathing three levels up — was planted chapters ago. That's good architecture. But I'll push back on one thing: the promise of the *Red* is not yet delivered in this section. It's teased beautifully. But if you're telling me this is a hard magic system, I need more on-ramp before the pit.

## 2. PROSE — The Voice

Let me address the ape in the room. The voice of this book is its greatest asset and its greatest risk.

Where it works — and I mean *really* works — is in the island chapters. The way Rook's consciousness renders the world through body-knowledge, the compound nouns (*keeping-arm, bold heart, far mouth*), the way meaning lives in the *shape* of a sound rather than the word itself. The chapter where the seer lays its finger on Rook's throat and reads his blood? That's one of the best scenes I've read in a student manuscript in years. It does through implication what most writers can't do with explicit exposition. The voice isn't a gimmick there — it's the *mechanism* of the storytelling.

Where it becomes a crutch: the boat chapters (the crossing, Chapter 24-ish) start to strain. The voice is doing the same things in the same rhythms — *the body reads, the body counts, the far mouth leans* — and in the more static setting of a hold, without the rich physical world of the island to anchor each compound noun in a *new* sensory reality, the style starts to eat itself. I caught myself admiring the technique rather than feeling the scene. That's the danger of a voice this distinctive: when the plot momentum drops, the reader notices the machinery.

**Recommendation:** On the boat, let the voice crack open a little more. Rook is hearing *words* — the big one's kind-sound, the argument-shapes. Let those word-fragments bleed into his narration more. The voice should be *changing* in the boat chapters, not just sustaining. Show me the language coming back before the pit.

## 3. PACING — The Structure

The island chapters sing. Chapters 20 and 22 — the confrontation on the high stones and the night raid — are masterfully paced. The raid sequence in particular is one of the best action scenes I've seen in draft: the way Rook's perception fragments when the Red comes up, the way time stretches and compresses, the way we feel the cost of every choice. That's not just good action writing — that's action writing that *means something*, where every blow is a character decision.

Where it drags: the crossing. The dying sequence on the boat — the crew thinning, the fever taking them one by one — is doing important work (establishing the big one's arc, the steady-handed woman's pricing of Rook) but it's doing it in a rhythm I've already heard. *Another one dies. The cook makes the joke-sound. The thick-armed one's face goes flat.* These beats repeat without escalating. In a revision, I'd compress this section hard. You can lose two of the three crew deaths and lose nothing. The reader needs to *feel* the pack thinning, not just be told it's happening beat after beat.

The pit chapters (26–27) recover beautifully. The pricing sequence — where each crew member is valued and led away — is devastating, and it works because it's *structured*. Each pricing reveals something about the world, the patron, and the person being priced. That's what pacing IS — each beat does more than one thing.

## 4. THE MAGIC SYSTEM — The Red

Now we get to the part I'm most curious about. The Red.

You know I'm a hard magic guy. I believe readers should understand the rules well enough to anticipate how magic will solve — or fail to solve — a problem. The joy of a hard magic system is the "aha" moment when the reader goes: *oh, that's how it works, and that means THIS is possible.*

Right now, the Red is soft. Beautifully soft, but soft. It heals him. It makes him fast. It steadies the Kala's breathing — sometimes. The seer can feel it. But I don't understand its *cost* yet, and I don't understand its *limits*. The "sometimes" of the blood-warmth helping the Kala is the key problem: if I don't know why it works sometimes and not other times, I can't anticipate, and if I can't anticipate, I can't have that satisfaction of a hard magic payoff.

**What I'd want:** You don't need to turn this into Allomancy. The primal voice doesn't need a physics textbook. But you DO need to plant a clear rule — even one — that the reader can hold. My suggestion: make the Red *cost something specific.* Right now Rook feeds it and it makes him fast and strong, but the only cost is "the still place has to hold it back." What if the cost is physical — what if every time the Red comes up, it burns something the body needs? What if the healing isn't free — what if it ages him, or burns his own warmth reserves so that after the healing burst he goes cold for days?

Give me ONE clear rule. One cost. One limit. Then the reader can play the game.

## 5. CHARACTER

**Rook** is extraordinary. The feat of sustaining a first-person voice this alien over this many chapters — and making the reader *feel* him, root for him, understand him — is real craft. The moment where he barks the "glad sound" at the bold small one over the frog is one of the most quietly human scenes I've ever read in a fantasy novel. That's character work.

**The big one** (I assume he gets a name later) is well-drawn through action. The holding-center metaphor is doing heavy lifting, and it works because we see it *break* on the dock. That's a good arc — the strong leader whose strength becomes irrelevant in a bigger system. Classic. Effective.

**Marrow** — the runt, the named thing — is the emotional anchor. When he puts his body between the Kala and the raiders, I felt it. When his still-sound comes through the dark of the pit at the end, I *really* felt it. Keep this character central. He's the heart.

**The patron** is the one I want more from. Right now the patron is a type — the pale-eyed calculating owner — and the type works, but in the pit chapters the patron becomes more idea than person. I'd like one scene, even a short one, where the patron does something *unexpected* — not cruel, not calculating, something that makes Rook (and the reader) unable to slot the patron into the "brand made vast" category. Complexity is the enemy of archetype, and the patron is currently an archetype.

## 6. THE ENDING — Does the Pit Landing Work?

Yes. Unambiguously yes.

Here's why: the ending works because it's *inevitable without being predictable.* Every piece — the brand on the neck, the name behind the teeth, the crown in the body, the Kala's breathing through stone, Marrow's still-sound in the dark — was planted twenty pages ago. When Rook kneels in the dark and "lays the squared stones in the still place," he's doing what the Kala taught him. The ending is the callback that doesn't feel like a callback because it's *earned* — it's the thing the whole book was building toward, the moment where the boy takes inventory of everything the world took from him and everything it couldn't.

And then the Red pulses. And the pulse says *up.*

That's a great ending for Book One. It promises the next book without being a cliffhanger. It resolves the emotional arc of this section (Rook finds what he *is* in the dark) while opening the plot arc (what will he *do* with it?). The last line — "now I am not the only thing in here counting back" — tells me Marrow is there, the big one is there, and the game has changed. I want to read Book Two.

## Final Thoughts

This is a book with a genuinely original voice doing a genuinely original thing. The risk of a voice this strange is that it walls out readers — and there will be readers who bounce off the first chapter because they don't want to work that hard. That's fine. This isn't a book for every reader. It's a book for readers who want to be *inside* a consciousness they've never been inside before.

The craft improvements I'd ask for in revision: tighten the boat chapters, give the Red one clear rule, and complicate the patron. Everything else — the island chapters, the raid, the pricing, the pit landing — is working at a high level.

This is the real thing. Keep going.
