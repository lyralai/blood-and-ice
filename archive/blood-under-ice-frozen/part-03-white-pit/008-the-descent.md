# Chapter 8 Draft: The Descent

They washed me before they killed me.

That seemed wasteful.

Khar Vane did not think so. Two of them held my arms while a Hollow Antler acolyte poured cold water over my head from a bone bowl. Fen-Kin watched from the edge of the clearing. Salt Veyr had come too, somehow, because Maren knew where history would turn profitable or fatal and did not like hearing about either secondhand.

Senn stood behind her with his throat wrapped fresh.

He would not meet my eyes.

Good. I did not know what I would have done if he had.

The water ran down my face, into my mouth, over the wound under my ribs. The red heat disliked the cold. It curled tighter inside me.

Vessa marked my brow with white ash.

Asha tied three bone beads into my hair and spoke words that made Cairn stare at the ground.

Harvek cut my palm with a little crescent blade.

Yara caught the blood in a black cup.

She had removed the red cloth from her eyes. They were pale, almost colorless, and too steady.

"Rook," she said.

The name sounded wrong in her mouth. Not because she said it badly. Because she said it as if it had already happened and she was only catching up.

She touched the cup to my lips.

I did not drink.

Harvek's hand closed on the back of my neck.

I drank.

My own blood tasted of iron, cold water, and something sweet underneath. The sweetness frightened me.

Yara listened to my swallow.

Then she said a name I had never heard.

The Khar Vane answered it.

She said another.

They answered.

The third time, she touched my chest.

The word she gave me was not Rook.

It was shorter. Harder. A death-name. I knew that without translation. A name meant to be carried down and left below if the body did not return.

I hated it.

That helped. Hate gives the hands something to hold.

They took me from the antler clearing to the white ground beyond the trees.

The White Pit was not a hole at first.

It was a place where the world had given up color. Pale stone, pale dust, pale roots, pale bones half-buried in the slope. The ground dipped slowly, then broke open without warning into a throat of rock.

No clean bottom.

Cold breathed up from it.

Not wind. Breath.

Every tribe stopped at a different distance.

Salt Veyr nearest the path out.

Fen-Kin where reeds still grew in wet cracks.

Hollow Antler by the carved stones.

Khar Vane at the lip.

That told me whose place this truly was.

Harvek tied a rope around my chest. Not to save me. To lower me far enough that the fall did not kill me before the pit had a chance.

I laughed then.

It came out wrong.

Too low.

Yara's head tilted.

The Red pressed behind my teeth, amused by rope, by law, by the little theater of people pretending the pit needed permission.

I bit my tongue.

Mine, I thought.

The heat waited.

Maren came to the lip. No one stopped her. That was interesting.

She touched the cord still tied around my wrist. Salt Veyr debt under bark, reed, bone, and blood.

Then she put something in my hand.

The broken half of the bone toggle.

The first weapon I had made useful here.

Not a knife. Barely a point. A memory with an edge.

She closed my fingers around it and said one word in her language.

I did not know it.

Senn did.

His face changed.

Maybe it meant come back. Maybe it meant paid. Maybe it meant do not make me regret the fish.

I kept the bone.

Cairn stepped forward next. Asha did not stop him.

He leaned close. "Wake-right," he said in the almost-language.

Then, after a breath: "Or I cut."

I smiled because fear had nowhere else to go.

"Try," I said.

He understood that well enough.

The rope tightened.

Harvek spoke. Vessa spoke. Asha spoke. Yara sang under them all, quiet enough that her voice seemed to come from the pit instead of her body.

They lowered me.

The white world rose around my face.

The sky became a circle.

Faces leaned over it: antler, reed, salt, tooth.

Then stone took them.

The pit wall scraped my shoulder. Old blood had dried in the cracks. Not all old. Some dark. Some brown. Some black. My feet struck a ledge, slipped, struck another. The rope burned under my arms.

Halfway down, something moved below.

The rope stopped.

Above, voices argued.

Below, the movement came again.

Wet stone.

Breath.

The red heat unfolded.

No, I thought.

But I was fourteen and bleeding and being lowered into a white throat by people who had found a lawful way to want me dead.

No was a small word.

The rope dropped.

I fell the last body-length into darkness and hit bone hard enough to hear one of my ribs crack.

The thing below me breathed in.

So did the Red.

That was how I learned the eighth law of the island.

Some doors only open inward.
