# Chapter 21 Draft: Standing Witness

Morning made the crown worse.

Night had fire and rain and song. Night lets people believe a thing because everyone is tired and wet and afraid to be the first mouth that says no. Morning has smoke in the hair, sour bellies, children asking what changed, and old women counting how much meat a new law costs.

The bone band lay on the hide beside me.

I had not slept with it on. That was my first argument of the day.

Solek found me before the sun cleared the low teeth of the ridge. He carried a cup of seal broth in one hand and a willow switch in the other.

"Drink," he said.

"Hit someone else."

"Drink first."

I drank because refusing food is a rich man's kind of anger. The broth was grey and hot. It put a little strength under my ribs. The Red shifted at the heat like something turning in a den.

Not king, I thought.

It gave me a memory that was not mine: red hands on white ice, a circle of men kneeling with their mouths open, a boy with his throat cut laughing through another mouth.

I spat broth into the dirt.

Solek watched the spit steam. "Dream?"

"Something old."

"Old things are liars with long practice."

That was the closest Solek came to comfort.

He tapped the bone band with the willow switch. "Wear it when you stand witness."

"I said terms. The terms are the crown."

"Terms no one can see are wind."

"Good. Let them breathe it."

He hit my knuckles with the switch. Not hard. Enough to make the red thing notice.

I looked at him.

"There," he said. "You felt it."

"I felt a stick."

"You felt answer."

He was right. I hated him for spending no extra words on being right.

Asha waited by Harvek's law stone with Cairn behind her and six Fen-Kin standing far enough apart that no one could call them a guard. They all carried spears with the heads wrapped. Wrapped blades meant restraint. It also meant there were blades under the cloth.

Khar Vane hated the sight of them.

The camp had changed in one night. People moved around me as if my shadow had teeth. Some would not turn their backs. Some turned their backs too slowly, making a show of trust. Dremm sat near the lower fires, one side of his head bound in pale cloth, surrounded by young warriors pretending not to be his ring.

His missing ear had done what my mercy had not. It had given him shape.

Before, Dremm was a boy who loved blood too much.

Now he was proof, and proof is harder to beat than a boy.

Yara saw me looking. "They call him Half-Hearing."

"Do they think that is praise?"

"They think wound means truth."

"Wounds mostly mean someone was close enough to cut you."

She smiled without softness. "Tell them."

Harvek struck the law stone with the back of his knife. Once. Twice. Three times. Camp talk lowered, but did not die. Khar Vane liked law best when it already agreed with appetite.

Solek set the bone band in my hands.

I put it on.

The camp exhaled.

The Red liked the sound.

No.

Asha stepped forward before any Khar voice could claim the moment. Smart. If she waited, she would look admitted by Khar Vane. If she moved first, she became part of the terms, not a guest under them.

"I stand witness," she said.

Tovek translated for Salt Veyr near the outer carts. Maren was not there. That meant she was doing something more useful than listening.

Asha lifted a narrow cord of dark hair. Mine. Cut from the back of my neck while I slept, I realized, and my hand went to my knife before sense caught up.

Cairn's spear shifted. Still wrapped.

Otta barked from behind me, "If you can sleep through hair-taking, boy, do not glare as if you guarded it."

Laughter broke the tight place in the camp. Not much. Enough.

Asha tied the hair into a bone tube no longer than my thumb. "This is not leash. This is witness. When red takes him past hearing, Fen-Kin will call old blood by old blood. If he answers, law holds. If he does not, law kills."

The young warriors around Dremm muttered.

One of them, a broad boy with fresh ash cuts on his ribs, stood. "Khar Vane does not need marsh mothers to call its crowned."

Harvek did not move.

That meant the answer was mine.

I looked at the boy until he remembered I had eyes.

"Name," I said.

He swallowed. "Bruk."

"Bruk. If the thing in me wears my face and tells you to eat your brother, will you ask Fen-Kin to call me back?"

His mouth twitched.

"Answer in law," Solek said.

Bruk's jaw worked. "Yes."

"If it tells you to cut Harvek's throat?"

Harvek's smile showed black teeth.

"Yes," Bruk said.

"If it tells you that your hunger is holy and every dead man belongs to your mouth?"

No one translated that. They did not need to.

Bruk looked at Dremm.

There it was. The little glance that made a faction.

"Yes," he said, but the word came late.

I stepped down from the stone. The crown bones clicked against my brow.

"Then you need her."

I pointed at Asha.

"You do not have to like the hand that pulls you from a cliff."

Dremm rose then.

The camp went still in the old way, the way it had before Blood Snow, when everyone hoped someone else would decide what kind of morning this was.

Dremm came to me with no weapon and one ear. He looked smaller without the war around him. Younger. That made me angrier.

"I hear with one ear," he said. "I heard enough. If Fen calls you back, I hold the rope. If Fen fails, I hold the knife."

It was good answer.

It was also performance.

I gave him what the camp needed before he could take more.

"Then stand beside Asha when she calls."

His face changed.

So did hers.

Asha understood first. Dremm as martyr was dangerous. Dremm as part of the leash was useful and humiliating, which meant he would either grow or break in public.

The young warriors hated it. Good. Let their hate have work to do.

Vessa did not come to witness.

Near dusk, Naim came instead, the Hollow Antler acolyte with doubtful eyes and antler dust under his nails. He carried no staff, no bowl, no painted face. Only a strip of white bark folded twice.

He gave it to Tovek, not to me.

That was Vessa's smarter move.

Make every word pass through price.

Tovek read, frowned, and looked at me as if calculating whether bad news could be sold twice.

"Hollow Antler recognizes no blood-crown made under foreign witness," he said. "Vessa invites Fen-Kin to cleanse its name by withdrawing from Khar Vane war law before moonrise tomorrow."

The camp snarled.

Asha said nothing.

I understood the shape late and then all at once.

Vessa had stopped trying to put a rope around my neck. Now she was cutting the ropes around everyone else.

No monster in that. No pit. No blood.

Just a woman with clean hands making allies ashamed of standing near me.

The Red did not stir.

That frightened me more.
