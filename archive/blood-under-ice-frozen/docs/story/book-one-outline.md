# Book One Outline

## Working Title

**Blood Under Ice**

## Book One Promise

A feral boy becomes a tribal symbol after surviving ritual sacrifice, but the power that makes him impossible to kill may also make him impossible to trust.

Book one should stay mostly on the Blackjaw Isles. The story needs to earn tribal leadership before it expands.

**Target length (locked 2026-06-13):** ~40-42 chapters / 120-160k words. Packet 6 (ch33-36, Winter Ledger) is the last major block of administrative strain. Peak crisis in ch37-38 (live caller test under failure + Guest weaponizing the crown's own tools). First external signal as Book One cliffhanger (ch40-42). Ending state: following earned but visibly held by systems + old-blood check; one public fracture paid; Guest educated by the restraints used against it; island awake and now visible to the world.

## Part One: Wild Boy

**Purpose:** establish Rook as a physical survivor before the story asks him to become political.

### Chapter 1: Black Shore

Rook wakes on a black stone beach after a wreck or flight. He is cold, hungry, and alone except for whatever animal memory still lives in his body. He scavenges, hides, and learns quickly that the island does not waste the dead.

Key beats:

- immediate survival problem
- first glimpse of island ecology
- Rook's old northern blood hinted through cold endurance
- no full magic explanation

### Chapter 2: Hunger Law

Rook tracks carrion and steals food from a tribal cache. He sees signs of organized people before he meets them. The island's social world enters through objects: knots, carved bone, smoke marks, butchered seals, antlers.

Key beats:

- Rook is animal-clever, not socially fluent
- Salt Veyr signs introduced
- Hollow Antler taboo markers introduced
- first sense that food is politics

### Chapter 3: First Blood In The Trees

A small hunting party finds him. He is wounded badly enough for the Red to stir but not fully take over. He survives in a way that creates rumor.

Key beats:

- first partial red-state
- witnesses escape
- Rook sees fear as tool before he has words for power

## Part Two: Four Tribes

**Purpose:** move from survival into political capture.

### Chapter 4: Salt Knots

Salt Veyr captures or bargains with Rook. Maren Salt-Hand sees that he is more useful alive than dead. Rook learns that the island has laws and that laws are another kind of trap.

Key beats:

- first translator/interpreter
- knot-debt culture
- practical logistics
- Rook treated as trade risk

### Chapter 5: Antlers In Fog

Hollow Antler takes jurisdiction. Vessa argues that the boy's blood and the hunting-party deaths require ritual handling. Rook does not understand the words, but he understands when people discuss his death politely.

Key beats:

- ritual authority
- old law
- dread through ceremony
- Rook's hatred of beautiful speech

### Chapter 6: Fen Names

Fen-Kin intervenes. Asha Fen recognizes signs of old northern blood and argues that careless sacrifice may wake a worse debt. Cairn meets Rook and sees a rival instead of an omen.

Key beats:

- old-blood introduced
- first possible identity beyond animal survival
- political split over what Rook is

### Chapter 7: Khar Vane Demand

Khar Vane demands the White Pit. Harvek Bone-Mouth frames it as law: if the boy is nothing, the pit takes him; if he returns, the island has spoken. Everyone sees the compromise as useful for different reasons.

Key beats:

- Khar Vane culture
- cannibal sacrament as law, not random shock
- pit established as political technology

## Part Three: The White Pit

**Purpose:** deliver the transformation without making it cheap.

### Chapter 8: The Descent

Rook is prepared for sacrifice. Yara Khar sings his temporary death-name. He is lowered or thrown into the White Pit with wounds, hunger, and ritual fear already working on him.

Key beats:

- first full naming conflict
- Rook's terror stays childlike
- sensory focus: bone dust, cold stone, old blood

### Chapter 9: Below Hunger

The pit is not empty. Rook faces animal, prisoner, old rite, or environmental death. He is injured past ordinary survival. The Red wakes fully.

Key beats:

- possession as survival
- laughter that is not his
- healing as horror
- memory fragments from ice-blood

### Chapter 10: The Boy Returns Wrong

Rook emerges alive. Not cleanly victorious, not triumphant. Wrong. Witnesses see enough to build a religion and enough to fear that religion.

Key beats:

- Yara names the event before Vessa can
- Khar Vane kneels or begins the kneeling process
- Hollow Antler loses interpretive control
- Fen-Kin recognizes catastrophe

## Part Four: Blood Crown

**Purpose:** leadership begins before wisdom.

### Chapter 11: Meat And Meaning

Khar Vane takes Rook into its camp as omen, hostage, guest, and weapon. Rook learns hunger law. Dremm adores him. Solek insists law still binds him.

Key beats:

- Rook's first followers are the wrong followers
- cannibal rites shown with internal rules
- Harvek tests him

### Chapter 12: The First Restraint

Rook is provoked into violence but manages to stop before killing someone politically useful. This matters more than another massacre. Some followers are disappointed.

Key beats:

- restraint as power
- Dremm's bad loyalty
- Harvek's respect grows
- The Red resents denial

### Chapter 13: Salt Debt

Maren forces a practical bargain: if Rook wants followers, he needs food and boats. He cannot eat myth. This chapter grounds the rising cult in logistics.

Key beats:

- politics of stores
- first negotiated alliance
- Rook learns that fear does not preserve fish

### Chapter 14: Antler Countermove

Vessa declares Rook a breach that must be contained. Hollow Antler tries to isolate Khar Vane through taboo, cutting off ritual exchange and marriage routes.

Key beats:

- power through social pressure
- Rook sees nonviolent force
- Khar Vane wants war

### Chapter 15: Fen Challenge

Asha sends Cairn to test Rook or draw him out. The result should be emotionally complex: Cairn sees the boy under the monster, and Rook sees what trained old-blood discipline looks like.

Key beats:

- rival mirror
- old-blood discipline
- possible anchor relationship

### Chapter 16: Bone-Mouth's Lesson

Harvek teaches Rook that followers may use him as much as he uses them. A chief who cannot disappoint his people is already eaten.

Key beats:

- mentorship with threat underneath
- Khar Vane law deepened
- Rook begins to understand symbolic captivity

### Chapter 17: The Feast Dispute

A sacramental feast becomes a political crisis. Rook must choose whether to endorse Khar Vane's harshest custom, reject it, or reshape it. Whatever he chooses costs him.

Key beats:

- cannibalism as central moral/political pressure
- Dremm pushes excess
- Solek defends law
- Yara watches for the song

### Chapter 18: The Second Pit

Hollow Antler or a coalition forces a second rite, not to kill Rook but to prove control over him. Rook refuses to be interpreted.

Key beats:

- public confrontation
- The Red almost takes over
- Rook chooses a third path

### Chapter 19: Blood Snow

The first major tribal battle. Rook uses strategy, terrain, and reputation before unleashing the Red. The battle should prove he can command, not just survive.

Key beats:

- logistics from Salt Veyr
- old-blood knowledge from Fen-Kin
- Khar Vane shock violence
- Hollow Antler ritual counterattack

### Chapter 20: The Crown That Bites

Book climax. Khar Vane wants to crown Rook as sacred war-chief. The other tribes see disaster. Rook accepts some form of leadership but rejects full possession by the myth, creating an unstable compromise.

Key beats:

- victory with moral debt
- Rook publicly names the Red as not-the-king
- some followers hate that restraint
- final image: the island has not been conquered; it has been awakened

## Ending State

By the end of book one:

- Rook has a following.
- Khar Vane is no longer politically containable.
- Hollow Antler has lost monopoly over meaning.
- Salt Veyr has been pulled into war logistics.
- Fen-Kin has not decided whether Rook is kin or catastrophe.
- The Red is stronger and more articulate.
- Rook knows that being followed can be another kind of pit.

## Open Questions For Human Review

- How dark should Khar Vane practice be on-page versus implied by law and aftermath?

## 2026-06-13 Scope Decision & Structure Update (Subagent Task Deliverable)

**Recommendation:** Book One target length ~40-42 chapters total (ch1-40/42). Packet 5 (ch21-32: After the Crown / Stores And Splinters) and Packet 6 (ch33-36: The Winter Ledger) successfully delivered the hard earning of leadership through systems under strain (cradle tenth, counting power, road-sworn/winter-sworn living debt, Reed-Bend small loop + "the road's due", Naim fracture, Half-Ear/Keff faction, Guest admin/strategic learning from the tools used to contain it, explicit boy-king cost of "more voices = thinner center").

**Peak Crisis (ch37-38):** The visible breaking of the winter ledger under combined material failure (quiet frames / child death under a promised small loop or cradle), faction push for old rite on the small, and live public test of the Fen caller / wrapped-spear discipline. The Guest — educated by ch26 training + ch29-36 store language, small loop weight, "the due", and living debt transformations — anticipates the rhythm, turns the discipline back at the caller, offers strategic "clean" solutions using crown tools (tribute via fear, cut weak claims first, collect the due with teeth the boards cannot match). The core ch20 crown term ("Fen may call him back. If he does not return, bind or kill under the terms that made the crown") is invoked live and tested in front of full four-tribe + small + road-sworn + cradle + Yara witnesses. Rook holds via accumulated tools but the return carries visible new costs (deeper physical toll, Asha names the training risk publicly to all witnesses, relational fractures widen, small witnesses see the thing answer the name). This is the mid-Book One peak: 1-2 major turning points + the required big public rite/test.

**First Major External Consequence / Cliffhanger (ch40):** Immediately after the public call and the choice that strains the "The Red is not king" term, the first undeniable mainland signal arrives (lone runner, small boat, or washed-up dead with ice-blood marks/token naming "Rauth" / "the pit that answers with laughter" / "the blood that woke on the black isles"). Witnessed by the same group. The tribes must interpret the sign under the fresh cost of the call and the ledger failure. Rook's closing reflection synthesizes the arc: pit survival -> crown under law -> winter ledger strain -> live call test -> choice that educated the Guest with the boy's own tools -> first external eyes. The island has been earned and strained; the larger consequence is now inevitable. The boy-king problem has grown with every knot tied.

**Refined Book One Ending State (ch40-42):** Rook has the following and the order, but it is visibly held by living debt systems, the Fen old-blood check, and Salt's thin boats more than pit myth. The "The Red is not king" term has been publicly tested and re-affirmed at high cost; the Guest has learned that the law, the call, the due, and the small loop are doors it can stand inside. Visible fractures paid (Naim's choice public or a small death owned; faction scar; Asha/Cairn more distant but necessary). Salt's administrative power is the practical government keeping the count alive, but promises have multiplied mouths faster than boats can move. The island is awake; the center is thinner because enemies can point to every new mouth the name must feed. The first mainland consequence/cliffhanger forces confrontation that the crown is no longer only an island political problem. Rook holds the name but knows the Guest learned the law he built to contain it. The White Pit was only the beginning.
