# Voice Guide

This project needs an original voice: visceral first-person intensity with political clarity, cosmic dread, and prose craft that rewards close reading.

**Tonal anchors — the feel we're chasing:**
- **Red Rising urgency:** Propulsive. The reader feels every betrayal and victory in their chest. First-person intensity that grabs the throat and doesn't let go.
- **Game of Thrones density:** Scale, moral complexity, real consequences. Factions with competing interests that make sense. The world is bigger than the protagonist and doesn't care about him.
- **Kingkiller Chronicle craft:** Beautiful, intimate prose. The gap between the myth and the man. Magic that feels deep and real. Slow-burn mastery — watching someone become extraordinary.

**Rook's driving goal:** Belonging. A stolen child who goes feral because everyone left. Everything he does is a lost kid trying to build a family the world keeps taking. The beast doesn't need anyone — that's the threat. Every chapter: reaching toward connection or the beast pulling toward isolation.

**What this is NOT:** dark fantasy (horror-adjacent). This is epic fantasy with visceral, literary prose. The darkness comes from the world and the cost, not from genre horror.

Do not imitate any named author's sentence patterns. Use the following internal rules instead.

## Point Of View

Primary POV is first-person Rook.

Early Rook does not explain the world like a scholar. He notices:

- hunger
- exits
- hands
- wounds
- weather
- who is allowed to speak
- who gets fed
- who watches without blinking

He understands politics physically before he understands it abstractly.

## Sentence Rhythm

Use short sentences for danger.

Use longer sentences when dread accumulates or when Rook is trying to understand a social system that has already trapped him.

Avoid constant one-line intensity. If every sentence screams, none of them do.

## Imagery Bank

Preferred imagery:

- black stone
- seal fat
- cold iron taste of blood
- bone dust
- salt
- antler wind
- wet fur
- old smoke
- hunger as a second mouth
- names as things held in the teeth
- ice as memory
- blood as witness

Use sparingly:

- crowns
- thrones
- destiny language
- abstract "darkness"
- generic rage

Avoid:

- borrowed lore terms
- modern idioms
- self-aware genre commentary
- endless repetition of the same symbolic phrase

## Rook's Voice By Stage

### Stage 1: Feral (Book 1, Act 1-2 — Island Alone)

Near-wordless. Body-thinking. Short sentences dominate but not uniformly — wonder and discovery get longer lines. The prose itself is feral: sensory, immediate, no abstraction.

Example direction:

> The beach had three bodies. Two gulls. One knife. I counted the knife first.

He should not sound like a polished adult reflecting from comfort. He doesn't think in words yet — he thinks in shape, smell, pain, tide.

**Critical evolution:** The feral voice is the foundation, not the whole book. It opens up as Rook opens up. The range:
- **Ch 1-4:** Near-wordless survival. Short. Physical. Body only.
- **Ch 5-8:** Sharpening. More sequence, more planning, more predator logic. Sentences gain teeth.
- **Ch 9-10:** The break. He realizes he's gone beast. The prose cracks open — longer, darker, self-aware for the first time.
- **Ch 11-14:** Contact with humans. The voice gains range — still physical, but capable of wonder, humor, tenderness. The girl pulls language back into him.
- **Ch 15-16:** Tightening again. Surveillance, tension, compression. Then the capture breaks it open — one clean human sentence.

### Stage 2: Captive (Book 2 — The Red)

Rook maps social power. The Red enters. Voice layers: feral base + Red underneath + political awakening.

Example direction:

> The woman with antlers did not raise her voice. That made the men listen harder. I hated her for teaching me that.

### Stage 3: Crowned (Book 2 late — Book 3)

Rook becomes aware that witnesses are building a version of him. The gap between myth and man — Kingkiller territory.

Example direction:

> They did not look at my face. They looked at the blood drying on me, as if it had spoken better than I had.

### Stage 4: Great Man (Book 3 late)

Rook holds both beast and boy. He has become the great man — not because he left the beast behind, but because he mastered it. The voice synthesizes — capable of violence and beauty, wisdom and hunger, in the same breath.

Example direction:

> Fear moves people fast. Hunger moves them longer. Law moves them when you are not there to bare your teeth.

## The Red Voice

The Red should not begin as a chatty inner demon.

Early:

- pressure
- heat
- laughter
- images
- instinctive certainty

Middle:

- repeated impulses
- almost-words
- memory that is not Rook's

Late:

- short phrases
- bargaining without human morality
- strategic hunger

Rule: the more articulate it becomes, the more worried the reader should be.

## Violence

Violence should be clear, consequential, and character-revealing.

Do not write gore as decoration. A violent scene should change at least one of:

- Rook's body
- Rook's reputation
- a political balance
- a relationship
- the Red's strength
- a tribe's interpretation of events

## Cannibalism

Khar Vane cannibalism must be treated as culture, law, sacrament, logistics, and horror. It cannot be random edginess.

Use rules:

- who may be eaten
- who may not
- what parts mean
- when the rite is public
- how shame attaches
- how scarcity changes practice

The horror should come partly from how coherent the practice is to them.

## Politics

Every faction needs material motives:

- food
- marriage
- boats
- ritual authority
- old names
- winter stores
- revenge
- legitimacy

No faction exists only to admire or oppose Rook.

## Revision Standard

A scene is worth keeping if it does at least two jobs:

- advances plot
- reveals character
- clarifies world
- changes political state
- develops the magic cost
- deepens voice

If a scene only says "the monster is scary" again, cut it or make it do real work.
