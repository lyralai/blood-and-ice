# Chapter 19 Draft: Blood Snow

The first blood after the second pit fell without permission.

It was not a battle at first. Battles are what singers call violence after someone survives to shape it. At the start it was only three hungry men, one blocked path, and a charm-seller with more pride than sense.

Hollow Antler acolytes stopped a Khar Vane road crew carrying Salt grain through the high pass. Dremm was with the crew because I had put him there. That meant the first mistake wore my handprint.

The acolytes said the second pit proved nothing because Khar law had polluted the question.

Dremm said something back.

No one later agreed what.

Then a young acolyte struck an old Khar Vane woman with a white staff. Not hard enough to kill. Hard enough to make witness.

Dremm broke his jaw.

After that, every law arrived too late.

By noon, two Khar Vane were dead, one Hollow Antler acolyte was missing, and Vessa's taboo had become a thing with blood under it. Harvek did not rage when the bodies came in. Rage would have comforted the camp. He looked at me instead, and I understood the lesson: followers ask permission after spending your name.

"You put him on road," Harvek said.

"Yes."

"You own part."

"Yes."

The Red approved of ownership when it smelled like guilt. Guilt is blood turned inward.

Maren arrived before sunset, furious because one of the dead Khar Vane had been carrying her grain. Asha came with Cairn and six Fen-Kin scouts. Vessa did not come. Vessa sent antlers.

They were planted on the ridge above the high pass by dusk.

War markers.

Not total war. Not yet. A measured strike, ritual enough to claim defense, sharp enough to demand answer.

Harvek gathered chiefs, speakers, witnesses, and me. Dremm stood at the edge with dried blood on his sleeve. He had the look of a boy expecting punishment and hoping for praise by accident.

I wanted to hit him.

That was when I knew I could not.

"Antler expects raid," Asha said. "They have prayers for raid."

"They have arrows for it too," Cairn added.

Maren spread a rough hide map on the ground. It was not pretty, but pretty maps are for people far from weather. This one had stones for ridges, fish bones for paths, cord for marsh water. Useful things.

"If Khar Vane storms the high pass, Salt loses two routes and gains nothing but heroic corpses," she said.

Dremm muttered, "Corpses can be useful."

Solek looked at him. "Yours least of all."

Harvek turned to me. "Answer."

I hated that word.

I studied the hide map. The high pass cut between black ridges and dipped near a frozen wash where snow still held in shadow. Hollow Antler would expect Khar Vane up the throat of it, screaming. They would have white-cloaked archers in the trees, acolytes with staff-hooks, maybe charm smoke to make warriors see judgment in fog.

"Do not go through the pass," I said.

Dremm made a sound.

I ignored him.

"Make them watch pass. Send fires. Horns. Old shields. Make them hear Khar Vane where Khar Vane should be."

Cairn leaned closer.

I pointed to the frozen wash. "Fen can move here?"

Asha looked at the map, then at me. "Slowly."

"Quietly?"

"If Khar Vane does not breathe like bulls beside us."

Maren laughed once.

I moved a fish bone. "Salt takes grain carts to lower road at dawn. Visible. Weak. Antler sends watchers to count profit. Khar Vane waits above wash, not pass. Fen brings them through."

Harvek's eyes narrowed. "And you?"

I touched the ridge stone.

"I stand where they expect monster."

No one liked that.

Good plans often begin by disappointing every appetite.

At dawn, the high pass filled with Khar Vane noise and almost no Khar Vane men. Boys beat shields behind rocks. Old women blew horns. Smoke pots made the ridge stink of war. Hollow Antler answered with silence, which meant they were listening.

I walked alone into the lower mouth of the pass.

Not truly alone. Cairn shadowed left somewhere in scrub. Yara watched from a stone high enough to make song if I died interestingly. Dremm was supposed to be with Harvek above the wash. I did not know whether he obeyed.

Snow lay in dirty sheets under the ridge shade.

Blood shows well on snow.

That was why Vessa had chosen the place.

The first arrow struck near my foot. White-fletched. Warning or bad aim. I kept walking.

The second cut my arm.

The Red woke smiling.

Not yet.

An acolyte stepped from the trees ahead, staff in both hands, antlers rising over his head like a dead tree. Two more appeared behind. Then five. Then enough.

"Breach," the first called.

"Boy," I said.

He did not like that. Good.

They advanced carefully. Not fools. They wanted me wounded, frightened, red. They wanted witnesses to see the thing answer.

So I ran.

Not at them. Away.

It shocked them. It shocked me too. The Red howled inside my chest, furious at my back turned to enemies. Arrows hissed. One struck my shoulder shallow. Pain flashed red. I kept running down the pass, dragging them after me, making myself coward, bait, boy.

Then the wash erupted behind them.

Khar Vane came over the snow lip without horns. Fen-Kin beside them, mud-dark and silent. Harvek first, axe low. Asha's spear took an acolyte through the thigh, pinning not killing. Cairn hit another with the butt of his knife. Khar Vane wanted slaughter. Fen-Kin made the first shape restraint.

For three breaths the plan held.

Then Dremm saw the acolyte who had struck the old woman.

He broke formation.

The young acolyte ran. Dremm followed, and three Khar Vane followed Dremm because bad loyalty spreads faster than command.

The line bent.

Hollow Antler archers found the bend.

Men fell.

My fault, I thought, because I had put Dremm on road, because I had wounded him, because I had thought shame could become discipline in three days.

The Red offered to fix it.

This time I let it rise halfway.

Not all. All was pit. All was no boy.

Half was worse in some ways because I could see what I chose.

I hit the first archer from below the ridge and felt his knee open under my hand. I did not take his throat. I took his bow and snapped it. I drove another into snow and held his face down until he stopped fighting, then let him breathe. Every mercy hurt like lifting stone. Every non-killing had to be chosen against a red intelligence that knew efficiency.

Dremm caught the young acolyte near the antler marker.

"No!" I shouted.

He heard.

He killed him anyway.

The snow changed color.

Battle ended soon after because small battles do. Not with a horn. Not with clean victory. People ran out of courage, blood, or reasons to stand. Hollow Antler withdrew into trees with their wounded. Khar Vane held the wash. Salt carts crossed the lower road untouched.

Useful victory.

Ugly victory.

Dremm stood over the dead acolyte, breathing hard.

I walked to him.

Everyone watched. Of course they did.

"You disobeyed."

He looked at me with wet eyes and a bloody mouth. "I answered."

"You disobeyed."

"He struck Gran Otta."

"You disobeyed."

His face twisted. "Then punish me, pit-boy."

There it was. The challenge. The plea.

The Red wanted his submission in blood.

Harvek watched from the wash. Asha watched. Vessa's surviving acolytes watched from the trees. Yara watched from her stone, already holding the song by the throat.

I took Dremm's axe.

He let me.

I threw it into the red snow between us.

"Carry him," I said, pointing at the dead acolyte.

Dremm blinked.

"Back to Antler line. Alone. No weapon. Tell them his death is yours, not Khar law."

"They will kill me."

"Maybe."

His lips peeled back. "You would give me to them?"

"You gave yourself when you broke line."

The words hurt him. Good. They hurt me too. Better.

Dremm looked at Harvek. Harvek gave him nothing.

At last Dremm lifted the dead acolyte.

The snow kept taking red from both of them.

He walked toward the trees.

No one sang.
