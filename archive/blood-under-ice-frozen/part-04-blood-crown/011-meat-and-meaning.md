# Chapter 11 Draft: Meat And Meaning

Khar Vane did not carry me like a wounded boy.

They carried me like proof.

Four of them made a litter from spear shafts and a hide cloak stiff with old smoke. They set me on it with care that was not gentleness. Care can be another kind of fear. A man uses both hands to lift a hot pot because he respects the burn, not because he loves the pot.

I tried to stand once.

My rib answered with a white bite behind the eyes. My knees folded. Two Khar Vane warriors caught me before I struck the stone. One laughed. The other did not. The one who laughed was young, broad in the shoulders, with a fresh scar down his cheek and bone rings tied into his braids.

Dremm, I learned.

He grinned at my blood as if it were a song he already knew.

"Returned," he said in the trade-tongue, bad but clear enough.

I spat at his foot.

He looked delighted.

That was when I knew he would be trouble.

Harvek walked ahead of the litter and did not look back. Yara walked beside me, humming under her breath. Her song had no words now. Words were for people who wanted an argument. Humming sank below argument. It got into the walking. It taught the warriors how to hold their shoulders and how to glance at me without seeming to stare.

Maren followed at a distance with Salt Veyr. I heard her voice once, sharp as a fish knife, cutting through a Khar Vane guard's refusal. She would not let them take me out of sight. That steadied me and worried me. Maren did not spend effort unless she expected a return.

Asha and the Fen-Kin moved in the reeds below the white ground, never close, never gone. Cairn's knife flashed once between stems. He wanted me to know he remained.

Hollow Antler did not follow us down the Khar Vane path.

That was worse than if they had.

Vessa had turned away at the pit, and all her acolytes had gone with her, their antler frames knocking in the wind. A person who leaves without trying to win has chosen a different ground. I did not know enough politics then to name it, but my body understood. When a hunter stops chasing, check for snares.

The Khar Vane camp sat under black uplands where the stone rose in broken teeth. It was not hidden. Salt Veyr hid from weather and raiders. Hollow Antler hid inside fog. Fen-Kin hid inside marsh. Khar Vane put its fires where anyone could see the smoke and decide whether hunger was worth bravery.

Their shelters were low and round, hide over bone frames. Skulls hung above some doors. Animal, mostly. Not all. Long racks stood near the central fire, but the meat there had been wrapped in dark leaves and tied with red cord. Nobody snatched from it. Children ran past and did not touch.

Rules, I thought.

Good.

The worst people are not those with terrible rules. The worst are those who decide rule by appetite and call it freedom. Terrible rules can be learned. Appetite changes when no one is watching.

They brought me to the central fire.

Every face turned.

I had seen hunger in many shapes. Mine. Senn's shame-hunger. Maren's counting hunger. Vessa's naming hunger. Asha's old fear made into duty.

Khar Vane hunger had teeth, but it also had order. The old sat nearest the heat. Children ate before warriors. A woman with one arm ladled broth into bowls and slapped Dremm's hand with the spoon when he reached too soon. Dremm took the slap and grinned at me as if to show me he could obey when obedience had witnesses.

They set the litter by Harvek's seat, which was only a flat stone with a wolf-sized hide over it. No crown. No carved back. No shining thing.

Only a place everyone knew not to sit.

Harvek crouched in front of me. He held up one hand before touching my bandage, asking permission or pretending to. I did not give it. He touched me anyway.

His fingers pressed around the broken rib. I bit down hard enough that my cracked tooth flared.

"Hurts," he said in my tongue.

That surprised me.

He smiled.

"Good."

He stood and spoke to the gathered camp. His voice did not fill the space. It made the space close around it. He pointed to the pitward road, to the blood on my chest, to Yara, to his own knee, then to me.

The camp answered in low voices.

Yara translated none of it. She watched my face instead. Maybe she wanted to see whether the boy understood the song before it was sung. Maybe she had already stopped caring about the boy.

Dremm pushed forward with a bowl.

Steam rose from it. Broth, dark and fatty. Bits of meat floated inside. My stomach clenched so hard pain jumped under my ribs.

Hunger does not ask what kind of meat.

That is why law must.

I smelled seal fat. Smoke. Bitter herb. Blood too, but every camp smelled of blood. My hands wanted the bowl before my mind caught up.

Solek the Thin stepped between us.

He was so narrow his bones seemed to have been sharpened under the skin. His hair was white, though his face was not old. A line of black ash crossed his mouth from ear to ear. He carried no weapon that I could see.

That meant he was dangerous in some other way.

He spoke once.

Dremm's grin died.

Good, I thought again.

Solek took the bowl from him and sniffed it. Then he dipped one finger, touched the broth to his own tongue, and waited. The camp waited with him. After three breaths he handed it to Harvek. Harvek drank. Only then did Solek give it to me.

"Guest first after law," Solek said in the trade-tongue.

His voice was dry as old hide.

Not miracle first. Not chief first. Guest first after law.

I remembered the White Pit and almost laughed. Law had eaten well today.

The broth burned my tongue. I drank anyway. There was meat in it. Seal, I decided. Or wanted to decide. The Red stirred, amused by the difference between knowing and choosing not to know.

I stopped after half the bowl.

Dremm looked wounded.

"Eat," he said.

Solek turned his head.

Dremm lowered his eyes.

I understood then that Khar Vane had brought me into a camp already split around my shape.

Dremm wanted the thing from the pit, bloody-mouthed and hungry, because that thing made every old rule feel newly permitted.

Solek wanted the rule to survive the miracle.

Harvek wanted both until one became impossible.

Yara wanted the version that would last longest in mouths after all of us were bones.

And me?

I wanted to sleep without waking in the dark below.

They gave me a place in Harvek's shelter, which was not kindness either. Omen. Hostage. Guest. Weapon. The same hide roof can cover many names.

Before I slept, Yara came and sat by the entrance.

She did not bring a drum. She did not sing. Without song she looked younger, though still not young. Her pale eyes caught firelight and kept it.

"Rook," she said.

The name was careful now.

I held it close.

She touched her throat, then pointed to the camp. "They need mouth."

"For what?"

She smiled a little. "For you."

I hated how much I understood.

When you cannot speak enough to own yourself, other people loan you their tongues and charge interest.

Yara leaned closer. "Below-Eater returned you."

"No."

Her smile widened, not happy. Interested.

"No?"

I touched my chest. "Rook returned."

The Red shifted under my ribs, not angry. Patient.

Yara looked down at my hand, at the place where my own name sat like a fist over the wound.

"Then Rook must teach the camp what that means before hungry boys teach it for him."

Outside, Dremm laughed at something too loudly. Several warriors laughed with him. Harvek did not.

Yara rose.

"Sleep if you can," she said.

I did not ask what happened if I could not.

The camp settled in layers: children first, then old ones, then those with duties, then warriors pretending they were not listening for me. Smoke pressed under the hide roof. My rib clicked. My stomach held the broth as if uncertain whether food from Khar Vane counted as eating or promise.

Near dawn, I woke with my hand at my mouth.

The Red had dreamed of the pit-man crawling.

It had liked the dream.

I put my knuckles between my teeth and bit until pain was mine again.

That was how I learned the eleventh law of the island.

When people make meaning from your wounds, they will keep you bleeding if you let them.
