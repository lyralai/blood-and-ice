# Chapter 3 Draft: First Blood In The Trees

Maren did not let me sleep near the fire.

That was wise. I would not have let me sleep there either.

She gave me a strip of hide and pointed to the lee side of a fish rack where smoke crawled low and the wind could not get its teeth all the way into me. Two men slept between me and the boats. Senn slept where he could see me if he woke. His throat was wrapped in moss and linen. His knife was gone from his belt.

My knife now.

Not really. Nothing is yours when everyone agrees it can be taken.

I lay with my back to the rack and watched the camp through slits in the drying fish. Children were tied to mothers by little cords when they slept. Nets were rolled under bodies. Food was hung higher than weapons. That told me what Salt Veyr feared most.

Not death.

Waste.

The red heat under my ribs had quieted after the old woman tied her knot. It did not leave. It settled like a thing under snow, warm enough to make a hollow. Every time my wound pulled, I felt it listening.

I did not sleep much.

When dawn came, the camp rose without trumpet or prayer. People simply became useful. Fire fed. Fish turned. Cord twisted. Boat skins checked for tears. A girl younger than me carried a bucket of guts as if it were treasure. No one was too young for work here. No one too old either. Even the legless man near the smoke pit had children bringing him things to mend.

Maren came when the sky was still gray.

She brought Senn.

He looked worse in daylight. The cut under his jaw was swollen purple around the moss. He had slept and woken angrier for it.

Good. Anger is easier to read than kindness.

Maren pointed at me, then at Senn, then inland.

Senn made a sound through his teeth. It hurt him, which pleased me more than it should have.

Maren struck him across the ear.

Not hard. Enough.

He lowered his eyes. Not to me. To her.

That mattered.

She took a strip of dried fish from her belt pouch and held it out. I reached. She closed her fist before I touched it.

Then she pointed at the black trees above camp.

Work first.

Food after.

I hated the shape of the lesson because I understood it.

We climbed after her.

The trees began where the wind weakened. They were low and twisted, bark black as old burns, leaves narrow and tough. The ground changed under them. Beach stone became wet soil. Wet soil became moss with hidden holes. Every step wanted an ankle. Senn moved well there. Better than me. He knew which roots held and which only pretended.

He noticed me noticing.

That pleased him.

For half the morning Maren said nothing. She let Senn show me traps: bent saplings with bone hooks, pits covered in reed mats, cord snares set for the small gray hares that ran under the roots. Salt Veyr did not waste metal on the forest. They used bone, gut, patience.

At the third snare, Senn stopped.

The cord had been cut.

Not chewed. Cut.

Maren crouched. Her hand went to the knots at her belt. Touch, touch, touch. Counting trouble.

Senn looked at me as if I had done it.

I showed him both hands. Empty.

Then I smelled smoke.

Not camp smoke. This was thinner. Bitter. Green wood and old fat.

I tapped my nose and pointed uphill.

Senn frowned. He could not smell it yet.

Maren could. Her eyes moved once, left to right, counting trees.

She made a hand sign. Senn went low. I copied him because being proud is a good way to die in places other people understand better than you.

We moved.

The smoke led us to a clearing where three bodies hung from a black branch by their ankles.

Hares. Skinned clean. Our snares had caught them. Someone else had taken the meat, left the bodies, and tied a strip of white bark around each empty neck.

Maren went still.

Senn made a small sound.

Not fear.

Recognition.

The forest answered.

The white bark around the hares' necks was the same color as the cloth the old woman had carried at the beach. The hunters who had taken what the snares caught had left their mark the way the dead left names on the antler pole. Something in the cold of my bones knew the shape of this claim. The red under my ribs knew it too, and liked it.

A spear came out of fog and struck Maren in the shoulder.

She turned with it instead of away, which saved her life. The black point cut through fur and flesh and slid past bone. She fell without crying out.

Senn lunged toward her.

Wrong.

I hit him behind the knee and drove him down as the second spear passed where his chest had been.

He cursed me. I did not need the words to know.

Three shapes stepped from the trees. White bark masks. Antlers tied above their heads, not full racks, only cut branches shaped into points. Their cloaks were gray and green. If they had stayed still, I might never have seen them.

Hollow Antler, though I did not know the name yet.

I knew hunters.

The first came for Maren. The second for Senn. The third watched me.

That was a mistake.

Watching is not holding.

I threw mud at his mask and ran at the one reaching for Senn. Not brave. Senn had a belt knife now? No. Gone. Maren had it. Senn had only hands. I needed him alive because Maren wanted him alive, and Maren had food.

Simple.

The masked one heard me and turned. His spear shaft caught my ribs.

Pain opened me.

The red heat rose so fast the world bent around it.

I saw the man's wrist before I understood the spear. Saw the place where glove ended and skin began. Saw the pulse there. Smelled fish oil on his sleeve and old blood in the seam of his mask.

The heat laughed.

I bit him.

Not like a child.

Like something that meant to take the hand home.

He screamed. Senn rolled free. Maren, on one knee, drove a little hooked knife into the thigh of the one above her. The forest broke into bodies and fog.

The third hunter came behind me.

I felt him before I saw him. The air changed. My wound knew.

I dropped.

His blade cut hair from the top of my head. I drove backward, shoulder first, into his knees. We fell together. He was heavier. Stronger. His mask pressed against my face, antler points catching in the moss. He smelled of bark smoke and sour milk.

His hand found my throat.

The heat loved that.

It climbed my spine with red fingers.

Again, it said without words.

I got one hand between us and pushed my thumb into his eye through the mask hole.

He made a wet sound and let go.

Senn hit him with a stone until the mask cracked.

Once.

Twice.

Third time, Maren barked a word.

Senn stopped with the stone raised. He was shaking. Blood ran from his throat where the old cut had opened.

The masked hunter under him was alive.

That was useful.

Maren stood swaying. The spear point had gone through the meat of her shoulder and left red down her sleeve. She looked at the hanging hares, the white bark, the wounded men, Senn, then me.

Her gaze stopped at my mouth.

I tasted blood.

Not mine.

The heat under my ribs purred and wanted me to swallow.

I spat instead.

It came out red on the moss.

Maren saw that too.

The cold in my bones had held the red back from finishing what the mask and the white bark had started. The red had wanted to take the eye and the throat and keep going until the forest learned the name that had returned to the shore. Both sides had been held by a boy who still did not know why the island kept trying to name him with blood.

No one smiled.

That was how I learned the third law of the island.

If blood speaks in front of witnesses, someone will claim the words.
