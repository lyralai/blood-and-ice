# Chapter 2 Sample: Hunger Law

Maren's people counted while they walked.

Not out loud. Their fingers moved against cords at their belts, knot to knot, touch to touch. The old woman did it without looking. So did the two behind me. Even the boy I had cut, though his hand shook and blood kept sliding between his fingers.

I watched the cords more than the spears.

Spears tell you who can kill you now. Knots tell you who plans to remember later.

They took me up from the beach through a split in the cliff. The path was narrow enough that one wrong step would send a body down to the stones. I kept one hand on the rock and one on the knife. No one tried to take it.

That worried me.

People let you keep a weapon for three reasons. They are fools. They are kind. Or they know it will not matter.

Maren did not walk like a fool. No one on that cliff smelled kind.

At the top, the island opened.

Black grass bent under the wind. Pools of rainwater sat in stone bowls. Farther inland, low trees leaned away from the sea as if the weather had spent years teaching them obedience. Smoke rose from somewhere beyond a ridge. Thin smoke. Careful smoke.

The antler pole stood at the cliff top.

I stopped beside it.

The bones tied to it were not animal bones.

Finger bones, some of them. Small ones. Child ones maybe. They knocked against the hollow antlers whenever the wind shifted. The sound was not random. The pieces were tied at different lengths so the gusts made patterns.

Maren watched me listen.

She said something in her language. The others laughed, except the wounded boy.

I did not need the words. The meaning was plain enough.

Shore-meat hears the dead.

I touched one of the bones.

The red heat under my ribs went still.

That frightened me more than its laughter.

The bone was smooth from weather. Old. Not hungry. Not kind. It held a chill that did not belong to the wind.

A memory rose.

Snow under my knees. A hand on the back of my neck. Someone telling me to say the names properly because names were how the dead stayed fed.

The memory broke before I could see the face.

I pulled my hand back.

Maren's eyes narrowed. She had seen enough to know I had felt something and not enough to know what. That is the worst amount for another person to see.

She pointed down the path.

We walked.

Their camp hid behind a ridge where the wind passed over instead of through. Hide tents crouched low around racks of fish. Nets hung from black-wood frames. Children scraped scales into bowls. An old man with no legs sat by a smoke pit, twisting cord. Everyone looked when we entered. No one rushed.

That told me more than a shout would have.

These people had rules for strangers.

Maren called out. A woman near the fish racks answered. A man with a seal-hide apron looked at my knife and then at Maren. She shook her head once. He went back to gutting fish.

The wounded boy was taken aside. He kept glaring at me while they packed his throat with moss and pressure.

I stared back until he looked away.

Small victory. Useful victory.

Maren led me to a flat stone near the smoke pit. She pointed at it.

Sit.

I did not.

She sighed, took a strip of fish from a rack, and placed it on the stone.

Then she pointed again.

Sit, and eat.

That was different.

I sat because hunger sat me. I kept the knife on my thigh under my hand. The fish was dry, oily, and hard with salt. I chewed slowly this time. Throwing food back to the ground once was lesson enough.

The camp watched me eat.

Not with pity. Pity has a smell. It is sweet and rotten.

They watched like merchants watch a scale.

Maren sat across from me. She had taken off her fur hood. Her gray braid was tied with copper rings, each stamped with a mark. Not decoration. Accounting.

She placed three things between us.

A fish bone.

A strip of red cloth.

The sharpened bone toggle I had used on the boy's throat.

She tapped the fish bone and pointed at the racks.

Food.

She tapped the cloth and pointed at my wound.

Blood.

She tapped the toggle and pointed at the wounded boy.

Debt.

Then she slid all three toward me and waited.

I hated her a little for making the world simple. Simple things are traps you can see, which does not make them less traps.

I picked up the fish bone.

Food first.

Maren's mouth twitched.

I picked up the red cloth and pressed it to my side. Pain flashed white. The heat under my ribs stirred, annoyed at the pressure, or pleased. I could not tell.

Then I picked up the toggle.

The wounded boy made a sound across the camp.

I looked at him. He had stopped glaring. His hand was at his throat. He knew the object. He knew the debt. He knew everyone was watching what I did with it.

I could keep it. That would mean one thing.

I could throw it away. That would mean another.

I could give it back.

I did not know their law, but I knew people. Give a hurt boy back the thing that hurt him and he may think you are mocking him. Keep it and he may think you promise to finish the work.

So I broke it.

The toggle snapped badly because my left hand still hurt. I had to brace one end against the stone and lean. When it cracked, the whole camp heard.

I placed the two pieces between us.

Maren stared.

The old man with no legs began to laugh.

Not loudly. Not kindly. Like a door opening somewhere under the floor.

Maren said a word. The camp repeated it in smaller voices.

"Neth."

I did not know if it meant fool, guest, debtor, or boy-who-breaks-the-wrong-thing.

The heat under my ribs whispered without words.

Break more.

I put my palm flat on the stone.

No.

It pressed back, amused.

Across the camp, the wounded boy stood. The moss at his throat was red. An older woman tried to stop him, but he shook her off and came toward me.

His hand was empty.

That did not make him harmless.

He stopped outside knife range. Smart enough. His eyes were wet from pain and shame. He spoke to Maren, then to the camp, then to me.

I understood none of it until he touched his throat, pointed at the broken toggle, and spat at my feet.

Not on me.

At my feet.

A boundary.

Then he drew his own belt knife, turned it, and offered it hilt-first to Maren.

The camp changed. Shoulders lowered. Someone muttered. The old man stopped laughing.

Maren did not take the knife.

She looked at me.

Ah.

There it was. The trap under the lesson.

If I took his knife, I accepted something. If I refused, I accepted something else. If I cut him, the camp would learn one story. If I spared him, another.

The heat liked the knife. It liked the boy's open throat more.

I stood.

The camp went quiet enough to hear fat drop into the smoke fire.

I stepped close to the wounded boy. He was taller than me by a hand. Older by maybe two years. Afraid, but standing. That mattered.

I took the knife from his hand.

His jaw tightened.

I turned and gave it to Maren.

She did not smile this time.

Good. I was tired of her knowing more than me.

I touched my chest.

"Rook."

Then I touched the broken toggle, the fish bone, the red cloth, and finally the boy's chest.

I did not have words for what I meant.

Maren gave them to the camp in her language.

The wounded boy swallowed. The motion hurt him. I saw that and respected him for not showing more.

He tapped his chest.

"Senn."

Not Little Senn yet. Not to me. Just Senn, with blood at his throat and hate in his eyes and his knife no longer his.

Maren tied a new knot in one of her cords.

There are moments when a life turns and no horn sounds. No god speaks. No blade falls.

Only an old woman tying a knot.

That was how I learned the second law of the island.

Hunger starts every bargain.

Debt decides whether it ends.
