# Chapter 10 Draft: The Boy Returns Wrong

No one touched me after I came out.

That was the first gift the pit gave me.

Space.

I lay on the white stone with blood cooling under me and sky burning above. My rib moved wrong. My shoulder was torn. My mouth tasted of a thing I did not want to name. The Red sat inside my chest, heavy and pleased, like a guest who had eaten well and now waited to see whether the house would thank him.

I tried to move.

My body refused.

Good, I thought.

Pain came back slowly. First as weather. Then as knives. Then as mine.

Mine.

I held that word with both hands.

Yara Khar began to sing.

Not the song from before. This one had a hard beat, and every Khar Vane around the pit answered it by striking weapon hafts against stone. Once. Twice. Again. Bone rings clicked in their hair. Their faces had changed.

Before the pit, they had looked at me like a question.

Now they looked at me like an answer.

That was worse.

Vessa stepped forward. Her face was white with anger under ritual calm. She lifted one hand and spoke over the Khar Vane beat.

Yara sang louder.

Harvek did not.

He watched both women and smiled his scar-mouth smile. He understood something I did not: the first person to name a thing owns part of it forever.

Yara pointed at me.

She sang the death-name she had given me.

Then she broke it.

I heard the break. Everyone did. The melody bent around the name and snapped it in half. Khar Vane answered with a shout that shook dust from the pit stones.

Not dead, their voices said.

Returned.

Vessa's mouth tightened. She tried another phrase. Hollow Antler acolytes answered, but weakly. They had the old law. Yara had fresh blood. Fresh blood is louder.

Asha Fen came to my side.

Cairn followed with his knife out.

Harvek's men shifted.

Asha ignored them and crouched near me. Not too near. Respectful of the space, or afraid of it.

Her eyes moved over my wounds.

I saw the question in her face.

How are you alive?

I wanted to ask her the same.

Cairn looked at my mouth and went pale.

I wiped it with the back of my hand. Red and darker than red came away.

Memory flashed: the pit-man crawling, my hand on his ankle, the heat pulling.

I turned and vomited onto the white stone.

The Red disliked that. It curled away from shame as if shame were cold water.

Good.

I had found something it did not want.

Maren pushed through the Salt Veyr line then. Someone tried to stop her. She hit him in the throat with two fingers and kept walking. Her shoulder was bandaged. Her face gave nothing away until she saw me shaking.

Not monster. Not omen. Not law.

Inventory.

She counted wounds. Breath. Blood loss. Distance to boats. Khar Vane numbers. Hollow Antler anger. Fen-Kin spears. Food cost.

I almost smiled.

Maren would weigh a god if it ate from her stores.

She knelt and touched the cord still on my wrist.

Salt Veyr debt under bark, reed, and bone.

Still there.

The touch steadied me more than kindness would have.

Harvek saw.

So did Vessa.

So did Yara, and that mattered most because Yara was turning the moment into memory faster than anyone could stop her.

Harvek came forward.

Cairn raised his knife.

Harvek stopped outside its reach. He looked amused, but not careless.

He spoke to me.

I did not know the words.

Yara translated, maybe for me, maybe for the crowd.

She touched the pit. Touched her own mouth. Touched her chest. Then pointed at me.

The pit has spoken.

No. Not exactly.

The pit had swallowed and choked.

Harvek took one knee.

The world shifted.

Not because I cared about his knee. Because every Khar Vane behind him saw it and had to decide what their own bodies believed.

One by one, they lowered.

Not all the way. Not submission like beaten dogs. A warrior's crouch. A promise with teeth.

Yara did not kneel.

She sang.

Distantly, through pain and fever and the heavy satisfaction in my chest, I understood the trap.

They were not giving me power.

They were putting power around me like a collar and calling it honor.

The Red liked the kneeling. It liked Harvek's old scarred mouth. It liked the weapons on stone. It liked being mistaken for a god because gods are allowed to be hungry.

I pushed myself onto one elbow.

My rib screamed. My vision went black at the edges.

The Khar Vane beat stopped.

Everyone waited.

I had no speech for them. No law. No song. No clever trader's knot. No old-blood genealogy. No antler name.

Only the word I had held in the pit when the heat tried to become all of me.

I touched my chest.

"Rook."

My voice broke. It was ugly and small and mine.

Yara's song did not know what to do with that.

For one breath, neither did the island.

Then Harvek smiled wider.

He liked it. That worried me.

Vessa turned away first. A person retreating to plan does not look like a person retreating. But I knew the difference between done and not done. Vessa was not done.

Asha put one hand on Cairn's shoulder and whispered something that made him lower the knife.

Maren tightened the cord at my wrist.

Not ownership.

Reminder.

I closed my fingers around it.

The Red settled, patient and full.

That was how I learned the tenth law of the island.

If people kneel to the thing that saved you, you must decide whether to stand as yourself or be worshiped as your wound.
