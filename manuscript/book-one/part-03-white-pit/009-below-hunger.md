# Chapter 9 Draft: Below Hunger

I do not remember the first scream.

That is how I know it was mine.

The pit took sound and chewed it before giving it back. Every breath came back larger. Every scrape became claws. I lay on bones and white dust with my side split around the broken rib and the rope loose across my chest.

Above, the sky was a coin.

Too far to spend.

Something moved in the dark beyond my feet.

I held the broken toggle so hard it cut my palm. Pain helps count the body. Palm. Rib. Side. Cheek. Tongue. Knee. If you can count the body, you may still own it.

The thing breathed again.

Not animal.

Not only.

It crawled into the little light: a man, or what hunger had left after eating the word man thin. His hair hung in ropes. His lips were gone on one side. One arm ended at the wrist. Around his neck was a Khar Vane bone ring and an old iron collar grown into skin.

He had been put here before me.

Not killed.

Kept.

His eyes found the blood on my hand.

He smiled with half a mouth.

The Wound-Guest stirred.

I tried to stand. My rib said no. My knee slipped on bone. The pit-man came fast.

He hit like falling rock.

We went down together. His teeth snapped beside my face. His good hand found my wound and pushed fingers into it.

The world went white.

Then red.

The heat did not rise this time.

It arrived.

All at once.

Every wound became a mouth and every mouth opened.

I heard laughter under the pit. Not in my ears. In the blood that had not yet spilled. The pit-man's smell unfolded: old meat, old fear, Khar Vane smoke, sickness, my blood on his fingers, his blood waiting under skin.

I knew where his throat would tear.

I knew how much pressure a thumb needed to blind.

I knew the broken rib did not matter if breath could be stolen from somewhere deeper.

No, I thought.

The thought was small and far away.

The Wound-Guest loved the pit-man because the pit-man was simple. Hunger wearing a body. A door already open.

Mine, I thought again.

Ours, answered the heat without words.

Then I was moving.

Not well. Not beautifully. Nothing in the pit was beautiful. I drove the broken toggle into the meat under his jaw where I had cut Senn, but deeper, upward, hard enough that the bone snapped again and stayed in him. His blood came hot over my wrist.

He bit my shoulder.

The Wound-Guest laughed through my teeth.

I bit back.

There are things a person does to live that cannot be made clean by needing life.

I know that now.

I did not know it then.

Then there was only dark, bone, pressure, the pit-man's hand striking my ear, his knee in my belly, the sweet wrong strength rising as my body spent tomorrow to survive tonight.

He tried to crawl away.

That is the part I remember too clearly.

Not the killing blow. Not the blood. The crawl.

His fingers dragged pale lines through dust. He made a sound that might have been a word. The Wound-Guest heard plea and translated it as weakness. My hand caught his ankle.

Stop, I thought.

The heat pulled.

Something tore.

After that memory breaks into red pieces.

Bone under my knees.

The taste of iron.

The rope in my hands.

My rib clicking every time I breathed and breath coming anyway.

A voice above, not words, only fear.

Yara singing faster.

The pit wall under my fingers.

The Wound-Guest did not climb like a boy. It climbed like the pit owed it height.

Halfway up, my left hand slipped. A nail tore off. I watched it fall into the dark and felt nothing.

That frightened the part of me still watching.

Feel it, I commanded.

The heat ignored me.

At the lip, hands reached down.

Khar Vane hands.

Hollow Antler hands pulled back.

Fen-Kin spears came up.

Salt Veyr swore.

Harvek's scarred mouth opened in a grin that showed too many teeth.

I did not want his hands on me.

The Wound-Guest did.

It liked witnesses.

It liked the way fear ripened when brought close.

My fingers found the pit lip. White stone broke under my nails. Someone shouted. The rope tightened around my chest. I came up over the edge on my belly, dragging blood, dust, and pieces of the dark with me.

The sky was too large.

I tried to say my name.

What came out was laughter.

That was how I learned the ninth law of the island.

Survival is not innocence.
