# Chapter 29 Draft: The Open Store

The store stood open because I had ordered the bar lifted before the sun cleared the trees.

No one inside but the counters. No one armed but the watchers. Salt Veyr, Fen-Kin, Hollow Antler mothers, and Khar Vane who had followed the crown far enough to stand in the cold and see what their tenth had bought.

Maren had three boards this time. Senn held the charcoal. A second Salt boy I did not know stood ready with a spare stick and a face that said he would rather be on a boat. Asha came with Cairn, the wrapped spear across her back like a warning that had learned to wait. Otta stood for Khar Vane because Harvek had sent her instead of himself. "She sees what is not there faster than most men," he had told me when I asked why the one-armed woman. "And she has already lost what the cradles fear."

Mother Harl brought one other Hollow Antler woman whose name I did not catch. The woman carried a white cloth folded small, not a cradle frame. That was the only mercy.

A crowd pressed at the edges. Keff was there, red hair tied back, standing too close to Dremm. Runa watched from behind them with her arms folded like she already knew the numbers would lie. Two Salt boat-women with empty baskets stood near the road. A Fen-Kin mother with a gray braid and hands that had buried children waited beside Asha. More faces than I wanted. More eyes than the store had food to feed.

"Count what is," I said. "Name what is not. Aloud."

Maren gave me one hard look. She had promised "some" before Mother Harl came. Some was not a number anyone could eat.

She nodded once and went in.

The smell hit first. Old fish oil, smoke that had soaked into the racks for three winters, the sharp bite of salt that no longer covered the emptiness underneath. My stomach turned before my mind caught it. The boy on the black shore would have crawled for that smell. The Wound-Guest liked it for different reasons.

Not food, it pressed, low and interested. Count.

It had learned that word from the last time the boards moved.

Senn started. "Twenty-one smoked seal. The tenth took four. Road fines brought seven. Three went to the wounded carriers from the lower pass and the guards who stood the watch."

"Guards eat from crown store after the tenth," I said, loud enough for the ring of watchers. "That was the order."

Maren did not look at me. "Eleven cod racks. Two taken for the Fen training camp when the wrapped spear work began. One more for the road crew that repaired the cart after the fines."

Asha said nothing. The gray-braid Fen mother shifted her weight but kept her mouth shut.

"Four oil skins left. One used for the axle repair and the wounded. The other three were already spoken for the cove boats before the fines came in from Dorrik's watch."

Maren's voice stayed flat. She was not hiding the numbers. She was choosing which ones to let the wind carry.

The Wound-Guest rose under my ribs like heat from a stone that had been in the sun too long.

The inlet, it offered. Boats have oil. Boats have names that eat.

I pressed my tongue against the back of my teeth until the taste of blood cut the wanting. The red heat slowed but did not leave. It counted with them now.

Mother Harl stepped to the first empty rack. She did not touch the boards. She looked at the gaps the way she had looked at cradles the day before.

"The tenth brought fish," she said. Her voice carried without shouting. "Fish fills a belly for a day or two if the child is not already thin. The littlest need oil that does not choke the lungs when the lamps are lit against the dark. They need the winter root boiled soft so the cramps do not take them before the snow stops. They need the moss that was promised when the first cradles crossed from the black shore three winters back. None of it is on these racks."

She named the missing like she was reciting names that had already gone under the ice. Not accusation. Inventory.

The gray-braid Fen mother spoke next. "The fen camps gave smoked meat for the spear work. We were told the tenth would answer the cradles first."

One of the Salt boat-women with the empty basket said, "We gave from our own for the road wounded. Our own children still need."

Maren turned on her. "And we will give again when the next cart comes from the inlet. But the inlet store keeps the boats on the water. If we empty it for every cradle claim, the boats do not come and the next tenth is nothing but words."

That was the truth she had not wanted spoken where Hollow Antler and young Khar could carry it back to their camps.

I watched her mouth go tight the way it did when a knot would not hold. Her eyes moved to the two Salt boat-women with the empty baskets, then back to the boards. She had named the inlet in front of everyone who would carry it to the cove. The boat-women would remember who had put their winter on the public tally. Maren had made the choice I forced: her people's boats against the naming I had demanded. The nod she gave later would cost her more than the oil skins. I felt the weight of it settle between us, heavier than any debt cord.

The air changed. Not anger yet. The kind of quiet that comes before people decide whether to believe the count or the hunger.

Keff spoke from the Khar side. "We gave the tenth. We guard the roads so the carts can move. And still the cradles cry empty? Then the ones who count are keeping two tallies."

The red-haired one beside him laughed once, the same sound he had made when Dremm's ear came off. They called him Half-Ear now, though the name had started as a joke and turned into a title the young ones repeated. "If the inlet stays fat while cradles go thin, the road gift can be made honest. We guard the pass. We take what the counters hide."

Dremm smiled with his mouth closed. His missing ear was still bound but the young ones around him had started copying the shaved side of his head. They looked at the empty racks like the boards had stolen from them. Half-Ear nodded at Keff like the plan had already been spoken between them.

Otta made a small sound. Not quite a laugh. "One tally. It is short because winter is longer than any one store and the blood-crown has more mouths asking than the old places were built to answer."

She meant the new road guards. She meant the ones who had come to kneel after the pit and the crown. She meant the young Khar who followed Dremm because restraint looked like slow starvation.

The Wound-Guest pressed harder.

Names have bellies, it said. The counters lie with numbers. Open the inlet with teeth and the cradles will not need to ask again. The littlest will feed the story if we take what the boats hide. Their small mouths will make the crown strong.

I tasted iron. Sweat started at my hairline and ran cold down my neck even in the chill. The red pressed at the edges of my sight like a second set of eyes. I counted the witnesses in my head the way Cairn had taught with the wrapped spear: Maren. Asha. Mother Harl. Otta. One for each tribe that had watched the pit. Rook. I said the name inside my teeth until the heat pulled back a step. It had learned the word "store" from the last counting. Now it was learning the names that could open one without blood.

I stepped to the open door so every eye had to find me instead of the gaps.

"The next fines go to oil and root before any war share or guard portion," I said. "Maren names the boat that will bring them from the inlet. Asha sends two Fen to walk with the cart. One Hollow mother rides so no one can say the count lied when it arrives."

Maren's head came up. She had not agreed to that much of Salt's private winter in the open. Her eyes met mine across the boards. The look said I had made her choose between her own people's boats and the public naming I had forced. She nodded once, sharp. The nod cost her something with the Salt women. I saw the boat-women watching her back. They would remember who had put their winter on the boards for every mother to carry home. The cost was not just oil. It was the next time a cart needed guarding and the cove asked why Salt blood bought a crown that named their hunger out loud.

The gray-braid Fen mother said, "The fen will walk."

Mother Harl studied me. "You wanted the lacks named where all could hear. They are named. Now the blood-crown must answer its own truth with more than another promise."

The Hollow woman with the white cloth finally spoke. "Vessa would have named this pollution before it was counted."

Mother Harl did not look at her. "Vessa is not here. The lacks are."

That was the first time I saw the crack in Hollow Antler show its edges in daylight without Vessa's words around it. Naim was not in the ring, but the doubt had legs now.

The store stayed open after the counting ended. No one barred the door again. The empty racks and the low barrels were for anyone who wanted to walk past and see what the tenth and the fines had actually bought.

A Salt boat-woman stayed behind. When most of the ring had gone, she came close with a small wrapped bundle. Dried root, the good kind that did not mold fast. She set it on the lowest rack without looking at me. Her hands were cracked from the oars and the salt. She kept her eyes on the bundle as if meeting mine would make the gift too large.

"For the littlest," she said. "My own can wait one more day. The inlet will not starve them if the next cart is honest." She paused. "If the crown can keep the pass clear without taking what we hid for our own." She walked away before I could answer or name the knot it tied on me.

That was trust, thin as the cloth she had used. It pressed heavier than fear. Fear broke when the red came. This could break when the numbers did not add and the boat-women remembered who had made their winter public. I felt the weight of her small act settle in my chest like a second debt cord, one that could snap faster than any I had tied with blood.

Keff and two of Dremm's knot did not leave as fast. They stood near the road where the cart tracks still showed. Half-Ear was with them, the red-haired one who had laughed when the ear was taken. He spoke low but the wind carried it to where I still stood by the open door.

"The inlet," Half-Ear said. "If the counters will not empty it for the cradles, we can make the road gift honest. Three of us on the lower pass. Take what the boats hide before the next cart is counted. The mothers will thank us when the lamps are lit and the root is in the pot."

Keff nodded. "Dremm will not stop it. The crown talks of fines. Fines do not fill bellies."

Dremm did not stop them. He only watched me from across the mud to see if the crown would answer with law or with the red thing that had climbed out of the pit.

The Wound-Guest liked the plan too. It offered the shape of Half-Ear's throat under a hand that did not belong to a boy anymore, the inlet store under Khar hands, Maren watching her boats bend.

I walked to them instead of letting the heat answer.

"The next cart comes under four-tribe eyes," I said. "If any of you touch it before it reaches the cradles, you answer to the same law that took Dorrik's oil. Unarmed. In front of the mothers."

Half-Ear met my eyes. He did not spit. He smiled the way Dremm had smiled when the ear came off.

"Bring him to the law racks at dusk," I told Otta, loud enough for the three of them to hear. "Half-Ear. No weapons. He will answer for the road gift he plans to take from the inlet before it is counted."

That night the Wound-Guest did not sleep. It walked the empty racks with me. Every missing skin became a reason. Every named lack became a door the thing could learn to open with the right words in the right ears.

I held my own name until the counting in my head stopped.

Rook.

Yara stood at the edge of the firelight and watched the mothers walk back with white cloths and light baskets. The Wound-Guest counted their steps. It had seen the root bundle and the red-haired one planning the inlet. It was learning which doors the empty racks opened.
