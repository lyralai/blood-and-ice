# Chapter 28 Draft: Vessa's Clean Knife

Vessa's next move arrived clean.

No masks. No cradles. No blood. No accusation shouted across mud.

Just three Hollow Antler workers with baskets of dried moss, poultice bark, and wintergreen oil for the road boys' wounds.

They came under a white strip of cloth tied to a plain branch. Peace sign. Helper sign. Knife sign, if you knew how to look at hands.

Maren knew.

"Do not let them near the tally boards," she said.

"They brought medicine."

"Medicine can count."

I had to think about that longer than I liked.

Mother Harl came behind them, slower, without a cradle frame. That made everyone uneasy. A woman who has put down a symbol has either come as herself or as something worse.

Naim walked with her.

He was Hollow Antler, younger than Vessa, with a narrow face and eyes that kept leaving the ground then returning to it as if the sky accused him. I remembered him from the second pit. He had held one of the ritual ropes and looked sick when I did not die properly.

He carried no staff.

Mother Harl bowed to no one. "Hollow Antler sends wound care for those harmed under unsettled road law."

Maren snorted. "Pretty."

Harvek said, "Refuse it."

The wounded Salt boy heard him and went pale. Pride is easier before pain starts pulsing.

Vessa had cut clean indeed.

If I refused medicine, road law looked cruel. If I accepted, Hollow Antler touched the consequences of my failure and named them unsettled. If I let them tend Salt Veyr under Khar Vane guard, the image would travel faster than truth: Antler healing what blood-crown broke.

The Wound-Guest did not care. Wound care bored it unless wounds opened wider.

That helped. Sometimes the thing's disinterest showed where the real danger was.

"They can tend wounds in the open," I said. "Salt Veyr names what is used. Fen witnesses. Khar Vane keeps hands wrapped."

Mother Harl's eyes moved over me.

"You make every kindness a court."

"You made every cradle a blade."

For the first time, the old woman almost smiled.

Naim flinched at the exchange. Not fear. Recognition.

The Hollow Antler workers laid their baskets on clean hide. Maren had Senn mark every bundle. Asha arrived midway through with Cairn and the wrapped spear, because news now grew legs faster than people.

The road boy sat on a stone while Naim cleaned black grit from his cheek.

He was gentle.

That bothered me.

It is easier when enemies enjoy pain. Then your hate has a clean place to sit. Naim worked like someone who had cleaned many wounds and disliked every one.

"Name?" the road boy asked him.

"Naim."

"You tied pit ropes."

Naim's hand paused.

"Yes."

"Wanted him dead?"

The question struck me harder than it struck Naim.

Naim looked at me once. "I wanted the rite true."

Maren murmured, "That is not an answer."

"It is ours," Naim said.

Mother Harl watched him with a tightness around her mouth.

There. A crack.

Not between Hollow Antler and us. Inside Hollow Antler.

Vessa had sent care, but care required hands, and hands belonged to people who could start thinking while they touched consequences.

Asha saw it too. She always saw old lines, even when they were not blood.

"Does Vessa call the road law false?" Asha asked.

Mother Harl answered. "Vessa says law born under pollution must be cleansed before it can bind the innocent."

Harvek barked a laugh. "Cleansed means owned by Antler."

"Cleansed means named correctly."

"By Antler."

Mother Harl did not deny it.

Naim tied the road boy's cheek with clean bark-thread.

"If a law feeds cradle stores," he said quietly, "must it be false because the boy made it?"

The air changed.

One of the Hollow Antler workers looked down fast. Mother Harl closed her eyes for half a breath.

So Naim had doubts, and Vessa had either missed them or sent him because doubt made the gift look honest. Maybe both. Smart people use cracks. Sometimes cracks widen.

I said nothing.

That was hard. Harder than blood.

Naim finished the binding and sat back.

The Salt boy touched his cheek. "Hurts less."

"It will hurt more tonight," Naim said. "Clean wounds complain before they close."

He could have been talking to all of us.

When the tending ended, Mother Harl asked to see the cradle store.

Maren refused before I could answer.

"Why?" Mother Harl asked.

"Because you will count it with Antler eyes and tell people what it lacks."

"It may lack much."

"Everything lacks much. That is why counting matters."

The two women stared at each other, old grief and trade math, neither softer than the other.

I thought of winning. Of saying yes and looking generous. Of saying no and looking strong. Both answers had traps with my footprints already inside them.

"Tomorrow," I said.

Maren's head snapped toward me.

I kept going before she could cut me with sense.

"Tomorrow Mother Harl sees the cradle store with Salt count, Fen witness, and one mother from each tribe. No Vessa words carried in. No crown songs carried out. What is there is named. What is missing is named. Nothing else."

Mother Harl studied me.

"You think naming less will make truth cleaner?"

"No. I think too many people use truth as a coat for wanting."

Cairn made a small sound. It might have been approval. It might have been indigestion.

Mother Harl said, "I will come."

After she left, Maren rounded on me. "You gave her a stage."

"I gave her witnesses."

"Same wood. Different carving."

"Can we fill the store before tomorrow?"

That stopped her.

Then she smiled, sharp and unwilling. "Some."

Harvek groaned. "More counting."

"More road work," Maren said. "More fines. More hauling. More proof."

The Wound-Guest sank low, bored by baskets and names and women's eyes measuring stores.

Good.

Let it sleep through the work that might actually rule the island.

That night, Yara made no song of the road punishment. She made no song of the medicine. She watched Naim cross back to Hollow Antler ridge and hummed one note under her breath.

Not enough to become memory.

Enough to warn me she had heard a future trying to start.
