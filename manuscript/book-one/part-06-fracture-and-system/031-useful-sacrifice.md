# Chapter 31 Draft: Useful Sacrifice

The boards still carried the marks of the tenth.

Maren had left them out on the flat stone where anyone could read the charcoal if they could read. Most could not. They watched the numbers in the way they watched clouds: something that decided whether they ate or walked hungry. The cradle share was gone. What remained for the winter road guards was thin. Three more spears were needed at the lower pass before the snows locked it. Three more mouths that would not fight well on short rations.

I stood with the law racks at my back because that was where the counting had moved after the store was named open. Harvek sharpened a hook against the rack wood. Solek stood with his arms folded so thin they looked like they might snap in a wind. Maren counted under her breath and did not look at me.

The Wound-Guest was quiet. It did not care about boards or shares until the numbers touched meat.

Dremm came from the direction of the young fires. Keff walked on his left, red hair tied back with a strip of hide. Runa was on his right, her face still carrying the road dust she had not washed off since the axle work. Two others came with them whose names I did not use because they only opened their mouths when Dremm opened his first. Dorrik stayed at the edge of the knot, the one who had fixed the cart under Salt eyes. He watched the ground.

"Crown," Dremm said.

The word sat wrong in his mouth. It had since the mutilation. He used it like a bone he wanted to crack.

Maren stopped counting. Harvek's sharpening slowed but did not stop.

"The lower pass needs three," Dremm said. "We have the bodies. We do not have the food to keep their hands steady when the wind cuts. The tenth took what should have stayed with the teeth that guard it. The road thieves rebuilt a cart and walked away with their shares still owed."

He looked at the boards.

"Restraint took from us. Restraint let the ones who stole keep breathing while the ones who bled for the crown go lean. Restraint is waste when winter does not care about names."

Keff's hand flexed. He did not spit this time. The lesson from the store had stuck enough for that.

Dremm kept his eyes on me. "We have the three who broke the road. Dorrik. Keff. Runa. They took oil that was not theirs and put stone in a Salt face. The law still lives under the crown. Make one of them the useful sacrifice. Or let the knot choose. The old rite on an oath-breaker. His meat keeps the three we need fed through the first snows. His blood tells every hold that watches the new roads that the pit boy still knows how to take what is owed. The other two will stand the pass harder when they know the price of failing twice."

The camp did not go quiet. It went still in the way a dog goes still before it decides whether to bite or run.

The Wound-Guest woke.

Not with the old blind hunger. With something slower. It pressed against the inside of my ribs like a hand testing cloth for weak seams. Images came without words first: Keff's throat under a clean cut, the body carried to the fire pit where the young knot waited, the smell of it filling the air so that even the ones who had not eaten since the tenth would remember who fed them. Then the story moving: to Hollow Antler mothers who would fill the next cradle share themselves rather than risk one of their own becoming the next useful thing. To the small holds beyond the ridge who had begun sending watchers instead of carts. Fear that brought tribute before the snows closed the passes.

It almost spoke.

*This is not waste. This feeds the teeth that hold the roads. This teaches the island what a crown costs when it is not fed.*

My tongue found the place where I had bitten it during the wrapped-spear lesson. The iron taste was still there if I pressed.

Maren spoke first. "The three already pay with work and double share. That is the crown's count."

"Work does not fill bellies that stand watch in the wind," Dremm said. "Meat does."

Harvek looked at me over the hook. His face gave nothing at first, then the corner of his mouth pulled once, tight, like a man who had just watched a student use his own lesson against him. Relief that the old price had not been taken in front of witnesses. Disappointment that the teeth he had raised were being fed something thinner than meat. He had taught me once that a chief who could not disappoint his people was already eaten. Now he was seeing it applied to his own knot, and the lesson landed heavier than he had expected.

Solek said, "Khar Vane law on road breakers allows the old price if the crown names the need. The pit still holds the right."

Asha had come without being called. Cairn stood with the wrapped spear across his back, the gray cloth still hiding the wood. She watched Dremm the way she watched weather.

"The three are already named," I said.

Dremm's good eye narrowed. The bound side of his face did not move the same.

"Then name one," he said. "Or stand aside and let the knot do what the crown will not."

The heat under my ribs pushed harder. The Wound-Guest pressed like a second set of lungs trying to speak through mine. It offered the choice clean but showed more now: the debt bowl lifted from this stone and carried to the storehouse door, the blood poured across the threshold so every future tally would carry the mark of what had been paid in flesh and fear. The three standing the pass thin but alive, bringing word back that oath-breakers were not eaten but bound, and small holds sending tribute because the living example traveled farther than meat. Hollow Antler mothers doubling the tenth themselves rather than risk one of their own becoming the next useful thing. It was learning the crown's own tools.

It almost spoke. The iron taste flooded back across my tongue. Sweat started at my hairline. Red crept at the edges of my sight.

*This is not waste. Name one. Let the bowl travel. Let the three stand thin and useful. The island will learn what the pit boy keeps without feeding it.*

I bit down on the old wound from the wrapped-spear lesson and counted the three aloud in my head. Dorrik. Keff. Runa. The wrapped spear was across Cairn's back at the edge of the light. I did not reach for it. I said my name once, under my breath, where only the Guest could hear it.

Strategic. Not the old blind kill. A cut that paid for roads and fear at once. And the Guest was filing the bowl idea away for later use, I could feel it turning the images like a man testing a new knife.

I could feel it wanting me to say the name.

I walked to the three instead.

Dorrik did not lift his head. Keff met my eyes and did not look away. Runa's hands were fists at her sides.

"You took from the cart," I said. "You put stone in a face that carried Salt flags. You made the road into a question about whether the crown's law had teeth."

Keff's mouth twisted. "The store took first. We only took what was already owed to the teeth that guard."

Runa's fists tightened until the knuckles showed white. She did not speak, but the look she gave me said she would rather the old price than this living chain.

None of them spoke again.

"The pass needs three who can hold it when the snow comes. Dead men do not hold passes. They only make songs."

I turned so the whole knot could hear, and the ones who had drifted closer from the young fires.

"The three will swear to the road. Each will give blood from the arm into the debt bowl. Not to feed the fire. To mark what is owed. Their winter shares go first to the road store and the guards who stand it. They become the first road-sworn under the crown. They stand the lower pass and any other the crown names. They work the repairs unarmed until the debt is walked off in watches and labor. This is the useful sacrifice. Three living spears that pay instead of one feast that ends."

Maren's eyes sharpened on the boards — she was already counting what the reassigned shares would cost Salt logistics and what the mark would mean for every future tally. Asha watched the three with the old-blood stillness that said a name spoken in blood was heavier than one spoken in air; her gaze flicked once to the wrapped spear on Cairn's back. Yara, who had not sung, made a single low note under her breath that did not become a verse. It was enough to carry the shape of what had just been made public.

The heat did not leave. It changed. The Wound-Guest turned the images. Now it showed the three standing the pass through snow, thin but alive, bringing back word of carts that came because the small holds had seen what happened to oath-breakers who were not eaten. It showed the blood in the bowl being carried to the storehouse door and poured across the threshold so that every tally after would carry the mark. It showed Dremm's knot watching their own made useful without being meat, and some of them beginning to wonder whether the crown's restraint was another kind of strength.

It was learning.

That was the cost that did not show on the boards.

Dremm's face had gone flat. "You call three cuts and work a sacrifice."

"I call it what feeds the pass through winter."

Keff made a sound in his throat. Runa looked at Dremm like she wanted him to give the order the crown would not.

Harvek set the hook down on the rack. "The law allows the old price. It does not require it when the crown names another."

Solek nodded once, thin and careful. "Blood debt can be paid in living labor if the witness holds."

Maren stepped to the boards. "Three shares reassigned to road store. Blood mark to be noted. If they break the oath, the debt becomes the old price with no crown to stand between."

Asha spoke last. "Old blood sees the difference between a name spoken and a throat opened. The wrapped spear still waits if the boy forgets which he chose."

She was not speaking only to me.

I had Otta brought because she knew how to take blood without making a show of it. She came with her one arm and her tongue already sharp. "More counting," she said. "Less dying. The island may survive one of those."

The three were lined at the stone. Otta took a small bone blade from her belt. She cut Keff first, a line across the inside of his forearm, shallow enough that it would close before the next watch. The blood ran into the same ash bowl that had taken my portion at the feast dispute. Dorrik next. Then Runa, who did not flinch. The bowl held more than it had before. Not enough to feed anyone. Enough to mean.

I made them speak the words.

"By the crown and the pit and the road that was broken, we pay with what we took and stand where the crown points. Our shares go first to the guard. Our hands work until the debt is walked off."

Maren marked the board. "Blood debt. Road-sworn. Shares reassigned."

The young knot watched. Some looked at the bowl like it was a new kind of answer. A low sound moved through them — not quite relief, not quite anger. One of the younger ones (the one who had stood behind Keff at the store) looked at the three with something close to gratitude that they were still breathing. Another would not meet Dorrik's eye. The knot was not one thing.

Dremm turned without a word and walked back toward the fires. Keff and Runa and the two others went with him. Dorrik stayed a moment longer, then followed when I nodded toward the pass.

The Wound-Guest sank, but not all the way. It left a pressure behind the ribs like a lesson being remembered. Not the old laughter. Something that had teeth and could wait for snow.

That night Yara did not sing of the blood bowl or the three who had been made useful without being meat. She sat by the law racks and hummed one low note that did not become a verse. It was enough to carry.

Maren came to me after the counting was put away.

"You turned their hunger into three more guards and a tally that will travel," she said. "That is not nothing."

"It is not meat."

"Meat would have fed three nights. The pass needs the whole winter."

She left me with the boards.

Asha did not leave.

"The thing inside you wanted the kill," she said. "I saw it in the way you held your mouth."

"It offered a road that did not need three living men."

"And you chose the harder one."

"Dead men do not bring small tribes to the table when the snow thaws."

She studied me the way she studied the wrapped spear before she handed it over.

"Then the boy still owns the name. For now."

She walked toward the Fen side of the camp. Cairn stayed a moment with the spear across his back, then followed.

I stayed by the racks until the fires burned low. The debt bowl sat on the stone where anyone could see the mark. The three who had given it were already on the lower pass with tools and no weapons, under Salt eyes and Fen witness.

The storehouse door was barred. The boards were covered. The hunger was still there in the camp, in the young fires, in the knot that had walked away with Dremm.

The heat stayed after they left. Not the old blind push. A slower thing, like it was turning the debt bowl in its mind the way a man turns a new knife. It had liked the bowl traveling to the storehouse door. I kept my hand on the wrapped spear until the red at the edges thinned. My tongue still tasted the place I had bitten during the caller lesson. The three were already walking toward the pass. Dorrik carried the bowl. I stayed by the racks until the fire burned low enough that I could not see the mark in it anymore. The young knot watched from the edge of the light. Some looked at the three like they had been given something. Others looked at the bowl like it was a new kind of answer. The red did not leave. It settled lower, patient, the way it had after the wrapped spear work. It was learning the shape of the living debt.
