# Chapter 32 Draft: The Small Tribe Bargain

The runner came up the new road spur three days after the cradle store was named and counted in the open.

He was small for the distance he had walked, reed-thin, with black mud dried to his knees and a strip of white cloth tied around one wrist. No knot in the cloth. That told me more than his face did. Salt Veyr would have tied a debt before they sent a boy this far alone.

Maren saw the cloth first. Her eyes went to the lower flats the way a boatmistress reads a tide line.

"Reed-Bend," she said. "They cut where the tide leaves the black sand bare for three hours. Eel spears. Reed knives. They keep to themselves until the ice comes for the weak."

I had not known the island had people that low who still carried a name worth sending.

The boy stopped at the edge of the counting stone. He did not kneel the way Khar Vane did when they wanted something. He stood with his weight on the back foot, ready to run if the law here turned out to be teeth.

"Keva sent me," he said. "The bend-reed cutters. We heard the crown makes roads that do not take from the small. We want that side of the road."

Harvek stood with his arms crossed because the road law was still half his to break or keep. Solek the Thin was beside him, listening with the whole of his narrow face. Asha had come with Cairn because any new blood on the island needed a genealogy before it could be trusted. Naim stood a little apart, no staff, carrying only the white cloth of a witness who was not Vessa. Mother Harl had asked for that. After the clean medicine and the store argument, she wanted eyes on what the crown did next.

The Wound-Guest woke the moment the boy said "small." It liked small. Small was easy to close a hand around.

"Name what was taken," I said.

The boy swallowed. His throat moved like a bird's. "Two eel runs. One by men who said they guarded the crown road and the crown takes its share. One by men who said the crown road was poison and the true law would come for us if we did not pay the fog woods first. Our winter fish is low. The children cough at night. Keva says if the stores are truly for the small, we will give what we can cut and dry. But we need the road to say we are not meat for anyone wearing your sign or hating it."

The Guest pressed harder. Images came fast and clean: the coughing children in the dark, small bodies easy to lift, the tide flats as a thing a hand could close around until nothing moved. The white-cloth boy in front of me, his wrist bound, taken before he could run. My ribs burned. Sweat started on my palms where they gripped the wrapped spear. The red was already at the edges of my sight like water rising.

Maren's mouth went thin in the way it did when numbers did not add. "How much can the bend give without starving its own?"

"Reed bundles for the new winter shelters. Two baskets of dried eel each turning of the moon. The best we have left after the runs were hit. We keep enough for the weak and the old and the ones who cannot walk the flats."

"And what do you want in the knot?" Maren asked.

The boy looked at me. Not at Harvek. Not at the antler shape Naim carried in his hands like a question he had not decided how to ask.

"The law that says no one takes from the bend without answering to the crown. And a share of what the cradles take. Small. Enough for our own winter hole. Keva says the island is learning to count under the blood-crown. We want to be inside the count before the count decides we are the part that can be left out."

Harvek made a short sound that was not quite a laugh. "You want protection without sending sons to the pit law."

"We have no sons left to send," the boy said. "The raids took the last ones or the fog took them winters back. Keva buried the last under reed so the antlers would not find the bones."

Asha spoke then. "Do your people carry old blood that remembers names?"

The boy hesitated. "Some. The grandmothers who still speak to the tide. Keva's own grandmother told stories of fire on the mainland before the ice came and the names changed. We do not sing them. We cut reed instead."

Cairn's hand moved toward the wrapped spear on his back, then stopped. The Fen discipline was there, but the boy was not a threat. He was a hunger that had learned to walk on two legs and ask first.

I felt the Guest push again. Take the reeds. Take the eels. The flats were open and the small folk were already afraid of two sides. Their fear would feed the story faster than any pit. My voice came out rougher than I wanted. "Bring Keva. Or speak for her with witnesses who will remember the words. The bargain will be four-tribe and one small. Salt Veyr counts the knots. Khar Vane keeps the teeth off the lower road. Fen-Kin names the blood if blood is what wakes. Hollow Antler watches what the name does to the dead who belong to the bend."

Naim had started to speak. The words died in his mouth. He looked at the white cloth in his hands, then at the boy, then at the ground. For the first time since the medicine baskets, he chose not to say the thing Hollow Antler would have wanted said. He chose silence instead of owning the dead of the bend for Vessa. The cost showed in the way his shoulders pulled in, like he had swallowed something sharp. I saw it. The crack inside him was no longer just a crack. It was a choice he had made in front of witnesses, and the choice had been against his own tribe's interest in naming the small first.

This was the first time he had chosen against his own tribe's naming power in front of the other three. It would not stay private. The silence was a new kind of knot between us, one made of mutual suspicion rather than trust. He had seen what the small needed and had not taken the chance to claim it for Hollow Antler. I had seen him choose it. Neither of us could pretend we had not.

The boy nodded once, sharp as a reed knife cutting.

It took the rest of the light for Keva to reach us.

She was older than Mother Harl, thinner in the way people get when every winter takes more than it gives back. Her hands were reed-cutters' hands, the palms ridged and the fingers stained dark from the black mud. She brought the boy and a girl no older than me who carried a basket of the last dried eel and said nothing. Keva did not bow. She stood on the flat stone by the road spur where the law was supposed to live now and looked at the four of us like she was counting tides she did not trust.

"You are the pit boy," she said.

"I am Rook."

"Some call you the returned. Some call you the hunger that learned to wear a crown. Keva of the Reed-Bend does not call you either until she sees what the name costs the small who live where the big camps do not look."

Maren laid out the offer the way she laid out tally boards. Exact. No poetry. Reed bundles measured by arm-span. Eel baskets counted by weight and by the moon's turn. The share that would come back to the bend as a protected winter hole, witnessed so no one could take it later and call it gift.

The Guest was loud now. The coughing children again. The white-cloth boy taken. The flats closed in a fist. My hand tightened on the wrapped spear until the red thread bit my palm. I made myself speak the anchor out loud before the images could finish.

"The small loop," I said. "That is what we tie tonight. Not the whole hand."

Harvek's eyes flicked to me. He had heard the strain.

The Guest settled low, not gone, but listening. A small, low feeling like a laugh that had nowhere to go yet. It had heard the name I gave the knot. It would remember the shape of it.

Harvek said the road part. "In exchange the bend sends one of its own to stand witness when the lower spur is walked. No one from the bend raises claim or steel against the crown law. And if the crown law is broken on your flats, you send word before you take your own revenge."

Solek spoke the old Khar words that turned a promise into something that could be eaten if broken. The words about debt and meat and the pit that waits for the ones who lie under law. His voice was thin and certain. "Small debt is still debt. The law does not grow smaller because the mouth is small. A debt that begins small can still be eaten when it grows."

Asha asked for the blood. A drop from Keva's hand onto a bone bead she carried under her shirt. "For the genealogies. If the old blood in the bend wakes wrong later, we will know where the line began. The bead will carry the choice the grandmothers made when they sent you here."

Keva held out her hand without argument. Cairn made the cut with a small knife, clean and quick. The blood beaded dark on the pale bone. Keva watched it like she had watched many things leave her and not come back.

"If the blood wakes wrong," she said, "you will know it started at the bend because we chose the crown over the fog."

Naim watched the bead form. His mouth moved once, then closed. He had the look of a man who wanted to say that the dead of the bend had already been hidden from antlers once, and that naming them now under a crown might be another kind of theft. He said nothing. The silence cost him. I could see it in the way his fingers tightened on the white cloth until the knuckles showed. He was choosing, in front of all of us, to let the small name their own dead rather than let Hollow Antler reach them first through him.

Maren tied the knot on a new cord she carried for these things. Salt style, with a length of reed from Keva's basket woven through the turns so the bend would know its own part in the count. Four knots for the big tribes and one small loop for the Reed-Bend. She pulled it tight and bit the end with her teeth the way she did when the number mattered.

When the reed was woven through and the small loop pulled closed, Maren spoke without looking up. "Now the count has to feed another mouth that did not fight for it. The road will have to hold for people who cannot hold it themselves. That is the price of making the small inside instead of outside."

Harvek watched the loop form. "Small meat still has teeth if the law breaks. If the bend sends word of a taking, we answer. If they lie about a taking, the pit law still waits. The crown does not grow soft because the hands that feed it are small."

Asha touched the bead where it hung against Keva's wrist for a moment. "The line is longer now. The grandmothers who speak to the tide are inside the count. If the old blood wakes in them later, we will know where it began and what choice bought it here."

Naim looked at the finished knot. He did not speak. The not-speaking was louder than anything he could have said about the dead. It was the first time I had seen him refuse the shape Hollow Antler would have put on the small.

The Reed-Bend girl who carried the eel basket shifted her grip. Her fingers tightened until the basket pressed hard against her hip. She looked once at Keva, quick and sideways, like she was checking whether this counted thing was real or whether it would be taken back the moment the big people stopped watching. The look was small and it did not last, but it said more than the knot about what being inside the count meant to the ones who had been outside.

Keva looked at the loop in her palm and closed her fingers around it.

"There is one more thing," she said.

The air changed the way it does when a boat hits a rock you did not see under the water.

"Two nights before your runner reached us, a woman came from the high fog woods. She carried no cradle. She carried an antler with the hollows filled with salt and a strip of white cloth. She said the stores the crown had opened were fed by the pit-thing's hunger. That any child who ate from them would be claimed by the wrong dead when the ice came and the names were called. She said the true law would protect the bend if we kept our winter fish for the fog woods and the antlers instead of the crown."

Vessa's reach. Already at the edge. Poison in the water before we had offered the cup.

Harvek's face went hard as old bone. "And did you believe her?"

Keva looked at the knot in her hand. "We believed she would come back with more than salt and cloth if we said no out loud. Then your road men came the next day and took the third eel run. They said it was the crown's right and the crown boy would thank them for feeding his law. We hid what was left. That is why we walked here. Between two hungers, we choose the one that names its price where we can see the hands."

I felt the Guest rise hard and clean. The road men were Dremm's knot or Keff's or one of the young ones who still thought restraint was waste. They had reached the bend ahead of the law and taken what they wanted under my name. Opposition from inside the crown and from outside it, both moving faster than the center could walk.

"Name the men who took the fish," I said. My voice was still rough from holding the images back.

Keva did not look away. "They wore no sign we could carry back. But one had a missing ear wrapped in hide and salt. They called him Half-Ear. He laughed when we hid the children. Before he left he cut a crude antler into the mud by the run and spat on it. He said the fog woods would come for the rest soon enough."

Half-Ear. Not just a shadow. A name with a mark left behind. Dremm's faction had already decided the small were meat that could be made to serve before we made the bargain. The antler in the mud was Vessa's language turned against us, or turned to show the small who the real law was supposed to be.

The knot was tied. The bend law existed now on paper and cord and witness. But the reach had already happened. Vessa had spoken first with her antler and her salt. Dremm's shadow, wearing Half-Ear's name and mark, had taken first with a wrapped ear and a laugh and a cut in the mud.

My authority had arrived late to its own extension.

"Harvek," I said. "Half-Ear answers for the taking. Not with the small. With us. Bring him to the counting stone before the knot is hung. The bend will see that the crown names its own thieves before it names their protection."

Harvek's mouth tightened. He did not like it. But the law was the law he had helped tie.

Runa stood a little behind Harvek's shoulder. Her eyes flicked to me when I named Half-Ear, then away fast. The young ones who had been circling Dremm's knot since the missing ear were not all with him, but they were not all with the law either. One of them shifted his weight like he might speak. Runa's hand moved, small, a warning or a claim. The extension to the Reed-Bend was already being tested by the people who were supposed to carry it.

That night, after Keva and her people were given a place under Salt guard and the knot was hung where the road spur met the main count, I walked the edge of the camp with the wrapped spear in my hands.

The Wound-Guest was quiet. Not bored. Waiting in the way it waited after the second pit, after it had learned that restraint could be turned into a larger story.

It had learned something new tonight.

Small tribes could be added without a pit. Without blood snow. Without throwing anyone into the white. Just by being the one who named the law that reached them first.

Or second.

Vessa had spoken first.

Harvek's people had taken first.

The Reed-Bend had chosen the crown because the other two offers had come with knives already drawn.

I stopped at the place where the new spur left the central ground. The tide flats were out there in the dark, black sand and reed and the small people who lived on what the water left behind. They were now under a crown that had not yet decided whether it protected what it named or consumed it to feed the thing inside.

Keva's fingers closing around the small loop in her palm. The Reed-Bend girl pressing the eel basket hard to her hip. The crude antler cut in the mud by the run. All of it under the same name now.

The small loop on the knot felt heavier than the cord that held it.

I did not sleep.

The island was larger than the camps, and the camps were already speaking in more voices under my name. Not just the big four. Now the small ones had a loop. The grandmothers who spoke to the tide. The children who coughed at night. Every new voice that the count had to feed made the center thinner, not stronger, because the ones who already hated the crown could point to every small mouth we had to feed and say the hunger was spreading. The flats would remember who had come to them first with a bargain and who had come with salt and a missing ear and a cut antler in the mud.

I would have to remember faster. The more voices spoke under the name, the more places there were for other hands to reach in before the knot could hold. My hand on the wrapped spear still shook, small and low, long after the pressure had passed. The thing inside had heard the name I gave the small loop. It would use it when it chose.
