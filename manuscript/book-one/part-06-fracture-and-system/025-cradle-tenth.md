# Chapter 25 Draft: Cradle Tenth

Mother Harl did not accept the bargain.

She carried it.

There is a difference. I learned that before the sun cleared the ridge, because Maren made me stand beside the first storehouse while every tribe pretended not to watch.

The storehouse belonged to Khar Vane by raid right, though Salt Veyr had built the door, Fen-Kin had smoked half the fish inside it, and Hollow Antler had blessed the threshold three winters before. That was the island. Every thing had one owner until hunger came. Then every thing had four histories and a knife behind each.

Harvek stood with both hands wrapped in sealhide, not from peace but because he had promised no open steel during counting.

"This is foolish," he said.

"You said that already."

"I will say it until it stops being true."

Maren had three tally boards set on a flat stone. Senn crouched beside them with a charcoal nub, his tongue pressed between his teeth. Dremm stood behind him like a post driven too deep. His missing ear was still wrapped. Young Khar Vane drifted around him in little knots.

Not around me.

Around him.

That mattered.

Maren pointed at the door. "Open it."

No one moved.

Harvek looked at me. "Give the order, crown."

The word still felt wrong on my head. It had teeth under it.

"Open it," I said.

Two Khar Vane men lifted the bar. Old smoke rolled out, thick with fish oil and dried bladderwrack. My stomach clenched before my mind caught up. The boy I had been on the black shore would have crawled over teeth for that smell.

The Wound-Guest liked the smell too.

Not food, it pressed.

Store.

It knew the difference. That frightened me more than hunger.

Maren sent Salt Veyr counters inside first. Khar Vane growled at that. Fen-Kin witnesses followed, then two Hollow Antler mothers with white masks pushed up on their heads. Mother Harl did not enter. She stood outside with one palm on an empty cradle frame and watched the door as if it were a mouth that might lie.

Senn scratched marks. "Thirty-two smoked seal. Eleven racks cod. Six oil skins. Two barley sacks gone wet on bottom."

"Not barley," Maren said. "Mash feed."

"Mash feed," Senn corrected.

Harvek snorted. "You put children on warrior count and they will eat warrior food."

One of the masked mothers said, "Children become warriors if winter lets them."

The young Khar Vane near Dremm laughed, ugly and low.

The mother turned her head.

The laugh died.

I saw it then: the cradle tenth was not soft. It put mothers into war counting. It made men who liked blood stand still while women named what hunger had already taken from them. Khar Vane did not know how to fight that without looking smaller than the cradles.

Maren knew. Of course she knew.

"Tenth is four seal, two racks, one oil skin share, and no mash feed," she said.

Harvek's jaw worked. "Four?"

"Rounding up."

"Salt rounding."

"Cradle rounding," said Mother Harl.

Harvek looked at me again. He wanted me to make the cost mine. He wanted the young ones to hear it from my mouth.

So I gave it to them.

"Set it aside."

Dremm's knot shifted. One boy, red-haired and narrow-eyed, spat into the mud.

"We bled for that store."

The Wound-Guest rose fast.

So did my shame, because part of me agreed with him.

I had slept hungry under rocks. I knew what it meant to find food and have older hands take a piece for names I had never heard. The island was full of reasons to become teeth.

I walked to the red-haired boy.

He did not step back. That was bravery or audience. Most courage has witnesses hidden inside it.

"Name," I said.

"Keff."

"Who fed you first winter?"

His mouth tightened.

"Answer."

He looked toward Dremm. Dremm gave him nothing.

"My mother."

"Where is she?"

"Dead."

"Named?"

His hand curled.

"Yes."

"Then you know what this buys."

He said nothing.

"If you spit again," I said, "you clean every cradle frame in Hollow Antler camp with your tongue where mothers can see. Not because they are soft. Because they carried you before you could hold a knife."

The Wound-Guest wanted more. It offered the image of his teeth on stone.

I held still.

Keff looked at the mud, at his spit, at the mothers.

Then he bent and smeared the spit away with his sleeve.

It was not obedience. Not fully. But it was a first small shape of law.

Maren's eyes flicked to me, quick as a knife flash.

Approval? No. Calculation.

She sent two Salt Veyr boys to carry out the cradle tenth. Khar Vane watched food leave their store without blood answering it. That was a wound I had made in them. Maybe a necessary one. Necessary wounds still fester if left proud.

Harvek leaned close enough that only I heard him.

"You are teaching them to lose."

"I am teaching them what keeping costs."

"Warriors follow full hands."

"Then feed them after the tenth."

"With what?"

I looked at the storehouse. At the boards. At the road beyond the camp. At Salt Veyr faces already learning where power sat when numbers were written.

"Roads," I said.

Harvek's smile showed old teeth. "Now you begin to sound like someone worth fearing."

Mother Harl watched the first fish rack set beside the cradle frames. She did not thank me. Thanking would have made the bargain mine and hers together. She was too careful for that.

But one of the younger mothers touched two fingers to the rack before lifting it.

Not blessing.

Count.

Senn scratched the mark.

The sound of charcoal on wood was small. Smaller than a blade leaving a sheath. Smaller than a throat opening for a song.

But every head turned to hear it.

That was when I understood Maren had taken more than a tenth.

She had taken the counting.
