# Chapter 26 Draft: The Wrapped Spear

Asha came for me after the cradle tenth, when Khar Vane were still angry enough to sharpen tools that did not need sharpening.

She brought Cairn and a spear wrapped from point to heel in gray cloth.

No blade showed. No wood showed. It looked like a dead thing prepared for carrying.

Dremm saw it and laughed once. "Fen brings a sleeping stick."

Cairn looked at him. "For a sleeping boy."

Dremm's hand went to his missing ear.

I stepped between them before either could make my morning easier to understand and harder to survive.

"What is it?"

Asha set the wrapped spear across both palms. "A caller."

"Spears call death."

"This one calls names."

The Wound-Guest stirred.

Names were hooks to it. Names were handles on meat. Names were what people gave one another so grief knew where to sit.

Asha saw my face change. Her mouth tightened.

"You feel it already."

"I feel everything already."

"No. You feel hunger, threat, shame, blood, cold. This is different."

She led me away from the storehouses, up to a place where black stone rose through the snow like knuckles. Cairn followed with the wrapped spear. Harvek sent Solek after us as Khar Vane witness. Maren came without being invited. Mother Harl did not come, but two Hollow Antler women stood far below with cradle frames on their backs.

No act stayed private now.

That was another kind of leash.

Asha took the spear from Cairn and laid it on the stone.

"Old blood does not return because someone asks politely," she said. "When the red takes you deep, words slide off. Pain may reach. Cold may reach. A known name may reach. A trained body may reach faster than all three."

"You want to train the thing."

"I want to train the boy before the thing learns faster."

The Wound-Guest laughed inside my ribs.

It had almost-words now. Not full speech. Not yet. But pressure gathered in shapes.

Train.

It liked that word.

Asha unwrapped the spear.

The shaft was ash-gray and old, carved with shallow lines that circled but never touched. The blade was not iron. Bone, maybe. White as milk under ice, with a red thread tied below it.

Cairn said, "Do not look at the thread when she calls."

So of course I looked.

The red thread seemed ordinary until my blood noticed it. Then the world leaned.

The Wound-Guest rose like a dog scenting the open door.

Not dog. Not anything that had ever curled beside a fire. It rose old and pleased and certain that the wrapped spear had been made by hands that knew how vessels broke.

My mouth filled with iron.

Asha struck the butt of the spear against stone.

Once.

The sound went into my teeth.

"Rook," she said.

I hated my name in her mouth because it reached me.

Again the spear struck.

"Rook of no shore."

My knees bent. Not from weakness. From wanting to spring.

The Wound-Guest pressed forward.

Kill caller.

Not words. Shape. Certainty.

Asha's eyes did not blink.

The spear struck a third time.

"Rook who came back."

The red thread brightened. Or my eyes lied.

Cairn moved behind me. I heard his feet in snow, careful, ready to cut tendons if old blood drowned me. Solek's breathing slowed. Maren stepped farther away, smart woman.

"Answer," Asha said.

My jaw locked.

The Wound-Guest filled my tongue with heat. It wanted to answer in a sound older than names. It wanted to make the Fen woman kneel and the stone remember. It wanted to show them the pit had not given back a boy. It had given back a door.

The spear struck.

"Answer."

I bit my tongue.

Blood came sharp and mine.

Mine.

That word mattered.

"Rook," I said.

It came out broken.

Asha lowered the spear half an inch.

"Again."

"Rook."

The Wound-Guest recoiled, not beaten. Irritated. Curious.

Rook, it pressed.

It tasted the name as if it were a bone with marrow.

Asha heard something in my breath and went pale.

"What did it do?"

"It listened."

Cairn cursed softly.

Asha wrapped the spear in one quick motion. "That is the danger."

Solek said, "I thought the danger was failing."

"No," Asha said. "Failure kills him. Success teaches what lives in him where the door is."

The Wound-Guest settled back, warm and pleased.

Door.

This time the almost-word was clear enough that I flinched.

Asha saw that too.

Her fear changed. Before, she had feared a loss of control. Now she feared an education.

"Again tomorrow," she said.

"You just said success teaches it."

"And doing nothing lets it teach itself."

Cairn took the wrapped spear. His face was tight with the unfairness of being right beside something he had trained his life to understand and still not understanding enough.

I wiped blood from my lip.

"If I fail?"

Cairn answered before Asha.

"Then I do what she brought me to do."

He said it cleanly. No hate. That made it worse.

The Wound-Guest liked him.

Strong hand, it pressed. Clean cut.

I almost laughed because the alternative was letting them see my fear.

"Good," I said. "Stand close tomorrow."

Cairn's eyes narrowed. "Why?"

"If you are the knife waiting for me, I want the thing to learn your face too."

Asha did not like that.

Neither did I.

But the Wound-Guest was listening now. The island had become a school, and everyone kept bringing lessons.
