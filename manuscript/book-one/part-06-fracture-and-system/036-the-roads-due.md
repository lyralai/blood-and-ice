# Chapter 36: The Road's Due

The ice had skinned the black sand for three turnings when the Reed-Bend came again.

The runner was the older one this time, the one with the man's shoulders under the hide. He carried the small loop cord in both hands, the Salt turns cut clean through with a reed knife and a new loop of black sand tied beside the first. The girl walked behind him, empty-handed, her fingers red from the cold and the reed knives. Keva came last, thinner, the ridges on her palms cracked deeper from the ice and the mud that no longer gave.

They stopped at the counting stone. The place where the law was supposed to live now had more eyes on it than the first time. Maren was already there with her boards. Harvek and Solek stood with the road law still half theirs to break or keep. Asha had come with Cairn because the blood bead still held the line. Naim stood a little apart, no staff, the white cloth in his hands like a question he had not decided how to ask. Mother Harl was with the cradle witnesses. One of the road-sworn, Runa, was there because the three had been walking the pass thinner since the useful sacrifice transformation. Yara was at the edge, humming a single low note that did not become a song.

Keva held the cord out without kneeling.

"The share the knot named did not come," she said. "The first basket we hid for the weak and the coughing ones was taken after your law was tied. The men spoke the crown name. One had an ear wrapped in hide and salt. He laughed when we showed the small loop and cut the new sand loop into the cord with his own knife. He said the name on the small would pay its due in the center or the flats would remember who came first with teeth. A child died waiting for the hole the crown had counted her into. We buried her under reed so the antlers would not find the bones. The wind carried the name anyway. The grandmothers who speak to the tide say the name must pay the road's due now or the bend will cut the cord and the law will have no reach where the tide leaves the sand."

Half-Ear. The order to bring him to the counting stone had not held or had not reached. The faction had reached the bend again, under the name, and taken what the law had promised to protect. The due was the name for the small loop's claim under winter. It was a hand that could be closed.

Maren's mouth went thin. She looked at the boards she carried. "The inlet is thin. The next fines from the road are already marked for the cradles and the road-sworn's winter shares. The boat-women will ask why the small on the flats eat before the ones who keep the boats moving. If we send a basket to the bend now, the next inspection will show the tenth short again."

Harvek's face was hard as old bone. "The men who took after the knot are the crown's to answer for. The bend will have the share the knot named. Small debt is still debt. The law does not grow smaller because the mouth is small. A debt that begins small can still be eaten when it grows."

Solek spoke the old Khar words that turned a promise into something that could be eaten if broken. The words sounded thinner in the cold air over the counting stone.

Asha touched the bead where it would have hung against Keva's wrist if the cord had not been cut. "The line is longer now. The grandmothers who speak to the tide are inside the count. If the old blood wakes in them later, we will know where it began and what choice bought it here. The blood bead still holds the choice the grandmothers made when they sent you the first time. The due is owed on that line."

Naim's mouth moved once when Keva said the child had died waiting. He looked at the white cloth in his hands, then at the cut cord, then at the ground. He chose silence again. The cost showed in the way his shoulders pulled in, like he had swallowed something sharp. The knuckles showed white on the cloth. He was choosing, in front of the small and the four tribes, to let the bend name their own dead rather than let Hollow Antler reach them through the crown's failure. This was the second time. It would not stay private. The silence was a new kind of knot, one made of the crown's late arrival and the choice not to claim the dead for the antlers. The three-name rule he had given me carried the weight of the old line now; the blood name spoken by the last Rauth would close paths the isolation had tried to starve.

Mother Harl spoke for the cradles. "If the small take from the tenth to pay the due the crown named, the empty frames will multiply. The mothers will remember that the small on the flats were fed before the frames were filled."

The Wound-Guest rose the moment Keva said "the road's due."

It liked the name. The due was a claim the small loop could make on the center. It was a hand that could be closed around the flats until the due came to the story in fear and tribute. Images came fast and clean: the bend sending the rest of the eel and the reed to feed the crown's hunger; the tide flats as a thing the name could close until nothing moved; the coughing children as the ones who would make the small loop strong if the crown took what the fog woods had left them and called it the due. The pressure under my ribs climbed like the ice on the sand. Sweat started on my palms where they gripped the wrapped spear. The red was at the edges of my sight like water rising on a flat that had no shore. My voice came out rougher than I wanted.

"The small loop," I said. "The road's due is the law's price. Not the pit's meat."

The Guest settled low, not gone, but listening. A small, low feeling like a laugh that had nowhere to go yet. It had heard the name I gave the claim. It would remember the shape of "the road's due."

I felt the weight of the small loop on the cord I still carried from the first knot. The more voices spoke under the name, the more places there were for other hands to reach in before the knot could hold. The bend had chosen the crown because the other two offers had come with knives already drawn. The name had arrived second. To make the small believe the name had teeth, the crown would have to pay the due with its own before the ice closed the flats.

Runa shifted her weight behind Harvek's shoulder. Her eyes flicked to me when I named the due and the two road-sworn who would carry it. Dorrik and Keff were the ones I chose. They had been the first three. One of them had been involved in the taking before the knot. Now they would walk the due to the bend and stand there as the crown's presence until the turning. The faction that had taken under the name would see the crown using its own to pay what they had stolen. The young knot would see the living debt stretched to the flats.

"The next three fines from the road will go to the Reed-Bend winter hole," I said. "Maren will mark the new due line on the boards. Two of the road-sworn will carry the first portion to the bend and stand the due there. The crown's teeth will stay where the law was late. Half-Ear and the ones who took after the knot will answer with their own shares or the name will close the hand on the faction that reached the bend first."

Maren's mouth tightened because the new "Reed-Bend due" line would have to be marked and re-counted every turning, and the boat-women would see the draw from the inlet priorities. Harvek did not like sending his own to stand the flats, but the law he had helped tie required the answer or the pit would lose its teeth on the small. Asha watched the wrapped spear in Cairn's hands; the red thread had already taught the thing how to wait for the call and how to use the names the boy gave the claims. Naim's silence was louder than anything he could have said about the dead child. Mother Harl's eyes said the cradles would remember that the small took from the tenth before the frames were filled.

Keva took the cord back after Maren re-tied the due knot into it — a new turn of reed from the bend's own and a bone bead from Asha's line. Her fingers closed around the small loop and the new due knot the same way they had the first time. The girl from the bend watched, quick sideways look at Keva, then at the two road-sworn who would walk with them. The look said the counted thing was real only if the name kept paying. If the due did not come, the loop would be cut and the crown would have no law where the tide left the sand.

Runa's hand moved, small, a warning or a claim. The extension to the Reed-Bend was already being tested by the people who were supposed to carry it. One of the young faction shifted his weight like he might speak. The due the crown had named was already pulling from the center faster than the law could walk.

That night, after Keva and her people were given a place under Salt guard and the re-tied cord was hung where the road spur met the main count, I walked the edge of the camp with the wrapped spear in my hands.

The ice had reached the black sand. The tide flats were a darker line under the moon. The small loop on the cord pulled against the turns. It felt heavier than the first time. The new due knot pulled against the turns. The red thread on the spear bit my palm when I tightened my grip. I could still see the Reed-Bend girl's fingers on the eel basket and the quick sideways look she gave Keva when the knot was tied. The coughing from the direction of the bend carried on the wind.

The Wound-Guest had liked the name "the road's due." It had offered the flats as a thing a hand could close. I said my name until the images thinned.
Rook.

The cord stayed in my hand until the cold made the reed stiff. One more mouth the count had to feed. One more place a hand could reach before the knot held.
