# Chapter 35: The Wrapped Call

The counting stone was crowded again. Winter had thinned the stores faster than Maren's inlet boats could fill them, and the small loop from Reed-Bend had added mouths that coughed in the night. Keva stood with her granddaughter, the girl holding an eel basket like it was the last thing between her and the fog. Half-Ear was there too, his missing ear wrapped in salt-hide, the red-haired Keff beside him, Dorrik and Runa at the edge of the knot. The road-sworn had not broken their oath yet, but their eyes were on the basket and the thin count Maren spoke aloud.

Maren's voice was flat with the numbers. "Root is low. Oil for lamps is one turning from gone. The bend sent reed and eel, but the pass guards eat what the fines bring and the cradles take their tenth before the rest is divided. The inlet boats cannot run in this ice without more hands or more risk."

Mother Harl's cradle frames clicked. Naim stood near her, no staff, two fingers to his left wrist then palm down when he thought no one watched. The fasting sign. The inner law he had given me still sat like a debt knot I had not decided how to pull.

"The crown promised the small would not starve under its name," Keva said. Her voice was reed-dry. "We gave what we cut. The children still cough."

Half-Ear laughed, the sound short and wet. "The red thing eats the count the same as it eats meat. Give it one of the road-sworn who still owes and the rest will walk the pass without taking from the bend. Useful. The old price."

The Wound-Guest rose at "small" and "cough" and "eat the count." It had learned the language of stores. Images came clean: the bend girl's basket overturned, the eel meat in the debt bowl poured across the stone so every future tally carried the mark of what the crown fed. The coughing child lifted easy because small things did not fight the hand that closed. Pressure under my ribs. Sweat on my palms where the wrapped spear rested. Red at the edges like the tide coming in under black sand.

I counted the witnesses the way Asha had taught. Maren. Harvek. Solek. Asha with Cairn. Mother Harl. Naim. Keva and the girl. The three road-sworn. Otta at the edge with her one arm. Yara humming low, not a full song yet.

"The small loop is counted," I said. "The fines go to the cradles and the pass first. No one takes from the bend."

Keff spat. "The store took first. The red thing knows it."

The Guest pushed harder. Let me close the hand. The girl is small. The cough will stop if the name feeds on what made it. I bit the inside of my cheek until iron flooded. Self-name. Rook. Rook who came back. The small loop is not meat.

Asha stepped forward. She had the wrapped spear in her hands, the gray cloth half-unwound. Cairn's hand was near the bone blade at his belt. The crown term was in the air: Fen may call him back. If he does not return, bind or kill.

"This is not the stone for training," I said.

Asha's eyes were on my face, not the stone. "The call is not training when the red is already at the edges."

She struck the butt against the counting stone.

Once.

The sound went into my teeth like the first time on the black stone.

"Rook."

The Wound-Guest did not wait for the second strike. It rose before the sound finished, anticipating the rhythm it had learned in the fog woods. The red thread on the bone blade seemed to lean toward me. I saw the bargain form inside before Asha could speak the next name.

Let the spear-mother name the bend as yours in blood. I will close the hands on the ones who reach before the knot. Three turnings. The small will feed the name. Their cough will make the crown strong.

The image was clean: Asha striking the spear while I held the bend girl by the wrist, the white cloth turning red, the cough stopping because the hand had closed. It was not blind hunger. It was the shape of the caller turned into a tool. The discipline Asha had taught the boy was now a door the Guest knew how to stand inside.

My knees bent. Not from weakness. From the wanting to spring and take the offer before the third strike could reach.

Asha struck again.

"Rook of no shore."

The Guest answered the name with the name. It pushed the repetition back at the caller. Rook calls the spear-mother. The old blood answers the pit. Let the Fen see what their wrapped thing has taught.

Asha's face did not change, but her next strike was harder. Cairn's hand closed on the bone blade. The crown term was spoken aloud by Solek the Thin, his voice carrying like law.

"If the boy does not return, the Fen may bind or the Khar may end it under the terms that made the crown."

The red pressed until my vision was only edges. Sweat ran cold down my back. The wrapped spear in Asha's hands was no longer a tool for recall. It was a thing the Guest had tasted and now wanted to answer in its own time.

I counted louder inside. The bend girl. The coughing one. The small loop heavier than the cord. If I let it take the caller the knot breaks and Reed-Bend starves under my name and the island learns the crown is only teeth that learned to count. Rook. Rook who holds the small loop. Rook who came back from the pit because the boy chose the harder path.

The third strike came.

Asha's voice was steady but thin. "Rook who holds what the red would close."

The Guest offered one last image, patient: the debt bowl poured, the road-sworn walking without taking, the bend sending more because fear makes small things generous. Then it laughed low under the ribs, the sound of a thing that had learned the call has a shape and the crown has a door.

I bit until more iron came. I spoke the name out loud, the way the training had forced.

"Rook."

My voice cracked on it. The red receded like tide pulling back from black sand, but it left marks. My hand on the spear shaft would not stop shaking. Blood from the bitten cheek ran down my chin and dripped on the counting stone. When I looked up, Asha had stepped one pace back. Cairn's hand was still on the bone blade. The bend girl had pulled the eel basket tighter to her hip the way she had when the knot was first tied.

Asha lowered the wrapped spear. The red thread was darker at the knot, as if the blood from my mouth had found it across the space.

"We trained the vessel," she said. Her voice carried to the stone and the witnesses. "Now the thing inside knows how to wait for the call. It knows the crown answers when the boy is named. That is the useful part. That is the part that will wear the crown if we keep teaching it the shape of the law that holds it."

She looked at me the way the old blood looked at the line when it recognized what had come back from the pit. The Rauth endurance had let the force grip without burning through. The Fen had known it from the first check. Now the caller had taught the Guest the door had a rhythm, and the rhythm had a name the thing could answer.

No one spoke for a moment. Yara's hum stopped. Half-Ear's laugh did not come. Maren looked at the numbers on the board and then at my shaking hand.

The small loop felt heavier than it had when the knot was tied. The island was larger than the camps. The more voices spoke under the name, the more places there were for the Guest to stand inside the call and wait for the boy to slip.

I wiped the blood from my chin with the back of my hand. The tremor did not stop. Asha did not step forward again. The crown term had been spoken in the open. Fen had been forced to act. The return had cost more than the surge.

The Guest settled low, patient, having learned that the wrapped call was not only a leash. It was also a lesson in how the crown named its own limits.

I turned from the stone. The bend girl watched me go. Her fingers were white on the basket rim.

The cough would not stop because I had held. It would stop when the stores added or when the hand closed. The Guest knew both paths now.

I walked the spur with the wrapped spear in my shaking hand, counting the small loop and the witnesses and the cost that did not show on the board.

The red was learning faster than the boy.
