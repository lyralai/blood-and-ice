# Chapter 30 Draft: Naim Without Staff

The inspection left a taste like wet ash in the mouth.

Maren had shown what the store held and what it lacked. Mother Harl had named the lacks louder than the holdings, but the witnesses had seen both. Salt count. Fen eyes. One mother from each side. No one had walked away fed or satisfied. That was the point of the day.

I stayed after the others left. The bar was back on the door. The tally marks were fresh on Senn's board. Dremm's knot of boys had drifted off to sharpen things they did not need to sharpen. Harvek had said only "more roads then" and gone to count spears instead of losses.

The light was going. Blackjaw ridge cut the sun early this time of year.

Naim came from the ridge side.

No white cloth. No basket. No mothers behind him. He walked like someone who had left his shadow on purpose. No staff in his hand. The one he had carried at the second pit was gone or set down where Vessa could not see the absence.

I did not call a guard. I wanted to see what a man without a staff thought he could do.

He stopped at the distance where a thrown knife might reach but a hand could not. Close enough to speak without shouting. Far enough that the Red could not take him before I decided.

"You left the store open longer than the count required," he said.

"Counting takes time when people are watching."

"Mother Harl counted the same gaps you did."

"She named them louder."

Naim looked at the door, then at the ground between us. "She names what Vessa tells her to name. The gaps are real. The meaning is chosen."

The Red stirred at the word "gaps." It liked holes. It liked the idea that something had been taken from the inside and the outside had not noticed yet.

I kept my hands where he could see them. "You walked here without the others. Without the staff."

"A staff makes a voice official. Without it I can only carry what I have not been told to say."

"That is a careful way to speak treason."

He did not flinch. "It is a careful way to keep from naming the wrong thing and poisoning the wind for the children who are not born yet."

I waited. The red heat under my ribs pressed once, testing. I pressed back with the part of me that had learned to count witnesses before I counted exits.

Naim took one step closer. Still outside easy reach.

"Vessa teaches the mothers the outer law. The law that says any blood spilled under wrong weather belongs to Hollow Antler to name or unname. She says the pit was a breach because the four tribes stood together and the old blood answered without a priest's mouth to guide it. That is what she carries to the cradle frames, the empty ones she shows when she wants the other tribes to flinch."

He stopped. His eyes went to the ridge, then back.

"The inner law is different. It lives in the hands that carve the first antlers and in the carvers who remember before the current mothers were born. Old Brannoc knows the words. If a vessel survives the White Pit and the blood answers without a name given by the priesthood, the hollows must be left open for one full turning. Three names must be offered before the mourning paths can close against those who stand with the vessel. One name from the dead. One from the living witness who is not Antler. One from the blood itself, spoken by the vessel when it is ready."

The Red rose hard at that. It pushed an image of wind moving through black trees, carrying names that were not mine into every cradle frame on the ridge, names that made the wood whistle like the dead were still trying to speak. Then it showed me my own mouth forced open, something that was not my voice naming the island's hungers one by one while the cradles drank the sound. The red heat climbed my ribs like a hand closing.

I bit down on the inside of my cheek until the iron taste was sharp enough to hold. Sweat started at my hairline. I counted the exits the way the wrapped-spear lessons had taught me to count when the thing pushed. One ridge path. One store bar I could drop. One knife at my belt I would not draw. My voice came out strained. "You are saying Vessa is using only half the law."

"You are saying Vessa is using only half the law."

"I am saying she is using the half that keeps her power and hiding the half that would make her tribe share the risk. If the hollows stay open too long without the three names, the dead do not rest. The wind learns the wrong names and carries them to every child, Antler or Salt or Fen or Khar. That is the inner law the mothers are not taught when Vessa speaks of empty cradles."

The realization came slow and cold while the pressure still sat under my ribs: this gave me a lever on the meaning of the pit without having to own the mothers who carried the frames. Vessa could still point at empty cradles. I could point at the law she had not finished saying.

"Why tell me this?"

Naim's face did something complicated. Not a smile. Not a wince. His voice caught once, then steadied the way a man steadies a frame he is afraid to drop. "Because if she wins the meaning of you, she will use the unborn winters to name my line as polluted for having held the rope at the second pit and not pulled hard enough. My sister's child is in one of the frames that has no name cut yet. Winter-late. The mother still touches the wood where the name should go, like the name might appear if she keeps her fingers there long enough. If Vessa closes the paths while the hollows are still open, that name may never be spoken. The child will be counted with the wrong dead."

He was not asking for pity. He was naming a cost the way Maren named fish. I watched his hand move to his left wrist without thinking — two fingers pressing skin, then open palm down before he caught the motion and let it fall. The sign he had not meant to show. The same hand that had steadied the frame now hid the thing that might save the child he had not named. That made the "I will not kneel" land harder than any oath I had heard since the crown.

The Red offered a cleaner answer. Take the unstaffed man. He is already open. No ritual wall. The name he carries can be eaten and the doubt with it. The mothers will see and some will break before the next count.

I did not move. I let the pressure sit under my ribs until it hurt in the old familiar way. Then I spoke around it.

"You want me to keep the hollows open."

"I want the three names to be offered before any paths are closed. You are the only one who can speak the blood name without Vessa owning the mouth that says it."

"And if I speak it, you will stand with the crown."

"No." He said it fast, the way a man steps off a rotten board. "I will not kneel. I will not sing your name into the pit. I will not tell the mothers you are a blessing. I will only tell the ones who already doubt that the law Vessa carries is not the whole law. That is all I can give without becoming the thing I am trying not to name."

It was the cleanest refusal I had heard since the crown went on.

I believed him. That was the problem.

Mercy from a man who has put down his staff is still a knife. It just arrives without the ceremony that would let you see the edge coming.

"What do you want in return for the inner law?"

"Nothing you can give today. Tomorrow, when Mother Harl brings the fasting mothers to sit outside the camp and not eat until you name what you are or feed what you broke, remember that the fast is not the whole law either. The mothers who sit will be the ones whose cradles are already named. The ones whose children are still winter-late will stay back if they can. Speak to the right mother with the right sign and the fast may crack before it starves anyone useful."

He told me the sign once, crisp. Two fingers to the left wrist, then open palm down, the way a woman might set a cradle frame on the ground when she is too tired to carry it upright. "The mother who will answer is the one whose sister was named wrong in a prior rite and whose boat never came back from the salt runs. That is the sign the fasting mothers know. Reach her with it and the fast may crack before it starves anyone useful to either of us. The ones who have not yet cut a name will stay back if they can."

I memorized it. The Red memorized the face Naim would wear when he lied about where he had been.

Naim turned to go.

"Wait."

He stopped.

I pulled a small knot from my belt. One of Maren's counting knots, the kind that meant a single oil skin owed to the road work. I tossed it so it landed between us. The knot was heavier than it looked, seal-gut cord with a tight Salt twist that still smelled faintly of the fish oil it had once marked. I watched how it sat in the mud between us.

"If the mother answers the sign, bring this back with her mark on it. If she does not, keep it or burn it. I will know what the answer means either way."

The Red offered the clean kill image again while the knot lay there: Naim's throat with no staff and no ritual wall, quick and quiet, the doubt eaten before it could travel back to the ridge. I counted the exits the way the wrapped-spear lessons had taught me — ridge path, store bar I could drop, knife at my belt I would not draw — and let the pressure climb until my palms were damp and the edges of my sight went thin and red. Then I spoke around the iron in my mouth. "Take it or leave it. The choice is yours."

Naim looked at the knot for a long time. He crouched without taking his eyes off me, picked it up, and tested the weight in his own hand the way I had watched him test the road boy's binding. He did not hold it long. "You are learning how to make debts that do not require blood."

"Debts are how the island stays fed when blood is not enough."

I felt the weight of the information settle as I said it. Accepting the sign and the knot meant I now carried the inner law like a debt cord of my own. If the fast cracked the wrong way, the mothers would remember whose hand had offered the test. The Guest laughed once, low, inside the ribs, as if it had just learned the shape of this new gap.

He almost smiled. It did not reach his eyes.

"Be careful what you feed, Rook. The thing inside you likes names that have not been spoken yet."

He left the way he had come, back toward the ridge, no staff, no basket, no white cloth. Just a young man carrying half a law and a knot that might or might not buy him a future name for a winter-late child.

I stayed by the store door until the light was gone.

The Red did not sleep. It kept showing me the open place where a staff should have been. It kept offering the image of Naim's throat with no ritual wall around it. It kept whispering the shape of the name he had not spoken. I felt it settling lower in the ribs, patient, as if it had just learned the shape of this new gap — information that arrived without a staff, names that could be carried like knots, and the space between a man and the law he had been told to keep.

I did not take it.

My hand was still pressed to the place on my cheek where I had bitten, and the red at the edges of my sight took longer than usual to clear.

But I understood why Vessa had sent him with the clean baskets the day before. She had not missed the crack. She had aimed it.

Now the crack had a name and a use.

That was the new pit. Not the White one. Not the witnessed hollow. This one had no ropes and no witnesses except the two of us and the thing that wanted to eat the space between.

I went back to the camp fire. I did not tell Maren or Harvek or Asha what Naim had said. Not yet. First I would test the knot and the sign. First I would see whether mercy that sounded like information was only another way for Hollow Antler to own the next story.

The Red laughed once under my ribs, low and patient.

It could wait.

Names always could.
