# Chapter 34: Dremm's Offer

The snow had begun to bite the counting stone. Thin flakes melted on the boards Maren had left weighted with a salt stone against the wind. The numbers were lower than the last count. The road-sworn had stood the lower spur through two turns of the moon and the small loop had sent reed and eel, but not enough to fill what the pass guards burned keeping the wind off their faces. Three more mouths at the exposed watch needed winter shares that did not exist yet. The small loop was tied. The road-sworn paid living. Winter did not care.

I stood with the law racks at my back because that was where the counting had moved when the store was named open and the bend had asked to be inside it. The wrapped spear leaned against the rack wood where Cairn had left it after the last naming. Asha stood with him at the edge of the light, old-blood still, watching the spur. Naim was there too, no staff, the white cloth folded small in his hands. Mother Harl had asked for eyes on what the crown did with the small who had come asking. Yara sat on the low stone with her back to the wind, humming nothing yet. Harvek sharpened a hook against the rack. Solek stood with his arms folded so thin they looked like they might snap. Maren counted under her breath and did not look at me.

The Wound-Guest was quiet. It did not care about boards or shares until the numbers touched meat or the small could be closed in a hand.

Half-Ear came from the direction of the young fires. The red-haired one with the hide wrapped over the missing ear. He had laughed when we hid the children at the bend. Keff walked on his left, red hair tied back with a strip of hide, the one who had spat at the store and learned not to. The third was the one who would not meet Dorrik's eye after the axle work. Dorrik stayed at the edge of the knot again, watching the ground like the cart he had fixed under Salt eyes still weighed on him. Dremm was not with them. The earless one had become the mouth.

"Crown," Half-Ear said.

The word sat wrong in his mouth the way it had in Dremm's. He used it like a bone he wanted to crack in front of the small witnesses who had come up the spur to see if the count was real.

"The lower pass needs the stores the small loop promised," Half-Ear said. "The three road-sworn stand thin because the crown would not let the old price be paid at the racks. The bend sends what it chooses because it saw the crown choose work over meat. Winter does not eat numbers. Winter eats what the teeth show it can take before the snow locks the spur."

He looked at the boards, then at the Reed-Bend girl who had come with Keva's runner, the one who still pressed the eel basket hard to her hip when she thought no one watched.

"Name one," Half-Ear said. "One of the three who broke the road. Or one from the bend who can be made the useful thing. The old rite in front of the small witnesses. His blood and a piece to the fire so the story travels that the pit boy knows how to take when the count fails. Their fear will bring the full baskets before the ice takes the lower road. The guards will eat. The small will learn the crown has more than Salt's knots and Fen's names. Restraint is waste when winter does not care about living examples."

The camp did not go quiet. The wind cut. The small witnesses went still in the way people go still when they learn the hand that counted them can close.

The Wound-Guest woke.

Not with the old blind hunger. With something slower, patient, the shape of a hand testing cloth for seams that had not been there before the small loop was tied. Images came without words first: the Reed-Bend girl pressing the basket to her hip until the eel dried hard against her, then the flats as a thing a hand could close around until the coughing children sent baskets of first blood or winter-late before the snow locked the spur. Then the story moving: small holds beyond the ridge who had begun sending watchers now sending tribute instead of carts, the name "pit boy" becoming the thing the small paid to before the ice took their weak, fear that brought measured stores without more counting work or four-tribe escorts. The debt bowl carried not to the storehouse door this time but to the bend fires, poured so every small who watched would see the mark and send before asked.

It almost spoke.

*This is not waste. This feeds the teeth that hold the roads when the numbers lie. This teaches the small what the crown costs when the count is thin. Fear is the store that does not freeze. Tribute before the ice takes the name.*

My tongue found the place where I had bitten it during the wrapped-spear lesson. The iron taste was still there if I pressed. The cold wind cut through the bone band on my arm. Red crept at the edges of my sight like water rising under ice.

Maren spoke first. "The three already pay with work and double share reassigned to the road store. The small loop sends measured. The count is what holds the spur."

"Work does not fill bellies that stand watch when the wind cuts through hide," Half-Ear said. "Meat and fear do. The bend held back on the last run because they saw the crown choose the living debt over the old price. They will hold back again when the snow comes harder. Name one and the story travels faster than any cart."

Harvek looked at me over the hook. His face gave nothing at first, then the corner of his mouth pulled once, tight, like a man who had watched a student use his own lesson against the knot he had raised. Relief that the old price had not been taken in front of the small. Disappointment that the teeth he had raised were being fed something thinner than meat again. He had taught me that a chief who could not disappoint his people was already eaten. Now he was seeing it applied to the ones who had wanted the meat, and the lesson landed heavier than the last time.

Solek said, "Khar Vane law on road breakers and oath allows the old price if the crown names the winter need. The pit still holds the right when the stores are thin."

Asha had not moved. Cairn's hand was near the wrapped spear on the rack. She watched Half-Ear the way she watched weather that could turn the road.

Naim's eyes went to the Reed-Bend girl, then to the white cloth in his hands. He did not speak. The choice he had made at the small loop knot — silence on the bend dead rather than claim the naming for Hollow Antler — was still on him. It showed in the way his shoulders pulled in when the proposal touched the small. The three-name rule he had given me in the dark carried more weight now that the vessel was the last Rauth; speaking the blood name in the old way would re-open paths the thirty years had tried to close.

Yara made a single low note under her breath that did not become a verse. It was enough to carry the shape of what was being asked in front of the small witnesses.

The heat under my ribs pushed harder. The Wound-Guest pressed like a second set of lungs trying to speak through mine. It offered the choice clean but showed more now: the debt bowl lifted from this stone and carried down the spur to the bend fires, the blood poured so every small who watched would remember the mark and send the full winter price without being asked twice. The small loop pulled tighter by fear without the body needing to move. The faction that had wanted the meat standing thinner than the road-sworn because the story of the useful thing would travel with their own blood in the bowl. It was learning the crown's own tools and the weight of the small loop at once.

It almost spoke. The iron taste flooded back across my tongue. Sweat started at my hairline despite the wind. Red crept at the edges of my sight.

*This is not waste. Name one. Let the bowl travel to the small. Let the fear bring the baskets before the snow locks the spur. The island will learn what the pit boy keeps when the count is thin.*

I bit down on the old wound from the wrapped-spear lesson and counted the small loop aloud in my head. Reed-Bend. Keva. The girl with the basket. The wrapped spear was against the rack wood at the edge of the light. I did not reach for it yet. I said my name once, under my breath, where only the Guest could hear it.

Strategic. Not the old blind kill. A cut that paid for roads and fear and the small loop at once. And the Guest was filing the tribute idea away for later use, I could feel it turning the images like a man testing a new knife it had not held before the small had asked to be inside the count.

I could feel it wanting me to name the one.

I walked to Half-Ear and the knot instead.

Half-Ear did not lift his head. Keff met my eyes and did not look away. The third one looked at the ground the way Dorrik had after the axle.

"You want the teeth to be fed," I said. "You say the small hold back because they saw the crown choose work over meat at the racks. You say winter does not care for living examples."

Half-Ear's good eye narrowed. The bound side of his face did not move the same. "The crown counts like the boat women now. The pit boy makes his own knot pay the small price while the small send what they choose."

"The small loop is tied," I said. "The road-sworn pay living. The crown does not eat what it counts."

I turned so the whole knot could hear, and the small witnesses, and the ones who had drifted closer from the young fires.

"The teeth that asked for the useful thing will show the price first. Half-Ear. Keff. The one who will not meet eyes. You will give blood from the arm into the debt bowl. Not to feed the fire. To mark what is owed for the proposal. You will stand the most exposed spur with the three road-sworn. Unarmed for the watches until the winter price is walked off in labor and collection. You will carry the debt bowl to the Reed-Bend and any small that watch the roads. You will collect the winter price — measured reed and eel and root, counted in front of the small witnesses — as living payment for what you asked. The bowl will carry the mark of your blood so the small see the crown's teeth are the ones who wanted the useful thing. If the small short the winter price or if you take extra or use teeth, the old rite will be paid by you in front of the small witnesses. This is the useful thing under the crown. The ones who called restraint waste will carry the count that feeds the pass."

Maren's eyes sharpened on the boards — she was already counting what the reassigned shares would cost Salt logistics and what the mark would mean for every future winter tally from the small. Asha watched the faction with the old-blood stillness that said a name spoken in blood by the proposers was heavier than one spoken by the crown alone; her gaze flicked once to the wrapped spear. Yara, who had not sung, made the single low note again, now carrying the shape of teeth turned inward. Naim's hand tightened on the white cloth. The Reed-Bend girl pressed the basket harder to her hip, but she did not look away. The small witnesses saw the proposers become the hand that would collect instead of the one that closed.

The heat did not leave. It changed. The Wound-Guest turned the images. Now it showed the faction carrying the bowl down the spur, thin but alive, bringing back the full winter price because the small had seen what happened to the ones who asked for meat and were made to carry the count instead. It showed the blood in the bowl being carried to the bend fires and poured so that every small who watched would see the mark of the proposers and send before the snow locked the spur. It showed the road-sworn and the new winter-sworn standing the exposed watch together, the small loop pulled by the visible living deterrent rather than by a dead example. It was learning.

That was the cost that did not show on the boards.

Half-Ear laughed once, short and ugly. "The crown makes its teeth count like the boat women. The pit boy turns his own knot into Salt runners."

Keff made a sound in his throat. The third one looked at Dremm's bound ear where it had been wrapped and salted after the racks. None of them spoke again.

Otta came with the knife when I nodded. She cut the shallow lines on their arms into the debt bowl without ceremony, the same way she had cut the road-sworn. The blood marked the wood. Maren took the boards and marked the new winter-sworn shares reassigned to collection duty on the spur and the small. Harvek paid one war-chief share again, his mouth pulled tight. Solek spoke the old Khar words that turned the promise into something that could be eaten if broken. Asha asked for the blood bead from Half-Ear for the genealogies. "If the line wakes wrong later, we will know where the proposal began." Naim witnessed without claiming the small for Hollow Antler. Yara's note carried the shape.

The faction took the mark. They did not cheer or argue loud. They walked away quiet. One muttered to another about Salt law under a Khar crown. The resentment had gone organized. It did not split the knot in the open. It went into the way they would stand the spur and carry the bowl — doing the work because the crown had named it, waiting for the small to short or for the winter price to fail so the old rite could still be asked for in front of witnesses.

The Wound-Guest settled low in the ribs, not gone, but listening. A small, low feeling like a laugh that had nowhere to go yet. It had heard the name I gave the new bound: winter-sworn. It had noted the shape of the winter price traveling with the proposers' blood in the bowl. It had filed the idea that a name could pull stores from the small without the red-state rising if the boy made the ones who wanted meat carry the collection. It turned the images one last time before it sank: the faction standing the exposed spur with the original three, the small sending full baskets because they had seen the crown's teeth were the ones who had proposed the useful thing and were now the hand that collected instead of the one that took. The small loop heavier because the center had made its own knot walk the count.

I walked the spur at night with the wrapped spear. The snow bit. The small loop was heavier than the cord that held it. The island was larger than the camps. Now the teeth that had wanted to close the hand on the small were the ones walking the spur to collect what the count needed. The Guest had learned the shape of a name that brought baskets without the body needing to move. Useful. The part that frightened most was that the useful shape was inside the law I had just made, and the ones who had called restraint waste now carried the price they had asked for.

The wind cut. The boards at the counting stone would show the new winter-sworn shares reassigned in the morning. The debt bowl would travel with the faction when the small sent the next run. The small witnesses had seen the crown turn the proposal without eating what it counted. The faction had seen the crown make its own teeth pay the small price first.

I gripped the wrapped spear until the red thread bit my palm. The iron taste lingered. The red at the edges of my sight took longer to clear in the cold. The Guest was patient. It had noted the winter price and the shape of the proposers carrying it. The small loop was tied. The road-sworn paid living. Now the ones who had wanted meat paid living too. Winter did not care. The crown had to.

The pass would hold or it would not. The small would send or they would not. The faction would collect or they would give the old price in front of the witnesses they had wanted to use. The name traveled with the bowl. The hand that had wanted to close was the one that now carried the count.

I said my name once more under my breath where only the Guest could hear it. Rook. The small loop. The winter-sworn. The winter price. The count holds. The teeth that asked carry it.

The wind cut. The snow bit the stone. The boards waited for the next numbers. The island was larger than the camps, and the crown had made its own knot walk the part of the road that wanted meat.
