# Chapter 6 Draft: Fen Names

Hollow Antler walked me through fog until the sea became a memory.

Their forest had rules different from Salt Veyr's cove. No one counted knots. No one wasted motion on visible trade. They touched trees as they passed. They stepped over roots without looking. They left little strips of bark in branch forks, each marked with cuts I could not read.

The blind old man walked ahead of me.

That seemed wrong until I saw how the others watched his feet. Brannoc did not need eyes because the path had already agreed with him.

Vessa walked behind.

I liked that less.

A person behind you can become many things before you turn.

The cord Maren had tied still hung around my wrist. Hollow Antler had not removed it. They had tied a white bark strip over it, making Salt Veyr debt into something else without asking either of us.

That was power too.

Not cutting a thing. Renaming it.

We climbed until the trees thinned and the ground turned pale under the moss. Bone dust, I thought at first. Then stone. Then bone again. The island liked making one thing look like another.

At a high clearing, antlers hung from every branch.

Some were animal. Some were carved wood. Some were too smooth and yellow to be either.

Wind moved through them. The sound was not music. Music wants you to feel with it. This wanted you to remember what had been done before you were born.

Vessa brought me to a standing stone marked with old red lines. Dried blood or paint. I did not touch it to learn which.

She said something.

Two masked acolytes took my knife.

I let them.

That surprised all of us.

The red heat under my ribs shifted, displeased. But knives matter less when everyone already believes you are dangerous. Let them take the small tooth if it made them forget the jaw.

Vessa saw the thought move through me. Her eyes narrowed.

She began to speak.

Not to me. To the clearing.

Words poured out in a rhythm that made the masked ones answer at fixed places. Call and response. Law learned by mouth until thinking was no longer required. I understood none of the words and all of the danger.

At each answer, an acolyte touched a part of me.

Head.

Throat.

Wound.

Wrist cord.

Blood on my sleeve.

They were building me from pieces.

The heat under my ribs pressed against my skin.

I breathed through my teeth.

Not yet.

Vessa came close enough that I could see pale scars at the corners of her mouth. She had learned not to smile too widely, or someone had taught her with a blade.

She touched the wound under my ribs.

Pain flashed.

The clearing sharpened red.

Her finger came away wet.

She lifted it toward her tongue.

A spear struck the standing stone beside her hand.

Everyone froze.

The spear quivered in the old red lines. Its shaft was reed-wrapped. Its point was dark metal, narrow and leaf-shaped.

Vessa did not look frightened.

That meant she was either brave or had expected this and hated that it came before she was ready.

From the lower fog came people with mud on their faces and reeds in their hair.

Fen-Kin.

I knew that later. Then I knew only that they moved like marsh water: slow until they were not, soft until they closed over your mouth.

The woman leading them was broad-shouldered and gray at the temples. She carried no shield. Only a spear and a necklace of bone beads under her open collar. Each bead had marks cut so small I could barely see them.

She looked at me once.

The look hit harder than Vessa's.

Vessa saw a word written badly.

This woman saw a name she did not want to remember.

"Asha," Brannoc said.

The first name he had spoken where I could understand its edges.

Asha Fen answered him with respect and no obedience.

Then she spoke to Vessa.

The clearing became argument.

Not shouting. Worse. The kind where every word has ancestors standing behind it.

I watched hands.

Vessa's fingers stayed open, ritual-clean.

Asha's thumb rested against the shaft of her spear.

The masked acolytes looked between them and learned fear.

A boy my age stood behind Asha.

No. Not boy, not in the way I was boy. He was clean in the eyes. Fed enough. Trained enough. His hair was braided tight against his skull, and a knife lay flat along his forearm in a hide sheath. He watched me with hatred that had been taught carefully.

I showed him my teeth.

He did not look away.

Interesting.

Asha pointed at my wrist cord, then at my wound, then at the standing stone. Vessa answered with the bark strip and the blood on her finger. Asha said one word that made the Fen-Kin behind her touch their bead necklaces.

The red heat inside me moved toward that word.

Not hungry.

Attentive.

Asha saw me hear it.

Her face changed.

She stepped toward me.

Vessa moved to block her.

The boy behind Asha moved faster.

He was in front of his spear-mother with the forearm knife bare before I had shifted my weight. Good. Very good.

I liked him for half a breath.

Then I hated him because liking is an opening.

Asha put a hand on his shoulder.

"Cairn," she said.

His name.

He did not lower the knife.

Asha came around him and stood before me. She smelled of marsh mud, smoke, and crushed reeds. No perfume of ritual. No fish oil. She reached for my face slowly.

I let her touch my cheek.

I do not know why.

Her fingers were callused. She turned my head toward the weak light and looked at my eyes. Then she pushed hair back from my brow and made a sound low in her throat.

Not pity.

Grief, maybe.

I knew grief less well.

She spoke a phrase. Vessa snapped an answer. Asha ignored her and touched one of the beads at her throat.

Tovek had said blood.

Vessa had said wrong weather.

Asha said something older.

I felt it in my teeth.

Snow under my knees. A hand on the back of my neck. Say the names properly. Names are how the dead stay fed.

This time the memory did not break at once.

I saw a woman's sleeve. Blue-black wool. A white scar across brown fingers. A cup of blood set in snow.

Then the heat under my ribs rose and swallowed it.

No, I thought.

Mine.

The heat laughed softly.

Asha stepped back as if she had heard it.

For the first time since the beach, an adult looked afraid for me instead of afraid of me.

That was worse. Fear for you is a rope. It pulls.

Asha turned on Vessa and spoke with a force that made the antlers knock though no wind had moved.

Brannoc smiled.

Vessa did not.

Cairn leaned close enough for only me to hear and said a word in a language almost like mine.

"Wake-wrong."

Two words, maybe. One meaning.

He touched his own chest, then pointed at mine with the knife.

I understood him.

If I woke wrong, he would open me.

I smiled then because I was tired and hurt and young enough to think defiance could feed me.

Cairn did not smile back.

Good.

At least one person in the clearing knew the shape of his promise.

Asha raised both hands. Her people spread out, not attacking, not yielding. Vessa's acolytes tightened around the standing stone. Brannoc lifted his hollow antler and listened to something below all of us.

The island held its breath.

Then from far off, beyond the fog woods, beyond the marsh paths, beyond even the black uplands, a horn sounded.

Low.

Long.

The masked acolytes flinched.

Asha closed her eyes.

Vessa's mouth became a thin white line.

Cairn whispered a word I did not know.

The red heat under my ribs knew it.

Khar.

That was how I learned the sixth law of the island.

Some names do not enter a room.

They arrive, and the room becomes theirs.
