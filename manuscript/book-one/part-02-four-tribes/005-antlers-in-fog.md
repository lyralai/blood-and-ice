# Chapter 5 Draft: Antlers In Fog

Maren tried to move me before sunset.

That told me she was afraid.

Salt Veyr did not hurry unless waiting cost more than motion. Within the hour, two boats had been pulled above the tide line and turned into walls. Fish racks came down. Children carried bundles. Old people tied knots into cords and tucked them under their shirts. No one said panic. They only made themselves ready faster than panic could name them.

Tovek stayed near me.

He had decided he was my bridge whether I wanted crossing or not.

"Hollow," he said, making antlers with his fingers.

I looked toward the trees.

He shook his head and pointed to his ear.

Listen.

At first I heard only camp work and sea. Then the wind changed.

Bone against bone.

Not the cliff pole. More than one. Many. A dry knocking in the fog above the cove.

The camp stopped pretending not to hear.

Maren cursed.

Even without knowing the words, I admired the shape of it. Short. Useful. Not afraid enough to waste breath.

The fog came down in strips through the black trees. Figures walked inside it. Slow. White bark masks. Antler frames. Spears held upright, points wrapped in cloth instead of bare.

That made them worse.

A naked spear only promises one thing. A covered spear may promise law.

The masked prisoner beside the smoke pit began to make noise through his bindings. One of the Salt Veyr men hit him. Maren stopped the second blow.

She wanted him alive.

So did the people coming from the fog.

There were nine of them. The one in front wore no mask.

She was tall and narrow, with hair the color of ash bark braided down the front of her chest. Antlers rose behind her head from a frame strapped to her shoulders. Small bones hung from the tines. Not finger bones. Teeth. They clicked when she moved.

Her face had no paint. That made it feel more naked than the masks.

The camp bent around her arrival. No one kneeled. No one smiled. People made space as if she were fire carried in a bowl.

Vessa, I learned later.

Vessa of the Hollow Antler.

She spoke to Maren first. Not loudly. Never loudly. Her voice made people lean toward it, and I hated her before I knew why.

Maren answered with her chin high and one shoulder bleeding through new stitches.

Tovek whispered near my ear. "Vessa."

He tapped his throat, then the air.

Speaker.

Vessa's eyes moved to me.

I have been looked at many ways. As meat. As nuisance. As tool. As danger. Vessa looked at me as if I were a word written badly on skin.

She came closer.

Senn stepped half in front of me.

I did not understand that.

Neither did he, by the look on his face.

Vessa noticed and smiled without warmth.

She spoke one phrase.

Tovek did not translate.

Maren snapped at him.

He swallowed. "Blood," he said in my language, pointing at me. Then, after a pause, "wrong weather."

Vessa reached toward my wound.

I showed the knife.

Every covered spear lowered one handspan.

Vessa did not blink.

She said something to Maren. Maren spat at the ground. Tovek's mouth tightened.

"Law," he told me.

I knew law. Law is what strong people call the shape of their hand before it closes.

Vessa turned and pointed to the masked prisoner. Her people untied him. Salt Veyr spears came up. Hollow Antler spears came down. For a breath the cove held itself between killing and not.

Maren lifted one hand.

Salt Veyr stopped.

Vessa touched the ruined eye of the prisoner with two fingers. He cried out. She listened to his pain as if it were testimony. Then she touched the blood on his cheek and drew a red line across her own palm.

The Hollow Antler behind her began to hum.

Low. Wordless. The kind of sound that can become a cage if enough people agree to stand around you making it.

The red heat under my ribs stirred.

I pressed my hand flat against the cord on my wrist.

No.

It pressed back.

Vessa heard something in me. I saw it. Not with ears. With the little change around her eyes.

She pointed at me, then at the prisoner, then at the hanging fog.

Tovek's voice went rough. "You go."

"No," I said.

It was a small word. It did not do much.

Vessa smiled then.

Not because she understood the word. Because she understood refusal.

She said a longer thing. Tovek translated piece by piece, hunting for words I had.

"You... blood-cut. Boundary-broke. Witnessed by branch. Taken for naming."

"No," I said again.

Maren spoke sharply. She lifted the cord at my wrist and shook it once.

Debt.

Mine, her gesture said.

Vessa answered without looking away from me.

Tovek did not translate until Maren made him.

"Debt eats after law," he said.

I almost laughed.

Not because it was funny.

Because there it was. The island speaking clearly at last.

Hunger starts every bargain. Debt decides whether it ends. Law eats after.

Something always eats.

Senn muttered and stepped closer. Maren caught his sleeve. He shook her off before remembering she was Maren and he was not.

Vessa saw that too.

She saw everything useful.

Then another voice spoke from beyond the camp.

Older. Male. Cracked like dry wood.

An old man came from the fog carrying an antler in both hands. He had no mask and no eyes. The sockets were closed with scar tissue. The antler he held had been carved hollow until it was almost lace.

The camp changed more for him than for Vessa.

Old Brannoc.

He lifted the antler toward me. Wind moved through its holes though the air in the cove was still. It made a sound like a throat trying to remember water.

The red heat under my ribs went silent.

Dead silent.

I hated that most of all.

Old Brannoc spoke one sentence.

Vessa's face hardened.

Tovek breathed out through his nose. "Pit," he said.

Maren's hand closed around the cord on my wrist.

Too late.

I knew it by the way every adult stopped deciding and started pretending the decision had been made by something older than them.

Vessa raised her hand.

The covered spear points uncovered.

Maren looked at her fish racks. Her boats. Her children. Her wounded shoulder. Senn. Me.

Then she let go of the cord.

I could have fought.

The heat wanted it.

But I had learned enough to know when a blade would only make the cage smaller.

Hollow Antler took me into the fog.

Senn called something after us. His voice broke on the wound I had given him.

I did not turn.

If I had, I might have mistaken that sound for friendship.

That was how I learned the fifth law of the island.

People who cannot afford to save you will call it respect when they let you go.
