# Chapter 4 Draft: Salt Knots

Salt Veyr did not carry the dead first.

They carried the useful.

Maren tied her own shoulder with one hand and made Senn bind the masked hunter whose eye I had ruined. The other two were dragged by their ankles to the edge of the clearing. One breathed. One did not. The dead one was searched with care, not grief.

Knife. Cord. Bark tokens. A little pouch of white ash. Three finger bones drilled for string.

Maren took the ash and bones. Senn took the knife. He looked at me while he tucked it into his belt, daring me to care.

I did care.

I cared enough not to show it.

The hunter with the ruined eye wept behind his mask. No one comforted him. Maren cut the antler twigs from his hood and threw them into the mud. That made him struggle harder than the binding had.

So the twigs mattered.

Good.

People tell you where to hurt them by what they cannot bear to lose.

We returned to the cove slower than we had left. Senn walked close to me now. Not friendly. Not trusting. Close the way a man keeps a hot coal in sight after it spits from the fire.

At camp, everything stopped.

Not for long. Salt Veyr hated wasted motion. But the first look came from everyone: children, old men, fish women, net boys, boat patchers. They saw Maren bleeding. Saw Senn bleeding. Saw the bound masked man. Saw my mouth red.

Then they looked at Maren for the story.

Power is when people wait for your version before believing their eyes.

Maren gave orders.

The camp moved.

The prisoner was staked beside the smoke pit. His mask stayed on, though one eye hole had gone dark and wet. A woman packed Maren's shoulder. Senn was sat on a stone and scolded by three people at once. I stood where no one had told me to stand and held still.

Holding still becomes a kind of weapon if people expect you to leap.

Tovek Reed-Eye came then.

He was thin in a way that looked chosen. His hair was bound in seal cord, and one eye was milky blue. The other missed nothing. He wore more knots than Maren and fewer knives than a fool would.

He walked around me once.

I turned with him.

He laughed softly and said a word.

I knew mockery even without language.

Maren snapped at him. He answered. They spoke quickly. Senn tried to speak too, but the wound in his throat made him cough. Tovek listened to the cough as if it were also a language.

Then he faced me and touched his chest.

"Tovek."

I did not give my name. Maren already had it. Names spread faster than fire here.

He grinned, showing one black tooth, and pointed at the masked prisoner.

"Antler."

He curled both hands above his head like horns.

Then he pointed at the white bark token and drew a line across his own throat.

Boundary.

Warning.

Claim.

He pointed at me, then at the blood on my mouth, then at the prisoner. His brows lifted.

Question.

Did I bite him because he was enemy? Did I bite him because I was beast? Did I bite him because something in me knew his blood?

I did not know which answer would keep me fed.

So I gave him the answer that was true enough to use.

I pointed at Senn.

Then I pointed at the masked prisoner.

Then I showed my teeth.

Tovek's grin faded.

Good.

Let them think I had chosen. Choice is safer than hunger. People bargain with choice.

Maren watched from her stone while the wound woman stitched her. She said something to Tovek. He argued. She said one word.

The camp heard it.

All the little movements changed. The fish women went back to cutting, but slower. The old man with no legs whispered to a boy, who ran toward the boats. Senn stared at Maren as if she had struck him again.

Tovek sighed and turned to me.

He touched his chest. "Tovek."

Touched Maren. "Maren."

Touched Senn. "Senn."

Touched the masked prisoner and made the antler sign. "Hollow."

Then he touched a knot at Maren's belt and pointed at me.

"Debt."

That word he said in my language.

Badly. But close enough.

I went cold in a way that had nothing to do with wind.

He knew one of my words.

That meant my kind had been here before, or his kind had gone where my words lived, or words could cross water without bodies. None of those were safe.

Tovek saw that he had found a hook.

"Debt," he repeated, touching Senn's throat. Then Maren's shoulder. Then me.

I understood enough.

I had hurt Senn. I had saved him. I had drawn trouble. I had fought trouble. All of it could become one knot if Maren tied it right.

Maren stood. The stitching was not finished. The wound woman cursed her, but quietly.

The old boatmistress came to me with a new cord in her hand. Undyed. Clean. She held it where I could see.

She tied one knot.

Pointed at Senn.

Second knot.

Pointed at herself.

Third knot.

Pointed at the masked prisoner.

Fourth knot.

Pointed inland, where fog gathered in the trees.

Then she put the cord around my wrist.

Not tight. That made it worse.

A shackle that pretends to be loose is still teaching you where the door is.

I lifted my knife.

Every spear in camp moved.

Maren did not.

She tapped the cord on my wrist. Then she tapped the food racks. Then the boats. Then her own chest.

Protection.

Price.

I lowered the knife.

The red heat under my ribs disliked that. It wanted the sudden answer. Cut cord. Cut throat. Run.

But I had seen the camp. I had seen the boats and the nets and the children tied to mothers by sleep-cords. I had seen how many hands turned one fish into winter.

Running would make me free until hunger found me.

Staying would make me owned until I learned the owner.

I let the cord sit on my wrist.

Tovek said something that made Maren snort. Then he crouched and drew four marks in the mud.

One like a wave.

One like antlers.

One like reeds.

One like teeth.

He tapped the wave and pointed at the camp.

Salt.

Tapped the antlers and pointed at the prisoner.

Hollow.

Tapped the reeds and pointed inland, lower, toward wet ground.

Fen.

Tapped the teeth and pointed beyond the ridge where the black uplands cut the sky.

Khar.

At the last mark the camp became quieter.

There are names people say like tools.

There are names people say like wounds.

Khar was the second kind.

The masked prisoner began to laugh.

It sounded awful with his ruined eye and bound mouth. Wet. Broken. Pleased.

Tovek slapped him silent.

Too late.

The laugh had already gone through the camp.

Maren looked at me, then at the black uplands.

I knew that look. It was the look of a person watching weather change far away and counting how many boats could reach shore before it arrived.

She touched the cord on my wrist once more.

Not protection now.

Warning.

That was how I learned the fourth law of the island.

A debt can keep you alive until someone richer buys it.
