# Chapter 15 Draft: Fen Challenge

Asha did not send a message with bark or cord.

She sent Cairn.

He came at dusk, walking out of the reeds with both hands empty and a knife visible at his hip because Fen-Kin courtesy was not the same as foolishness. Water shone on his boots. Mist followed him like a dog that had learned silence. Khar Vane children saw him first and scattered toward the fires, laughing because they were afraid and did not want the fear to be named.

Dremm reached for his axe.

Solek said, "No."

Dremm's hand stayed on the haft. "He comes into our teeth."

"With hands empty."

"Fen hands are never empty."

Cairn heard that. He smiled without showing teeth.

I had been sitting on Harvek's flat stone because Harvek had told me not to. That was not as brave as it sounds. If I sat there, everyone saw the insult and waited for the chief to answer. If Harvek answered too hard, he admitted the stone mattered more than the boy. If he did not answer, he admitted I could take space near him. I did not understand all of that when I sat. I only knew I was tired of being placed.

Harvek had looked at me once.

Then he had smiled.

That was worse than anger.

Cairn stopped at the edge of the central fire. He looked at the stone under me and then at Harvek. "Your guest grows teeth."

"Guests bite when underfed," Harvek said.

Maren, who had stayed because taboo made every road expensive, snorted into her cup.

Cairn's eyes moved to me.

We had fought once in the trees, if a thing can be called fighting when neither side knows what the other is yet. He had watched me bleed red-wrong and keep moving. I had watched him hold fear in the same place most boys kept pride. That made him dangerous. Proud boys can be dragged by their pride. Cairn held his like a tool and put it away when it did not cut.

"Asha asks whether the pit left a boy," he said.

Tovek translated for those who needed it. I did not need all of it. I understood enough.

Dremm barked, "The pit left more."

Cairn did not look at him. That was a skill too.

"Asha asks," he said again, "whether Rook can answer without the thing answering first."

The camp went quiet in the wrong way.

The Wound-Guest stirred under my ribs. It liked being discussed by enemies. It liked challenge most of all. Challenge is an open door for hunger with witnesses.

I stood.

Pain came with me. Less than before. That frightened me. Healing too fast feels like theft when you do not know who will collect.

"How?"

Cairn reached to his belt slowly and drew the knife. Every Khar Vane weapon came half-free.

He reversed it and laid it on the ground.

"No iron," he said. "No teeth. No song. Old-blood test."

Solek's face hardened. "Fen games have Fen judges."

"Then send me away and answer Vessa with axes."

That landed. I saw it strike Harvek, Maren, Solek, even Dremm though he pretended not to feel it. Hollow Antler had made a wall of mouths. Fen-Kin had brought a door small enough for pride to fit through.

Harvek gestured.

A ring opened near the fire.

Cairn stripped off his outer hide and stood in a sleeveless tunic marked with green-black stitching. Scars ran over his arms in thin pale lines, not the blunt trophies Khar Vane favored. These were training scars. Measured. Repeated. A person had hurt him carefully for years and called it love.

I removed nothing. I had little enough.

Otta came close and hissed, "Your rib."

"I know where it is."

"So will he."

She bound me anyway, tighter, angry hands making mercy.

The rules were simple because old things often are. Stay inside the ring. No weapons. No biting. No calling the red thing. First boy to touch both knees and one hand to earth yields. If blood came, blood came, but blood was not the point.

"What is the point?" I asked.

Cairn heard and answered before Tovek could. "To see who is holding the body."

Then he moved.

I had fought hunger, cold, grown men, dogs, sea, rope, fear. I had not fought someone who had spent his life learning how a shoulder lies before a fist.

Cairn did not rush. He cut angles without cutting skin. He touched my bad side with two fingers and was gone before rage arrived. He made my feet answer questions too quickly. Every time I tried to turn pain into speed, he let me spend it and kept his own.

Dremm shouted for me to break him.

Yara did not sing.

That helped more than shouting.

Cairn struck my ribs with the heel of his hand.

White filled me.

The Wound-Guest rose through it, delighted.

No.

Cairn's next strike came toward my throat. The red thing knew nine ways to take his wrist and open the soft place under his jaw. It gave them to me like gifts. I saw them bright and perfect. I wanted them.

I dropped instead.

Not from weakness. By choice.

His hand passed over me. I caught his knee, not to break, only to pull. He turned with it, good, too good, but my shoulder hit his hip and the two of us went down together.

Pain cracked open behind my eyes.

The Wound-Guest surged.

I put my own hand on the ground.

One knee.

Second knee.

My hand.

Yield.

The camp made a sound like a blade pulled halfway out.

Cairn froze above me. He had not won. I had given him the shape of winning before the red thing could make a different story.

Dremm stared as if I had spat out good meat.

Harvek's eyes narrowed.

Solek breathed once, almost a laugh.

Cairn stood and offered me a hand. I looked at it. Taking it would make one meaning. Refusing it would make another. I was so tired of meanings I wanted to bite both.

I took the hand.

He pulled me up.

Under cover of the movement, he spoke too low for the ring.

"There is a boy."

I hated how much those words hurt.

"Tell Asha," I said.

His fingers tightened once around my wrist. "I will tell her there is a boy. I will also tell her the boy is losing."

Then he let me go.

Dremm left before the fire settled. He did not stomp. That would have been easier. He walked away quiet, which meant the wound had gone deeper than pride.

Yara watched him leave, then looked at me with the face of someone hearing three songs fight over one ending.

That night the Wound-Guest did not dream of killing Cairn.

It dreamed of the moment I yielded.

It studied the shape of it.

That was the first time I wondered whether restraint taught the monster too.
