# Chapter 13 Draft: Salt Debt

Maren came at midday and made Khar Vane count fish.

That was braver than bringing spears.

Spears would have let them answer with spears. Fish made them answer with winter.

Salt Veyr arrived in a narrow line along the lower path: six adults, two sledges, one boy with a wrapped throat, and Tovek Reed-Eye walking as if he had sold the path to both sides and expected payment twice. The sledges carried sealed baskets. Dried fish. Salt cakes. Lamp fat. Cord. Needles. The smell hit the Khar Vane camp before the people did.

Children came first.

Not close. Khar Vane children knew hands could be lost for reaching wrong. But they looked. Hunger leaned their bodies forward.

Maren saw that.

Of course she did.

Harvek came to meet her by the outer fire. Dremm stood behind him with his broken nose packed in dark moss. Solek stood beside him. I sat on a stone because Otta had threatened to tie me down if I walked before my rib set. Yara sat near me, which meant everyone who looked at me also remembered there was a song listening.

Maren looked at the whole arrangement and snorted.

It was the first honest sound I had heard all morning.

Tovek translated before anyone asked.

"Salt-Hand says miracles eat too."

Some Khar Vane warriors growled.

Maren opened the first basket.

The growling stopped.

Food is a stronger argument than pride when the wind is cold.

Harvek spoke. Tovek translated in pieces for me, either out of duty or because he enjoyed owning my understanding.

"Bone-Mouth says Khar Vane does not beg for shore scraps."

Maren answered before Tovek finished.

"Salt-Hand says she did not hear begging. She heard boys singing a new hunger up the hill and came to learn how many mouths the song had."

Tovek smiled while translating. He liked dangerous language best.

Harvek's scar-mouth widened.

He liked it too.

That was the trouble with strong people. They admired each other while everyone else counted the cost.

Maren pointed at me.

The camp followed her finger.

I disliked that enough to stand.

My rib clicked. The Wound-Guest opened one red eye inside me. I sat back down before it mistook pride for need.

Maren saw. Her face did not change, but her hand moved once against the knot cord at her belt.

Counting weakness.

Counting mine.

She came to me without asking Harvek. Two Khar Vane stepped in her path. She looked at them the way she looked at rotten rope.

"Move," she said in trade-tongue.

They did not know the word. They knew the meaning.

Harvek let her pass.

Maren crouched before me and took my wrist. Salt Veyr cord still there, dirty now under bark strip, reed fiber, and bone ring. Claims layered until my arm looked like a tree where hunters had marked territory.

She turned the cord, inspected the knots, and tightened one.

"Debt," she said.

"I know."

Her eyes sharpened because those were two words she had not taught me.

"You learn quick."

"Or die."

Tovek laughed behind her. Maren did not.

She pointed to the baskets. "Fish. Salt. Fat. Cord."

Then to the camp.

"Mouths."

Then to me.

"You."

I understood the shape. Khar Vane had claimed me. Claiming me had made their camp larger without adding food. Warriors had come from outer hunting bands before dawn to see the pit-returned boy. More would come. Songs draw mouths faster than nets draw fish.

Maren touched the cord again.

"Your blood makes debt."

"I did not ask them."

"Blood does not ask."

That was unfair enough to be true.

Harvek came closer. "Salt-Hand sells?"

Maren stood. "Salt-Hand bargains."

The difference mattered to her. Maybe to him. Not to hunger.

She named her price in knots, gestures, and Tovek's careful trade words.

Safe path for Salt Veyr boats past the black upland coves.

Return of two net-menders taken in last spring's raid, if alive.

No Khar Vane taking from shore racks without marked debt.

One hostage of rank while food moved.

And me present, hearing it.

That last part made Harvek look at her with new interest.

"Boy hears little," he said.

"Boy learns fast," Maren answered.

Solek's ash-marked mouth twitched.

Dremm muttered something. Several warriors answered. I did not need Tovek to know they disliked trade words wrapped around warrior throats.

Harvek listened until the muttering became one breath from disrespect.

Then he turned.

The muttering died.

Chief, I thought again.

Not because he could kill. Many could kill. Because his people killed the noise inside themselves when he looked.

Harvek spoke to me.

"You want men?"

The question confused me enough that I answered truthfully.

"No."

That made Tovek laugh harder.

Harvek did not.

He pointed at Dremm, at the watching warriors, at children by the baskets, at Yara. "They come."

I said nothing.

"You send away?"

I looked at Dremm. Broken nose, bright eyes, knife at belt, worse loyalty growing in him like mold in wet bread. I looked at the children smelling fish. At the old woman with one arm who had ladled broth. At Solek, who wanted law to remain heavier than song. At Yara, who would remember my answer better than I said it.

Send them away?

I did not have a place to send myself.

Maren saved me from answering because saving someone from one trap is a good way to drive him into yours.

"If they come," she said, "they eat. If they eat, someone fishes. If someone fishes, someone rows. If someone rows, Salt Veyr decides whether the boat comes back."

Tovek translated into Khar Vane with relish.

This time the growl became real.

Dremm stepped forward. "We take boats."

Maren looked at him.

"You can take a boat," she said. "Can you mend one?"

He flushed under his bruises.

"Can you salt twenty baskets before rot? Can you read tide under black fog? Can you keep children from eating winter in autumn because a song made them brave?"

No translation was needed for every word. Her voice did work by itself.

Dremm looked at me, as if I should save him from being made small.

I understood him then in a way I did not want.

He did not love the Wound-Guest only because it was violent. He loved it because it made the world simple. Bleed and win. Kneel and rise. Kill and become true.

Maren was showing him a world where someone could defeat him with fish counts.

The Wound-Guest hated that world too.

It shifted in me, bored and irritated.

I forced myself to look at the baskets.

"What hostage?" I asked.

Maren turned.

Harvek watched.

Tovek's grin faded a little. He had not expected me to step into the bargain.

Good.

Maren pointed at Dremm.

The camp stiffened.

Dremm's hand went to his knife.

Harvek did not move.

"No," I said.

Maren raised one brow.

I pointed at Solek.

That made everyone talk at once.

Solek himself went very still.

I did not choose him because I liked him. I chose him because Dremm as hostage would turn Salt Veyr camp into a challenge circle before sunset. Solek would hate the insult, obey the law, and learn what Maren counted. Also, Khar Vane would notice if their law-speaker did not return. That made him valuable alive.

I did not have all those words.

I had a body sense of pressure and a memory of ropes.

Harvek understood enough.

His smile was slow.

Maren looked at me as if I had stolen another knife.

"The boy starts counting," she said.

I did not know whether it was praise or warning.

The bargain took the rest of the day. Two baskets opened. Three stayed sealed. Knots were tied. Solek gave a strip of ash-marked hide as pledge. Maren gave a salt cord. Harvek cut his palm and pressed blood into both, which made Salt Veyr uncomfortable and Khar Vane satisfied.

At sunset, the first ration was cooked.

Seal broth, dried fish, bitter greens, no hidden rites. Solek said the law over it before eating. Dremm glared at the bowl as if trade had fouled it.

I ate slowly.

Maren watched me from across the fire.

Yara watched both of us.

I realized then that something had changed without any weapon drawn.

That morning, Khar Vane had claimed me as their proof.

By night, Salt Veyr had a knot in the proof, food in the camp, and their hands around the road by which my followers would survive.

No one had asked what I wanted.

But for the first time, I had changed the shape of the cage from inside.

That was how I learned the thirteenth law of the island.

Fear moves feet. Hunger keeps accounts.
