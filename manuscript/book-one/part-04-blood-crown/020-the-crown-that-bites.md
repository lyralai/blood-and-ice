# Chapter 20 Draft: The Crown That Bites

Dremm came back at midnight.

No axe. No dead boy. No right ear.

He walked into camp through the lower fires while the whole tribe pretended not to watch. Blood had dried black along his neck. Someone had cut the ear cleanly. Hollow Antler, then. Khar Vane would have torn it.

He stopped before Harvek's stone.

Then he knelt to me.

I hated him for that more than for disobeying.

"Don't," I said.

He put his forehead to the dirt.

The camp inhaled.

Yara closed her eyes.

Harvek looked older in the firelight. Solek looked unsurprised, which meant he had feared this shape already.

Dremm spoke into the ground. "I carried my death. They took ear for hearing false. Vessa says next they take tongue if I speak your law."

"Then do not speak my law."

He lifted his head. His eyes were fever-bright. "It is already spoken."

That was the trouble with mercy. People can turn it into banner faster than cruelty, because cruelty is common and mercy asks explanation. Explanation becomes doctrine if enough frightened mouths need it.

By dawn the camp had named the ridge fight Blood Snow. By midday they had named my half-red restraint as proof that the Wound-Guest could be bridled. By evening three young warriors had cut shallow lines across their own ribs and packed them with ash, claiming they would hold hunger as I did.

Solek beat two of them with a staff.

That did not stop the third.

Harvek called council under hard rain.

No central fire. Rain killed ceremony and left only wet people with wet tempers. Sometimes that is better. Fire makes every face important.

Maren came wrapped in oilskin, furious about mud on her boots and pleased that Salt carts had crossed. Asha came with Cairn, who would not meet my eyes after Blood Snow. Vessa did not come, but her absence sat among us like a white stone.

Harvek spoke first.

"Khar Vane wants crown."

I thought I had misheard.

Then the camp answered from beyond the council: a low sound, not cheer, not chant. Agreement made animal.

"No," I said.

Maren rubbed both hands over her face. "That was fast."

Solek said, "It began at the pit. We are late naming it."

"No," I said again.

Harvek ignored me, which chiefs do when a boy's no does not yet weigh enough. "Not chief. Not my stone. War-crown. Blood-crown. A named place in Khar law. Holder leads rites of answer when tribe is under stain."

"That sounds like chief with fewer chores."

Maren smiled despite herself.

Harvek did not. "It is leash and weapon."

"I know."

"Good."

Asha's shell beads clicked softly as she shifted. "If Khar Vane crowns him, Fen-Kin cannot call this contained."

Solek replied, "If Khar Vane does not crown what it already follows, it follows without law."

That was worse. I saw it at once and wished I did not. A hunger named can be limited. A hunger denied grows private teeth.

Vessa had wanted containment. Khar Vane wanted permission. Salt wanted roads. Fen wanted answerability. Harvek wanted a weapon pointed outward and bound inward. Everyone had a use for the shape of me.

"What do I get?" I asked.

The rain made the silence larger.

Maren looked at me with new approval. Asha too, though hers was sadder.

Harvek's mouth curved. "Now you ask chief question."

"What do I get?"

"Law around the hunger."

"Not enough."

"Voice in war."

"Already taking it."

"Protection."

I laughed. Could not help it.

Harvek waited.

I looked at Solek. "If there is crown, there are terms."

"There must be."

"The Wound-Guest is not king."

Yara, somewhere behind me, made a tiny sound.

I turned so the watchers beyond council could hear.

"The thing from the pit does not command. It does not eat by right. It does not answer challenge. It does not make law. If I go red and do not return when called by name, bind me. If binding fails, kill me before you follow it."

Rain struck hide, hair, stone.

Dremm stood near the outer ring, one side of his head bandaged. He looked as if I had cut the other ear.

Harvek said, "Hard term."

"Yes."

"Who calls name?"

That mattered. I hated that it mattered.

Not Harvek. Too much use. Not Yara. Too much song. Not Maren. Too much price. Not Dremm. Too much love. Not Vessa. Never Vessa.

I looked at Asha.

She did not blink.

"Fen-Kin," I said. "Old blood calls old blood. Asha or one she names."

Cairn's head lifted.

Asha's face did not change, but the beads at her cloak moved though she stood still.

"Fen-Kin does not leash Khar Vane weapons," she said.

"Then do not crown me."

Solek looked at Harvek. Harvek looked at the camp. The camp waited, hungry for an answer that would let hunger feel clean.

Maren said, "Salt Veyr will witness terms if roads stay open."

Of course she would. A bargain that made everyone dependent on everyone else was a trader's favorite kind of knot.

Harvek stepped close.

"You understand crown bites wearer first?"

"Everything here bites."

He barked one laugh. "True."

The rite happened in rain because waiting for clear sky would have given fear time to grow speeches.

Solek brought the crown from the law racks. It was not gold. It was not even a circle. It was a brow-band of blackened bone plates tied with red cord, each plate carved with an old hunger law. It smelled of smoke, ash, and hands.

Yara began to sing.

"No," I said.

Her voice stopped on one note.

"Words after," I said. "Not during."

That angered her. Good. Songs should sometimes wait outside the door.

Solek stood before me with the bone band. "Name terms."

So I named them in trade-tongue, and Tovek carried them, and Solek reshaped them into Khar law while Asha listened for traps and Maren listened for cost.

The Wound-Guest is not king.

The blood-crowned answers to law before appetite.

The blood-crowned cannot call feast, raid, or sacrifice alone.

If the blood-crowned goes red and does not answer his name, Fen old-blood may call him back.

If he does not return, Khar Vane must bind or kill before following.

Every term made the camp unhappier.

Every term made the crown more possible.

At last Solek placed the bone against my brow.

It was cold.

Then warm.

Then too warm.

The Wound-Guest rose like something hearing a door open.

No, I told it.

Not king.

It laughed without sound.

Dremm knelt first. Of course he did. Then Otta, which mattered more. Then three warriors. Then an old man. Then children who did not understand and therefore understood too much. Harvek did not kneel. Solek did not. Asha did not. Maren definitely did not.

Good.

Let some people stay standing. A boy needs witnesses taller than worship.

Yara sang after.

Not during.

Her song made the rain listen. It named Blood Snow, the second pit, the feast bowl, the ear taken for false hearing, the boy who told his own followers to kill the thing they wanted to praise.

I hated the beauty of it.

That night, after the crown was taken off and locked beside the law racks, I went alone to the edge of camp. The island breathed fog. Somewhere in Hollow Antler woods, Vessa would be learning that Khar Vane had put law around the breach instead of surrendering it. Somewhere in Fen marsh, Asha would be deciding whether she had saved a boy or tied herself to a catastrophe. Somewhere by Salt water, Maren would be counting profit and graves with the same hand.

Under my ribs, the Wound-Guest waited.

Stronger.

Quieter.

Learning law the way fire learns walls.

I had thought the White Pit was the place meant to swallow me.

I was wrong.

The first pit is only the one with sides you can see.
