# Chapter 12 Draft: The First Restraint

In the morning, Khar Vane brought me a prisoner.

Not to kill.

Not at first.

They brought him with his hands tied behind his back and a rope around his neck. He was Hollow Antler, young enough for his beard to still grow in patches. One side of his face was purple from a fist. His antler frame had been stripped away, which seemed to shame him more than the rope.

Dremm dragged him into the fire circle and threw him at my feet.

I was sitting because standing made my rib click and the click made the Wound-Guest attentive. Harvek had ordered a woman named Otta to wrap my chest tighter. She had done it with no pity, which I preferred, and had called me bone-stupid in Khar Vane when I tried not to breathe.

The prisoner landed hard.

Every warrior looked at me.

I knew a lesson when it wore rope.

Dremm put a knife into the dirt between us. Bone handle. Black blade. Good balance. He had chosen it with care. That was how I knew the knife mattered less than the choosing.

"Antler took you," he said in trade-tongue. "Antler put you below."

The prisoner stared at the ground.

I looked for Harvek.

He stood by the smoke rack with his arms folded. Solek sat beside him, thin hands tucked into his sleeves. Yara leaned against a post, listening to a song not yet made. None of them stopped Dremm.

So this was allowed.

Or allowed to see what I would do.

Dremm crouched. His eyes were bright. "Blood for blood."

The fire popped.

The knife waited.

My hand wanted it.

Not because of the prisoner. I did not know him. Maybe he had helped lower me. Maybe he had only stood in fog and answered Vessa's words because children raised under law answer before they think. I did not care enough about him to want him dead.

But I wanted the knife because everyone expected my hand to close around it.

Expectation is a path through snow. Easy to follow. Hard to leave without making everyone see where you go.

The Wound-Guest pressed warmly against my ribs.

It did not command.

That was worse.

It trusted me to know.

I picked up the knife.

The camp breathed in.

The prisoner shut his eyes.

I hated him for that. Eyes closed ask another person to become the whole world. I had been made into enough worlds for other people.

"Look," I said.

He did not understand.

I kicked his knee.

His eyes opened.

Dremm laughed. "Good."

No, I thought.

The laugh went through me like a hook. I saw myself from outside for one breath: small, wrapped in bandages, blood still dried in the cracks of my hands, holding a knife over a bound man while warriors waited to love whatever I did.

If I killed him, Dremm would get a story.

If I spared him, someone else would.

There was no clean ground. Only ground I chose to dirty myself.

The prisoner whispered something.

Not in trade-tongue. Hollow Antler words. A prayer, maybe. A name. His mother's. Vessa's. The island's.

Solek's head tilted.

He knew the words. Harvek saw Solek hear them. Yara did too.

The Wound-Guest liked the prisoner's fear. It rose through me with sweet patience, showing me how easy the cut would be. Under jaw. Across wrist. Into belly and upward. Let the camp see. Let the camp know.

I put the knife against the prisoner's throat.

His pulse hit the black edge.

Mine, the heat said without words.

No.

I moved the blade.

Not away.

Down.

I cut the rope around his neck.

For a breath, no one understood what had happened. The rope fell loose. The prisoner's mouth opened. Dremm's smile stayed on his face but forgot what shape it was.

Then voices erupted.

Khar Vane did not like confusion. They liked blood, law, challenge, answer. I had given them a thing between those.

Dremm stood so fast his knee struck the prisoner. "No."

There was that small word again. Easier to say than make true.

He grabbed the prisoner's hair and yanked his head back. "Antler debt."

I rose.

Pain blackened the edge of the world. I stayed up because sitting would have given the moment away.

"Mine," I said.

That word the camp understood.

The Wound-Guest warmed around it.

Careful, I thought, but maybe to myself.

Dremm's face changed. Not anger first. Hurt. That made him more dangerous. A boy can survive anger. Hurt looks for a god to make it clean.

"Yours to kill," he said.

"Mine," I repeated.

I put the knife point against the prisoner's bound hands and cut the cord.

This time everyone understood.

Dremm struck me.

He should not have.

His fist caught my cheek, not hard enough to break anything, hard enough to open the split the sea had given me. Blood ran hot into my mouth. The camp moved. Harvek did not. Solek did not. Yara's eyes sharpened.

The Wound-Guest came up laughing.

It had waited for a door, and Dremm had opened one with his fist.

My ribs stopped hurting.

The fire slowed.

Dremm's throat showed itself: pulse, scar edge, place where teeth could close.

I stepped inside his reach.

He was bigger. Stronger. Whole. None of that mattered in the red narrowing. I drove my forehead into his nose. It broke with a soft crack. His hands came up. I caught one finger and bent it until he dropped to one knee.

The camp shouted approval.

That fed the heat.

Again, it urged.

Dremm looked up at me through tears and blood, and he was smiling.

Smiling.

He wanted me to become it.

That brought me back more sharply than fear.

The Wound-Guest could use enemies. It could use pain. It could use threat. But a follower who offered himself as a door was a different kind of trap.

I saw Dremm old, maybe. Or dead. Or wearing my hunger as permission while boys behind him learned how to love their worst selves.

I released his finger.

He swayed.

I put the knife in his hand.

His eyes widened.

"Cut," I said.

He looked from the knife to me.

The camp went quiet.

I touched my own chest, then pointed at the freed prisoner, who had crawled three body-lengths away and stopped because every weapon in camp had found him.

"I said mine."

My trade-tongue was poor. My meaning was not.

Solek stood.

That changed the air.

He came forward and took the loose neck rope from the dirt. He held it up so all could see the clean cut I had made. Then he spoke, dry and long, in Khar Vane. His words were law-words. I heard the shape even where meaning failed.

He pointed at me.

At the prisoner.

At Dremm's bloody face.

At Harvek.

The camp answered reluctantly.

Dremm answered last.

Harvek smiled then, but not like at the pit. Smaller. More private. More dangerous.

Yara began to hum.

Not triumph.

Interest.

The prisoner was taken away, still alive. Not free. Khar Vane mercy was not a door open to daylight. It was a smaller cage with cleaner rules. But he breathed.

Dremm remained on one knee with the knife in his hand and blood running over his mouth.

I expected hate.

He gave me wonder.

"You could," he whispered.

I knew what he meant.

I could have killed him. The prisoner. Dremm. Maybe more before they brought me down. The possibility sat in the circle like another person.

"Yes," I said.

He shuddered as if I had praised him.

Wrong answer, I thought.

Too late.

Harvek came to me after the circle broke.

He did not touch my wounds. He looked at the cut rope, then at Dremm, then at me.

"A chief kills," he said in my tongue.

I waited.

"A chief also leaves men alive to remember why."

I did not answer because I did not want to learn from him and knew I already had.

When I sat again, my rib pain returned with interest. My hands shook. The Wound-Guest curled under the shaking, not angry, not beaten.

Curious.

It had learned that not killing could make the camp hold its breath too.

That frightened me more than if it had raged.

That was how I learned the twelfth law of the island.

Mercy is not clean. Sometimes it is only violence made to wait.
