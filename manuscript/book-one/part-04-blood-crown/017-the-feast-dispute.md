# Chapter 17 Draft: The Feast Dispute

The feast began as law and almost became hunger.

Khar Vane held it three nights after the antler taboo, when the first hidden road from Salt Veyr opened through Fen marsh and brought fish, sour kelp, two sacks of hard grain, and bad news. Hollow Antler had named two more marriage lines unclean. A fever charm had been refused to a child in the uplands. Three young warriors wanted to raid a shrine and hang Vessa's acolytes from their own frames.

Harvek said no.

They came to me next.

That was how I learned refusal does not end desire. It redirects it toward the nearest weaker wall.

Dremm did not ask. He stood with the three and looked at me as if my answer had already been sung.

"Antler starves old women with bark," he said. "We answer."

"With what?"

He smiled then, and I saw the pit in him. Not the White Pit. A smaller one. One he had been digging in himself for years.

"Feast."

I thought feast meant food.

It did. That was the problem.

They brought the wrapped meat from the high rack at dusk. Red cord. Dark leaves. Solek walked before it carrying a bowl of salt water and ash. Yara followed with no drum, only a bone flute she did not play. Children were taken into shelters. Some old ones stayed. Otta stayed and looked angry enough to bite through wood.

I knew before anyone told me.

The body had belonged to a Hollow Antler raider killed in the spring, before I came. Not fresh. Smoked, salted, held for rite. Khar Vane did not eat every enemy. That would have made them wolves with songs. They kept certain bodies for certain answers, turning defeat into warning and warning into meal.

Terrible rules can be learned.

Learning them does not make them less terrible.

Solek spoke the law in Khar tongue first, then trade-tongue for me and Maren and Asha's watching envoys. "No child eats. No guest is forced. No flesh is taken without name. No hunger before witness. Enemy strength is not stolen. Enemy debt is remembered until the last mouth swallows."

Dremm watched me through all of it.

He did not want law. He wanted permission.

Harvek cut the first piece and placed it in the ash bowl. Not in his mouth. To the dead, Solek said. Debt first. Then he cut for the old woman who had lost two sons to Hollow Antler arrows. Her hand did not tremble when she took it. She chewed with her eyes open.

I wanted to look away.

I did not.

Maren stood beside me, face hard as dried fish. "If you endorse this, Salt Veyr will not carry your food openly."

"If I reject it?"

"Khar Vane asks why it followed a boy who spits on its dead."

"What do you want?"

"A world where my boats are not burned because boys need meaning."

Useful. Cruel. True.

Asha had not come herself, but Cairn stood at the edge of firelight. Fen-Kin witness. He looked sick and did not hide it. That made me trust him more.

Dremm took his piece when offered. He lifted it high before eating, eyes on me.

Several young warriors did the same.

The Red stirred with amused recognition. Meat was meat. Blood was memory. Taking an enemy into the body was only a cruder version of what old powers did all the time.

That thought was not mine.

I stepped back.

Yara saw.

Of course she saw.

The wrapped leaves came toward me.

Solek held them, not Dremm. That mattered. His eyes asked whether I knew the size of the answer.

I smelled smoke, salt, old fat, ash.

The camp held itself still.

If I refused completely, I would be judging the tribe that had taken me in after the pit. If I ate, I would become the story Dremm wanted: pit-returned, enemy-eater, sacred hunger with a boy's face.

There had to be a third path. There is always a third path in stories told by people warm enough to think. In life, sometimes the third path is only a different wound.

I took the strip.

Dremm's smile opened.

I walked to the ash bowl and laid the meat beside Harvek's first offering.

The smile died.

"No," Dremm said.

I spoke before he could make that no larger.

"Debt first."

Tovek translated. Then Yara, softly, into Khar tongue with better rhythm. I hated needing her and was grateful anyway.

"The dead eats before Rook," I said. "The law eats before hunger. If Antler made debt, name debt. Do not feed Dremm's mouth and call it answer."

Dremm came forward. Solek moved between us. Harvek did not.

"You shame the feast," Dremm said.

"You tried to make it raid."

"You know nothing of our dead."

"Then teach me without asking me to become your worst hunger."

His fist hit my mouth.

I fell because my rib was still a weak door and pain came through it. The camp roared. Dremm stood over me, breathing hard, horrified and glad.

The Red rose clean and eager.

It gave me his throat again. Always throats. Always the easiest red answer.

I spat blood into the ash bowl.

Silence cracked outward.

Solek whispered something like a prayer or a curse.

Blood in the debt bowl. Living blood over dead debt. I had not meant it. That did not matter. Meaning does not wait for intention.

Yara's face changed.

Harvek finally stood.

Dremm saw too late that he had not struck a boy in private anger. He had struck the pit-returned beside the law bowl during feast witness and made new rite with my blood.

I pushed myself to my knees. Not yielding this time. Rising.

"Dremm pays," I said.

His eyes widened.

I did not ask for death. The Red hated me for that.

"He feeds the roads he wanted to burn. He carries Salt Veyr loads through Fen marsh until Antler taboo breaks or his feet do. He learns what hunger costs before he asks other mouths to sing it."

Maren's brows lifted.

Cairn almost smiled.

Solek looked at Harvek.

Harvek looked at the camp.

A chief who cannot disappoint his people is already eaten.

"Law holds," Harvek said.

Dremm stared at him as if abandoned by his own bones.

That hurt to see. I wanted not to care.

The feast continued, changed. Some ate. Some did not. The ash bowl sat between fire and dark with my blood cooling over enemy meat.

Yara played the bone flute once near midnight. One thin note. No song yet.

I knew she was saving it.

When Dremm passed me later, he did not look at my face.

"I would have followed you anywhere," he said.

"I know."

"That was the gift."

"No," I said. "That was the danger."

He left before I could decide whether I had spoken wisdom or only made another enemy from a boy who had wanted too hard.

The Red whispered without words under my ribs.

It approved of enemies.
