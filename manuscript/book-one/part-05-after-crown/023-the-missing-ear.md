# Chapter 23 Draft: The Missing Ear

Dremm's ear became three different things before noon.

To Hollow Antler, it was punishment. A clean sign that false hearing had been cut away.

To Khar Vane elders, it was debt. A warrior had lost part of himself while carrying dead across enemy ground.

To the young fools around his fire, it was proof that restraint was another word for letting enemies carve you.

By the time Otta dragged me to the lower fires, two boys had shaved the hair above their right ears and smeared white ash where flesh still lived. A third had tied a strip of pig skin to his head and called himself Half-Hearing's left hand.

Otta hit that one with a ladle.

"If you want to look wounded," she said, "I have hooks."

The pig-skin boy vanished.

Otta shoved a bowl into my hands. "Eat where they can see teeth doing useful work."

"I already ate."

"You drank broth. Broth is food for people with no argument left in them."

So I ate.

The lower fires were where Khar Vane kept the heat that did not belong to chiefs. Children, old women, skin-scrapers, smoke-tenders, wounded fighters, dogs too scarred to hunt. The tribe was truer there than at the law stone. Less proud. More dangerous, because pride can be spoken to and need cannot.

Dremm sat with his back against a black stump. He had unwrapped the wound.

The missing ear was ugly. Not because it was bloody. It was too neat for that. Hollow Antler had cut him with ritual skill, making a clean absence. A shaped silence.

The young warriors watched it more than his face.

That told me the problem.

"Cover it," I said.

Dremm looked up slowly. "No."

"Cover it."

"Ash does not hide. Blood-crowned taught that."

He knew exactly which words would please the boys around him.

I squatted by the fire. "What did I teach?"

He leaned forward. "That mercy costs flesh."

The Wound-Guest liked that sentence. It liked how simple it was. Simple things fit in mouths and travel far.

"Wrong."

The boys stirred.

Dremm's face hardened. "I carried their dead."

"Because I sent you."

"And they cut me."

"Because you killed after I told you not to."

His good ear reddened. "They were enemies."

"They were beaten."

"Beaten enemies grow."

"So do bad songs."

Yara stood at the edge of the firelight. I had not seen her arrive. Singers are dangerous that way. She held a strip of dark cloth in one hand.

Dremm saw it and smiled as if she had come to crown him in smaller form.

"Sing it, then," he said.

Yara's face closed. "I came to measure wound."

That frightened him a little. Good.

She stepped behind him and wrapped the dark cloth around his head, covering the absence. He tried to pull away. Otta put one hand on his shoulder and pushed him back down with the bored strength of someone who had held fevering men through worse complaints.

"The missing ear is not yours alone now," Yara said. "That is why you do not get to wave it at children."

One boy said, "He paid blood."

Yara turned. "Everyone pays blood. Most are too poor to buy meaning with it."

The boy shut up.

I watched Dremm's hand curl near his knee. He wanted a weapon. He wanted permission. He wanted me to strike him so the young fools could know what story they were in.

I gave him work instead.

"You will go to Senn each morning," I said.

Dremm stared.

Senn, sitting near the Salt carts, looked as if I had thrown hot grease at him.

"You will carry tally boards," I said. "You will learn what the stores hold and what your disobedience cost in burial salt, road guards, wound herbs, and lost trust."

"I am no Salt scratcher."

"You are Half-Hearing. Hear numbers with the side left to you."

The boys hated it.

Dremm hated it more.

Harvek had come near enough to listen. He said nothing. Solek beside him looked almost happy, which on Solek meant one eyelid moved.

Dremm stood.

For a breath I thought he would swing at me.

For a worse breath I hoped he would, because then the red thing could solve the morning.

Asha's voice came from behind me. "Name."

Just one word.

Mine, held ready.

The Wound-Guest fell back laughing.

Dremm heard it too, not the laughter but the check. He saw Asha. He saw the wrapped Fen spears. He saw that even his anger had witnesses now.

"You put me under Salt boy," he said.

"I put you under cost."

"Same thing."

"Then learn the difference."

His hand left his knee.

He looked at Senn. "If he mocks me, I take his tongue."

Senn stood with his wrapped knife in hand. "If you can count that high."

Otta made a sound that might have been a laugh if she were weaker.

The camp waited.

I waited too, but not like before. Before, I waited for violence and then tried to survive it. Now I waited to see which law would hold after my mouth made it.

Dremm walked toward Senn.

Every young warrior at the fire watched. Every Salt guard watched. Fen watched. Khar watched. Even the dogs lifted their heads.

Dremm stopped one pace from Senn.

"Show me tally board," he said.

Senn did not move.

Then he picked up the board and shoved it into Dremm's chest hard enough to make insult but not fight.

"First mark is yours," Senn said. "Burial salt."

Dremm looked down.

I could see him wanting to throw the board into the fire.

He did not.

That was not victory. Victory is a word singers use after someone else cleans the blood.

It was a hinge.

Small, ugly, and holding more weight than it wanted.
