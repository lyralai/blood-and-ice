# Chapter 22 Draft: Salt Price

Maren Salt-Hand arrived with three carts, six armed boatmen, two ledgers, and a boy carrying a stool.

That told me more than a war horn.

People bring stools when they mean to stay until the other side gets tired.

She set the stool in the mud before Harvek's law stone and sat as if Khar Vane had built the whole camp to give her knees relief. Her oilskin cloak shone with rain. Her hair was braided in tight salt cords, and every cord had a little shell worked into it. Not pretty. Counting marks.

"Your crown leaks cost," she said.

Harvek grunted. "Rain does that."

"Rain does not eat stores."

She nodded to the first ledger-bearer. He opened the board and began reading in the trade tongue while Tovek stood beside him, smiling like a knife that had learned manners.

"Salted seal, twelve barrels. Smoked cod, nine racks. Lamp fat, four skins. Boat repair on two hulls damaged moving Khar wounded. Road guards for the lower pass. Burial salt for bodies returned after Blood Snow. Compensation for the boy Senn's old wound still unpaid under third-knot judgment."

I knew Senn. I had cut him before I knew what debt was.

He stood behind Maren now, thinner than I remembered and watching me with the careful hatred of someone who had decided not to waste it.

"That was before the crown," I said.

Maren looked pleased. "Debt does not rot because men sing over new bone."

Khar Vane muttered at that. Salt Veyr muttered back. The Fen-Kin stayed quiet, which was worse than muttering because it meant they were learning.

Harvek said, "Name price."

"I name guarantees."

He bared his teeth. "Same thing with softer skin."

"No. A price ends. A guarantee behaves."

That was the kind of sentence I hated because it sounded like a trap and a lesson at the same time.

Maren raised one finger.

"First. Salt roads remain open under blood-crown law. No Khar Vane takes toll in meat, bone, witness, or song from any Salt cart marked with my hand."

"Reasonable," Solek said.

Harvek glared at him.

Solek shrugged. "Unpleasant and reasonable are cousins."

Second finger.

"Winter storage. If Khar Vane gathers followers beyond its own mouths, one storehouse near the black inlet comes under joint seal. Salt lock, Khar guard, Fen witness."

"Hollow Antler will call that theft from ritual famine," Asha said.

Maren smiled. "Hollow Antler calls hunger holy when someone else carries fish."

I liked her for that and distrusted the liking.

Third finger.

"A hostage."

The camp sound changed.

Not loud. Deep.

Khar Vane did not hate hostage law. Khar Vane understood hostages. They hated that Salt Veyr had said it first.

Harvek leaned forward. "Whose?"

Maren pointed at me.

"His."

The Red rose so fast my vision reddened at the edges.

Mine?

I had no one.

That was the first thought. Then the second: that was why she wanted one. A boy with no kin could promise with blood all day and pay with none of it.

"I have no family," I said.

"You have followers."

Dremm laughed from the side. It was a bad laugh, too eager.

Maren did not look at him. Smart again.

"Not a prisoner," she said. "A living guarantee. Someone under your public protection stays with Salt Veyr until the first winter store is sealed. Fed, witnessed, returned if terms hold."

"And if terms do not hold?"

"Then everyone knows whose protection failed."

There are knives that cut skin and knives that cut story. Maren had brought the second kind.

Khar Vane would have sent Dremm, and that would have made him important. Fen-Kin would refuse to provide the body because the check on me could not also become Salt's leverage. Harvek would not send a blooded fighter unless he wanted the camp to think Salt owned his war arm.

The answer was waiting before I wanted it.

Senn.

The boy I had wounded. Salt Veyr blood. Old debt. A living guarantee turned both ways.

I looked at him.

He understood and hated me faster than anyone else.

"No," he said.

Maren did not turn. "You are not asked yet."

"No," he said again.

His hand went to the scar near his ribs, under his coat. My scar. My debt.

I could have let Maren command him. That would make the bargain easier and the hatred cleaner. But the crown was on my head that morning, and terms no one can see are wind.

I stepped close enough that his guards tightened.

"I cut you when I was animal," I said.

"You are still animal. Now with jewelry."

Someone behind me hissed. I raised a hand before Khar Vane could defend my honor and make everything stupid.

"Yes."

That stopped Senn's next words.

"Stand as guarantee," I said. "Not hostage. Guarantee. You live in Khar camp until the storehouse is sealed, not Salt. You count what we owe. You tell Maren if I cheat. You tell me if she cheats."

Maren's eyes narrowed.

Good. A bargain that only one side likes is a hook.

Senn stared at me.

"Why would I do that?"

"Because I owe you and you want the debt in your hand."

"I want your blood."

"Then count fish first."

He looked at Maren. For the first time, she did not answer for him.

That mattered.

Asha watched me with the look she used when a spear found the gap between ribs.

Senn spat in the mud. "If I come, I sleep near Salt carts."

"Yes."

"I keep my knife."

"Wrapped unless you eat."

"I do not kneel."

"Good."

Dremm said, "Salt boy gives terms now?"

I turned.

The Red wanted the turn to become strike. It showed me Dremm's throat and how easy a thumb can close breath. I held my hands open.

"Yes," I said. "That is what witness means when it is not song."

Dremm looked at the young warriors around him and found no answer that did not make him sound like a child.

Maren shut the ledger.

"Joint storehouse, road right, Senn as guarantee-counter, old wound debt folded into the first winter tally," she said. "Salt Veyr accepts if Fen-Kin marks it."

Asha said, "Fen-Kin marks terms, not obedience."

"I sell salt, not obedience."

"Everyone sells obedience by another name."

Maren smiled. "Then I price mine clearly."

By evening Senn's bedroll lay near the Salt carts inside Khar Vane camp. No one liked it. That made it sturdy.

He sat with his knife across his knees, wrapped in cloth, and watched the fires like each one owed him an apology.

I sat across from him because I was tired of people kneeling.

After a long time he said, "If the thing takes you, I will not call your name."

"Good."

He looked up.

"Stab me," I said. "Names are Asha's work."

Senn did not smile.

But he did unwrap the knife enough to check the edge.
