# Chapter 24 Draft: Hollow Antler's Third Answer

Vessa did not send warriors.

She sent mothers.

They came down the ridge in white bark masks, twelve of them, each carrying an empty cradle frame on her back. No spears. No axes. No antler staves except the old woman at the front, and hers was cut short, more walking stick than weapon.

Khar Vane reached for blades anyway.

Solek shouted before Harvek did. "Wrapped hands."

That command saved the morning from becoming a song Yara would hate and sing anyway.

The mothers stopped beyond arrow cast. One by one they lowered the cradle frames into the mud and stood behind them. Empty wood. Empty straps. The kind used to carry infants while cutting peat or smoking fish.

I did not understand until Otta spat.

"Cruel old antler," she said.

Maren was there within breaths, because Salt Veyr smells a public meaning faster than smoke. Asha came slower, grim. Dremm and Senn came together with a tally board between them, which would have been funny on another morning.

The old woman with the short staff removed her mask.

Not Vessa.

Older. Brown skin folded around eyes like cracked leather. Her hair was shaved close except for one white braid down the center of her skull.

Tovek whispered, "Mother Harl. Keeps cradle names."

"What are cradle names?"

He gave me a look. "The names of children dead before first winter."

The Wound-Guest did not rise. It listened.

Mother Harl planted the short staff in the mud.

"Hollow Antler asks Fen-Kin whether it stands with the eater's crown or with the unborn winters."

No one spoke.

Not because the words were loud. Because they were aimed past me.

At Asha.

The empty cradles were not threat. They were accusation. Every tribe on the island had dead children. Hunger made sure of that. If Asha stood with Khar Vane, Vessa would make Fen-Kin answer every empty cradle that Khar mouths might fill before others.

This was the third answer.

First, Vessa had tried to own my death.

Second, she had tried to contain my blood.

Now she was making everyone else pay for standing near me.

I looked at Asha.

Her face had gone still in a way that made Cairn step closer.

Mother Harl lifted both hands. "Fen-Kin old blood says it can call back the red hunger. Will old blood call back empty bellies? Will it call back children eaten by a war-crown's road? Will it mark every store taken for this boy's legend?"

Khar Vane snarled at "this boy."

I almost smiled. Being made small can be useful if everyone else is trying to make you too large.

Asha said, "Fen-Kin marks what it witnesses."

"Then witness this."

The mothers turned the cradles around.

Each frame had marks cut into the inner wood. Names. Tiny names. Some old, some fresh, some so worn by handling that they were almost smooth.

Even Harvek looked away.

Mother Harl said, "Hollow Antler closes mourning paths to any who feed Khar Vane expansion under the blood-crown. No cradle name will be spoken for them. No winter dead will be counted with ours. Let Fen-Kin decide whether its old blood is worth nameless children."

There are punishments that cut the living.

There are punishments that cut the dead and wait for the living to bleed.

Senn whispered, "Can they do that?"

Maren answered, "If enough people act like they can."

I heard the whole island then. Not with the Wound-Guest. With the part of me that had learned to count exits, then hands, then roads.

Salt roads. Fen witness. Khar hunger. Hollow names. No tribe was a beast alone. Each held a piece of what the others needed to remain people. Vessa did not need to beat us. She could make every bond shameful until bonds broke themselves.

The Wound-Guest offered a red answer.

Twelve mothers. One old throat. A little blood in the mud and the accusation would stop.

No.

It pressed harder.

No.

Asha's head turned slightly. She had felt it.

So had Dremm, somehow. His hand twitched, not toward his axe but toward the wrapped place where his ear had been.

I stepped forward before Harvek could, before Maren could price silence, before Asha had to choose alone.

"Mother Harl," I said.

Tovek translated. Carefully.

Her eyes moved over me like I was a tool left dirty.

"I am not asking Hollow Antler to bless the crown," I said.

"Blessing is not owed to stains."

"Good. Keep it."

That surprised her. It surprised Khar Vane more.

"Keep your blessing," I said. "Keep your mourning paths. Keep your cradle names. But do not spend dead children to hide a living bargain."

The mothers stiffened.

I had stepped on sacred ground. I knew it by how everyone stopped breathing.

Good. Sacred ground is still ground. Feet go there.

"Salt Veyr feeds children with roads. Fen-Kin keeps old blood from becoming red madness. Khar Vane holds the lower pass now because Hollow Antler tried to use a pit as answer and the answer climbed out. You want them ashamed of standing near me. Fine. Make me the price in open mouth."

Mother Harl's fingers tightened on her staff.

"What price?"

I looked at the cradle frames.

"For every storehouse sealed under blood-crown law, one tenth goes to winter cradles of all tribes. Salt counts it. Fen witnesses. Khar guards it. Hollow names the dead who are owed from it, but cannot keep the food from children whose parents anger Vessa."

Maren made a sound like pain.

That meant the price was real.

Harvek said, "You give our stores?"

"No. I give my expansion."

Solek's eyes sharpened.

I turned so Khar Vane heard.

"Any mouth that follows my crown eats after the cradle tenth is set aside. If that makes the crown too costly, stop kneeling."

Dremm stared at me as if I had asked him to swallow seawater.

Senn looked at the tally board, then at Maren. "Can count."

Maren closed her eyes. "Of course you can."

Asha said, "Fen-Kin can witness such a store."

Mother Harl had not moved. "Vessa did not offer bargain."

"No," I said. "She offered shame. I am offering weight."

The old woman's eyes narrowed.

"And when hunger comes harder?"

"Then we see who breaks law first."

For a long moment I thought she would refuse because refusing would hurt Vessa's enemies more than accepting would feed children.

Then one of the masked mothers behind her touched a cradle mark with two fingers.

Mother Harl heard that small sound.

So did everyone.

"Hollow Antler will carry the offer," she said.

"No," I said.

The red thing smiled under my ribs.

I kept my voice even.

"Hollow Antler will carry the food when it is counted. If Vessa wants to refuse children stores, she can do it with hands full."

Mother Harl looked at me then. Truly looked.

Not at the crown. Not at the blood. At the boy underneath, dirty and frightened and learning how to put weight where songs wanted wind.

"You are becoming less easy to kill," she said.

"That is what everyone keeps complaining about."

She put her mask back on.

The mothers lifted their empty cradles and climbed the ridge.

No one cheered.

That was how I knew the morning mattered.
