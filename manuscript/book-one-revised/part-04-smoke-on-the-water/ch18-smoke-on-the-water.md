# Chapter 18: Smoke on the Water

There are things on the water that sit it wrong.

---

I am up on the seaward stack in the green half-light of the low, where my green runs out and goes to bare wet rock and the channel opens past the last of the mangrove into the wide flat water and the haze. I come up here before a hunt to read the tide — the set of the swell, the dark roads under it where the fat river-pig runs the shallows. The water lies past the pinch the way it always lies, hot and pale and old under the standing haze.

And there is a thing on it that has no business being there.

One shape. Long, dark, sitting the swell the way a thing sits it that has lost the fight with the water and is being taken where the water wants. But it holds a line — not the dumb hold of a drift-log pushed flat, the lean hold of a thing that has a will in it still, pointed, even now. Even dying.

Marrow is the one who stills first. The curled hand working slow against his chest. I read the water before I let his stillness in.

The shape is a boat. The word comes up out of me from far down, from under all the green I packed over everything before the green, and it comes up whole. *Boat.* The whole skin draws tight. The small hairs lifting along the arm. The gut clenching to a fist. The body knows the word before the head can turn it. The body wore the word.

And the boat is wrong. It wallows. The oars that work are out of time, the hull sits too low. There are not enough oars for the size of it. I read it the way I read a wounded thing — the listing, the slowness, the desperation of a body that feels the ground going under it. The boat is running from something.

---

They beach the thing on my spit. Not where the dead expedition died — further along, past the old ribs in the mud. They run the boat in on desperation and luck, and the keel grinds up the sand, and the dark uprights come over the sides.

Not many. Fewer than the boat was built for. Some move wrong — stiff, favoring, one with an arm bound in torn cloth. They are hurt. Not the green-hurt — the hurt of things that have been hit. There is a smell off the boat I know from the far-down place: the iron-smell of someone else's blood in the seams of the deck. They have been in a fight and come away broken.

One of them — big, slow-moving, the one the others turn to when the work stops — puts a hand on the hull and leans there, breathing. And the green closes around them. The fever-air, the seep. I do not sicken. Every other human who has walked onto this shore has gone down inside twenty lows. They do not know it yet. The big one reads the green wall and sees cover. He does not see the teeth in it.

The body says: *they are going to die here.*

---

I lie above them on the bone-spit lookout and I read them the way I read a herd.

The big one first. He moves through the camp the way Gull-Marr moved through the tribe — slow, certain, touching things as he passes. A shoulder here. A hand on a back there. Not the hand that owns. The hand that holds together. He stops at the one with the bound arm and crouches and unwraps the cloth and his hands are gentle on the wound. Then the tired settles — the tired of a thing that has held something together too long.

The thick-armed one is the law behind the holding. He carries an axe like a thing that belongs on his hip. The body reads him: he is not the strength — he is the fear of the strength, the held blow. And under it, something I did not expect: he is afraid. Not of the green. Of the big one's face when the dying goes too far.

The young one is the runner. The climber. All quick joints, no weight in the settling. He is the age I was when the green took me. The big one puts a hand on the back of the young one's neck and holds it there a breath. Not the brand-hand. Something else.

And the far mouth opens so wide it hurts. I have seen this. Not the shape — the thing under the shape. The holding-together. The shoulder-laid and the hand on the back of the neck that means *you are mine and I am yours and the thing that is coming for us has to come through that first.* I have seen it in the Kala's arm. I have seen it in the old male standing foot-for-foot at my side. It is the thing the tribe gave me. And here it is on two legs, made of the same shape that made the hold and the brand — and it is not the brand. It is the other thing.

A woman reads the sky — she stands at the tide-line with her face up, reading the wind. She has a heading that does not match the big one's. They argue, short and sharp, and the argument is the argument of two minds that trust each other enough to be wrong at each other.

A steady-handed woman whose hands go to wounds the way my hands go to a kill. A big gentle one who cooks and makes sounds nobody laughs at, and the not-laughing has affection in it.

---

The green takes them fast.

The first low: the coughing one. The wet tearing, the chest drowning. The steady-handed woman's hands on him, the orders, the professional set. Before the dark the big one goes in to read what her hands find, and when he comes out his face has changed. The coughing one dies in the night. They cover him with stones.

The second low: a sting in the shallows. The steady-handed woman cuts the puncture, packs it with paste from wrong leaves — she does not know this green. The leg goes the color of the rot. She shakes her head.

A third goes into the green to cut limbs and comes out with a bite that swells and turns hot. She cuts the arm off at the elbow with the thick one's axe. He lives one low past the cutting and dies in the dark.

The thick-armed one stands over the dying and his face goes flat and he turns away and walks to the tree line and stands with his back to the camp. The young one follows — not touching, not speaking, just there — and after a while the thick-armed one puts his hand on the young one's head, brief, rough, and they come back to the fire.

---

By the third low the big one stands at the tide-line and reads the green wall and the thinning pack at his back. He is counting days. The sky-reader stands beside him and points at the green — inland, away from the death-shore. He shakes his head. She points again, sharper, her voice going up. The argument goes. And the big one puts both hands flat on the rail of the boat and looks at them — his own hands — and nods. Once. The nod of a thing that has drawn a new line because the old one was killing everyone behind it.

They break camp. They go inland, toward my green, toward the high stones.

I follow them through the canopy. The far mouth leans at every step they take into the deeper shade. They do not know what lives in the high green. They do not know about the Vorth.

Marrow follows me. Behind us the bone-spit holds its dead and the green comes down over the camp. Ahead of us the hurt pack walks deeper into my island with the holding-center at its front and the sky-reader behind him and the young one in the branches above. And I count the lows they have left against the thing they are walking toward, and the number is going down.
