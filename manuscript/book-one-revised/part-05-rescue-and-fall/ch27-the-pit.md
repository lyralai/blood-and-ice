# Chapter 27: The Pit

They come for us on the second low in the hold.

---

Things in iron, different from the dock-helms — heavier, the faces covered, the short iron longer. Pit-guards. The body reads them the way it reads everything: the weight of the step, the set of the shoulders, the professional blankness of things that move property and do not name it. They are not cruel. Cruelty is a thing a face does. These faces do nothing. They are the hands of the place, and the hands do the work.

They open the Kala's cell first.

The chain comes off the wall. The Kala does not stand. The great body lies on the stone and the breathing goes in and out and the dark eye reads the open door and does not move toward it. The pit-guards go in with poles — long iron poles with hooks at the end — and they put the hooks in the chain and they drag the Kala out of the cell and across the stone floor, and the sound the great hide makes on the cut stone is the sound of a thing being dragged that cannot be dragged gently.

The Kala makes a sound. Not the gentling-sound. Not the *I am taken* sound. A new one. Low, deep, from the chest, the sound a thing makes when the last of the ground it knows goes under it. The sound goes through the iron bars and through the hold and through me, and the Red comes up.

Not all the way. The pressing Red — the thing that pushes at the still place, that says *break the bars, break the hands, take the Kala and go.* The still place holds. The still place says: *not here. The bars are iron and the guards are many and the Kala is chained and the place is stone under stone. Wait. Read the ground.*

I hold the Red down. I press my front to the bars and the Kala goes past on the stone floor, the great silver-dulled hide dragging, the chain on the neck, the eye finding me as she goes past and holding me — holding me the way she held me on the bank, on the king's seat, in the hold of the boat — and the eye says the thing the gentling-sound says: *be still. Nothing comes.* Said to me. By the thing being dragged away on the stone. The mother gentling the boy from the floor of a cage.

The eye goes past. The Kala goes down. A shaft in the floor at the far end of the hold — a vertical cut in the stone, a thing that goes down, the brown-dark opening under the great body as the pit-guards work the chain and the Kala descends. The sound of the chain on the stone. The sound of the breathing going further away. The sound of the gentling-sound, fainter, fainter, gone.

---

They come for me.

The cell opens. The chain comes off the wall — not off my wrists, off the wall. The wrists stay ironed. The hands go to the arms, professional, the lifting-and-moving hands, and I go out of the cell and into the hold and the body reads the hold the way it reads every new ground: exits, threats, the shape of the space.

One exit. The ramp the cart came down. Three pit-guards. The shaft in the floor. The body reads the shaft: a thing that goes down into the stone, a vertical hole, the kind of hole the island had in the limestone where the water goes underground and does not come back.

They take me to the shaft.

A cage. An iron cage on a chain, the size of a body, the kind of thing that goes down into a hole and does not come back up. The pit-guards put me in it. The door closes. The chain goes taut. And the cage descends.

---

The shaft goes down a long way.

The stone slides past — cut stone, then rough stone, then stone that is not stone but rock, the raw rock under the made-hill, the bones of the earth the city was built on. The air goes colder. The wet goes thicker. The brown-dark goes to the black-dark, the dark that does not have a torch-gap in it, the dark that is the dark all the way down.

And then the dark opens.

The cage comes out of the shaft into a space that is vast. The body reads vast before the eyes confirm it — the way the sound changes, the way the air moves, the way the echo goes out and does not come back for a count that means the ceiling is high and the walls are far. The torch-light comes in — not from torch-gaps but from iron baskets hung on chains, the fire burning low and red in them, the light going down into a space that is deeper than it is wide.

The pit.

I smell it before the light gives it to me. Old blood. Not the green-blood, not the fresh-hot kill-blood of the island. Old blood, soaked into stone, layered, the smell of a thing that has been bleeding for years in the same place and never been washed. And under the blood-smell, the smell of things caged — the close packed reek of bodies held where they cannot move, the piss and the sour breath and the open sores of the kept. The smell of the hold. The smell of the first hole. The body wears it again now in one breath, and the skin on the back of the neck crawls and tries to get away from a hand that is not there.

The cage descends past levels. Tiers. The body counts them — one, two, three — each one a ledge of cut stone with cells on it, the cells opening off the shaft the way the cells in the hold opened off the channel. Bodies in the cells. Things in the cells. Some moving, some not. Some human, some not. The body reads them in the torch-light the way it reads a herd — the alive ones and the dying ones and the dead ones, the professional count, the thing the body does when it enters new ground.

The cage stops at a level. The door opens. The pit-guards take me out and the chain goes to a ring in the wall and I am on a ledge of cut stone in a shaft in the earth, and the Kala is three levels up.

I know this because I can hear her.

Not the gentling-sound. The breathing. The great chest going in and out through the stone, the vibration of a thing too low for the ear, felt in the bone. Three levels of cut stone between us and the sound still comes through, the way water comes through the limestone channels under the green. I press my front to the rock wall and the breathing comes through it, barely. In. Out. The long pause. In again.

She is alive. Three levels up. Dying. And I am down here.

---

They put me in a cell.

Stone on three sides and iron on the fourth. Smaller than the hold-cell — meaner, the sleeping-place of a thing that is taken out and put back. The floor is dark with old blood. The walls are scored with the marks of things that clawed the stone and did not stay quietly in it.

I have been here before.

Not this cell. The other one. The first one. The dark of the hold, the wet wood sweating, the boy warm against my side, the hand closing on the back of the neck. The body knows the shape of the hole the way the feet know the shape of the ground. The body has been in a hole its whole life — the hold, the island, the crown, the boat, the under-city, and now this. The body wears the word *owned* the way it wore it the first low, and the word has never come off, and the crown did not take it off, and the Kala's arm did not take it off, and the king's seat did not take it off. The word was always there. Under the name. Under the crown. Under the keeping-arm. The brand on the neck and the word *mine* in the hand that closed and never let go.

I sit in the cell on the level three down from the Kala and the body reads the ground. The pit-guards. The shift-changes. The sound of the cage-chain. The water in the channel. The stone in the walls. The things in the cells — the breathing, the silence, the bodies that move and the bodies that do not. The dark.

And above — far above, up through the levels, up through the shaft, up through the stone — the patron. The narrow face. The pale eyes. The body reads the patron the way it reads the weather: the thing that does not come down into the dark but shapes everything in it. The patron does not visit the pit. The patron does not need to. The patron owns the ground the pit is cut into, and the owning reaches down through every level, and every chain in every cell is the patron's chain, and every body in every cage is the patron's body. The patron priced me differently on the dock and priced me differently in the hold and the pricing is still happening, up there in the cut-stone city, in a room I cannot see, in a currency I cannot read. The disc. The eye. The blood. The patron is calculating the value of the thing that does not die, and the calculation has not finished, and the not-finishing is the thing that keeps me alive. The patron will not let me die until the calculation is done. The pit is the place where the reading happens. The patron is the eye at the top of the shaft, looking down, and the boy at the bottom is the thing the eye has been searching for, and the finding is the thing the patron will not let go of.

The patron is the brand, made vast. The brand was one hand on one neck. The patron is the hand that owns the ground the neck stands on.

And the body says, the way it has said everything since the first low on the island:

*Count.*

---

And then — from somewhere in the dark of the pit, from a cell I cannot place, from a level I have not mapped — a sound comes up. Small. Torn. Hoarse, half breath. A sound I know. A sound I have heard in the green and on the high stones and in the hold of the boat. A sound I made and laid on a thing on purpose, the human way, the one word I gave to the runt the tribe cast out.

The still-sound. Marrow's sound. Given back to me now across the dark of the pit.

I go to the bars. The sound comes from two cells down on the same level — close enough to hear, close enough to reach if the bars were not between us. Marrow. Here. The runt the tribe cast out, the thing I named and kept, here in the pit with me.

And beside his breathing — smaller, quicker, the rapid heart-beat sound of a thing too small for the dark — the bold heart. The fierce bright thing that rode Marrow's shoulder on the high stones. It is here too. In the dark, in a cage, making the small high sound it makes when it is afraid. The sound I have not heard since the green. The sound that was the first warm thing to come to me after the wreck, the first thing that climbed onto the boy on the beach and said *alive, alive, you are alive.* It is here. Afraid. And Marrow is making the still-sound at it the way I made the still-sound at him in the den in the rot — low, torn, two falls, the gentling folded in. The runt gentling the bold heart in the dark of a pit cell the way I gentled him. The thing passed forward. The sound I gave him becoming the sound he gives.

He makes the sound again. Torn, hoarse, the still-sound scraped through a throat that has been screaming or choking or both. And the sound says what it has always said, what it said in the den in the rot when I laid it on him the first time:

*I am here. I am here. I am here.*

I make it back. The rasp, the cough, the still-sound given through the bars into the dark. Two falls. Scraping over the gap where the three teeth are gone. And the sound goes across the dark of the pit and finds him.

The breathing two cells down changes. The held breath let go. The settling of a thing that heard the sound and read it and lay back inside it.

---

The big one is on the level above me.

I do not know this until the dark gives me his breathing — the heavy, steady breathing of the holding-center, gone flat the way Marrow's still-sound goes flat, a thing chosen as the thing the body does when the body cannot do anything else. One level up. Across the shaft. In a cell the same size as mine.

He does not speak. I do not speak. The shaft-gap between levels carries the breathing and nothing else. But the body reads him through the stone the way the body reads the Kala three levels up — the vibration, the weight, the settling of a thing in a cage. He is there. The holding-center is there, in the same pit, in the same iron, at the same depth.

I think about the high stones. About the king's seat and the keeping-arm and the old male with the torn ear standing foot-for-foot at my side. About the challenge, when I leapt the birthright and did not take the neck. About Grist climbing to the king who counts what the strong waste. About the Torn One setting her feet. About Stub on the half-leg. About the long even sound with the gladness folded in.

Gone. All of it. A sea away and a shaft away and a cage away. And the crown is a thing I held behind my teeth in the dark, and the teeth are broken in three places, and the dark does not care about crowns.

But the body cares. The body holds the crown the way it holds the name — not because the crown is useful in the pit, but because the crown is a thing the body *was.* The body was the soft wrong thing on lent ground and it became the king of the wild things, and the becoming is a thing no cage and no chain and no pit takes, because the becoming is *inside.* The ground did not lend the crown. The body took it. And the body does not give back what it has taken.

---

I kneel in the dark and lay the squared stones in the still place, faced and even, the way the Kala taught me on the clean ground among the dead.

The name my mother gave me. *Rook.* Mine. The one un-stolen thing, held behind the teeth.

The three brands, ground small — the first hand, the slaver-captain's brand, the patron's brand, the thing the big one's code became on the dock stone. Kept to hand back across the water.

The Kala. The great dark eye going out, turned to me with the gentling unbroken. The price. Set faced in the still place where it will not pour away. The one who carried me. The one who woke the name.

And the new thing — the thing that is not the crown and not the name and not the brand. The thing the crack holds. The fruit-sweetness and the kind-sound and the thick-armed one's holding-center call through the bars. The thing the pack did when it held together. The thing that sits in cages in the dark and makes the sound at the thing it took because the making is the thing, not the hearing. The thing that is in the cage across the shaft and the cage two cells down and the cage three levels up. The thing the green could not close and the crown could not close and the Kala could not close. The thing that is not the tribe and not the pack and not the brand. The thing that is just the holding, the holding that does not stop when the holding has no point.

I set it faced in the still place with the rest. And the still place holds.

---

And the Red stirs.

Not the killing-Red. The other thing. The thing the seer's finger found on the breastbone on the high stones. The deep-down thing. The pulse. The warm. The thing that closed the wounds too fast and kept the fevers off and held the swarms at a hand's width and steadied the Kala's breathing on the crossing when I pressed my front to her side. The thing that has no word and no law and no crown.

It pulses. In the pit-dark. In the stone-and-iron cage. In the under-city of the place the boats go. It pulses the way it pulsed in the fever-fog on the bank when the Kala found me — but different now. Different from the king's seat. Different from every time before.

Because before the raid, I held the still place and the still place held the root. Before the raid, the Red came when I needed it — when the green was eating me, when the challenge was on me, when the Kala was fading on the bank. Now the root does not wait for the needing.

The pit wakes it.

The pit with its blood-stone and its iron bars and its tiers of caged things and the smell of old killing soaked into the walls — the pit is the thing the island was. The pure survival-place. The place where the only law is the law that does not stop. And the root knows it. The root reads the blood-smell through the stone the way I read a track in the mud — the professional knowledge of a thing that has spent its whole life in this place — and the root pushes up against the still place the way the Hesh pushed at the moved line. Testing. Reading. Looking for the crack.

The root is me. That is the thing I learned on the raid stone. Not a thing *in* me — the thing the island burned me *down to.* A thousand lows of the fever and the rot and the things that hunt — the body went through it and the body survived it and the surviving burned away everything that was not the survival-thing. Burned away the off-switch. Burned away the part that knows when to stop. Left the root — the pure survival-core, the engine that runs the body when the body is past the point where the head can ride. And the root is deep. Deeper than the name. Deeper than the count. Deeper than the still place. The root is the bedrock. The name and the count and the still place are the things I built *on* it — the after-things, the Kala-things, the things that make me more than the root.

But the root is what keeps me alive. And the pit is the root's country.

The still place holds. For now. The still place is tired from the raid and the crossing and the cold and the grief, and the root is rested because the root does not tire — the root only waits — and the root knows what I am only beginning to read: every low in the pit, the still place gets a little weaker. Every time the Red comes up, the gap gets wider, the head gets less of the picture. And the pit is the place designed to bring the Red up, over and over, until the after-things are gone and the root is the only thing left.

This is what the patron priced. Not the healing. Not the warmth. The *weapon.* The thing that came through the raiders like weather. The patron does not know it is not a weapon. The patron does not know it is a boy with his survival-core burned bare. The patron sees a body that does not die and does not break and kills what it touches, and the patron has put that body in a pit to find out what it can do, and the finding will burn away the after-things until there is nothing left but the root.

The pulse pushes. The still place presses. And I hold — barely, barely — the line between what the island made me and what the Kala taught me.

But the pulse says a thing the pulse does not say in words. The pulse says: *this is home.* The root recognizing the pit the way the feet recognize the green. The body settling into the blood-smell the way the body settles into the canopy at the low-dark — the muscle-knowledge of a thing finding the place it was made for. And the settling is the horror. Not the killing. The *settling.* The body feeling at home in the place of chains and blood-stone and caged things, the root stretching out in the deep place like a thing turning over in a bed it knows.

The Kala taught me a different home. The fire. The squared dead. The place among the kept. The sound that says *be still, nothing comes.* The after-things. The things built on the root. And the after-things are what the pit will burn away, and the root will not burn, and when the after-things are gone the boy who held the Kala's arm will be gone with them, and the thing left will not be a passenger or a monster or a demon. It will be *me.* The me the island made. The me that does not stop.

I rise in the blood of the pit. The dark does not care. The iron does not care. The patron three levels up in the cut-stone city does not care.

But I care. Because I am still the boy. The after-things are still here. The name is behind the teeth. The crown is in the body. The Kala's sound is in the ear. And the line between the root and the boy is still drawn.

For now.

One low the hand will open. It opened once on the moving floor and dropped me into the warm black water, and the water carried me to the shore, and the shore made me. It will open again. And the thing it opens on will not be the small wrong boy it closed on.

But the thing it opens on will be me. Not a passenger. Not a possession. The thing the island made, wearing the boy's face, doing the boy's killing, and not remembering. That is the thing I carry into the dark. The thing the seer mourned. The thing the patron priced. The root that is home in the pit.

I am still counting. And the root is counting too — counting the breaths between the boy and the thing the boy becomes when the still place fails. And the number is not going down.

It is gone.
