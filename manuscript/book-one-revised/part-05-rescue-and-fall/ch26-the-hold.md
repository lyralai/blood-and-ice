# Chapter 26: The Hold

Under the city there is a hole, and in the hole they put us.

---

The cart goes down. A ramp cut into the stone, going under the made-hill, the walls closing in, the sky going to a slit and the slit going to nothing. The air changes. Not the green-seep, not the rot-breath. The stone-breath — cold, wet, the smell of things that have lived and died in the dark. The cut-stone walls sweat. The chains rattle on the boards.

The hole opens.

It is not a hold. It is not a pit. It is a thing between — a vast low space under the stone, the ceiling lost in the dark above, the floor cut flat and draining to a channel in the center where the water runs slow and brown. Cells line the walls — iron bars, stone backs, the kind of cage that is not made to open again. Things are in them. Bodies. Some moving, some not. The body reads them the way it reads everything: by smell first, by sound, by the weight of the breathing. Some are human. Some are not.

The Kala goes into a cell at the far end. The cell is too small for her. The great body folds into it and the chains go to the wall and the cell door closes and the sound it makes is the sound of a thing that does not open again.

I go into the cell beside her. The iron between us — bars, thick as fingers, the cold iron that does not warm in the hand. I can see her through them. The great dark eye, tracking me. The gentling-sound, faint, the last of it, coming through the bars.

The pack goes into the cells across the channel. The big one, the sky-reader, the steady-handed woman — each in a cell, each in chains. The cook, the young one, the thick-armed one — the iron closes over them. The crew that is left, split among the cells, the pack broken into pieces by the iron.

And the patron stands at the center of the hold, on the walkway over the channel, and reads us the way a thing reads livestock. The narrow face going from cell to cell. Pricing.

---

The pricing takes a low. Maybe two. The body loses the count in the dark.

The patron goes cell to cell with the things in iron helms. The narrow face reads the bodies. Reads the muscle. Reads the scars. Reads the teeth — one of the helms pulls the cook's mouth open and reads the teeth, the way I read the teeth of a kill to place the age. The pricing is not kind. It is not cruel. It is *accurate.*

The patron stands on the walkway over the channel and watches. Not helping — watching. The narrow face reads the pricing the way it reads everything: measuring, measuring, the number going up or down behind the pale eyes. The patron's eyes come back to me. To the disc. Every low the eyes come back — the pull of the eye on the thing that owns the mark, the gravity of the search ended. The patron prices me differently than the others. Not higher or lower — *differently.* The others are priced as muscle, as skill, as use. I am priced as a thing. A thing whose value is not in what the body can do but in what the body *is.*

The body reads this and the body goes cold. The reading-cold. The cold of a thing that has been priced before, in a hold, by a hand. The patron is pricing me the way the hand priced me. Not as muscle. As property. The deepest price. The one that says: *this is mine, and the mine is forever.*

The sky-reader is priced first. The patron reads her face and her hands and makes a sound — short, the sound of a thing placed. Navigator. The helms take her out of the cell and she does not look back at the big one. She looks at the patron. Holds the pale eyes with her own — the sky-reader's eyes, the eyes that read the wind and the water and the heading — and the look is not defiance. It is *measurement.* She is pricing the patron the way the patron priced her. And the patron reads the look and his face does a thing I have not seen on it: the narrow face shifts. A fraction. The patron does not enjoy being measured. The sky-reader is led away and the big one watches her go and his jaw works but he does not make a sound.

The steady-handed woman is priced with her. The patron reads the surgeon's hands and the pricing changes — the recognition, the number going up. The hands that close wounds are more valuable than the hands that carry. The steady-handed woman looks back through the bars at the cook and her face says the thing her hands say when they are on a wound: *I will come back.* She will not come back. The body knows it. The cook knows it. He makes a sound at her — the joke-shape, low, the last joke — and the corner of her mouth moves, and that is enough, and she is gone.

The cook is priced next. The helms read the burned hands, the big body, the age. The patron shakes its head. Not valuable enough to move. The cook goes back to the wall. He sits against the stone with his burned hands in his lap and his face has the stillness of a thing that has been priced and found ordinary. Then he does a thing I do not expect: he makes the joke-sound. Low. Bad. At himself. The sound a thing makes when the world has read it and found it wanting and the only answer is the wrong sound that no one laughs at. And across the channel, in his cell, the big one laughs. One short sound, torn out of the holding-center like a thing that escaped before the jaw could close on it. The cook hears it. The burned hands open once — the shrug, the *what can you do* — and close again. The two of them hold the sound between them across the iron, the joke and the laugh, the thing that survives the pricing because the pricing does not know what to do with it.

The thick-armed one is priced high. The helms read the muscle and the scarred face and the flat eyes and the patron makes the appreciation-sound. The thick one is taken out of the cell. He does not go quietly. He goes the way a thing goes that has decided the going is the last ground it will take — slow, the weight on the flat of the foot, every step read. The helms push him. He does not move faster. He sets the pace and the helms set theirs and the two speeds do not match and the thing in the corridor is a thing the patron's helms do not know how to read: a body that is not fighting and not obeying. The flat face goes through the hold door and the door closes and the heavy step goes down the stone and is gone.

The young one is priced last. The patron reads the runner's body — the joints, the quick-twitch, the light frame — and makes the placed-sound. Runner. Carrier. The young one's face is wet. He is not making a sound. He is fourteen lows old, maybe younger, and he is being led away into the dark of the under-city. He looks at the big one. The big one looks at him. And the big one makes the sound — the kind-sound, the word-shape, the thing the mouth makes at a small thing it is not going to hurt. The same sound he made in the hold of the boat. The same sound he made on the high stones when he put the hand on the young one's neck. The last sound the holding-center makes. The young one hears it and his face does the thing a face does when it breaks, and he is led away, and the sound hangs in the cell after him like a thing the iron cannot hold.

---

The big one is left in his cell.

Not priced. Not taken. Left. The body reads it through the bars — the patron reading the big one the way it read the cook, the slow weighing, the eyes going to the muscle and the scars and the age. But the big one is not priced as muscle. The big one is priced as *crew.* The helms know what he was. The captain. The holding-center. The thing that held the pack together and is sitting in a cell with the pack gone, and the pack gone is the thing the big one was, and without it he is a body in a cage, and the body in a cage is not worth as much as the body that held the pack.

The patron leaves. The helms follow. The hold goes dark — not all-the-way dark, the brown-dark, the wet-dark, the dark that has a little light in it from the torch-gaps in the walls. The kind of dark the body can read in.

I press against the bars between my cell and the Kala's. The iron is cold. The blood-warmth goes into it and the iron takes it and does not give it back. Through the bars I can reach the great side — barely, the fingertips on the dull hide. The breathing is bad. Long pauses. The gentling-sound has stopped.

Across the channel the big one sits against the wall of his cell. His wrists are raw where the iron has worked them. His face is the empty face — not the calculating face, not the holding face. The face of a thing that held and lost and is sitting with the losing.

He reads me through the bars across the channel. The small scarred thing in the next cage. The thing that broke his enforcer's wrist and put two of his crew on the ground. The thing whose wounds close in days. The thing he chained and took and brought here to pay a debt that was never going to clear.

And his mouth moves. A sound. Low. The same shape as the sound he made in the hold of the boat — the word-shape that is not a word I know, the kind-shape, the shape the mouth makes at a small thing it is not going to hurt.

I do not know the word. But the body reads the shape. And the body holds still.

And the crack — the crack the fruit opened, the crack the word-sound opened, the crack that holds the thing the pack does when it holds together — the crack goes deeper. Because the big one is making the kind-sound through the bars of a cage he is in too. The holding-center holding the line he drew even now, even in the cell, even with the pack gone and the debt a lie and the boy and the beast he took chained beside him in the dark. He is still holding. He does not know how to stop.

And the far mouth — the hole that leans toward the shape of the thing the hand can be when the hand is not owning — the far mouth reads the holding-center through the bars and leans.

And this low — this low the crack opens.

Not the fruit-crack. Not Fenn's crack. The *other* crack, the one that has been waiting outside the wall since the big one made the kind-sound in the hold of the boat. The big one is in a cage. The big one is priced and left and sitting with the losing. The big one is no longer the thing that chained me — the big one is the thing the patron chained. The chain on his wrists is the same iron as the chain on mine. The cell around him is the same stone as the cell around me. And the kind-sound he made in the boat — the word-shape, the mouth that makes a sound at a small thing it is not going to hurt — that sound was not the brand. It was never the brand. And the body reads this now because the body reads it from the *outside*, from the cage across the channel, from the holding-center broken open on the cell floor, and the reading is: he is in here with me. The big one who took me is in here with me. And the thing under his holding is the same thing under the Kala's keeping-arm, and it always was, and I would not let it in because the chain was on and the Kala was dying and the taking was the only reading.

The crack opens. The kind-sound goes through the wall and lies beside the fruit-sweetness, and the crack holds both, and the thing the crack holds is the thing the pack does when it holds together — and the pack is not a pack anymore, the pack is broken and priced and scattered through the under-city, and the holding-together is the only thing left of it, and it is in the cage across from me making the kind-sound at the thing it took because the making is the thing and the holding does not stop.

The green could not close this hole. The crown could not close it. The Kala could not close it. Nothing closes it but the shape of a thing that holds when holding has no point, and the shape is in the cage across the channel, and the hole leans at it.

I press my fingers through the bars against the Kala's side. The blood-warmth. The breathing. In, out, the long pause, in again. I count. The big one counts something of his own in the dark across the channel — the crew he had, the crew he lost, the thing he held and the ground that went under it.

And we sit in the under-city in our cages, the boy and the beast and the holding-center, and above us the eye looks out of every face of cut stone, and the green is a thousand lows and a sea away, and the cold has no seep in it, and the only warm thing is the blood under my skin that I cannot give away fast enough to keep the Kala breathing, and the count goes on.

The count goes on.
