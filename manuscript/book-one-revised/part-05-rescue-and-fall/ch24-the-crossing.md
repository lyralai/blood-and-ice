# Chapter 24: The Crossing

The pack dies by ones.

---

I count them through the deck. Not with ears — with the thing the body does when it has lain in a hold long enough to read the whole boat through the planks. Every step is a different foot. Every voice is a different shape. And when a foot stops stepping and a voice stops throwing, the body notes it the way the body notes a print going into the mud and not coming back.

The fevers take three more before the wind changes.

Three. The coughing one first — a young one, I think, from the quickness of the step that stopped. Then the other hard-faced one from the raid, the one I put on the ground by the throat, the one whose blade opened my arm. He dies of the fever, not the throat. The fever does not care what I did to him. The fever takes the body the fever finds. Then a third I have not read close — a body that stepped heavy and spoke low and is now silent.

The steady-handed woman works. I hear her boots. She is everywhere above — moving between the sick, the voice laying orders, the hands on the wounds that are not wounds but the body rotting from the inside. She cannot stop it. The body knows this from the sounds — the set in her voice that the big one's face had on the spit when the dying went bad. The holding-on-to-a-thing-you-cannot-hold set.

The cook works too. He boils the water. He makes the stew. He carries it to the sick, and the sounds he makes at them are the joke-sounds, the low bad sounds no one laughs at, and the not-laughing has affection in it, and the affection is the thing the dying hear last.

Until the low the heavy step stops.

Not the cook — one of the others. A body I have not read close, a crew-thing that stepped heavy and spoke low and hauled the rope without complaint. The fever took him the way it takes them — fast, the chest-sound, the drowning-from-the-inside. The cook is the one who carries the water to him. The cook's heavy step goes to where the crew-thing lies, and the joke-sounds come — low, bad, the sounds the cook makes at the sick — and this low the crew-thing does not answer them.

The young one goes to the crew-thing. The body hears it — the quick feet crossing the deck, the small sound the young one makes. A sound that is just the air going out of a body that has lost a thing it stood beside every low. The young one sits with the crew-thing. The cook stands over them both, and the joke-sounds are gone, and the big burned hands hang at his sides, and the cook's face has the thing the big one's face had on the spit when the dying went bad.

The crew-thing goes in the dark. The steady-handed woman wraps him in the sail-cloth and they slide him over the side, and the water takes him the way the water takes everything.

The pack that was thirty is seven.

The sky-reader has the heading. Her voice is up in the rigging with the young one, calling the wind, reading the water, the two of them doing the thing the body recognizes from the green — the heading-instinct, the thing that goes forward because forward is the only direction that has a shape. She does not sicken either. I read this: she is the kind that does not sicken easy. Some are. The green takes the easy ones first.

And the thick-armed one works with the bad wrist. No lighter. No shorter. The body hears him through the planks — the oar, the rope, the heavy step, the short command. He does not speak to me. He does not come below. He works the boat with the broken wrist and the flat face and the thing under the flat face that I read on the high stone when he stood over the big one calculating.

He is afraid. Not of the green. Not of the dying. Of the thing the big one is becoming — the thing the big one becomes when he reads the caged boy below whose wounds close in days. The thick-armed one reads the calculating and the calculating has a shape and the shape is *the debt* and the thick one is afraid of where the debt leads.

I do not know the debt. I do not have the word for it. But I read the fear off the thick one's step through the planks, and the fear is the shape of a thing that knows the holding-center is about to draw a new line.

---

The Kala dulls.

Low by low the great hide loses the silver. The gray goes flat. The skin that shone in the green half-light — that caught the canopy-dark and held it, alive, the living pelt of the thing that kept me — goes dead. The wrong air. The wrong water. The wrong food they push through the slat, which she does not eat, which sits on the hull floor and rots.

I try. The body tries. I take the fish-stew and I hold it to the great mouth and the mouth closes. I take the water and I pour it along the lip and the lip does not open. The Kala makes the gentling-sound less and less. The breathing slows. The great dark eye — the eye that read me on the bank, that read me on the king's seat, that read me in the dark of the hold every low since they chained us — the eye dims.

I press my front to the great side. The warmth is there. The tide of the breathing is there — in, out, in, out, slower, the long pauses between that mean a thing is going down into the place where the body does not come back from. I count the breaths. The way I counted the lows on the island. The way I counted the steps between the den and the kill-ground. The body counts because the counting is the thing the body does when it cannot do anything else.

And the Red stirs.

Not the killing-Red. Not the thing that came up on the high stone and went through the thick-armed one like water through a channel. The *other* thing. The thing the seer's finger found on the breastbone. The thing under the blood that is older than the crown. The thing that pulsed once in the pit of the fever-fog and once on the king's seat and once in the hold of this boat in the dark. The thing that has no word.

It stirs when the Kala's breathing goes long. It stirs the way a thing stirs that has been asleep and hears a sound it knows. And the warmth comes up — not the body-warmth, the *blood*-warmth, the deep-down warmth that closes the wounds too fast and rings the swarms off by a hand's width. It comes up and it goes into the front I press against the Kala's side, and the Kala's breathing — for a breath, for two — steadies.

Then the cold comes.

The body-cost. The shivering, the numb hands, the cut pulling open. These I know. These are the small cost. But underneath the small cost is the thing I learned on the raid stone: the warmth and the Red are in the same place. The island-place. The root. The survival-thing the island burned into me — the deep wiring that does not stop — the warmth comes from *there.* The healing comes from *there.* Everything that keeps me alive comes from the same root that runs the killing, and reaching for the warmth means reaching for the root, and the root is awake now. The root went down after the raid but the root liked the running and the root is ready to run again.

I press harder against the Kala's side. The warmth goes into her. The breathing steadies — barely. And the deep place stirs underneath the warmth — not the Red, not the killing-thing, but the *place* the killing-thing lives in. The place where the warmth and the killing are the same thing. The place the island built. And the still place presses down on it, and the pressing is harder than it was before the raid. The still place is tired. The still place is the muscle I built after the island — the name, the count, the crown, the Kala's teaching — and it is newer and shallower than the root underneath it, and the root does not tire. The root only waits.

Then it goes down. The warmth goes down. The cold goes down. The Kala's breathing goes long again. And I lie in the hold knowing that every time I reach for the warmth to keep her alive I go deeper into the place the island built, and the deeper I go the harder the root pushes up, and the still place is the only thing between the boy and the root, and the still place is a muscle, and muscles fail.

---

The steady-handed woman comes below on the fifth low.

Not the big one — her. She ducks through the hatch with the steady hands carrying things — a blade, a pot of the paste she makes, a strip of clean cloth. Professional. The hands going to the work because the work is there. She does not look at the Kala. She looks at me. At my arm.

The cut. The blade-wound from the night raid — the one the hard-faced one opened from elbow to wrist. She reads it from three body-lengths away, and her face does the thing her face does: the reading-thing, the professional-thing, the hands-going-to-the-wound thing. Except the wound is not a wound anymore. Five lows old, closed, the scar pink and clean, the edges pulled shut in a way no wound she has ever seen pulls shut in five lows.

She crouches. She takes my arm. I let her take it — the professional in in her hands, the way the hands go to the wound the way my hands go to a kill, automatic, sure. She turns the arm. Her fingers find the scar. She presses. The pressure is clinical — not gentle, not rough, the pressure of a thing that is reading the body through the fingertips the way I read the ground through the soles.

Her face changes.

Not the fear-change. The *fascination*-change. The change of a thing that has spent its life closing wounds and has never seen one close like this. She looks at the scar and she looks at me and her mouth makes a sound — a word, low, thrown up at the hatch. The big one's face appears in the hatch-light. She shows him the arm. The five-low scar that should be a five-week wound.

But she sees the other thing too. The thing the healing left behind. The cold — the low-grade cold that sits in me now after every time the Red has been up. She reads it the way she reads a fever: professional, the hands going to the forehead, the wrist, the throat. Her eyes narrow. And her hands stop.

They stop because the pulse under her fingers is wrong. Not two beats — one beat, but the beat is too hard, too deep, the heart pushing the blood the way a body pushes a fight. The healing is there — wrong-fast, impossible. But underneath the healing the pulse runs the way a pulse runs in a body that is fighting for its life even when the body is sitting still. Even when the body is at rest. Even when there is nothing to fight. The survival-thing — the deep wiring — does not know how to turn off. It runs and runs and the body answers, and the healing is not separate from the running. The healing *is* the running. The same engine. The same fire.

She does not know what she is reading. She is a surgeon and she has read a thousand pulses, but she has never read a pulse that runs this hard in a body at rest, and the reading is the reading of a woman who has found something past the edge of what she knows. Her face does a thing I do not expect. She looks at me — not the professional look, not the pricing look. The look of a surgeon who has put her hands on a body that is running itself to death and the running is the thing keeping it alive and she does not know which one to treat.

The look goes. The professional face comes back. But the food changes — the next low the pot comes down with dried fruit beside it, placed with a care that is not pricing. And the portion is larger. And the stew has something in it that was not in it before — a root, bitter, the kind that builds the blood. She is treating the cold. She does not understand it, but she is treating it, the way her hands go to a wound — automatic, sure, the muscle-knowledge of a thing done a thousand times.

She has not softened toward me. She has done the thing a surgeon does when the body on the table is doing something impossible: she has decided to keep it alive long enough to understand it. The pricing is still there. But underneath the pricing is the thing the pricing was built on — the hands. The hands that close wounds. The hands that read the pulse that runs too hard and did not pull away.

---

The big one comes below every low now.

Not to the Kala. To me. He crouches at the hatch and he makes sounds at me — words, low, steady, the holding-center voice, the voice the big one uses on the pack when the pack is going bad. He does not reach for me. He does not come close. He crouches at the hatch and he talks the way the Kala makes the gentling-sound — because the making is the thing, not the hearing. Because the holding-center does not stop holding when the thing it is holding cannot hear it.

I do not know the words. I have not had the road from head to mouth since the green took it. But the body reads the shape of the sounds the way it reads the shape of the Vorth sounds, the way it reads everything — body to body, the meaning under the word. And the meaning under the big one's words is the same meaning the hand on the young one's neck carried: *you are mine and I am yours and the thing that is coming for us has to come through that first.*

Not the brand-meaning. The other one. The one I saw in the camp on the spit. The one I leaned at through the green wall.

The crack widens.

---

On the ninth low the wind dies and the boat sits on the flat water and does not move. The heat comes down. The air in the hold goes thick and wet and the fever-seep thickens with it. Another one dies above — a quick foot-stop, a silence, the cook's voice, low, the joke-shape that no one laughs at. The cook is still standing. The cook does not sicken. The big burned hands keep the pot on the fire and the stew in the buckets, and the not-sickening of the cook is the thing the pack does not name but reads — the cook is the cook, the fire is the fire, and the fire does not go out because the cook does not go out.

The big one comes below. This low he comes all the way in. He does not crouch at the hatch. He stands in the hold, five body-lengths away, and reads the Kala on the floor and the boy against her side. The Kala's breathing is bad. The pauses between breaths are long enough to hold a whole word in. The big one reads this. His face does the calculating thing.

Then he crouches beside the Kala. Not me — *her.* He puts his hand on the great hide the way the steady-handed woman puts her hands on a wound — careful, reading. The Kala's eye tracks him. The big dark eye in the dulling hide, reading the thing that chained her, reading the thing that took her off the high stones, reading the two-legged shape that means *hand* and *hold* and *owned.*

He takes his hand off. He looks at me. And his face does a thing I have not seen on him — it cracks. Not the holding-center crack, the professional crack, the calculating crack. The *other* crack. The crack of a thing that has done a thing it thought was right and is reading the cost of the thing on the body of the thing it took it from.

He stands. He goes to the hatch. He stops. He turns back.

He undoes the chain on my arm.

Not the Kala's chain. Mine. The iron falls off the wrist and the wrist is free and the blood goes back into the hand and the hand is mine again. He did not have to do this. The cost of it — the big one has given the caged thing a weapon, and the caged thing broke his enforcer's wrist and put two of his crew on the ground, and the giving is a risk the holding-center should not take.

He takes it. He looks at me. And the sound he makes is low and steady and it has the shape of a word I almost — *almost* — have the road to catch.

Then he goes up the hatch and the light closes and I am free in the hold with the Kala chained beside me, and the free hand goes to the Kala's side and the warmth comes up again — the blood-warmth, the deep-down thing — and I hold it there against the great dulling hide, and the Kala's breathing steadies, and the steadying is a breath longer than the last one, and the breath after that, and I count every one.

The crossing is not over. But the count has changed. It is not going down to zero. It is going — almost, barely, by the width of a breath — back up.
