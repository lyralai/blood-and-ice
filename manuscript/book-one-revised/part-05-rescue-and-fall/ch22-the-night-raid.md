# Chapter 22: The Night Raid

They come for the Kala.

---

I do not read it in time.

The count said they would come for me — for the thing with the disc, the thing that keeps the monsters back. The count was wrong. The count was the count of a thing that leans at its own wanting and reads the world through the wanting. The count did not account for the big one reading the high stones with the eyes of a thing that has hunted value in every port and every sea, and the value the big one read was not the two-legged thing with the disc.

It was the Vorth.

A single great-ape Vorth — silvered, vast, the hide worth a lord's ransom in the fighting pits or the collector's cages of the old cities. The thing the sky-reader drew on her map. The thing the steady-handed woman priced in her head when she saw the warriors on the killing ground. The big one read the high stones the way I read a herd — counting, learning where it bunches thick, where it thins — and what he saw was not a tribe. What he saw was *stock.*

And the Kala is the slowest. The Kala is the vastest. The Kala folds down at the edge of the high stones in the dark, the keeping-arm over whatever is nearest, the great slow tide of her breathing the long even breathing of a thing that has been the mother of the tribe for longer than I have been alive and has forgotten how to be afraid.

They come in the dark before the low. Four of them. The big one is not there — the big one draws the plan and stays at the spit, because the big one is the holding-center and the holding-center does not walk into the thing that will break the center if it goes wrong. The thick-armed one leads. The young one is the runner. Two others I have not read close — hard-faced, quick-handed, the kind that does the taking.

They have nets. Rope nets, weighted at the edges, the weave tight enough to hold a thrashing thing. They have the short iron. They have a plan — the sky-reader's plan, drawn on the map, the way in and the way out through the green where the canopy thins at the landward edge of the high stones.

They come through my green in the dark and I do not hear them.

I hear everything in my green. Every footfall, every breath, every shift of weight in the canopy. But the thick-armed one has learned something in the lows on the spit. He moves the way a thing moves that has been taught by the green what the green kills — careful, placed, the weight on the flat of the foot, the breath held and let through the nose. Not silent the way I am silent. Silent enough. Silent enough for a four-thing raid in the dark before the low when the tribe is sleeping and the king is on the seaward stack reading the water for the thing the king fears is coming for him.

The king was wrong. The king was reading the wrong thing.

---

Marrow hears them.

Not the thick-armed one — Marrow does not hear the thick-armed one in time either. Marrow hears the Kala. The low sound she makes when the net goes over her — not the killing-sound, not the challenge. The *surprise* sound, the sound of a vast slow thing that has not been threatened in a thousand lows and does not have the sound for it ready in the great chest. A sound like the earth moving. The Kala comes up off the stone under the net and the weighted edges drag and the thick-armed one is there with the short iron and the rope, and the rope goes around the great wrists, and the Kala makes the sound again — louder now, the surprise going to something else, the thing under the surprise, the thing that says *I am taken* — and the sound goes across the high stones like a wave and hits me on the seaward stack and I am off the rock before the sound has finished.

Marrow is ahead of me. He has heard it first and he is already running — the rocking crooked gait, the wrong body at full speed, the high shoulder pumping, the curled hand closed to a fist. He is running for the Kala the way he runs for me when I have been too long in the dark, and he does not know what he is running toward and he runs.

He gets there first. He gets to the edge of the high stone where the Kala is being taken and he puts his small wrong body between the Kala and the thick-armed one and he makes the only sound he has — not the still-sound, not the name I gave him, the *other* sound, the sound I have never heard him make, the sound that comes out of a thing the order cast out when the thing it loves is being taken. It is small. It is torn. It is the sound of a body putting itself in front of a weight that will crush it because the body behind the weight is the body that kept it alive.

The thick-armed one does not hesitate. He backhands Marrow out of the way.

The small wrong body leaves the stone. Not far — a body-length, two — and hits the rock and rolls and the curled hand scrapes across the stone and the sound Marrow makes is the sound of a thing hitting ground wrong, the air going out of it, and he does not get up.

I am there.

---

The thing that comes out of the canopy onto the high stone is not the king.

I know this the way I know the tide — from the outside, from the body that watches itself do a thing the head has not agreed to. The king set the jaw and read the ground and counted the lows. The thing on the high stone does not count. The thing on the high stone is the thing the still place holds back, the thing under the crown, the thing that has wanted to be the hand since the hand was first laid on the back of the neck.

The Red comes up.

Not the omen-pulse the seer reads, not the slow warm wrongness under the blood. This is the *other* thing — the thing I have never let all the way up, the thing the second mouth feeds on, the thing I held back on the king's seat when the flat eye was on me and the wanting was bright and hot. The thing I did not feed.

I feed it.

The sound that comes out of me is not a human sound. It is not the rasp, not the cough, not the falls. It is the sound the Hesh made when it came through the green on the moved line, the sound the old male made when the rot-tooth went bad and the fever took the brain — the deep-chest killing-sound of a thing that has come all the way out from under the still place and is not holding any part of itself back.

The young one is the first to see it. He is in the canopy above the raid, the runner's place, and he looks down and the face he makes is the face of a thing that has been afraid of the green since it beached and has just found out the green was right. His mouth opens. No sound comes out. The body locks. The branch he is on sways.

The thick-armed one turns from Marrow's body on the stone and reads me and the axe comes up — fast, trained, the muscle-knowledge of a thing that has fought in tight spaces — and I go through him.

Not around. Not under. *Through.*

The speed is wrong. The speed is the thing the Red brings up from the place under the blood, the thing that is not muscle-speed and not wind-speed but the speed of a body that is being run by something older than the body. I take the thick-armed one's axe-arm at the wrist and the wrist bends the wrong way and the axe drops, and the other two are turning, the rope and the net in their hands, and I am at the first one before the net is off the Kala and the short iron in my hand is the short iron out of his hand and he goes down.

The Kala is making the sound. The deep-chest sound, the *I am taken* sound, and the rope is on her wrists and the net is on her back and she is pulling — the vast slow strength of her pulling against the rope — and the rope is holding because the rope was made for things that are strong and the maker of the rope knew what strong was.

I go for the rope. The second hard-faced one puts himself between me and the Kala and he has a blade — shorter than the short iron, curved, the kind that opens things — and he is fast and he is trained and he is not ready for the thing I am this low. The blade opens my arm — I feel it, the hot bright pain of a thing that cuts, the blood coming — and the wound does not slow me because the Red is up and the Red does not read the body's pain the way the body reads it. The cut is deep and the blood runs and the edges are already pulling shut when I take him by the throat and put him on the ground.

The young one drops from the canopy. He drops behind me, between me and the edge of the stone, and he has a blade — small, shaking, the hand that holds it not a fighter's hand — and he is making a sound, a word, the same word over and over, the body reads the shape of it even past the Red: *please, please, please.* His face is white and wet and the blade shakes and he is fourteen lows old maybe, maybe younger, and his eyes are the eyes of a thing that has just found out that the world has things in it that cannot be read and cannot be fought and do not stop.

The Red says: *take him.* The Red says: *the throat, the wrist, the thigh, the thing that is between you and the Kala, break it.* The Red is the thing under the blood that has no word and no law and no crown, and it runs the body like the hand ran the body in the hold — owning, certain, the grip that says *you are a thing and the thing is mine.*

I look at the young one. The blade. The shaking hand. The eyes.

And the still place — the thing behind the Red, the thing that held the line on the king's seat, the thing that did not feed the second mouth on Gull-Marr when the neck was there — the still place comes up behind the Red and reads the young one and reads the blade and reads the age of the thing in front of me and says one word, low, the way the Kala says *be still*:

*No.*

I turn from the young one. I go to the Kala.

---

The rope is thick. The weave is tight. My hands are bloody and the short iron is in my grip and I saw at the rope and the rope gives, strand by strand, and the Kala pulls and the net shifts and the great dark eye finds me over the shoulder and reads me — reads the Red still up, reads the blood on the arm where the cut is already closed, reads the thing her boy has become — and the still-sound comes out of the great chest. *Be still. Be still. Nothing comes.* Not to me. To the Red. The mother talking the beast out of the boy the way she talked the fever out of him on the bank.

But there are more of them. Not four — more. The big one was at the spit, but the big one is not at the spit. The big one is at the edge of the green with the rest of the pack — the sky-reader, the steady-handed woman, the cook, the others — and they have the Kala's trail marked and the rope and the chain and the plan, and the plan was never four raiders. The plan was four raiders and then the rest.

They come out of the green with the chains.

I fight. The Red runs the body and the body does things I cannot count later — the short iron and the teeth and the weight thrown low and sideways, the law of pulling the ground from under the strength. I break the first chain. I break the second. The thick-armed one is on me with the bad wrist and the weight and I put him down again, hard, and he does not get up this time, and the Kala is pulling the net and the net is half-gone and the rope is going and—

The third chain goes around my arm. The fourth goes around the Kala's neck.

And the nets come from behind — not the raid pack, the *rest*, the ones I did not see because the Red had me reading the four in front — and the nets come over the Kala from the landward side, weighted, layered, and the great body goes down under the weight of them, slow, the way the tide goes down, and the still-sound dies half-made in the great chest.

I go down on top of her.

Not the fighting-down — the *holding.* The body curling around the Kala's neck where the chain is, the arms going around the great head, the front pressed to the hide where the gentling-sound lives. Shielding. The thing the Kala did for me on the bank when the fever took me under — the body over the body, the keeping-arm over the thing that is kept. I do it with the chains on my arm and the blood on my front and the Red still up under the skin, and I will not let go.

The big one stands at the edge of the stone and reads the thing on the ground. The boy curled around the chained beast, the arms around the great head, the body making the shape of the keeping-arm the Kala taught him. And the thing on his face is not the face of a thing that has taken a score.

It is the face of a thing that has seen something it did not expect to see and cannot stop seeing.

He makes a sound. Low. The others stop. The nets settle.

And the small one comes.

Marrow. Off the stone where the thick-armed one hit him. Not dead — the body reads him through the Red's fading edge, the chest going, the curled hand twitching against the rock. He is up. Wrong — the high shoulder riding wrong, the gait listing, blood on the face where the rock took the skin. But up. And coming. Not away — *toward.* The small wrong body hauling itself across the stone toward the thing that chained him and the thing he loves, and the sound it makes is the sound from before — not the still-sound, not the challenge, the *other* sound, the one I have never heard him make before this night and now twice in it. The sound of a thing that will not be left behind.
A hard-faced one moves to stop him. The big one makes a sound — short, sharp. *Let it come.* The big one has read the boy on the ground curled around the beast, and the small one crawling toward the boy, and the thing the boy did when the small one was hit — the Red, the killing-speed, the going-through. The big one calculates: the small one is leverage. The thing that makes the boy comply. The thing that makes the killing-machine go still when the chain goes on.
Marrow reaches me. He puts the curled hand on my arm — the arm with the chain, the arm that is around the Kala's head — and he folds down against my side, and the small wrong body is warm against the great warm body, and we are three things chained on the high stone in the dark. The boy and the runt and the beast, curled together, the one around the other around the other.
The bold heart comes last. The small fierce thing that rode Marrow's shoulder — it drops from the canopy where it fled when the nets came, and it lands on Marrow's back and grips the wrong-made spine with its claws and makes the sound it makes when it is afraid — high, sharp, the sound of a thing that has nowhere else to go. It grips. It does not leave.

The big one reads the three of us on the stone. The boy, the runt, the beast, the bold heart. His face does the thing it did when he saw the disc on the high stones — the widening, the breath-hold. He is seeing something he did not expect to see, and the seeing is changing something behind his eyes I do not have a word for.

The Red goes down slow under the still place. The still place comes up. The name is there under the tongue — *Rook* — and the brand is there on the neck, and the disc is there at the chest, and the Kala is there under the chains, breathing, alive, and Marrow is there against my side, breathing, alive, and the bold heart grips the wrong-made spine with its claws and will not let go.

And I do not remember the thick-armed one's face when I took his wrist.

This is the thing the seer's finger found. Not a passenger. Not a thing *in* me — the thing the island *made* of me. A thousand lows of the green. A thousand lows of the fever that does not kill and the swarms that do not land and the rot that eats everything but the one thing it cannot eat. The body went through it and the body survived it and the surviving burned the survival-thing down to the root — burned away the part that knows when to stop.

The cold comes — the shivering, the wounds pulling, the weakness. These I could bear.

The gap I cannot bear.

The Red was up and the body moved and I was *in* the body the way I am in the body when the green takes me through the canopy — the body first, the head riding behind. But the Red does not take me through the canopy. The Red takes me through the place the island built — the deep survival-place where the killing is not a choice but a law, the law of the thing that does not stop, the law that kept me alive when every other thing on the fever-island died. And when the Red is up that law runs the body and the body does what the law says and the head is not there. The head is behind. The head is riding. And when the Red goes down the head comes back and the head does not have the whole picture.

I remember the thick-armed one's axe-arm. I remember the wrist bending the wrong way. I do not remember his face when it bent. I remember the hard-faced one with the blade and the blade opening my arm. I do not remember the sound he made when I put him on the ground. I remember the young one on the ground with the shaking blade and the still place saying *no.* I do not remember how close the still place came to saying nothing.

The gap is mine. The Red does not eat the memory — the Red *is* the memory. The Red is the deepest part of me running without the rest of me attached, and the part that runs without the rest does not record. It acts. It is the purest survival-thing the island left — the thing that does not think, does not count, does not hold the still place. It just goes. And the going is fast and the going is thorough and the going is the thing that kept me king of the wild things, and the going is the thing that would have taken the young one's throat if the still place had been one fraction slower.

Every time the Red comes up, the still place is slower. Not because something is eating it. Because the island wired the survival-thing deeper than the still place, and every time the survival-thing runs, the wiring holds a little better and the still place holds a little worse. The island made the thing that does not stop. The still place is the thing I built *after* — the name, the count, the crown, the Kala's teaching. The after-thing. The after-thing is newer. The after-thing is shallower. And the island-thing is the root.

I curl around the Kala's neck. The cold comes — the body-cost. The shivering, the numb hands, the cut on the arm pulling open. These I could bear. But the gap sits in me where the memory should be, and the gap is the shape of what the island made, and I do not know if the after-thing — the name, the count, the still place — is deep enough to hold the root when the root runs.

The warmth I could give her now — the blood-warmth that steadied her breathing on the king's seat — the warmth is there. But the warmth lives in the same deep place the Red lives in. The survival-place. The island-place. Reaching for the warmth means reaching for the root, and the root is awake now. The root went down but the root liked the running and the root is ready to run again. I cannot reach for the warmth without reaching for the thing that does not stop.

I do not reach. And the gentling-sound comes one more time, muffled by the net, aimed at the boy who held on.

*Be still. Nothing comes.*

Something has come. And I am counting the breaths between what has come and what comes next, and the number is not going down.

It is gone.
