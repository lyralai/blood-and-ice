# Prologue: Slack Water

The sea had lain like poured glass for three days, and on the third day the lookout raised a ship.

She came out of the haze bow-on, no wake, no answer to the glass, her sails hanging slack off the yards like wet washing. Coll watched her from the rail and felt the thing every sailor feels and never says: that a ship with no one at her wheel is not empty. She is occupied by whatever emptied her.

"Salvage," the mate said, the way another man might say grace. Vell had a nose for cargo the way a gull has a nose for guts, and a ship adrift in the warm latitudes with her hold maybe full was a year's wage bobbing on the flat. He took the boat himself. He took Rusk the bosun, and a steady man called Tam, and because someone had to bail and someone always made the boy do it, he took Coll.

They rowed across a sea so still it doubled everything — the dead ship and her dead reflection, joined at the waterline into one long wrong shape. The heat sat on the back of Coll's neck like a hand. Sweat ran into his eyes. He was sixteen and he had crossed this water twice and he had never once been cold on it.

He was cold before they reached the hull.

It came off her the way damp comes off a cellar — a breath of it, sliding out over the flat water to meet them, and the closer they pulled the deeper it went, until Coll's wet shirt turned to ice against his spine and his own breath began to smoke. *Smoke.* On the warm sea, in the dead calm, four men sat in a boat and watched their breath fog the air like it was deep winter, and no one said a word, because there was no word that did not sound like a child's.

"Tide's turned cold," Rusk said, which meant nothing, which was why he said it.

They made the chains and went up. The deck was sound. That was the first wrong thing — Coll had boarded wrecks, and wrecks were chaos, lines parted, gear adrift, the story of the bad hour written in the mess. This ship was *neat*. The lines were coiled. A pannikin sat upright on a hatch cover with a skin of something frozen in the bottom of it. Everything in its place, and over all of it the cold, and the silence, and that smell — not rot. That was the second wrong thing, and it took Coll a long moment to understand it, standing on a dead ship in the killing heat of the southern sea: there was no rot. In this warmth a corpse went green and swollen by the second day. There was no green here. There was no smell of death at all. There was only the dry, clean, cold smell of a thing that had been kept.

They found the crew amidships, in the lee of the boat, where men huddle.

For one mercy Coll thought they were sleeping. Then Vell turned one over with his boot, and the man came up light — too light, light as a bundle of canes, light as the husk a locust leaves clinging to a wall — and his face was the face of a man, all the lines of him still there, the stubble, the burst vein in one eye, but the skin had gone to grey paper drawn tight over the bone, and the eye was a dry stone, and there was nothing under the skin. Nothing. Drawn out and gone. No wound on him. No blood on the deck, not a drop, not a smear, not a thread of it anywhere on that neat clean cold ship — and a sailor knows, a sailor has scrubbed enough of it, that you cannot empty a man and leave no blood.

Tam began, very quietly, to pray.

"There's a hold full of cargo under us," Vell said, but his voice had gone thin, and he was not looking at the hatch. He was looking at the husks. He could not stop looking at them. "There's a year under our feet."

It was Coll who heard it. The others were grown men full of their own fear, loud inside their own skulls, but Coll was sixteen and frightened small and quiet, and so it was Coll who heard the sound come up out of the open hatch behind the mate. Not a footstep. A draw. A long, slow, patient draw, the sound a man makes pulling at a pipe that has nearly gone out, the sound of something taking in — and with it, up through the hatch, came the cold in a wave, real cold, cold that bit, cold that made the frozen pannikin and the smoking breath seem like nothing, and Coll's heart slammed once and he felt it — felt the warmth go out of his own chest toward the dark of that hatch, *toward* it, the way water leans toward a drain.

"Off," he said. He could barely make the word. "Off, off, get *off* —"

The thing came up out of the hold the way oil comes up through water.

He would spend what was left of his life trying not to remember it, and remembering it. Pale. Dry. The shape of a man the way the husks were the shape of men — all the lines still there and nothing inside — but moving, unfolding, tall, its skin drawn grey and tight and its mouth not a mouth and its eyes two holes that the cold poured out of. It made no sound but the draw, that endless thirsty draw, and where it turned its face the air went to frost, and Rusk, who was nearest, simply stopped. Stood. His breath smoked twice and then did not smoke again, and the bosun went grey and light and dry standing up, his blood going out of him into the cold and the dark with no wound to mark the road it took, and he folded down onto the clean deck a husk among husks, neat, in his place.

Tam went over the rail. Coll heard the splash and then nothing, no swimming, the glass sea taking him without a ripple, and that was a mercy too, maybe, the only one on offer.

Vell did not run. The greed held him one half-second too long — his eye flicked, even then, to the hatch, to the year of cargo — and the cold took him where he stood with his hand still half-raised toward his wealth.

Coll ran. He did not remember the rail or the rope or the water. He came to in the boat with no oar and no memory of casting off, one half-mile of flat glass between him and the neat cold ship, and he lay in the bilge and shook and watched his own breath, watching to see if it would stop smoking, certain it would stop, waiting through the long bright afternoon to go grey and light and dry.

It did not stop. The cold thinned. The killing heat came back over him like a blanket and he wept into the bilgewater for the gift of his own sweat.

When his own ship found him at dusk they asked him a hundred times what he had seen, and he told them, and they wrote *fever* in the book, because there is no other word a ship's book will hold, and they were a thousand leagues from any shore where men knew the older words. They did not turn back for the drifting ship. By dawn she had slid back into the haze she came from, neat and silent and full of cargo and the kept, carried on a current no chart marked, going wherever such things go to wait out the long calm between one ship and the next.

Coll lived. He never again crossed warm water without watching his breath. And once, years on and far away, an old woman who read the older words told him a thing he did not understand and did not forget: that the cold ones do not hunt the warm out of hunger, the way a shark hunts, mindless and clean. They hunt it out of *memory.* They were warm once, every one of them. They remember it. And there is no thirst in all the world like the thirst of a thing that remembers being alive.
