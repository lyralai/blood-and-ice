# Chapter 10: The Weaker, the Smarter

I am the weakest thing on the high stones.

This is the first law I learn among them. The body learns it before the head. It learns it the morning a half-grown one — younger than me, barely come into its strength — takes my wrist to move me off a warm rock it wants, and moves me, the way I move a stone off its seat at the channel, no thought in it, no malice in it, the whole of my weight nothing to the arm that lifts it. I am set down a body's length off and the young one is already not looking at me. I crouch where it set me with the wrist still ringing. And I understand, all the way down, the thing I have come up among.

I am the longest tooth in my green. I am the thing the green could not eat. Here I am the small one. Here I am the one that gets moved.

The shore taught me one shape of being and the high stones teach me the other. Down in my green I am the longest tooth. Up here strength walks past me on two legs and four. It hangs off the rock by one arm with a young one gripped to its chest. It brings down the fat-meat beast with a blow I could not throw if I threw my whole body behind it. Among the Vorth I am soft. I am slow. The hand that breaks a thing's neck on the shore would not turn a Vorth's head a finger's width.

So. I am the weakest thing on the high stones.

Good. Let me find the other thing I am.

---

I watch them work the green. I count it, the way I once counted the herd, learning the shape of it, where it is strong and where the strength runs out.

The Vorth take the fat-meat beast by main strength, and the green gives it to them because they are more and they are heavy. They pour down both sides of a channel and close it like a fist, and the thing in the channel has nowhere the strength is not. This is how they have always taken it. The young ones learn it off the big ones by the body — by doing the thing the big body does until the small body can do it too. There is no other way in them. The way is strength and the path to it is more strength, and a thing that has not got the strength has not got the way.

But strength runs out where the green goes thin and high. Up in the canopy, where the branch will not take the heavy body. Down in the deep rot, where the heavy foot goes through the skin of the ground into the soup beneath and the more you weigh the worse it holds you. Out on the bared channel-flats at low water, where the fat-meat beast is — but also the thing that is not the beast. The long quick thing. The burrow-thing. The clever meat that will not stand and be closed on, that is gone down its hole before the fist can shut.

The Vorth go hungry past those edges. I have seen a whole hand of them stand at the lip of a rot-thicket where a fat-meat beast went down to wallow, and not follow, because the ground will not hold them, and go back up empty while the beast wallows a spear-throw off — fat and stupid and safe behind nothing but the soft ground.

The green is thin in the exact places strength is no use. And I am light. I am the thing that learned the channel-flats in the dark with no one to show me, the thing that reads which stone hides a meal worth the reaching.

The body knows it before the head does. There is a place here shaped for me. Not the strong place. The other one.

---

And there is one other thing here shaped wrong, like me.

I have given him a name. Marrow — laid on him in the human tongue, the second word I have ever chosen and set on a thing on purpose. The rich thing the strong crack the bone for and leave behind. The leavings. He is a runt the tribe nearly culled at birth; he lives on his wits the way I live on mine. One shoulder rides high and uneven on him. One hand is curled and will not open all the way. He goes with a rocking, crooked gait — and then he is across the rock before you have read the gait, quick, quicker than the big ones, a quickness they have never once needed to fear.

The tribe means to be done with him.

I read it before I have the words for it. A low comes when the big ones turn their backs to him at the meat, all together, the way they turn from a thing already half outside the many. The dismissing sound lands on him and does not lift. They are driving him to the edge — out past the high stones, down to where the Skell are, the gray scuttling under-things that take what the strong grind off the bone and drag it down into the wet dark. The Skell get the leavings. And Marrow is the leavings, and the tribe has begun to hand him over the edge a little at a time, the way you let go of a thing you do not want to be seen dropping.

I will not let the green have him. Not this one. Not the only thing up here that is shaped wrong the same way I am.

---

So I take him hunting where the strong cannot.

I have made nooses on the shore, in the season I learned that some meat will not wait to be hunted and must be made to come to the hunter. A loop of gut, of plaited fiber, set in the run where the thing's own feet have worn the green pale, bent down and held by a sprung branch — so the thing puts its own neck in the loop and its own panic throws the branch, and the branch does the killing while I am asleep or away. The shore had no one to teach it to me. I taught it to the green and the green taught it back.

The burrow-thing runs the channel-edge at low water, fast and low, in and out of its hole, and no Vorth has ever taken one, because no Vorth is fast enough to close on a thing that small and that quick, and the hand that could crush it cannot catch it.

But two of us are not one of us.

I crouch at the channel-edge in the gray light and I read the run — the pale worn line in the mud where the small feet go. That is mine. I have the eye for the run; I have always had it. The setting of the loop and the bending of the branch I give to Marrow's hands, the curled one and the good one together, quick at it once I have shown him twice, his crooked fingers laying the trip lighter than my whole hand can. Then I send him wide, down the flat, to where the burrow-thing will be lying up in its hole. He goes low on the rocking gait, quick, and he scratches at the mud the way a digging thing scratches, and the burrow-thing, that has gone safe down its hole from every strong hunter the tribe ever sent, comes up out of the ground to run from the small noise — and runs its run. Toward me. Toward the loop. He baits; I read; the branch waits.

It comes up the pale line and puts its own neck in the loop and throws the branch. The branch snaps up. The thing hangs kicking and then hangs still.

And the two of us look at it, and then at each other, across the dead clever meat the green said no Vorth could have — and the thing that comes up out of me then I have no shelf for, no still-place, no grief in it at all. It comes up fast and bright and I let it. Marrow's high shoulder is shaking. The curled hand works at nothing, glad. The sound that breaks out of him is the small-one sound, the surprised-glad one, and I find I am making it back at him, the two wrong-made things laughing on the mud at the strong made fools of by a loop of fiber and a bent stick. I have not laughed since the shore. I did not know the mouth still had it in it. We did this. Small and small, where big could not go. It is the best thing I have felt since the warm closed over me, and there is nothing dark folded into it, nothing at all.

I carry the meat up to the high stones in my two hands, and Marrow rocks along quick at my side, and does not leave it again.

---

They do not know what to do with it.

I lay it down on the gray rock among them, the burrow-thing, the clever meat, the thing they have stood at the lip of the rot and watched go safe a hundred lows — and the heads come up and tip toward it, and then toward me, and there is a sound that goes around them low, half the dismissing sound and half another thing, a thing with a question bent into it.

The young one that found me comes and crouches by the burrow-thing and turns it over, looking for the wound, for the killing blow, for the place a strength went into the meat. There is no wound. There is no blow. There is only the loop — the thing the meat did to itself with my green branch for an arm. The young one cannot find where the strength was, because there was no strength, and it makes the question-sound, low, up at me. Marrow crouches at my heel and does not flinch from it now. He has fed it himself, his crooked fingers on the trip. He helped take a beast the strong could not.

And the Kala comes.

She comes down off the high shelf where she keeps the still ones, the great gray-silver bulk of her, and she crouches by the burrow-thing, and she does not turn it over looking for the blow. She looks at the loop. She lays one gray finger on the plaited fiber and follows it up off the neck of the dead thing, up the length of it to the frayed end where I cut it, and then she lifts the finger and looks at me — at the man-shape that made the loop, and at the small wrong-made one crouched beside him — and a thing goes over the milk-edged eyes that I have only ever had from her, the keeping one, turned now a new way. Turned the way the old gray one turned its head over the eye-and-thorn at my chest. Toward a thing it did not look for and now half-sees.

She makes a sound at me. Not the gentling sound. A new one. Short, up at the end, laid on me plain. I do not have it yet, I do not have the word, but I have its shape, and its shape is *what is this, that you do.*

And I do the thing I am learning to do. I show her.

---

I take her down to the channel-edge at the next low water, and Marrow comes, and we make the noose in front of her.

I do not have the words to say it. The words are coming — slow, stolen off their tongues one by one, *low* and *high* and *come* and *the gentling*, laid in the empty place behind my teeth where the name was torn out, a sound at a time, a true one — but I do not have the words for *this is a run* and *this is a trip* and *the thing kills itself while you sleep*. So I do not say it. We do it. Marrow sets the loop, quick, his curled hand and his good one. I read the run and lay my finger along the pale line the way she laid hers along the loop. I bend the green branch down, and I take her great hand — and she lets me take it, the hand that could close and end me, she lets the weak thing fold it down to the notch — and I notch it under her palm so she feels the spring of it, the held strength of the bent green waiting to be let go.

We go up the bank, the three of us, and crouch in the fern, the great gray bulk of her gone still beside us. And the burrow-thing comes, and runs its run, and throws the branch, and the loop takes it — and the Kala makes a sound I have never heard from her, a sound that goes up out of the great chest fast and breaks at the top. I know it. I have heard the small ones make it when a thing they did not look for comes good, when the green opens its hand and gives. It is the sound of a thing surprised into gladness.

The strongest thing I have ever been held by, and the weakest two on the high stones have made it glad — with a loop of plaited fiber and a bent green branch and the patience the shore beat into me. The branch does the killing. The bent green is the arm we do not have. This is the place that is shaped for us: where the strong thing's strength runs out, and we stand in it and hand them what is on the far side.

---

After the loop they let me into the work a new way.

It is slow. I am still the weakest. The young ones still move me off the warm rock, still take what they want out of my hands with no thought in it. But the question-sound follows me now where the dismissing sound followed me before. When a fat-meat beast goes down into a rot-thicket the heavy bodies cannot follow, the heads tip toward me. When a thing they cannot catch runs a run, they look at the man-shape and its loops, and at the small one rocking quick at his side. I am not strong and they have stopped waiting for me to be. They have begun to wait for the other thing.

I bring them the high-canopy meat the branch will not give the heavy body, because I am light and I learned the climb in a green that meant to drop me. I bring them the burrow-thing and the clever meat off the flats, Marrow's hands on every trap. And I bring them, one low, the red light.

This is the thing that turns it.

Their fire is kept — fed, banked, tended on the high gray, the held red light I counted across all that drowning green when I had only a coal nursed in a fist of fiber. They have had it longer than the oldest of them has been alive. It came down to them off the dead, hand to hand, low to low, and not one of them knows how it was first lit. But a fire that is only kept is a fire that can die, and theirs has come near it, low and starving in the long wet when the green will not dry. I have watched them feed it wrong, pile the soaked green on it and watch it sulk and gutter and shrink — because they keep fire, they do not make it, and a thing you only keep you do not understand. You understand a thing by making it. The shore made me make my fire a thousand times before it let me keep one.

So when theirs is dying in the wet, I go down into my green and bring up the dry. The dead heart-wood of the fallen giant. The fat-soaked fiber. The rendered fat of the fat-meat beast — the things that take the flame and hold it through the wet. I feed their dying red slow, from the heart out, and the red stands up off the gray ash and burns high and clean, the highest it has burned in lows, and the warmth lays on all of us up on the high stones in the wet dark.

And the heads, all of them, even the king's, high on the bare gray — the heads tip toward the man-shape that made the dead red live. Not like they tip toward the strong one. A new way. The way a thing tips its head toward something it does not understand and wants.

I have given them the one thing the strong cannot take by strength. You cannot be strong at fire. The fat-meat beast will not yield to a blow. There is no main-strength way to make the dead red live in the wet, and I have made it live — the weak thing, the small one that gets moved off the warm rock. And the warm rock is mine now, the low after the fire, because the young one that would have moved me looks at me first, and does not.

---

The seer comes to me again at the dark, after the fire.

The old gray one. The oldest I have seen, the body sunk in on itself, the milk-pale eyes still moving, still reading. It comes to where I sit in the new warmth of the fire I fed, and it crouches a long while, and it reads me slow. It lays its gray finger again, not on me, on the disc at my chest — the eye, ringed, looking, and the thorn driven through the looking, the mark that buzzes low in the drowned place of me. *I know this. This is mine.* And no when, and no whose.

And then it does a new thing. It turns the gray finger off the disc and lays it against the side of my head. Light. Not where the strength is. Where the other thing is. And it makes a sound, low, the same long sound it made over the mark — and it looks from the side of my head to the loop of plaited fiber still hung at my belt, and back to my head, and it makes the sound again.

I lean toward what it knows. I want it. I press at it the way I press my tongue at the gap where the name was. The old one has read the wrong thing in me — the green that could not eat me, the fever-air that drops every other body and leaves me clear, the venom that leaves no mark, the wounds that draw shut clean where every other wound goes to rot, the disc that knows me — and it has read the other thing too, the loop and the trip and the fed fire, and it has set them side by side. The thing that should not be, and the thing that thinks. It knows them, in whatever way the old gray blood knows, for two halves of one strangeness. It bows the great gray head over both at once, slow.

I strain after it and the strain finds nothing. There are no words in me for what the finger means. The buzzing rises under the mark and gives me no when, no whose, only *mine, mine,* and then it sinks and leaves me holding the shape of a question with no other side to it. I cannot turn it over — not yet, not with the few sounds I have. But I keep it whole instead of letting it go, the way I keep every light now instead of counting the lights between.

---

The Kala folds down beside me in the firelight when the seer has gone, the great gray-silver bulk of her, and lays the arm over me, and makes the gentling sound, the keeping one. Marrow rocks in close on my other side, where the warm reaches, and is let stay. And I lie against the warm that knows it holds me, with the slow vast heart going through my ear, the loop at my belt and the fed fire high and the warm rock mine for the keeping — inside the many that should have swept me, that have begun instead to wait on the thing only I can hand them.

I am still the weakest thing on the high stones. The young one will move me off the rock again, some low, with no thought in it. I will never throw the blow that brings the fat-meat beast down. I will never turn a Vorth's head a finger's width with my hand.

But there is a low pale line worn in the mud at the channel-edge where the clever meat runs, and a bent green branch waits over it with our loop set light across the run, and while I lie here in the warm the branch is the arm we do not have, doing the work the strong cannot do, in the place the strong cannot go. The shore taught me a law and I bring it up to the high stones whole. *Where you are not strong, be the thing the strong needs.*

I lay the law down behind my teeth, in the gap where the name was, beside the stolen sounds and the gentling sound that is not mine and the warm that chose to hold me. And the second mouth — the one no kill ever fed, that opened wide and empty over all that drowning green and would not close on meat or water — lies quiet under my ribs now. Full. Because they have stopped looking past me. They have begun to look at me. Not the longest tooth. The other thing. The thing only I can hand them.

The weaker, the smarter. On the shore the law was hand and tooth, and the strongest thing keeps the ground. Here is the new law, laid beside the old: *the strong takes what it can reach; the weak learns to make the green hand over the rest.* And the rest is the larger part. The rest is everything past the edge of the arm.

It is enough to be known by. It is more than enough. And beside me, where the firelight reaches, the small wrong-made thing I would not hand to the Skell sleeps with his curled hand open, glad, in the warm I fed.
