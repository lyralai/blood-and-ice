# Chapter 24: The Bait

The keeper stops setting meat at my line. He goes up the green and takes the one warm thing that ever filled the hole under my breastbone, and lays *that* out bleeding instead.

---

I read it in the gut first, then the head. By the green doing the thing it does not do.

There is a sound in the high country that does not belong there. I am down in the drowned roots over the bone-spit when it comes, two lows after I last ate the keeper's open hand and walked back up clear with the dull-water sliding off me, and the body has me out of the mud before the head finds the shape of it. A sound from the high stones. From my high stones. The squared dead and the kept fire and the great gray bulk of the Kala folded among the still ones — up there, where no spit reaches and no hooked iron has ever crouched at a bled line, where the tribe lays its dead faced to the sun and the small ones run between the legs of the big — up there a sound is going up that I know in the deep buried place where I know the gap behind my teeth.

The iron-on-stone sound. The net sound. The owning sound.

They have gone up.

I read this whole island for the slavers, low after low, and I read it wrong. The wrongness lands behind my breastbone like a stone dropped in still water, the rings going out and out. I thought the spit was the line. I thought the green was the wall — that the killing island that drops every other body, the fever-air and the venom and the bad water I alone walk clear through, was a wall the owning kind could not climb. That they would lean and lean at the edge of my green with the open hand and the dull-water and never get past the drowning death of it to the high stones. That up there, at least, the family was safe behind the same green that had tried to kill me my whole life and failed.

But the green does not kill them fast. It kills them slow. And while it killed them slow they walked up it, low after low, patient, and now the owning sound is going up off the high stones, and the green did not stop them, and the family was never behind the wall at all. The family was only further up the road.

I am moving before the thought finishes. Up out of the roots, up the bare gray, up my own green by the runs my own feet wore into it. There is no near mouth and no far mouth in me now. There is only the thing under both, the thing with no mouth at all, the thing that runs.

A small wrong gait comes up the green at my heel. I do not look. I do not have to. The high shoulder, the curled hand, the rocking quickness — Marrow, off his crown and onto my track the moment the sound went up, the way he has come every time the ground tilted under me. He says nothing. There is nothing to say.

---

I come up over the last shelf into the high stones and crouch in the lean of the great trees against the rock, where the shadow is, and I read it whole in one long breath, the body taking the shape of a thing before it goes down into it.

The kept fire is scattered. Kicked through, the red coals raked across the gray and going to ash — the held light the tribe made and tended and never let die across all the lows I counted it from the drowning green, out now. The squared dead are pulled from their faced rows. The small gifts the tribe lays among them, set squared and facing, are kicked loose and scattered down the stone. And there are owning shapes up here, six of them, eight, more climbing the road behind, with the hooked iron and the long net and the lashing-rope coiled on the shoulder. They stand squared and weight-even on the ground the tribe bled into being, taking up the room as if the high stones owe them the standing.

And the Kala is down.

She is down on the cleanest high shelf, the one the sun strikes first, where her small one is laid faced among the dead. Down on her side across the very stone she keeps. The net is over her, the great gray-silver bulk of her tangled in it, the long arm I have lain under fighting the mesh and the mesh holding. Two hooked irons are in her. I see them from the shadow — the barbed iron driven into the great shoulder and the haunch, the turned barb gone in to stay, to drag, to keep a live thing on the line, the dark blood welling slow and thick down the gray hide and pooling in the seams of the stone. She is making a sound. Low. Not the gentling sound, not the keeping one she laid over me in the fever. The other one. The one with the tear in it, the one she made over the dead small thing — but turned outward now, thrown up at the owning shapes that stand over her, and it goes nowhere. The high stones swallow it.

They have not killed her. That is the thing I read last and it is the worst, and the body has it before the head can shape why. Two irons in her and she is bleeding into the stone and they have not finished her. A thing they wanted dead, they would have finished. The great heart is right there under the gray hide and the iron knows where the heart is. They put the iron in her shoulder. They put it in her haunch. They put it where it holds and drags and keeps and does not kill, and they stand over her with the net holding and they do not close.

They are not looking at her at all. They are looking at the green.

The squared shapes stand over the downed Kala with their backs half-turned to her and their faces turned out, down the road, down the bare gray to the high drowned roots, to the dark — to me. To the place the shape lives that wears the keeper's mark and does not run and does not break, the thing the owned girl walked twice down the killing spit to stand at the wall of. They are not looking at the thing in the net.

They are looking at the thing the net is set to catch.

---

It is bait.

I know it the way I know the rot-pool from the drink, the way I knew the steaming meat at my line was bait — in the gut a long breath before the head lays it plain. And the head lays it plain, hard and flat and whole. There is no part of the law I do not already have. I made myself a king of the wild things on this law. The staked thing that screams to bring the bigger thing. The kill left open on the trail to draw the dispute up out of the rot-thickets. Bait is a thing of theirs set out as a thing of yours, set where the wanting will reach for it — and the keeper has laid out the one thing on this whole green that the wanting will reach for through any net, any iron, any law.

He read me right. He has read me right low after low, and I knew it, and I leaned anyway. He set the warm meat at my line and the dull-water in the bright gourd and the open hand, and the body slid all of it, the dull-thing pouring off me clear, the hook smelled out and named whole — and he watched the bark go clean each low and learned the thing under the body that does not slide. He learned the shape in the green has a hunger he can feed. And a man who reads that a thing has a hunger he can feed reads, after, *what* the hunger is for. He read the road between me and the owned girl and cut it. And he read the road up the green to the high stones, to the great gray thing the small wrong man-shape came down off its crown to guard, and he did not cut that road.

He walked up it. He took the warm thing off the end of it. He laid it bleeding on the stone where the wanting cannot help but reach.

The hook is not in the meat. The hook is in the thing I love. I want to turn it over and find the seam in it — the trick of it, the under-side, the place the law shows me how to slide it the way it has shown me every other hook this man has set. I press at it crouched in the shadow and it does not give. There is no smelling this one out and walking back up clear. The body has no head for what to do with it. Only the shape of its teeth.

---

I count the owning shapes from the shadow, because counting is what I do with a thing I cannot hold.

Eight up on the high stones, four more on the road. Twelve. The road behind them is not empty. There are more in the green below — I hear the iron of them, the net-men set wide the way they set wide at my line, holding the gaps, no shape drifting, no road left open they have not stood a body across. The patient one is among them. I cannot see him but I know his hand in the laying of this, the slow sure weighing-on, the net set not to drop in a panic when the dark comes but to close slow around a thing that has already walked into it. He has not set this net to catch a thing that runs. He has set it to catch a thing that comes.

He knows I will come. That is the whole of the bait. He does not need to drive me or crowd me or push me to the cliff. He has read that there is one thing in the green the wanting reaches for through any wall, and he has laid that thing bleeding in the open with the net wide around it, and he has gone still, and he waits for the reach.

I am the dispute now. I am the bigger thing the screaming brings. He staked the Kala out under the open sky on the cleanest high shelf, and he is the green, and I am the thing that cannot leave the screaming alone.

Beside me Marrow has gone still. Not the locked stillness of a thing that fears — the other stillness, the reading one, the high shoulder dropped low and the curled hand working slow against his thigh, his eyes going from the net to the wide-set iron in the green below and back, counting it the way I count it, and finding the same answer. He looks at the net and he will not look at me. That is the whole of what he has to say and he says it with his body: *this is the hook, and you are already in the water.* He does not pull my arm. He knows I read it. He stays.

---

I look back down my own green. The deep country lies below, the killing heart of it, and the thought comes that always comes.

I could go.

The old free thought. The high-circling one, the one that wears the wings of the wide-sailing carrion-birds that ride the heat off the canopy and answer to no ground at all. I let it come, because letting a thing come is how I read it true. I could go. Down off the high stones, back into my green, into the deep drowned country where no road runs and the fever-air lies thickest and the bad water stands in the still pools breathing up the death that takes them and not me. The owning kind cannot follow me there. They walk up the green slow, paying a low of fever and a low of rot for every length, and the deep green would lay them down. They could close their net on the high stones and find a bleeding monster and an empty road where the man-shape was, and never once lay the hook in me. No hand on the back of my neck, ever again. Free as the bird is free, riding the heat with nothing under it and nothing it owes.

And the Kala is on the stone with two irons in her, making the torn sound at the sky.

I crouch at the edge of my own high country and I look at the great gray bulk of her tangled in the net, the long arm fighting the mesh, the dark blood going thick down the hide into the seams of the stone, and I do not move toward the deep green.

Because she came down off the high shelf in the fever-dark and folded the great arm over the burning man-shape the green tried to kill and could not. Because she made the gentling sound over the top of my head, low, up and down, no edges, the keeping one, over and over, when every fast part of me screamed *hands, hold, the weight on the neck* — and she was not the hand. She was never the hand. Because the hole under my breastbone that no kill ever filled and no water ever filled, the hole that ate me from the inside while the green ate me from the out, went still against her warm. The only time in all my life I can reach that it went still. It lay down quiet under my ribs and was full. Because there is a place in her shaped like a thing taken, and a place in me shaped like a thing taken, and we lay down in each other's hole and neither of us ever had a word for it and neither of us ever needed one.

The keeper does not know any of that. He cannot read the body, and he cannot read the warm that knows it holds me. But he read the *want*. He saw the dark move up the green to guard her and not move to guard itself, and he laid the want bleeding on the stone, and he is right.

---

I have it whole, then. Crouched in the shadow with the scattered fire going to ash and the owning shapes turned out to the dark, waiting for my reach. I have it the way I have the spit and the seep and the loose rocks — every tooth of it. And the having does not change one thing the body means to do.

This is the law and I will not lie to myself: *the thing you reach for is the thing with the hook in it.* The oldest law I own, the one I made a king on. And folded inside it, deeper than any word I ever stole off the tribe's tongues, is the thing the law was never able to hold — that some things you reach for anyway. Some hooks you take in your own teeth with your eyes open, knowing the barb is turned, knowing the patient one is still in the green with the net wide, knowing the hand is already half-closed on the back of your neck. Because the thing on the end of the hook is the one thing the world ever set warm against the hole under your breastbone, and a thing that has filled that hole once is a thing you do not leave on the stone. Not for the deep green. Not for the high bird's freedom. Not for never feeling the hand again.

I am not fooled. That is the part I make myself hold. I read the whole of it — the bait, the spacing, the patient one's hand, the hook that no smelling-out can pull. I am not a beast walking blind onto a staked line. I am a thing that has counted the irons and named the trap and chosen the trap, because the alternative is to leave her on the stone, and I cannot, and he knew I could not before he ever climbed my green. That is the only thing he got. Not my eyes. My want.

Behind me, off down the high road, comes another sound — and this one I did not expect. Not iron. Not net. The murmur of the tribe gathered at the green's edge below the high stones, the strong ones, the prime blood, drawn up by the owning sound the same as I was drawn. And then the sound stops. They do not come up. I read the stillness off the night the way I read it off Marrow's body — Voss-Marr's hand in it, the proud shape that will not put his strong onto a road that runs for a human king's grief. The Kala is mine, not theirs. The slavers came up my green, for my mark, for the hunger they baited in me — and the strong of the tribe weigh the cost of climbing into a net set for the wrong-blood thing that took their crown, and they hang back. The murmur turns and goes down. They leave the high stones to me and the one small wrong shape at my side who would never once think to leave.

So it is just us. The runt I named and the king the tribe will not march for. I crouch in the shadow of my own high stones with the whole trap drawn clear in the gut and the head both, and I do the thing the bait was set for, the thing he staked her out to make me do, the thing I would do again.

I start down toward her. Down off the one high edge where no hand could reach me, down toward the net and the hooked iron and the patient one waiting in the green, toward the great gray warm that knows it holds me, bleeding on the stone — by my own feet, by my own choosing, with my eyes wide open. Marrow drops off the shelf at my shoulder, the rocking gait quick and sure on the bad ground, and does not ask me to stop, and does not stay behind.

And that is the reach that lays the only hook in me that was ever made to hold.
