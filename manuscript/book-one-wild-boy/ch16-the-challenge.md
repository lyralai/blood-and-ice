# Chapter 16: The Challenge

A thing weighed and not struck does not go away. It comes up the rock toward you a low at a time. Slow. No hurry, no sound, no bottom you can stand on, and the waiting for it is its own kind of dying.

I have lived in that waiting since the dead head of the Hesh cooled on the clean stone. The king did not strike then; the whole tribe was turned toward the small scarred thing that brought the apex down, and the strength-king cannot lay his hand on a thing the tribe has turned toward. So he went up onto his broken lip and let the lows go by, waiting for the gladness of the killed apex to thin off the high stones, so that when he came down at last he would come down onto a thing the tribe had set back down.

I read that law off him a season back — the low he weighed me and chose not to strike. That is old knowing now, laid behind my teeth. What is new is this: the seer has drawn its line, and a king does not weigh forever.

So I do not let it be set down.

---

There is a thing in the way of the king, and the thing has a name, and the name is Voss-Marr.

He is Gull-Marr's prime blood, strongest of the half-grown, the one the whole rock has watched grow into the next king the way you watch a sapling grow into the tree that will own the light. The line runs through him. When the old strength tires off the high stones, it is Voss it comes down to — every low of his life has been the rock telling him so. He stood beside me against the Hesh and his cunning was almost a brother's for a hunt; then it soured, because a thing was happening on the stones his whole blood had no law for. The wrong small thing was coming up the line he was born standing on.

That is the line I am leaping. Not the king's killing — the king's *place*. To challenge the king straight, over Voss's head, is to step over the heir as if the line he was bred into were a run laid in the mud for someone else's feet. Voss has stood at the foot of that line his whole life, waiting for his turn at the top. I do not mean to wait at the foot. I mean to take the top while he is still standing where the rock told him to stand.

He knows it before I do anything. I feel it come off him across the crowded rock low after low, the heir's weather turning hard — the proud set of him gone stiff, the air around him cooling toward me where the Kala cannot warm it. *That was mine. The line was mine. You bled beside me and now you reach past me for the thing I was made for.* He does not say it. He does not have to. It is in how he will no longer set his eyes on me, and in how the strong ones who run at his shoulder go quiet when I come up the rock — weighing the small thing as their heir weighs it, a thing that has stepped onto a run it was never bred to walk.

I do not unstep. The line he was born into is the line between me and the home I will not give back.

---

In the lows after the Hesh I do not have a word for the thing I do. I have the weather of it, and the weather is the weather I have worked since the green first tried to drop me: I make the weak thing needed, low after low, so the turning toward me cannot go quiet.

I read the country the strength cannot cross, and I keep reading it out loud. When the heavy old male with the torn ear takes the half-grown down to the rot-thickets to learn the fat-meat beast, I go ahead and lay the firm run with peeled root so the light feet can find it, and the big ones come behind me set foot for foot over ground their weight has no law for. They come back up the rock fed off a country that went hungry past them every season before me. When the small one with the quick heart goes high after the sap-nest and the limb gives, I read the give before the eye and am under the falling small one with my arms out, and the Kala makes the long meaning-sound over the small one kept, not-squared, not-faced-to-the-sun. I make the loops. I bank the fire. I mark the deep rot's still mouths with a squared stone, so the heavy ones learn the lie of the ground from a thing the weak thing set down for them.

Every one is a small gift laid where the whole rock can see it. I do not lay them for the gladness; I stopped needing that the first season. I lay them across the place where the king means to come down, so the tribe is still turned when he does. And I lay them where Voss must watch — the heir fed off the runt's run, the next-king learning his own ground from the wrong small hands. Each one shifts the line a finger's width off him and onto me.

I learned this on the shore, with the deep rot and the heavy thing too strong to lift. You do not stand against the weight. You make the ground it stands on do the standing. The king is the weight. The turning is the ground. I keep the ground from going firm under him.

---

The seer is the thing that breaks the waiting.

The ash-pale head comes across the crowded rock in the half-dark before a light, slow, the old bulk moving careful through the sleeping piles, and folds down at the lip where I lie in the warm with the Kala's tide at my back. The pale finger comes to the Eye-and-Thorn bound at my chest, the relic off the dead expedition, the wreck's one made thing the seer reads as a kin-omen. It makes the long meaning-sound over it, the *means*, the sound with no edge of asking in it, only the weight of a thing that is.

I lean into the *means*, wanting it to open. There is a knowing under that finger, an old buried thing about the disc and the dead men who carried it and the why of me — I press my own head at it, hard, the way you press a tongue to the gap where a tooth was, and the knowing buzzes and gives nothing. I have no word to ask it with. The seer does not carry the answer; the seer only carries that there is one. I want the rest. I am held off it by the gap in my own mouth, and the wanting is sharper than any refusing would be.

Then the finger lifts off my chest and turns. Not out into the dark — the thing in the dark is in the soup of the tribe's bellies now. Up. Toward the broken lip where the heavy flat-eyed shape stands black against the high green. The seer makes the meaning-sound up at the king, then down at me, then draws the line between us, slow, a run drawn in the mud for a thing that cannot read it yet.

*This one. And that one. And the two are one telling, and the telling is not done.*

And the finger does one more thing it did not do the low of the Hesh. On its way back it passes over Voss-Marr asleep at the foot of his line — and does not stop. It crosses the heir as if he were ground, and lays the telling on the king and the wrong small thing, and leaves the bred one lying in the run the omen stepped over. I have the whole shape of it. There is a telling on the high stones, and it does not run through the heir the rock built. It runs through me. The line is being leapt in front of the only one who can read omens, and the leaping is being named.

The seer folds the pale hand back and goes still. The king on the broken lip has watched the whole of it — the line drawn over the sleeping heir, the omen laid on the wrong small thing. A named thing cannot be left to thin.

He comes down off his broken lip.

---

He does not rush. The strong never rush the thing they have already weighed. He crosses the clean stone, the certain weight sliding, and the gentling goes out of the high stones ahead of him the way it goes out before the storm — the small ones pressing back into the piles, the bold one already gone off my shoulder and up the rock. Far off at the lip, Voss-Marr has come awake and risen, and he does not come down. He stands at the foot of his line and watches his father go to settle the thing that should have been his to settle, on the stone that should have been his stone, and the watching is a wound. He will remember that he stood still while the wrong thing was answered as an equal and he was passed like ground.

The Kala's tide stops at my back. The great gray-silver bulk of her comes up over me, the keeping arm closing, and she makes a sound I have not heard her make — low, with the teeth in it, set between the king and the thing she chose. She would stand between us. The thing that carried me out of the fever-fog would put her own great body in the run the king is sliding down.

I lay my scarred hand on the gray-silver arm and make the still-sound back at her, soft, falling, the one she taught me — *be still, you are held* — turned the wrong way this low, the small thing telling the great one to be still. Because if she stands between us, the king strikes her, and the law of the strength reads her standing as proof of the thing he means to prove: that the wrong small thing has no standing of its own, that the turning is borrowed off her bulk. If she fights for me, I am a kept thing on lent rock that pours away the moment the great arm is moved.

I will not be a kept thing. I have been a kept thing — the hand closed on the back of the neck, the brand laid in the flat tongue, *mine, be still, come, you are a thing*, the old barb turning in the warm dark behind my teeth where the three are gone. I take my hand off her arm and move out from under the keeping, onto the clean stone, the smallest thing on the high stones, alone on the open rock in front of the whole turning.

The Kala's sound dies in the great chest. She holds. I feel the holding cost her. She lets me stand out of her arm onto the killing stone, because she has read the same law I have: the only standing worth the name is the one no arm carries.

---

He stops over me.

The flat eyes go down the great length of him to the small scarred thing on the open stone, and there is no warmth in them and no letting-stand, only the weighing — but it is a new weighing now, not the old one. A season back he weighed a thing and chose not to strike it. Tonight the choosing is gone. He weighs a thing he has already decided to lift, and the question is not *can it stand* but *how does the standing end*. He is half again my height and many times my weight, the heavy bulk of the high canopy and the broken ridges, the strength that is the whole law of this rock. He could close the great hand on the back of my neck and end the telling in a breath.

He does not close the hand. That is the thing I read, and the reading sets the shape of what comes. If he closed the hand he would be ending me off-hand, with no thought in it, and the tribe has seen the wrong thing bring down the apex he turned away from — it would read the closed hand as the king putting away a thing he could not stand beside. He cannot put me away quiet. The seer has named the telling. He has to win it in front of the turning, in front of his own heir, so the whole rock reads that the line still belongs to the strong and not to the small thing that walked across it.

So he lifts the great head and makes the sound.

It comes up slow out of the deep of the chest, lower than the gentling, lower than the asking, a sound with the whole weight of the high stones in it and the teeth out — not the careful teeth he laid on the old male the low he reminded the strong whose ground it was. The full teeth. The bare ones. It rolls down the clean stone and the whole tribe goes still under it, the green going still under the storm before the storm breaks, and I do not have their word for the sound but I have every grain of its weather: *here. This stone. You and me. Now, in front of them all.*

The challenge. Laid down bare on the clean stone in front of the whole turning, and in front of the heir who should have stood here first.

And the thing the body does then, before the head can find the words to be afraid with — it does not step back.

---

There is a place where the deciding sits, and it is not in the head. The head has all the law. The head knows I am the smallest thing on the high stones, that there is more strength in the king's one arm than in the whole of me, that the body cannot win this the way it has won everything else — by being light and reading the ground. There is no ground to read on the clean stone. No rot to make the mouth, no high seat to turn the strength on. Only the open rock and the king and the small thing, and the king's whole law that on the open rock the strength is the way.

The head knows all of it, and the body does not step back.

It does not step back because of the far mouth, the one no kill ever fed, the one that leaned out of me my whole life into the empty and found nothing to lean on — it found a thing to lean on here. The Kala chose me before there was a thing to choose me for. The old male lifted my wrist before the whole bank and named me. The tribe turned, and the turning was the first ground my whole life that did not pour away the moment a hand opened. A thing too light to fight for itself is not too light to fight for the only home it has ever had. If I step back, the king's law reads it true: the wrong thing has no standing of its own, the turning was borrowed, the home is lent. And then the ground pours away and I am property again, the brand laid back on me in the only tongue this rock knows.

I will not give the home back. I gave a name away once to the hands that took me, all but the one behind the teeth, and I have spent every low since taking the rest of myself back off the killing ground a day at a time.

So I hold the name behind my teeth, in the gap where the three are gone, the one un-stolen thing, *Rook*, the word my mother gave me before the ship, before the hands. I bite down on it the stone-bite, the one you bite when the body wants to bolt and the bolting is the death of you. And I lift my own small head to the great flat-eyed bulk over me, the smallest thing on the high stones, with nothing in my two hands and no ground to read and no arm to stand inside, and I make the sound back.

It comes up out of me low and torn, scraping over the gap where the teeth are gone, nothing of the king's weight in it and all of his meaning. *Here. This stone. You and me.* The asking thrown back as an answering. The challenge taken.

The whole tribe reads it. The smallest, softest, wrongest thing on the high stones has stood out of the keeping arm onto the open rock and answered the strength-king's challenge in his own bare tongue, over the head of the heir who was bred to stand here, and the rock goes so still I can hear the steam working off the green below and the far small water in the channels and the slow vast tide of the Kala's breath behind me, holding, not coming between. At the foot of his line Voss-Marr has gone stiff as carved stone. Whatever was left of the near-brother is burning off him now. I do not look at it. But I feel it set, the way you feel a thing harden that will not soften again.

---

The king holds the weighing look on me a long breath after the answer, and a thing moves far back in the flat eyes that is the nearest the strength-stone comes to letting-stand: what one strong reads in another when the other does not break. He came down sure the wrong thing would step back, that one bare challenge laid full-toothed would set the telling back to *is* and not *could*. It did not step back. It stood out of the arm and answered.

I have not won a thing. The answer is not the winning. The strength is still his whole stone and there is still more in his one arm than in the whole of me, and the clean stone is still the ground my reading cannot read. The telling the seer named is not done. It is begun — laid down bare between us on the open rock, to be finished body to body in front of the turning, the next low or the low after, coming now as sure as the tide. No longer weighed and waiting. Said aloud, and answered, and owed.

He turns his great heavy body and goes back up onto his broken lip, slow, the certain weight sliding, and he does not look back. It is not the dismissing not-looking of the first season. It is the not-looking of a strong thing that has been answered on its own stone and must now go and weigh, again, the thing it was sure it had finished weighing.

The Kala folds down over me when the king is up, the keeping arm coming over the small thing that stood out of it. She does not make the still-sound this low, the *nothing comes*, because the thing that comes is named now and a thing on the high stones does not lie to the kept young about the named thing. She makes the other sound, the low even one with the gladness folded in — only it is laid on me soft, and there is a thing under the gladness I have no shelf for: what a strong one lays on a thing that stood where it did not have to stand. I make it back, low and torn, two falls scraping over the gap where three teeth were gone, the far mouth full at last, the near one easy.

The bold small one comes back to my shoulder in the dark when the rock has gone quiet, the quick heart easy against my neck, the keeping-place become the thing it keeps. I lie in the warm with the near and the far, the small one's quick heart and the Kala's slow vast tide, and I lay the new law down behind my teeth, in the gap where the name lies, the hardest the high stones have taught me yet:

*A standing no arm carries is a standing you must stand to the end alone. The home worth keeping is the one you must step out of the keeping arm to keep.*

I do not sleep. I lie in the warm with the challenge laid down bare on the clean stone, and over all of it I lay the next going-up, the one the seer drew the line to — body to body on the open rock where my whole life of reading the ground is worth nothing.

I have brought my green's apex up the rock dead, and the high stones have only the one thing left on them stronger than what I killed. I have answered it in its own tongue, over the head of the one who was made to stand there. There is no further left to go but the one. The strength-king is up on his broken lip in the dark, weighing. At the foot of his line the heir lies awake too, I think, learning the hard new law — that a line is only yours until a thing with no right to it walks across. The telling will be finished the next low or the low after, on the open rock where the only thing in my two hands is the home I will not give back.
