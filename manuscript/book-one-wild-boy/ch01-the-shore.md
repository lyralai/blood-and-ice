# Chapter 1: The Shore

Heat wakes me.

Not all at once. In pieces. The face first — wet, slick, a film of warm water standing on the skin that is not sweat and not sea but something thicker, the breath of the place gone to water and laid down on me like a hand. Then the smell. Rot and green and bloom and something gone sweet past sweet, the smell of too much living all pressed together and dying at the same time, like a hold full of bodies, except this is a hold the size of the world. Salt in the cracks of my lips. Under the salt, the iron taste of me, the taste of the inside of my own mouth gone out into the air where it should not be.

I open my eyes.

Green. Green over me, green and gold and black, a roof of it so high the sky is only rumor, only a wet white glare leaking down through a thousand leaves that do not move. Nothing moves. The air does not move. It lies on me. It goes into my mouth thick enough to chew and does not want to come back out. There is no wind in it, no relief in it. Only the green, going up and up, dripping, breathing, the great wet weight of it pressing down to hold me where I have been dropped.

I close my eyes. The heat comes for the rest of me, and takes its time, like a careful hand clearing a table one piece at a time so nothing falls and makes a sound.

---

Mud under me. Black mud. Not sand, not deck, not the straw of a cage. Mud and root and the half-rotted mat of fallen leaves, warm as a body, soft as a body, and under the soft a tangle of roots hard as bone laid in ridges to meet my back. They press through the thin wet rag I wear, through the skin, and hold me pinned to the warm of the world.

I tell my left hand to close. It closes. I tell my right. Nothing. I ask it again, and at last it answers, one finger at a time, slow as a thing waking from the dead, the knuckles grinding like the joints are full of grit. The pain comes up the arm and into my teeth. It is enormous. I am glad of it. I have never been glad of anything like this. The pain is mine. The slavers did not give it to me and they cannot take it back. It is the first thing in a long time that is only mine.

My tongue finds the gaps in my teeth. Three. Two below, one at the side. The holes are fresh — blood and socket and a whistle of air where teeth used to be, the gum soft and torn, and when I press them the hurt is bright and far down in the jaw. I do not remember them going. I remember a hand. I remember the floor coming up. The rest the sea has taken and will not give back. I spit. Red on black. The red sits on the mud a moment, the only true color in all that green and rot, and then a thread of black ants is on it before I have finished looking, and it is gone.

I do not ask *where am I*. There is no use in it. The body knows this work and does not wait for words. Hurt, wet, wrong, wake, move. Move or be meat. That is the whole of it. That is everything I have been taught and everything I am.

I move.

---

The ground runs down to the water in long shelves of black mud, slick with green slime and the wet ropes of roots that come down out of the trees and feel into the dark water like fingers hunting the bottom. The water is brown. Not the live green-black of open sea — brown, still, skinned over with a sheen that throws the white glare back oily, and where the roots break the surface it does not splash so much as swallow. Things move in it that I do not see, only the lines they leave, a slow seam opening across the still skin and closing again. It is not a sea. It is the place where the green has drowned and gone on living under the water, until there is no telling which has eaten which.

I get my knees under me. I get my feet. The world swings once, hard, and I put a hand to a root and wait for it to hold still, and it does, slowly, like the floor of the ship coming back level between the rolls.

I breathe out. The air is so wet and so hot that breathing is half drowning. The bad air of a fever-place — I have smelled it, in the hold, the swamp-breath that rises off still water at night and lays men down shaking and does not let them up. This is that air, thick with it, the rot-vapor standing so heavy I can see it, a haze low among the trunks like smoke that will not rise. I breathe it in. I breathe it out. I wait for the swimming behind the eyes, the cotton in the head, the first crawl of the shaking-sickness that this air carries the way the cage carried lice. I have seen it take a grown man between one watch and the next. I wait for it.

It does not come. My head stays clear. The air goes in foul and comes out and leaves nothing behind. I stand in the worst breathing-place I have ever stood in and my eyes do not swim and my hands do not shake, and somewhere under the hurt a small thing in me goes still, and listens — the way an animal goes still at a sound it does not yet have a shape for.

Then something cracks down through the high leaves and a great fall of warm rain comes all at once, no warning, just the sky letting go, and it hits my face and I flinch — not from the wet, from the sound. The sound is the whole world at once, a great green roar of water beating on a million leaves, with no edge to it and no end, and none of it is for me. The water does not care. The green does not care. I have been dropped here like a thing dropped, and the world will take me apart slow and never once look up from its work.

*Good*, I think — not in words, in the set of my jaw, in the way the back teeth come together. *Let it try.* I have been taken apart by hands that meant it. The world does not even mean it. The world is only weather. I have outlasted worse than weather.

---

Thirst comes before hunger. The hunger is there — it is always there, low, a second mouth somewhere under the first — but the thirst is louder, a dry rasp pulling at the back of my throat, and the body will be heard in its order.

The rain is a cruelty. It comes down all around me, more water than I have ever seen fall, and not a drop of it I can drink, for it runs off the leaves and down the trunks and into the brown still water that I know without being told will twist my gut if I take it. I crawl along the mud-shelf with my mouth open like a fool, catching what I can off the broad leaves, off the cup a torn leaf makes. It is warm and it tastes of the green it has fallen through, bitter, but it is wet, and the rasp eases. Then the rain stops as fast as it came. The roar climbs down into a drip, the green wringing itself out, and the heat closes over the place again like water over a dropped stone.

I follow a great ridged trunk to where the ground rises a little and goes harder. There, in the split of a rock the green has not yet drowned, I find it — a seep, a thin thread of water weeping clear out of the stone and gathering in a hollow the size of my palm. Clear. Not the brown of the swamp. Clear all the way to the bottom of the little cup.

I drink.

I put my mouth to the stone like an animal and I drink. It is not cool — nothing here is cool — but it is clean, a taste of rock and of nothing, and nothing has never tasted so good. It draws a line down my throat into my chest, and I can feel the shape of my own insides by the wet going through them. I drink the hollow dry, then crouch and wait while it fills drop by slow drop, and drink it dry again. Four times. By the fourth the rasp in my throat has gone. The low hunger has not. The water does not reach it. Nothing reaches it. I am beginning, even now, to understand that.

Then I sit with my back to the warm stone, wrung out, hollow, scraped down to the root. There is no one to see it. There is no one to make the low sound at me for it. I sit until the worst of the hollowness has passed through me, and the white glare does not change, and the green does not stop dripping, and I am still here.

---

There is one thing the green cannot reach.

I had a name once. Before the hands, before the floor that moved, before the cage and the dark and the tar-stink. My mother gave it to me. I do not have her face anymore — the sea took that too, all but a warmth and a closeness and a voice low over me in the dark — but I have the name. She put it in my mouth when I was small and I have kept it there. The slavers never got it. They took the teeth and could not take the thing hidden behind where the teeth had been.

*Rook.*

I hold it behind my teeth, in the soft gap where they are gone, and I do not say it. Saying it would be giving it to the air, and the air here is thick and hungry and gives nothing back. I only hold it. It is small and hard and whole when nothing else of mine is whole. It is the one thing on this whole drowned green shore that is wholly mine, that I carried off that ship in the only place they never thought to look — under the tongue, behind the gap, in the dark of my own mouth.

The slavers had their own sound for me. Low, made behind the teeth, ugly, a sound that is not a word so much as the felt-shape of a hand closing on the back of my neck. It meant *property*. It meant *come here*. It meant *shut your mouth*. They put that sound on me a thousand times and I never once answered it as a name, only as a man answers the lash, with the back and not the name. That sound is not mine and it is not me. I left it in the sea. The name my mother gave me is the other thing, the opposite thing — not enough to live on, not enough to fill the low hunger, and still something. In all this green it is the one something.

---

Night comes, and the green that drips and breathes all day turns into something that hunts.

I do not see the dark come down so much as hear the place change its voice. The drip goes on, but under it a new sound starts up, and another, until the whole black green is shrilling and sawing and clicking, a chorus of small throats, and over it the wet boom of larger things calling across the water, and under all of it the rising hum of a hundred thousand wings, the biting things lifting off the swamp to find the warm of me. They settle on the wet of my arms, my neck, the corners of my eyes, soft and silent and stinging, faster than I can brush them off. I cannot stay in the open. It will bleed me dry by morning, or worse — I have seen what the bite of the small things does in a fever-place, the sweat and the shaking. The body knows it, and pulls me up off the warm ground and sets me looking, low along the rock and root, the way a hurt thing looks for a hole.

I find a crack in the rock where the ridge breaks. Not a cave — a split, a gap I can pull into sideways, stone at my chest and stone at my back, tight as the cage and then tighter. I do not like the tight. The body remembers the tight. But fewer of the biting things can reach me in here, and the wanting-to-live is stronger than the not-liking, and I fold myself in. Knees to my chest. Arms around the knees. Face to the stone. And the smell of the place closes over me — the den-stink of standing water and old rot, a hollow where the green has died and gone to a black soup in the dark, thick enough that a man could choke on it sleeping.

I should not sleep here.

I know it before the head can catch up. This is fever-air, the very breath that lays men down. To curl into a rot-hollow and breathe its soup the night long is to wake shaking, or not to wake. The body should fight me, should drag me back to the bites rather than let me down into this. It does not. I breathe the foul thick dark and my head stays clear in it. My gut does not turn. The crawling sweat of the sickness does not start.

Why. I want to know why. The question comes up sharp out of the dark and there is nothing to answer it with — no words for the thing, no one to ask, only the foul air going in clean and the small still thing in me listening where the fever ought to be. I push at it. I cannot get hold of it. The head that would worry it is tired past all worrying, dragged down under sleep before it can close its hand on the answer. I press into the warm rot-dark and let it hold me, the only thing that has held me since the hands let go. I keep my mother's name behind my teeth. I sleep.

---

I dream in shards.

Hands. Big hands, rough, the skin like the rope, dragging me by the arm so the shoulder screams. A floor that moves — wet wood under me, the reek of tar and too many bodies, the world always tilting, so that even now in the dream I cannot find which way is down. Bars of rope and bone, a cage that breathes when the ship breathes. A voice low above me in no words I own, and the voice makes the sound, the ugly behind-the-teeth sound, and the sound means *down* and means *still* and means *you are a thing, and the thing may yet be thrown back to the water.*

The floor tilting past all tilting. A sound of wood giving. Water coming in warm and black across the moving floor, climbing, and the bodies in the dark all moving at once. The hands above me. The hands letting go.

I wake.

---

The light has come up dim and green and low, a drowned light filtered down through the leaves so it never quite reaches white. The night-chorus has worn down to a few last sawing throats, and the drip goes on, and somewhere out over the water a single great voice booms once and is quiet. I pull myself out of the crack and everything in me complains at once, the joints, the back, the gaps in my jaw, a whole body's worth of small voices all saying the same thing. I unfold slow.

I look at my leg for the first time in the light. A gash down the outside of the thigh, long as my hand, crusted thick with dried blood and mud, the lips of it dark, the skin around it red and tight and hot to the touch — a small fire lit under the skin. I have seen wounds go this way. I have seen what comes after, in the cage, in a wet hot hold — the red climbing, the smell turning, the leg going to green rot and then the body it is on going quiet and gone. In a place like this, wet and warm and full of small mouths, a wound like this is a death that has only not yet finished arriving. I press the skin beside it and it gives back pain, clean and deep. I let it be.

I drink at the seep until the cup is dry and dry again. Then I follow the ridge up where it lets me, slow, a hand on the stone, climbing out of the drowned green toward the white glare, and the trees thin, and the ground falls away below me, and I see the rest of it.

Green. Brown water. Green again. And no end to it. Hill on hill of green going up into haze, valleys drowned and steaming, a far line where the green meets a white sky and I cannot tell if there is sea beyond it or only more green. No smoke standing off a far hill. No boat dark on the water. No wall, no roof, no field, no smudge of anything made by hands. No other breathing thing that walks on two legs anywhere in all that drowning green, as far as the eye can throw itself before the world goes to haze.

I stand where the rock breaks out of the green, and the heat leans its whole wet weight on me, patient, like it has all the time there is, and at last I understand the thing the body has been trying to tell me since the heat woke me on the first morning.

This is a different alone.

The cage was alone — but the cage had walls. It had other bodies close in the dark, breathing, warm. It had the knowledge that hands would come. To hurt — but to come. To take notice. To know I was there. There is a kind of company even in the hands that hurt you; they have at least learned the shape of you. This has no walls. This is the whole green world laid out endless with one boy standing in it, and nothing in it that knows his one word, nothing that would answer it. The second mouth opens wider at the thought. The low hunger that no water reached, that no meat will reach. It wants to be known. There is no one. It will not close.

I look down at my own arms in the dim green light. Through the night the biting swarm should have feasted on me; I should be a map of welts, fevered, my eyes swelling shut. But the bites are few, and already fading, and where I would have been eaten alive the skin is near clean.

I do not understand it, and I try to. I reach for it — the air that went in clean, the rot-hollow that did not sicken me, now this — three wrong things in two days, and I push to make them into one thing, into a why, the way you press scattered teeth-marks to read what bit you. They will not come together. There are no words under my tongue for what my own body is doing without asking me, and the more I reach the further the shape of it slides off into the haze, and I am left only with the small still thing in me, quiet, listening, where the answer should be. So I set it on a back shelf and do not turn it over — not because I do not want to, but because there is nothing yet to turn it over *with*, and the green is full of teeth, and a boy who stands still wondering is a boy the green eats.

Then the heat presses harder and my leg is on fire and my mouth fills with the iron taste, and the moment lets me go.

I turn back toward the crack, the stone, the thin thread of clean water. I walk careful. The rock is sharp and the green is full of teeth, and there is no one to carry me if it opens me. There is no one. The body learns the lesson and writes it down where it writes everything: *the shore gives once. Then it makes you go further.*

I am alive.

I do not yet know whether that is mercy, or only the long way around to dying. The hunger does not care which. It only opens, and waits, and goes on being hungry in the dark behind the first.
