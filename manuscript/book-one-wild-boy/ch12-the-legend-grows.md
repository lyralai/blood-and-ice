# Chapter 12: The Legend Grows

The seaward stack goes sheer where the kelp-birds nest, and a small one is up it, climbing for eggs the way a young thing climbs — too high, too sure, hands ahead of the head. I am below on the wet shelf, reading the black the way I read everything. I see the foot go.

It happens again, is the thing. A different small one, a different low, and again I do not decide. The black already has my hands. The legs go.

---

I am up the black before the small one has finished falling.

There is no thought in it. There is the stack and the holds I know in the dark of me, the cracks my fingers have already lived in, and there is the falling thing, and the second is shorter than the first. I take it against the rock. Its weight slams the breath out of me and the stone takes my back and we slide and stop, and I have it pinned to the cliff with my whole body, my cheek in its hair, its heart going against my ribs like a gull in a fist.

Warm to warm. The wrong thing again, the thing that comes up off me when I hold one of these — the warmth standing up around us both, the way it stands off the stone at home. The small one is not cold. Its breath is fast. It smokes in the air. Mine does not. And it goes still against me, even falling, even saved, dead-still the way a hare goes under the shadow, and the stillness travels into my arm and sits there.

I set it on the ledge. I do not let it turn its head. I am gone down the stack before it can.

But not before it sees the hand. The hand that closed on its wrist. It has the hand, now, the way the first one had the face.

---

The third one I do not even touch.

The third is a clever one, small, gone out on the rotten ice past the spit where the black water shows through, and it is light enough that the ice holds it and does not know yet that it should not be there. I am across the channel. I cannot reach it before the ice reaches its mind.

So I do the only thing the rock gives me. I find a stone and I throw it — not at the small one, ahead of it, onto the good ice, a hard crack on the white where its eyes will go. It looks. It sees the safe ice the stone marks. It comes back along that line, light-footed, scared, alive.

It never sees me at all. It only sees that the ice spoke and saved it. That is a worse thing to be than a hand. I lie in the broken country after, and the legs keep going, every low, every fall, and the second mouth eats every saving and stays empty.

---

I find the first one at the border of mine.

The dry watercourse where my territory ends and the open shore begins, the line I mark and walk and bleed on — there is a thing on the flat stone there that the tide did not put. A whole seal-flipper, the good fat side up, cut clean. No pack eats here. No pack comes to my black. And it is laid. Set down with the fat showing, the way you set a thing for a thing you mean to feed.

I do not take it for a long time. I crouch above it with the gone teeth bare and the back-hair up, because a thing that is left is a thing left by a hand, and a hand is the slavers, a hand is the hold. I circle it three lows. The gulls do not get it. I get it. And when I eat it the meat-hunger goes quiet and the other mouth, the second one, opens so wide it hurts behind my breastbone, because someone knew where my line was, and stopped, and gave.

---

After the flipper there are other things.

A fish, laid the same. A fold of cured skin, soft, tanned, the kind a body could sleep warm in — I do not sleep in it, I cannot, but I carry it to the crack and I put it on the shelf of wrong things, with the warm stone and the things the world does not do, because it is a thing the world does not do. A pack does not feed the black shore. A pack does not give to the dark.

They are not afraid. I know fear-things — fear leaves nothing, or leaves a thing flung down and run from. These are set. Squared. Faced fat-up and cut clean. I have seen the big ones lay food for their own dead on the far shore, on the high stones, the same care in the hands, the same slow squaring. This is that. Not feeding a danger off their backs. The way you feed a thing you have decided is owed feeding.

I only have the flipper, eaten, and the warm empty behind my teeth that wants the hand that cut it more than it wanted the meat.

---

Across the pinch, on the firelit shore, something has gotten bigger, and I feel the shape of it the way I feel the tide coming, before I have any of its parts.

I do not hear what they say. I cannot. The water is wide and the wind takes their mouths. But it is not one small one's mouth anymore. It moves between the fires now. It goes to the far fires, the ones up-coast I have only ever smelled, strange smoke, other packs. It crosses water I have never crossed, and comes back to me in pieces the wind drops — a thing on the black — the cold will not — gives them back from the — a boy that is not —

A name out loud, traveling, growing, with my hand in it and my face in it and the frost-ring in it — and me down in my crack, not hearing it whole, only finding the food they leave at my line, because the mouths know now where the line is.

The black is mine. The seep, the warm stone, mine. The not-being-seen is the deepest mine of all.

---

Tonight I climb to the high black, above my crack, where I can see the whole far shore lit.

I have never done this. I have always kept low, kept dark, kept the spit between me and their fires. But the food is at my border and my name is on the wrong wind and the second mouth has stopped letting me lie still.

The fires are many. More than at any low I have counted. They are pulled close together on the near shore, the closest they have ever come to the water that divides us, and the small uprights and the big ones are out at the edge of the firelight, not working, not gleaning — facing the water. Facing my black.

They are looking for me.

And I am up on the high stone where the fire-glow can reach, where a thing that watches a long time can be watched back. I should go down. The beast wants down, flat, gone. I do not go down. The second mouth holds me there on the cold rock above my home with my whole front turned to their light, and I let it. I let them have the shape of me against the sky for one breath. Then two.

Across the water, a small one's arm comes up and points.

The watching has two ends now. I made it so. The black shore looks back.
