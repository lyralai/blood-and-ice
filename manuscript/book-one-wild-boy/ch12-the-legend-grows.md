# Chapter 12: The Legend Grows

The seaward stack goes sheer where the kelp-birds nest, and a small one is up it, climbing for eggs the way a young thing climbs — too high, too sure, hands ahead of the head. I am below on the wet shelf, reading the black the way I read everything, the holds and the slick and the green rot that means a foot will go. I know that stack. I have hung off it in the dark with the storm pulling at my back and the eggs warm in my mouth. I know which ledge is a lie. And I watch the small one set its weight on the lie.

I see the foot go.

---

It happens again, is the thing. A different small one, a different low, a different black, and again I do not decide. There is no place in me where the deciding would sit. The black already has my hands. The legs go before the falling thing has finished knowing it is falling.

---

I am up the stack before the small one has cleared the lie of the ledge.

There is no thought in it. There is the stack and the holds I know in the dark of me, the cracks my fingers have already lived in winters before this one, the gap where the bird-stink is thickest, the spur the cold split. My hands find them without me. They have lived this rock. And there is the falling thing coming down the black face with its arms wide and its mouth open on a sound, and the meeting of us is shorter than a breath. The first rescue was a long second. This is no second at all.

I take it against the rock.

Its weight slams the breath out of me. The stone takes my back, and the back-skin tears on the spur, and we slide, the both of us, my heels scrabbling the wet black for a hold and finding one, and we stop. I have it pinned to the cliff with my whole body, my chest crushing it to the stone, my cheek down in its hair. The hair smells of their smoke and their fat and the warm fug of a den that is fed and close. Its heart goes against my ribs like a gull in a fist, fast, fast, the small frightened drum of a thing that has just learned the ground is not under it.

Warm to warm. The wrong thing comes up off me the way it always comes when I hold one of these — the warmth standing up around the both of us, a close skin of it, the way it stands off the stone at home in the deep cold and will not let the frost down onto me. The small one is not cold against my chest. Its breath is fast and high and it smokes, a little ragged cloud of it past my cheek into the bitter air. Mine does not. We breathe the same wind, the both of us heaving. Its breath makes ghosts. Mine makes nothing. The frost rings the air a hand's width off my skin and does not come closer, and the small one is inside that ring with me, kept warm in the thing the world does not do.

And it goes still. Even falling. Even caught. It goes dead-still the way a hare goes under the shadow of the wing, the deep stillness that is the last thing before the teeth, the stillness that knows it is held by a stronger thing and has stopped its own body to keep from being the thing that moves. The stillness travels up out of it into my arm and sits there. I feel it pass into me. I know that stillness from the inside. I have been that hare in a stronger thing's hands, on a deck, in a hold, with the hand closed on my neck. I feel it come into me from the small one and I have to shut my teeth on what it wakes.

I set it on the ledge. The good ledge, the true one, where the spur gives a foot a seat. I do not let it turn its head. My hand on the side of its face holds the head to the stone, gentle as I can make a hand that has only ever held to kill, and I am gone down the stack before it can turn and find me.

But not before it sees the hand. The hand that closed on its wrist on the way up, the dark scarred hand with the working in the knuckles, the hand that is not a pack hand. It has the hand now, the way the first one had the face. It will carry the hand back to the fire and lay it down among the others. Another piece of me, gone across the water into their warm, where I cannot follow it.

---

The third one I do not even touch.

The third is a clever one, small, gone out on the rotten ice past the spit where the black water breathes through the white, where the floe goes thin and grey and rotten with the turning season. It is light, lighter than I ever was, light enough that the bad ice holds it and has not yet told it the truth. I am across the channel on my own black. I read the ice from here the way I read it under my own feet — the grey going to glass, the dark vein of water working up under the small one's tracks, the give that is coming. I cannot reach it. The water between us is open and cold and I will not go into it, and even if I went, the going would crack the floe and we would both go under into the black where the cold is a fist that closes and does not open.

So I do the only thing the rock gives me. I find a stone, a good throwing stone, smooth and heavy and sitting right in the hand, and I stand up out of my low and I throw — not at the small one. Ahead of it. Onto the good white ice, the thick ice nearer its own shore, a hard crack and skitter on the white where its eyes will go when the sound reaches them. It hears. It looks. It sees the place the stone struck, the safe white the stone marked, and some animal sense in it reads the line between where it stands and where the stone is, and it comes back along that line, light-footed, low, scared, fast — off the grey, off the rot, onto the white, onto its own shore, alive.

It never sees me at all.

It only sees that the ice spoke. That a stone came out of the dead air and struck the safe place and saved it. That is a worse thing to be than a hand. The hand at least was mine; the hand was a self that did a thing. This is no self at all. This is the island saving its own — the black, the white, the cold doing a kindness with no face in it — and the small one will carry that back to the fire, not a boy, not a hand, but a place that watches and saves. I lie down in the broken country after, behind the warm stone, and the legs keep going under me, every low, every fall, every small one that climbs too high or walks too far. The body has decided. And the second mouth eats every saving and stays empty, because a saving thrown from across the water with no face on it feeds nothing in me at all.

---

I find the first one at the border of mine.

The dry watercourse where my territory ends and the open shore begins, the dead stream-bed of round stones I mark and walk and have bled on to make it mine — there is a thing on the flat stone at the head of it that the tide did not put. The tide leaves weed and bone and the broken backs of crabs. The tide leaves a mess. This is not a mess. This is a whole seal-flipper, the good fat side turned up to the grey sky, cut clean off at the joint with a true edge. No pack eats on my black. No pack crosses to my line. And it is not dropped. It is laid. Set down square with the fat showing, the way you set a thing down for a thing you mean to feed.

I do not take it for a long time.

I crouch above it on the higher stone with the gone teeth bare and the back-hair standing up off my neck, the old growl working low in the chest without my leave, because a thing that is left is a thing left by a hand. And a hand is the slavers. A hand is the hold, the deck, the closing on the neck, the ugly sound they made behind their teeth that meant mine, be still, come, you are a thing. A laid gift and a baited net are the same shape to the part of me that lived in the hold. I circle it three lows. I watch the sky for the shape that means men. The gulls come for the flipper and I drive them screaming off it, and that is the thing I notice — that I am keeping it. That my body decided to keep it before my head knew it wanted it.

The gulls do not get it. I get it. And when I crack the joint and the fat comes away and I eat, the meat-hunger, the red mouth, the low hot one, goes quiet and glad the way it always goes. But the other mouth, the second one, the one no meat answers — it opens so wide behind my breastbone that I have to stop chewing and press my arm to my chest and breathe through it. Because someone knew where my line was. Someone walked to the edge of the dark shore and found the place I bleed to mark, and stopped there, and did not cross, and set food down on the stone with the fat turned up. Someone knew the line. And honored it. And fed across it anyway.

---

After the flipper there are other things.

A fish, laid the same, square and fat-side up on the border stone. A clutch of the kelp-bird eggs, the kind the small one climbed for, set in a curl of weed so they would not roll. And once a fold of cured skin, soft, tanned pale, worked supple by hands that knew the work — the kind of skin a body could roll into and sleep warm against the worst of the cold. I do not sleep in it. I cannot. The body will not lie down soft; the body knows that soft is where the hand comes. But I carry it to the crack and I lay it on the shelf of wrong things, beside the warm stone that should be cold, beside the things the world does not do — because it is a thing the world does not do. A pack does not feed the black shore. A pack does not lay gifts in the dark for the tooth that lives there.

And they are not afraid. This is the thing I keep turning, the way the tongue keeps going back to the gap. I know fear-things. Fear leaves a thing flung down and run from, dropped crooked, the print of the bolting foot still in it. These are not flung. These are set. Faced fat-up, cut clean, curled in weed so they keep. I have watched the big ones on the far shore lay food on the high stones for their own dead, in the cold season — the same slow care in the hands, the same turning of the good side up. This is that hand. Not the hand that throws meat to a wolf to buy its own back away. The hand that lays food for a thing it has decided is owed the feeding.

I only have the flipper, eaten, and the egg-shells, and the soft skin on the shelf I cannot use, and the warm empty behind my teeth that wants the hand that cut the meat more than the belly ever wanted the meat.

---

Across the pinch, on the firelit shore, something has gotten bigger, and I feel the shape of it the way I feel a tide coming up the channel — the whole weight of it, before I have any of its parts.

I do not hear what they say. I cannot. The water is wide here and the wind comes off the ice and takes their mouths and scatters the sounds before they cross. But it is not one small one's mouth anymore. Whatever it is, it moves now. It moves between the near fires the way fire moves through dry weed, fire to fire, mouth to mouth. And it goes further. It goes up-coast to the far fires, the ones I have only ever smelled on a turned wind, strange smoke, other packs, packs I have no shape of. It crosses water I have never crossed. And it comes back to me in pieces, in the scraps the wind drops over the channel and lets fall on my black — not whole, never whole, just the broken backs of words.

*A thing on the black.* *The cold will not.* *Gives them back from the.* *A boy that is not.*

A sound out loud, traveling, growing, with my hand inside it and my face inside it and the frost-ring inside it. And me down in my crack at the dead bottom of it, not hearing it whole, only finding the food they leave at my line because the mouths know now where the line is. They have a shape for me up there, in the warm, among the fires. They pass it hand to hand, fire to fire. It grows in the passing. I cannot reach it. I cannot answer it. And behind my teeth where the answering sound would live, in the gap where three were torn, there is only the warm hollow and nothing to send. They make a sound that means me, and I have lost the one that does.

The black is mine. The seep is mine, and the warm stone, and the crack the wind cannot find. The not-being-seen is the deepest mine of all. I have built my whole staying-alive out of it. And now the not-being-seen is leaking, fire to fire, across water I never crossed.

---

Tonight I climb to the high black, above my crack, where I can see the whole far shore lit.

I have never done this. Not once, in all the lows I have counted. I have always kept low, kept dark, kept the spit and the broken country between me and their fires, kept my front to the ground and my shape against the stone. The whole craft of living here is the craft of being one more black thing on a black shore. But the food is at my border. And the sound of me is on the wrong wind, growing in mouths I cannot see. And the second mouth has stopped letting me lie still in the dark with it.

The fires are many. More than at any low I have ever counted, more than the lights I count between the shores on the bad nights — they are pulled in close together on the near edge of the far shore, the closest to the dividing water they have ever come. And the small uprights and the big ones are out at the lip of the firelight. Not working. Not gleaning the tide. Not lifting the small ones against the warm. Standing. Facing the water. Facing my black.

They are looking for me.

And I am up on the high stone where the fire-glow can just reach across and lay its faint red on a thing that stands too long against the sky. A thing that watches a long time can be watched back. I know this in the body; I have hunted by it for years. I should go down. The beast wants down — flat, gone, one more shadow poured back into the crack where no eye finds it. The pull of it is strong as the red mouth at a soft throat. Down. Now. Be nothing.

I do not go down.

The second mouth holds me there on the cold rock above my home with my whole front turned to their light. The frost rings the air around me and does not touch me, and my breath makes no ghost on the night, and I stand inside the thing the world does not do and I let them have the shape of me against the sky. One breath. Then two. Everything I am made of says be unseen, and I stand and let myself be seen, because the other hunger is older now than the fear, and it leans across the water toward the warm the way it always has, and for once I let it stand me up into the open where it can be answered.

Across the water, a small one's arm comes up, and points.

The watching has two ends now. I made it so. The black shore looks back.
