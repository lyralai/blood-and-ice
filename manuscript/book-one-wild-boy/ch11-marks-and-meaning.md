# Chapter 11: Marks and Meaning

I take the skins back out at the dark, after the fire, when the high stone is mine and the Kala has folded down into her own still.

I have carried them up from the wreck a moon now, rolled in a sleeve of fat-soaked hide against the wet, and I have not opened them. There was no place in me to open them. The shore gave me only its own laws, and the laws of the shore do not read marks; they read tide and bank and the worn pale line in the mud where the small feet go. But up here among the Vorth a thing has loosened. The sounds are coming back, one stolen at a time, laid in the gap behind my teeth — *low*, *high*, *come*, the gentling, the question-sound — and where the sounds come the rest stirs. The drowned-deep place rolls in its sleep. And the body says, before the head ever does: *now. Try them now.*

So I unroll the skins on the bare dark stone in the red light. Careful. I bend my face close over the rows the dead men scratched into the dead skin, and I try to make them speak.

They will not.

I knew they would not. They would not at the wreck and they will not here. The marks lie in their rows, ordered as nothing in the green is ordered, line under line under line, small and dark and patient, and the body knows past all doubt that they are made to mean. There is a way of taking the rows in through the eye and having them come out the other side as sound and shape and the thing a man wanted to say before he lay down to die. I had that way once. I am sure of it the same as I am sure of the name behind the teeth — sure of the warm hollow where it lay, and finding only the warm, and nothing in it. The reading is gone like the name is gone. The slavers took one and the green took the other, and I am left bent over the rows with the buzzing low in the drowned place, the buzzing that is almost a shape and never breaks the brown water.

But I want it. That is the new thing. At the wreck I only kept the skins. Here I bend over them and *strain*, and the wanting has a tooth in it now.

---

I work at them.

This the shore taught me and the green cannot starve out: a thing you do not understand, you do not turn from — you crouch by it. You crouch by the run you cannot read, low after low, until the body learns the shape of the small feet that wore it. So I crouch by the marks, small, patient, going in through the only door there is.

The rows are not all one thing. The body sees it before the head. Most are the small busy rows, line on line, the kind I cannot crack — but here and there the dead hand laid down other things, larger, standing apart, and these the body half-takes. A shape that is the curve of a coast, the bend of a reach, the bar of black mud. I have walked that bend. I have stood on that bar among the bones. The dead man drew my green — the channels and the high stone and the drowned reaches, scratched small on the skin where they are scratched large on my soles. Where his lines bend I know the bend, because the feet walked it. This much I can read. Not in the head. In the feet.

And there are the other shapes. The small standing ones the busy rows make way around, the ones a hand drew slow and meaning. Among them, twice — three times — driven into the dead skin in dark deliberate line, the eye.

The eye. Ringed, open, looking. And through it, crossing it, the thorn.

The disc at my chest goes warm against the breastbone, or I think it does, the way a thing you stare at long enough begins to move. I lay the worked metal beside the drawn skin and they are the same mark — the true round, the true line, the thing on my chest and the thing the dead man scratched, one thing, made twice, by men, before the green. I put my torn finger on the drawn eye and then on the worked one and the buzzing comes up out of the drowned place, the long no-bottom wave: *I know this. This is mine.* And no when. And no whose. The brown water closes over it and leaves me only the two marks and the dark and the want.

I press it harder than I ever have. *Where. Who. When.* I go down after the answer the way I go down a flooded channel after the thing that made the wake — breath held, hands ahead of me in the dark — and there is no floor. The head finds nothing to close on.

---

But the skin shows me a thing past the marks. It shows me a place.

I find it when I lay the drawn coast against the green I carry in my feet. The dead man's lines do not stop at the bar. They run inland, up channels I have never followed, into the deep green under the high country — and where they end the hand drew the eye again, biggest of all, and around it a shape I take for stone laid on stone, climbing, a made hill. The hand drew arrows running to it. The small busy rows crowd thick around it, urgent, the way men crowd a word they are afraid of. They were going there. They did not die at the wreck. They died walking inland, *toward the eye*, and the skin is the line they were walking when they fell.

I know the place. Not from the head — from a low two moons gone, before the Kala took me in, when I followed a fat-tailed thing up a channel further than I should and came out under the canopy into a clearing that was not a clearing.

Stone. Stone laid on stone by hands, gone green and slick, climbing in steps into the dark of the canopy — a hill made and not grown, a thing the green had been ten thousand lows trying to pull down and had not pulled down. I went up it because the body goes up. And the steps had channels cut in them. Grooves worn smooth in the old stone, running down, step to step, all the long way to a basin at the foot — channels for a thing to run, the way the mud has its channels for the tide. Nothing ran in them now but the green wet. But the stone at their edges was dark. Old dark. Dark like the bar where the men bled out, soaked deep, past washing.

And on every face of the climbing stone, carved deep and carved again, worn but never gone, the eye. Ringed. Open. The thorn through it. The same eye that hangs at my chest and lies in the dead man's rows, cut here by the hundred into a hill of stone, looking out into the green.

I did not go up to the top. The body would not. The body that does not turn from the dark channel or the rotting fire turned from the top of that stone, went still all over, the hide standing up along my arms, and took me back down and out the way I came, fast, not running and almost running. I did not go back.

Now I lay the dead man's skin in the red light and the drawn eye points up the channels to the made hill, and I understand the body's turning. They fed the eye there. The peoples before — the men who made the disc and the men who made the hill, one kind or two, the rows do not say and I cannot read them — cut the channels so a thing would run down into the basin, step to step, dark and soaking the stone past washing. And the dead men on the bar knew. They had the rows. They could read what I cannot, and they read the eye, and they did not turn from it. They went up the channels toward the made hill, reaching for the thing the rows told them, and the green lay them down before they got there. The eye is still up under the canopy, looking out of a hundred stone faces at the place where they fell.

I want to know what they were reaching for. I want it the way I have wanted nothing made of marks — to climb the dark stone to the top the body would not let me climb, lay my hand in the worn groove, and *know*. The head reaches up the drawn line to the eye and finds the same no-floor. There are no words in me for what runs in cut channels down made stone. There is only the eye, and the dark soaked deep, and the dead men reaching, and the want.

---

The Kala cannot read the marks. She laid her great dark finger on my loop once and half-saw the thinking in me; she cannot lay it on the rows and find the man who drew them. The marks are a thing from the before — from the world that took me and the world I came from, both the same kind, all gone now to bone on the bar. I lean out over the dead skin toward that world and find it dead. The eye looks out from my chest and the rows and the stone hill and tells me nothing. Not because I will not hear. Because I have lost the mouth that hears.

Two things are mine and gone. The name, and the reading. The slavers got the one — they never knew to reach for the name, only the closing hand on the back of the neck, the brand, the low ugly sound that meant *mine, be still, come, you are a thing*, and that one stayed when the true one left, lodged in the warm dark like a barb I cannot work out of the flesh. The green starved the other low by low. I have the eye and cannot read it. I have the rows and cannot read them. I have the made hill in my feet and no word for what bled down its stone. I am a man bent over a thing made to speak, reaching for it with both hands, and the way is gone.

So I lay a law down behind my teeth. *A thing made to mean does not stop meaning because you have lost the way of it.* The run is still a run when you cannot read the feet. But the marks are not dead while I keep them — and I will not stop reaching at them. I roll the skins careful in the fat-soaked sleeve. I loop the disc back over my neck, the eye out, the thorn through it. I do not let it go.

---

The seer comes to me at the marks the way it came to me at the loop.

I do not hear it come. It is old past the others, the body sunk in on itself, the dark hide gone ash and near-white with the years, and it moves like the oldest things move, slow and without sound, so that I look up from the rows and it is simply there. Crouched at the edge of the red light. The milk-pale eyes already on me. Already reading.

It does not look at me first. It looks at the skins.

This is new. The young ones look at the skins and make the dismissing sound, the one laid on a thing of no use — dark scratches on dead hide, no meat, no warm, nothing the body can do with. The seer does not make the dismissing sound. It bends its great near-white head down over the rows, slow, and its eyes go along the lines, following the made-thing back to the hand that made it. It knows the rows are made. It does not know to read them. But it knows they are a thing that is read. It is the only one of them that knows the difference, and the knowing of it is itself a thing I did not look to find up here on the high stone.

Then the seer lays its finger on the disc at my chest.

Not on me. On the eye. It follows the ring with the old pale fingertip, around, and then the thorn, down the length of the barb that pierces the looking — and it makes the sound. The long low one. The one with the thing in it bent toward, turned up, the not-dismissing sound. I have it now, near enough; I have stolen enough of their tongue to hold the shape of it. It is the sound they lay on the squared dead and the gifts faced and set on the high stones. It is the sound for a thing that comes from before and means.

The seer reads the eye-and-thorn as it reads its own old signs. A thing of the kept. A kin-omen. A mark that means.

---

And then it does the thing it did at the loop, and worse.

It lifts the old finger off the disc, and it lays it against me. Not the side of my head this time, where the thinking is. Lower. It lays the flat of the worn pale finger against the side of my throat, where the blood goes, where the great pulse runs up under the skin into the head — and it holds it there, light, the way I lay my finger along the worn line in the mud, reading the thing that passes under.

It feels the blood go.

I hold still under it. The beast comes up a little at the touch on the throat — the old place, the place the hand closes, the place the brand lives — but this is not the closing hand. This is the lightest pale pad of a finger laid to listen. I let the beast settle. I let the seer read the blood. And the seer goes very still over the finger. Its milk-pale eyes change — the same change that went over the Kala's the low she half-saw the thinking, the same the old pale head did over the bones of the thing that owned my green: a thing turning toward a thing it did not look for and now half-sees.

It feels something in the blood.

I do not know what it feels. I do not have it to know. But I know my blood is one of the wrong things. The green that could not eat me. The water that knots every belly but mine. The venom that goes out of me fast and leaves no mark. The fever-air I sleep in and wake whole from. The swarms that ring me and never land. The dead men on the bar drank my water and breathed my air and lay down where they stood and died of the green I live in — died reaching up the channels for the eye, with my blood the one blood that could have walked there. The wrongness is in the blood. The seer feels it go under the finger and the milk-pale eyes change, and I understand, the body first and the head long after, that it has found the same thing the green found and could not finish — the thing that should not be, running warm and unkilled under the skin of the man-shape it took in.

It looks from the throat to the disc. From the blood to the eye-and-thorn. And it lays them side by side, two halves of one strangeness — the mark that is mine from before, and the blood the green cannot kill — and bows the great near-white head over both at once, the long meaning-sound coming up low out of the sunk old chest. The sound for the kept. The thing that comes from before and means.

---

I do not understand what it knows. I claw after it. There is nothing to close on.

I crouch under the bowed head, the dead marks spread, the eye against my breastbone, the seer's old finger gone now from my throat, and the head reaches after the thing and finds nothing — only the seer bent over me, naming me with the sound it lays on the squared dead, reading the blood I cannot read and the mark I cannot read, both at once. It is sure of a thing about me that I am not sure of. *The green kills my kind and does not kill me.* And under that, laid down deeper now, hot: *the old one feels it in the blood, and it is the same as the mark, and the mark is mine from before, and the mark is cut a hundredfold into the made hill the dead men died reaching for.*

I strain to turn it over. The head has no shape for it, no more than for the bones and the standing and the breathing on the bar, no more than for the channels cut to run dark down made stone. But I will not file it and leave it. I press it and press it, and the second mouth — the one no kill has ever fed, the one the Kala has begun to feed — leans out under it into the red light and finds, this once, that it is not leaning into nothing.

The bones did not turn. The marks do not speak. But the seer turns. The seer, bent over my blood and my mark, half-reading a thing I cannot read in myself — it is an eye that is not my eye, and it does not look away, and it does not make the dismissing sound. It cannot read me. It can only feel that there is a reading, and bow to the meaning it cannot reach. I bow with it. We are two now, both reaching, neither able.

But it is more than the bones gave. More than the green ever gave. To be felt for and found and named *kept*, by the oldest eye on the high stone, with the wrong blood going warm under its finger and the eye-and-thorn looking out from my chest — it is enough. It fills the near edge of the want. Not the whole of it. The whole of it wants the reading — the man who could take the rows in and give them back as my own true shape, the name out of the gap, the word for what bled down the channels of the made hill. That hunger does not close. It never will, until a thing turns and reads me whole.

But the seer turns. And the seer finds. And I lean into the finding and am, for this low, in the red light, on the high stone, with the marks spread and the Kala's slow vast heart going through the dark beside me — not fed full, but fed.

---

I roll the skins back into the fat-soaked sleeve when the seer has gone. I loop the disc against my chest, the eye out, the thorn through it. I lay the new law down behind my teeth, in the gap where the name was, beside the stolen sounds and the gentling that is not mine and the warm that chose to hold me and the brand I will give back someday across the water to the ones it belongs to.

*The thing that is locked is still a thing that means. And a thing that means draws an eye, even an eye that cannot read it.*

The marks are made to speak and I have lost the mouth that hears them. The eye is mine and tells me nothing. The blood runs warm where every other blood went to fever and went still, and I do not know why, and I cannot make myself stop trying to know. The made hill stands under the canopy with the eye cut into a hundred of its faces and the dead men's bones strung along the road to it, and I want to know what they were reaching for. But the oldest eye on the high stone has laid its finger on all of it and bowed its head, and named me kept — and that naming sits in me now beside the want it cannot fill.

The Kala folds down beside me in the failing red, the great dark bulk of her, and lays the arm over me, and makes the gentling sound, the keeping one. I lie against the warm that knows it holds me, the rolled marks under my arm, the eye against my breastbone, the wrong blood going warm under the seer's gone finger. Far up the channels the stone hill stands and waits with its hundred carved eyes. Somewhere in the dark of me, behind the gone teeth, in the drowned-deep place, the buzzing turns over once in its sleep — the marks, the eye, the hill, the name, all of it locked, all of it mine, all of it waiting on a mouth that can hear.

It will come back, or it will not. The shore taught me to keep the thing either way — and to keep reaching for it. So I keep it, and I hold the whole of it in the warm where the lost name lies, and I go on.
