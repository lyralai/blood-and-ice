# Chapter 16: The Capture

The big one knows I will come for her. That is the thing I learn too late.

---

I come down off the spine in the dark and the smoke pulls me in like a hook already set. Not her fire. There is no fire now. There is the smell of her — kelp-rope, woodsmoke, the seal-oil she works into her cracked hands — and it is wrong. It is moving the wrong way, dragged. Under it, the hard-sole stink of many men close together, waiting.

A bait is a thing you put where the animal already goes.

They have her. I know it in my teeth before I see it. Down in the black between two stacks of stone they have her on her knees with a man's hand in her kelp-rope hair, and her face is turned away, and they have not hurt her past holding. They are not here for her. She is the stone stacked on my line, pointing at me.

I should climb back into the cold dark. I know that road. I do not take it.

I go to her.

---

I come fast and low on the bare black where I leave no track, and the first one never hears me. I take him at the back of the knee with the seal-stone in my fist and he folds with a sound like wet wood breaking. His holding-spear rings on the rock and I have it — no. I do not have it. A net comes out of the dark.

It is weighted at the edges with stone and it is on me before the spear is in my hand. I am down, rolling, biting. I get the rope in my teeth and pull and a strand goes and I taste the tar of it. Old tar. Hold-tar. The buried sound comes up screaming behind my gone teeth, the sound the hand made, and the red rage is all the way up in me now. Good. Let it try.

I bite through a man's wrist to the cord under it. He howls. The blood that comes is hot in my mouth, alive, the heat-hunger of it singing all the way down — distinct, separate, nothing like the still cold that rings me — and the second mouth under my breastbone yawns wide and wants more, wants all of him, and for one black breath I let it.

Then there are four on the net at once.

---

I am good. Three of them out of the fight before they fold me — let the black keep that count. I open the leader's face from eye to jaw and he does not cry out. He only bears down harder. Patient, patient, the way he turned the right rocks while the lows ran out one after another.

But I am one boy. There is no more than a boy's strength in my arms, and it runs out the way a boy's runs out. They know this. They have counted it. They do not come one at a time like beasts. They come together like one animal with eight backs, and they bring the second net, and the third, and they bring rope.

A loop closes on my wrist. I bite the hand that set it. Another loop. My ankle. They lean their whole weight and the black comes up to meet my face and the salt-iron taste floods where the stone splits my lip.

The hand closes on the back of my neck.

---

The hand. The hand in the dark. The hold. It is here, it is now, it is real and warm and pressing my face to the cold stone, and the scream tears out of me at last, the whole shape of this exact closing. They made it. They have come walking across my island on two legs to fold over me again.

Rope at the wrist, the ankle, a loop drawn cruel around the throat so the more I pull the less I breathe. Blood in my eyes, mine, theirs, all of it iron, all of it warm where the wound should run cold. They drag me. The black slides under me, my black, every fold of it I bled to learn, sliding away behind me with my own blood smeared across it. I claw it. I get a fistful of scree and it pours out of my hand, every grain of it gone between my fingers, not one stone staying for me. That is the whole of it. The island does not hold what I cannot hold.

I snarl. I am bitten, bleeding, snarling — more beast than boy left in the rope, and the rope does not care, and the scree does not care, and the split lip floods salt-iron and does not care.

Warm. My neck, where the hand is, is warm where it should be ice. Wrong. Even now. Even now the body keeps its wrong small secret and does not smoke, does not freeze, heals already at the torn lip — none of it saves me. None of it ever was for saving me.

---

They haul me up the black toward their light and their many shapes and somewhere a man laughs, short, the laugh of a thing got that was hunted long.

And I see her.

She is on her knees still, the hand gone from her hair now, her face turned to me at last, full on, the firelight finding it. Her mouth is open. Her eyes are the worst thing on this whole island: they are not afraid for herself. They are wet, they are wide, they break on me. And I understand, dragging through my own blood in their ropes, that she is seeing what her warning could not stop — that she stacked the stones and walked the wrong line and turned her neck like a hauled-out seal and still it came down to this, to me in the net because I would not leave her in it.

She came free into my rocks. She laid her name down unguarded and breathed mine into the wrong warmth of my face, and the second mouth went quiet under her cold hand, the only time, the one time, and I came down off the safe dark to stand between her and the hook, and the hook took me instead.

I would do it again. That is the last clear thing in me before the red heat closes back over it. I would come down again. I would come down a hundred lows and let them have me a hundred times for the one time the second mouth went still.

They have made me property again. A hand on the back of the neck, a sound flung at me that means *come here, shut your mouth* — the hold-word, the boy-word, the word they always used because they never had the other. They think the word is a rope too. It is not. I hold the name they never found. My mother's. Pressed flat under my tongue like a coin, behind the three gone teeth, where no hand reaches. *Rook.* They can have the body in the net. They cannot have that.

They think they have taken an animal.

They have not. I hold the sound. I hold the name. I hold her face turning toward me in the firelight, and I carry all three down into the dark where they cannot reach.

I learned to care, and it caught me, and her face is the price, and I would pay it again.
