# Chapter 16: The Capture

The big one knows I will come for her. That is the thing I learn too late.

It is the one piece of my pattern I never thought to hide, because it was not on the black for him to read — not a track in the ash, not a turned rock, not the bled line he crouched at and studied. It was inside me, behind the breastbone, and I thought a thing kept that deep was kept safe. But he has been filling himself with me low after low, the way I filled myself with the tide, and a man patient enough to learn which way the ghost bolts is patient enough to learn the one thing the ghost will turn back for. He did not need to see it. He only needed to set the bait where I already go and wait for the going.

---

I come down off the spine in the dark and the smoke pulls me in like a hook already set. Not her fire. There is no fire now. There is the smell of her — kelp-rope, woodsmoke, the seal-oil she works into the cracks of her hands so they will bend in the cold — and it is wrong. It is moving the wrong way. Dragged. A scent should sit where the body sits and spread out slow from the warmth of it; hers is smeared in a line across the black, pulled, the way a thing is pulled that did not want to come. And laid over the top of it, thick, close, breathing: the hard-sole stink of many men packed together and holding still. Sweat gone cold in wool. Old iron. The grease they rub into the leather. The smell of bodies that have been waiting a long count of breaths and mean to wait a little longer.

A bait is a thing you put where the animal already goes.

I know it then, high up, before my feet have carried me halfway down. I know the shape of the trap the way I know rot-ice from sound-ice — not by stepping on it, by the way the whole picture stands wrong. The dragged scent. The waiting men. The fire put out so the dark would do their hiding for them. Everything in me that learned this island says *stop, climb, go back into the cold where no hand reaches.* And under all of it, lower, the second mouth turns over once in the dark and aches down-coast toward the smeared line of her, and my feet keep going.

They have her. I know it in my teeth before I see it. Down in the black between two stacks of stone they have her on her knees with a man's hand wound into her kelp-rope hair, the fist set close to the scalp so the whole weight of her head hangs off it, and her face is turned away from me, down and aside, the way you turn from a thing you cannot stop and will not watch arrive. They have not hurt her past the holding. I read that off her in one pass, the way I read a seal off the haul-out — no slack in her, no broken thing, only the held tension of a body kept down against its will. They did not come for her. She is not the catch. She is the stone stacked on my line, the leaning stone, set out pointing — except they have learned to point with her, with the live grieving shape of her, because they learned that I would come.

I should climb back into the cold dark. I know that road in my feet, every loose slide and every true hold of it, the high black going up and in where no spear has ever crouched. It is right there behind me. It would take me and it would not take them.

I go to her.

---

I come fast and low on the bare black where I leave no track, pouring myself down the dark between the stacks, and the first one never hears me. I take him at the back of the knee with the seal-stone in my fist, the round heavy stone I have killed seals with, and the joint goes sideways under it and he folds with a sound like wet wood breaking. His holding-spear rings on the rock. My hand is already out for it, my whole body swinging toward the long iron that could turn this — and I have it, the haft cold in my palm for the length of one heartbeat, the barb gleaming —

No. I do not have it.

A net comes out of the dark.

It is weighted at the edges with stone and it is on me before the spear is anything but a weight in my hand. The mesh drops over my head and shoulders and the stones swing in and wrap, and the more I surge the more it takes, the way a net is built to take. I am down. The spear is gone, knocked loose and ringing away into the black. I roll. I bite. I get a fist of the mesh in my teeth and pull and a strand parts and the taste of it floods my mouth — tar. Old tar. Hold-tar, the black sticky reek of the seams of the ship, the smell that lived in the dark of the hold low after low after low, packed into the wood under the piss and the bodies, and the body knows it before the mind can say it. The buried sound comes up screaming behind my gone teeth. The sound the hand made. The brand. It rises out of the dark where I packed it down and it fills my skull, and the red rage comes up all the way behind it, hot, flooding, every edge of me gone to teeth and want.

Good. Let the net try.

I bite through a man's wrist to the cord under it. He howls, high, a sound that does not belong to a man who carries hooked iron, and the blood that comes is hot in my mouth — alive, the heat-hunger of it singing all the way down the throat and into the gut, nothing like the still cold that rings me on the high stone, nothing like preservation, the opposite of stillness, all vitality, all rush, all want. The second mouth under my breastbone yawns wide for it. It does not want the wrist. It wants all of him, the warmth and the living heat of him, wants to take him whole into the place no meat has ever filled, and for one black breath I let it. I let the rage and the second mouth run together and I am all hunger, all heat, no boy left in it, only the open throat of the thing.

Then there are four on the net at once.

---

I am good. Let the black keep the count: three of them out of the fight before they fold me. The one at the knee. The one whose wrist runs red into the stone. A third I take across the throat with the edge of my hand and he goes down whooping for air that will not come fast enough. A thing that hunts me will carry the marks of having hunted me. That is mine. They cannot take that off me.

I open the leader's face from the eye to the jaw with my nails before they pin the arm, a long furrow welling dark, and he does not cry out. That is the worst of him, in the end — not the size, not the spread of him in the dark, but that he takes the wound and does not break stride. He only bears down harder, his weight coming onto me even and sure, the way he turned the right rocks while the lows ran out one after another. Patient with his own face running blood. Patient the way the tide is patient. He has been sure of this ending since the low he first crouched at my line, and a torn face does not move a man who is sure.

But I am one boy. There is no more than a boy's strength in my arms and it runs out the way a boy's runs out — fast, all at once, the deep well of it draining to mud while the rage still screams for more. They know this. They counted it long ago, lying out on the high ground learning which way I bolt. They do not come at me one at a time the way beasts come, each waiting its turn at the kill. They come together, one animal with eight backs and sixteen hands, and when the first net is full of me they bring the second, and over that the third, weighting me to the black under layer on layer of stone-hung mesh, and then they bring the rope.

A loop closes on my wrist. I twist and bite the hand that set it and taste salt and grease and get a curse for it, but the loop holds. Another loop. My ankle, dragged straight and lashed. They lean their whole weight down the lines and the black comes up to meet my face and the salt-iron floods my mouth where the stone splits my lip open against the rock.

The hand closes on the back of my neck.

---

The hand. The hand in the dark. The hold.

It is here. It is now. It is real and warm through the leather and pressing my face down into the cold black, the broad spread of fingers and the heel of the palm bearing my cheek into the stone, and it is the exact shape, the exact weight, the exact closing I carried buried under the tar all those lows, and the scream tears out of me at last, the whole shape of it, every low of the hold come up my throat at once. They made this. They built the iron and stacked the stones and dragged her scent across the black and waited in the dark, and it was all only ever the long slow making of this one closing — the hand on the back of the boy's neck, folding him down, making him a thing. They have come walking across my island on two legs to put it back where it was.

Rope at the wrist. Rope at the ankle. A loop drawn cruel around my throat so that the more I pull the less the air comes, so that my own fighting chokes me. Blood in my eyes now, mine and theirs, all of it iron, all of it warm where my wounds should run cold and do not. They drag me. The black slides under me — my black, every fold of it I bled to learn, the spit and the seep and the loose rocks and the bled line, all of it sliding away backward beneath me with my own blood smeared in a long streak across it. I claw at it. I get a fistful of scree and clamp down and pull, and it pours out between my fingers, every grain of it running away, not one stone staying in my hand. That is the whole of it, the whole truth of this island in one fist of running scree. It does not hold what I cannot hold. It was never mine to keep. It was only ever borrowed back from the sea a low at a time, and the moment my hand opens it pours away.

I snarl. I am bitten and bleeding and snarling, more beast than boy left in the rope, the words gone, the self gone thin, only teeth and the red and the choke of the loop. And the rope does not care that I am a beast. The scree does not care. The split lip floods salt-iron and the black does not care.

Warm. My neck, where the hand is, is warm where it should be ice — even now, even here, the body keeps its wrong small secret. No white smokes at my mouth though I am screaming the cold air in and out. The frost has rung its pale circle around me on the black and never once landed on my skin. The cut at my lip is already drawing its edges in while they drag me, knitting faster than a cut should, and not one of these wrong things stands between my neck and the hand.

---

They haul me up the black toward their light and their many shapes, and somewhere off to the side a man laughs, short and low, the laugh of a thing got that has been hunted long. The sound of a man setting down a weight he has carried lows. It lands behind my breastbone and stays there.

And I see her.

She is on her knees still, but the hand is gone from her hair now, and her face is turned to me at last, full on, the last of some far light finding it. Her mouth is open. Her eyes are the worst thing on this whole island — worse than the rope, worse than the hand, worse than the running scree. They are not afraid for herself. There is no fear in them turned inward, no care for her own throat or her own kneeling. They are wet and they are wide and they break on me, all of it pouring out toward me across the black, and I understand, dragging through my own blood in their ropes, what she is seeing. She is seeing the thing her warning could not stop. She stacked the stones one on one on one and leaned the top one up-coast. She walked the wrong lows with her neck wrenched back like a hauled-out seal watching its own blind side. She did everything a body can do to push the death off another body, and it came down to this anyway, to me in the net, because the one thing she could not do was make me leave her in it.

She came free into my rocks. No hand on her, no rope, the high water crossed in the dark by her own choosing, and she laid her name down between us unguarded — set it out open the way she laid the soft hide open with the bared seam turned up to the sky — and she breathed mine into the wrong warmth of my face and did not run from the wrongness of it. And the second mouth went quiet under her cold hand. The one time. The one time in all the lows of this black that the ache behind my breastbone went still, that the hunger no meat answers lay down and was at peace, and it was her skin that did it, her palm, the cold press of her against the wrong heat of me. I came down off the safe dark to stand between her and the hook. And the hook took me instead.

I would do it again. That is the last clear thing in me, the last true shape my mind holds before the red heat closes back over it and drowns it. I would come down again. I would come down off the spine a hundred lows and walk into the dark and the dragged scent and the waiting men, and I would let them have me a hundred times, for the one time the second mouth went still under her hand.

They have made me property again. The hand on the back of the neck, and now a sound flung down at me out of the big one's mouth — low, made behind the teeth, the felt-shape of the closing hand turned to noise. *Come here. Be still. Shut your mouth. You are a thing.* The brand. The hold-word, the one they always used because it was the only word they ever had for me, because they never found the other. They think the word is a rope too. They throw it down at me the way they threw the net, sure it will wrap and hold.

It does not.

I hold the name they never found. My mother's. The one she gave me before the slavers, before the ship, before the dark of the hold — the one un-stolen piece, kept low and safe under my tongue, pressed flat like a coin behind the gap where the three teeth went, where no hand has ever reached and no hand reaches now. *Rook.* It is whole in me again. The island unmade it, wore it down to an animal sound and then to no sound at all, and her freely given name woke it back, and here it is, behind my teeth, while the brand breaks against my face. They can have the body in the net. They can drag it up their black to their light. They cannot have that. It is not in the body. It is under the tongue, behind the gone teeth, and it is mine, the last and only thing that has always been mine.

They think they have taken an animal.

They have not. I hold the brand, ground small behind my gone teeth, kept only so that one low I can hand it back across the water to the mouths it belongs in. I hold the name my mother gave me, whole and woken and theirs to never touch. And I hold her face turning toward me in the last of the light, wet-eyed and breaking and unafraid for herself. Three things. I carry all three down into the dark where they cannot reach, down under the rope and the net and the closing hand, down where the body goes when the body is taken.

I learned to care, and it caught me, and her face is the price, and I would pay it again.
