# Chapter 10: The Shapes on the Water

There are things on the water that walk like me.

---

I am up on the high rock when I see them, the lookout rock, the one with the wind-scoured top where I go to read the channel before a hunt. Grey low-light. The smoke-grey water out past the pinch, flat and old. And on it, far, where the far shore lies low and black in the haze — shapes. Long dark shapes that sit the water wrong. Not seal. Not ice. They do not roll with the swell the way a living thing rolls. They cut it. They go where they are pointed.

Boats.

The word comes up from far down and cold, comes up whole and unbroken from under the ice of me, and my whole skin draws tight at once.

On the boats — small dark uprights. They move. They bend and pull and bend, all together, the same shape doing the same work. Hands at oars. The shape of hands.

And the iron taste floods my mouth though I have not bit anything, my own blood, fear-blood, the body opening its old wound to taste the danger.

---

I am off the rock before I know I have moved.

Low. Belly to the black. The way I take a seal blind down a known road, hand on cold stone, no part of me up where the light can find it. My heart goes like a hunted thing against the rock. Cages. The dark of the hold, the wet wood, the salt of too many bodies, the big hand closing on the back of my neck. That is what the shapes mean. Hands mean that. People mean that.

I go down off the high rock and into the deep of my ground, into the broken country of black stone where I know every pinch and shelf and a thing that does not know them dies. I go to my crack in the cliff, my own shape worn into the black. I press my spine to the warm stone. It holds its heat there steady, like a thing with blood in it, and I am small in the dark and the warmth leans into my back. My breath still comes fast. The second mouth — the hunger no meat answers — wakes wide and aches, and pulls me up, out, toward the water I have just fled.

I make myself stay. One light. Two. I do not go up. I drink at the seep, the finger-thin water cold down the inside of me. I am the longest tooth on this shore. I am the thing that clears the rock. I tell myself this in the set of my jaw.

And on the third grey light I am back up on the lookout rock on my belly, because I cannot not look.

---

They have not gone. They have stayed.

On the far black shore there is smoke now. Not storm-smoke. Standing smoke, straight up in the still, three threads of it, four — old smoke, cook-smoke, the smell of it not here yet but I know its shape. Fire that is kept. Fire a thing makes on purpose and feeds.

And around the smoke, small uprights moving. I lie still as the rock and I watch them the way I watch a haul-out before I choose the one I will take — counting, learning the herd, where it is thick, where it is soft. They go on two legs all of them. They carry. They put things down and take things up. Some are big and some are smaller and some — my breath stops in my chest — some are very small, and they do not work. They go quick and crooked between the legs of the big ones, the way young things go, and one of the big ones bends and lifts one of the small ones up against its body the way nothing on this island has ever lifted me.

Hide stretched over poles. Round dark humps of it set against the black, with the smoke coming up through. Dens. Made dens, not found ones. A whole shore of them, close, all facing the kept fire.

A pack. A herd of the upright thing. The shape of the thing I had before the hold, far down, far under the ice — I cannot reach it but I know the shape of its hole in me, the place worn smooth where it used to sit, the way the crack is worn smooth to my own back.

Tribe.

---

I should go. Every fast part of me says go, go deep, go where the hands cannot reach, a thing this big and this many will sweep the rock clean and the longest tooth is still only one tooth. I have seen what many of them do to one. I wore it on my neck.

But I do not go.

I lie on the cold rock and the cold files at my bare arm, ringing the skin sharp and still. The warmth in my blood does not feel it, comes up easy in the quiet and lies down in me and stays. I watch the small ones run between the legs of the big ones, and the second mouth opens so wide it hurts more than the gut-hunger ever hurt, hurts up under the breastbone, a pull, a leaning, the whole of me tipping toward that far black shore like the warmth tips toward the warm stone. Warm to warm. I want — I want to be down there small between the legs. I want the fire that is kept. I want the bending and the lifting up.

And the same breath, the beast in me draws back hard, flattens, bares the teeth at the shape of them, because hands, because hold, because neck. Want and away, both at once, tearing me along the seam where they meet.

I watch until the light goes. I watch their kept fires come up red on the black when the grey dies, little held lights, warm across all that cold water, and I do not have it and they do, and the not-having is a new mouth, a third one, opening.

---

There is one comes down to the water alone.

Small — not the smallest, but young, a young one, down at the tide-line of the far shore where the boats are drawn up. It crouches. It does a thing I know. It works the wet stone with its hands, turning the rocks, reaching under — gleaning the low tide, the way I glean it, the crabs and the weed and the things the sea leaves. A young thing of the upright pack doing the work I taught myself alone in the dark. The same crouch. The same patient hands.

And a sound comes across the water, thin, worn down by all that distance to almost nothing — a voice. The young one calls back up the shore to the dens. A high clear call, words in it, shaped sounds, a name maybe, throwing a name up the black to someone who will answer.

The road from the head to the mouth, open, easy, a thing it does not even think to do.

Something turns over in me so hard I make a sound — and the sound that comes is the rasp, the cough with the growl on its back, and I clap my hand over my mouth in the dark on the cold rock the way I have learned to, to keep the rest of it in.

I go in behind my teeth, into the gap where three are gone, to hold the one true thing, the word that is mine, the sound the slavers made that I carried off the ship — to set it against that clear far call, to find a sound I can throw back across the water the way the young one threw its name up the black —

and the gap is warm and dark and empty, and there is nothing in it shaped like the word, and the warmth comes up slow from the deep of me and lies down full in the place where it used to be.

I take my hand off my mouth.

Down across the black water the young one stands, its low-tide gleaning held against its chest, and turns, and looks out at the dark sea — out toward my shore, toward the high rock, toward me — and I go flat and still as the stone, heart slamming, the longest tooth on the shore hiding from a child.

It cannot see me. It is too far and the light is gone.

But the next low-tide it will be down at that water again. And the one after. And the second mouth in me has already started counting the lights between.
