# Chapter 10: The Shapes on the Water

There are things on the water that walk like me.

---

I am up on the high rock when I see them. The lookout, the one with the wind-scoured top where the turf gives way to bare black and the wind comes off the channel with nothing in front of it to break on but my face. I come up here to read the water before a hunt — the set of the swell, the dark roads under it where the seals run, the haul-out rocks slick with the herd. Grey low-light. The smoke-grey water lies flat and old past the pinch, the way it always lies. And far out on it, where the far shore stands low and black in the haze, there are shapes.

Long dark shapes that sit the water wrong.

I have read this water ten thousand mornings. I know how a thing sits it. Seal rolls with the swell, gives to it, is part of it, a dark back rising and going under and the water never the worse for it. Ice does not roll but it drifts, it goes where the cold and the current carry it, dumb, a thing with no will pushed by things with no will. These do neither. They sit up on the water with their will in them. They cut it. The swell comes and lifts them and they hold their line through the lift and come out of it still pointed where they were pointed. A thing that holds its line against the sea has a head, and a head chooses.

The word comes up out of me. Up from far down, up from under the long cold I have packed over everything that was before the black, and it comes up whole.

Boats.

The whole skin of me draws tight at once, the small hairs lifting along the arm, the gut clenching to a fist. The body knows the word before the head can turn it. The body wore the word.

On the boats — small dark uprights. They move. Not the dumb sway of a thing the sea moves; they bend and pull and bend, all together, the same shape doing the same work down both sides at once. I have watched the channel pull oars without knowing the name for what it pulled. Now I have it. Hands at oars. The shape of hands, many of them, bending and hauling the dark boats across the smoke-grey toward — no. Not toward. Along. Going where they are pointed, along the far black.

The iron floods my mouth though I have bitten nothing. My own blood, the taste of it, sudden and bright on the back of the tongue without a wound to give it — the body opening some old seam in me to put the taste of danger where I will not miss it. Blood without a cut. Fear-blood. I have not tasted it since the hold.

---

I am off the rock before I know the body has chosen to move.

Low. Belly to the black. The cold of the stone comes straight through the hide and into the meat of me and I do not feel it because there is no room left in me to feel it. The way I take a seal blind down a known road — hand to cold stone, then the next stone, no part of me lifted up where a watching eye could cut my shape out of the rock. My heart goes against the slab like a hunted thing in a fist. And it is not the boats my heart runs from. It is what comes up behind the word for them.

Cages. The dark of the hold, the wet wood sweating salt, the close stink of too many bodies packed where they could not stand. The big hand closing on the back of my neck from behind, slow, sure, owning the whole of me in the one grip — and the low ugly sound made behind the teeth that went with the hand every time, the sound that meant come, that meant be still, that meant you are a thing and the thing is mine. The hand and the sound were one. I feel the sound now as a weight on the back of the neck, the felt-shape of the grip, and the skin there crawls and tries to get away from a hand that is half the world away across the water and years gone into the deep.

That is what the shapes mean. That is what hands mean. That is what many-of-them, together, pointed, means.

I go down off the high rock into the deep of my ground, into the broken country of black stone where I know every pinch and shelf and drop, where a thing that does not know them puts a foot wrong in the dark and breaks and feeds the gulls. I know them. I go fast and silent through the place that is only mine, and I come to the crack in the cliff, the seam in the black worn to the shape of my own curled body, and I fold myself into it and press my spine to the warm stone.

It holds its heat there steady. It always holds it. A thing with no blood that warms like a thing with blood, and I have stopped asking it why a long time ago, the way I have stopped asking the frost why it rings me and spares my skin. It is one of the wrong things about me and the wrong things do not answer. The warmth leans into my back, into the knots the running has pulled tight, and I am small in the dark with the stone holding me, and still my breath comes fast.

And then the other thing wakes.

The far mouth. The hunger no meat has ever been shaped to feed. It does not run with the rest of me. It lifts its head wide in the dark under my ribs and it turns the wrong way — back, toward the water, toward the shapes I have just fled, toward the hands. It leans me up out of the crack toward the very thing the fast part of me is breaking its body to escape. Away in the legs and toward in the chest, the two of them in the one skin, pulling at each other till the pull sings.

I make myself stay. I dig my pads into the black and I make the body keep its shape in the crack. One light comes and goes grey over the channel. Two. I do not climb. I drink at the seep when the throat cracks, the finger-thin water cold all the way down the inside of me, a cold I can name, a cold that is only cold. I am the longest tooth on this shore. I am the thing that clears the rock, that the gulls follow, that the seals do not see come. I say it in the set of the jaw, in the lock of the back teeth, because there is no mouth left in me to say it out loud and the saying-in-the-jaw is the only saying I have. The island took the other. I felt it go.

And on the third grey light I am back on the lookout on my belly, because the far mouth has won, because I cannot not look.

---

They have not gone. They have stayed.

On the far black shore there is smoke now.

Not storm-smoke, ragged and torn flat by the wind. Standing smoke. Straight up in the still air, three threads of it, four, holding their lines the way the boats held theirs — old smoke, kept smoke, the grease and char of it that I cannot smell across all that water but whose shape I know in the back of the nose from before, from the deepest under-part of me. Cook-smoke. Fire that is fed. Fire a thing makes on purpose and tends and does not let go out.

I keep a coal. In its fist of kelp, breathing slow, banked low so it lives and never flames, my one warmth in all the black that I made and not found. My one ember. It is the nearest thing to a fire I have ever managed to hold alone. And there across the water are four of them at once, fat and red and tended, and a coal in kelp is to them what the cat's picked bones are to the running herd.

Around the smoke, small uprights moving. I lie still as the rock I lie on and I watch them the way I watch a haul-out before I choose — counting, learning the shape of the herd, where it bunches thick, where it thins and goes soft and a thing could come in along the blind side. They go on two legs, all of them. They carry. They set things down and take things up and set them down again, the endless handling of things that only the upright pack does. Some are big. Some are smaller. And some — the breath stops flat in my chest — some are very small, and they do not work. They go quick and crooked between the legs of the big ones the way young things go, all dart and stumble, and one of the big ones bends down and takes one of the small ones up off the black and lifts it against its body, up out of the cold, against the warm of itself, and holds it there.

Nothing on this island has ever lifted me. The stone holds my back. It does not lift me. It cannot bend.

Hide stretched over poles. Round dark humps of it set against the black with the smoke coming up through the top, and the red of the kept fires glowing low in the seams. Dens, but made ones — not the found seam, not the crack a body wears itself into, but a shape a thing built around itself and the ones it keeps. A whole shore of them, set close, all the dark mouths of them turned to face the kept fire in the middle.

A pack. A herd of the upright thing, gathered, warm, tending its own.

The shape of the thing I had before the hold turns over far down in me, under the cold, under all the years of black. I cannot reach the thing itself. But I know the shape of its hole. I have lived against the smooth edge of that hole the way I live against the smooth of the crack worn to my back — the place worn down by a thing that used to sit there and sits there no longer.

Tribe.

---

I should go. Every fast part of me says go, go deep, go where the hands cannot reach. A thing this big, this many, will sweep the rock clean and not notice the sweeping. The longest tooth is still only one tooth, and I have seen what the many do to the one. I wore it on the neck. I wore the hand and the ugly sound that came with the hand, and the count of winters they cut out of me, and the three teeth they tore from the front of my mouth trying for everything else I would not give. They got the body. They got the soft thing the sea later spat up half-drowned on the black. The many are how the one is unmade. I know this in the meat.

But I do not go.

I lie on the cold rock and the cold files at my bare arm, ringing the skin sharp and clean, drawing its bright still line around the limb. The warmth in my blood does not feel it. It comes up easy in the quiet and lies down along the bone and stays, and the frost finds the edge of the warmth and stops there, the way it always stops, a ring of white drawn close around a bare wet dark that it will not cross. The two of them in me, distinct, never mixing — the cold that would still me and the heat that will not be stilled — and I do not feel the cold and I lie in it and watch.

I watch the small ones run between the legs of the big ones. And the far mouth opens so wide it hurts worse than the gut ever hurt — not down in the belly where seal-meat goes, but high, up under the breastbone, a pull, a leaning, the whole weight of me tipping toward that far black shore the way the warmth in me tips and pours toward the warm stone at my back. Warm to warm. I want. I want to be down there small between the legs. I want the fire that is fed and does not go out. I want the bending and the lifting up, the being taken off the cold against a warm that chose to hold me.

And in the same breath the beast in me draws back hard. Flattens. The lip peels up off the teeth at the shape of them, a low thing starting in the chest, because hands, because hold, because the weight on the back of the neck and the ugly sound that owned me. Want and away, both at the full, hauling me two ways at once until I think the thing that holds them in one body must split and spill whichever one is under.

I watch until the light goes. I watch the kept fires come up red on the black when the grey dies — little held lights, warm across all that cold dead water, low and steady and fed. I do not have it. They do. And the not-having of it is a new place opening in me, a third mouth under the other two, and this one wants only the red lights and the warm that makes them, and there is no kill on this island that will ever close it.

---

There is one comes down to the water alone.

Small — not the smallest, but young. A young one, down at the tide-line of the far shore where the dark boats are drawn up out of the reach of the swell. It crouches. And it does a thing I know.

It works the wet stone with its hands. Turning the rocks, tipping them off their seats and reaching quick into the cold hollow under, the way you reach under before the things there can fold and dart. Gleaning the low tide. The crabs, the weed, the small soft things the sea leaves behind in its going. The same crouch I taught my own body in the dark with no one to show me. The same patience in the hands, the same reading of which stone hides a thing and which hides only cold mud. A young thing of the upright pack, doing alone at the water the exact work I clawed out of the black to keep myself alive, and it does it easy, in the open, in the last of the light, with the kept fires red behind it and its pack close up the shore.

And a sound comes across the water.

Thin. Worn down by all that distance to almost nothing, the way a smell comes from very far and is only the ghost of itself. A voice. The young one lifts its head and calls back up the black toward the dens — a high clear call with shape in it, with edges, sounds set against sounds, a word, maybe a name, thrown up the shore to someone who will catch it and throw one back. The whole easy road of it. From down in the head where the wanting lives, up the throat, out through the mouth, laid clean on the air. A thing it does not even know it is doing. A thing as easy to it as breath.

Something turns over in me so hard the body makes a sound on its own — and the sound that comes out is the rasp, the cough with the growl riding its back, the thing the throat makes now where words used to be made. I clap my hand over my mouth on the cold rock in the dark, the way I have learned to clap it, to keep the rest of the sound in, to keep my shape from giving itself up across the water.

And under the hand I want a sound to set against that clear far call. One sound. Mine. The one that means me and not a thing, to throw back across the black the way the young one threw its name up the shore. So I do the old thing. I go in behind the teeth, into the gap where three are torn out, the dark hollow where the one true thing has always lived — the name my mother set on me before the ship, before the hands, the name they never knew to reach for and so never took. I go in to find it and throw it.

And the gap is empty.

It is warm and it is dark and there is nothing in it shaped like the name. I run the tongue along the scarred edges of the gum, the hard ridges where the slavers' work healed over, into the smooth hollow where the word lived ten thousand nights, and there is no word in it. Only the gap. Only the warmth that comes up slow from the deep of me and lies down full in the place the name used to be, the way water lies down in the print a lifted stone leaves — taking the shape of the thing that is gone and being nothing like it.

The island took it. Not the men. The men never reached it. And tonight I learn what the taking costs me — that I have nothing to send back, no answer to lay on the air, no shape to set against a child calling easy up a shore. The young one has a sound for itself and I have a warm hollow where mine used to be. I reach and reach into it and bring up nothing but the warmth, and the warmth is not a name.

I take my hand off my mouth.

There is no sound to keep in. I have none to throw.

Down across the black water the young one stands. It gathers its low-tide gleaning up against its chest, the small cold harvest of it, and turns — and looks out at the dark sea. Out toward my shore. Toward the high rock. Toward the place where I lie flat and dark in the dark with the heart slamming the slab, the longest tooth on the whole emptied jaw of this island, gone flat and small and still to hide from a child.

It cannot see me. It is too far, and the light is dead, and I am one black shape pressed into a shore of black shapes.

But the next low tide it will be down at that water again. And the one after that. The water bares the same stones at the same hour, and the young one knows it, and the young one will come. And the far mouth in me, the one with no name left behind it to feed it, the one that leans and leans toward the warm across the water and finds nothing to lean on — the far mouth has already started counting the lights between.
