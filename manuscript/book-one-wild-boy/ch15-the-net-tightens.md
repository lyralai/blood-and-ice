# Chapter 15: The Net Tightens

There are new tracks on my black, and they are not hers.

Three sets. Spread wide, the way men walk when they mean to cover ground and lose nothing between them. Hard soles, not the wrap of her up-coast feet. They came at low and went back at low and they did not cross my line — but they stood at it. I find where they stood. The black is scuffed in a half-ring just short of where I have bled the stone, where the salt and the old smoke of me say *mine*, and they stopped there and looked in, and one of them crouched. The crouch-mark is deep on the toes. He was a long time looking.

The second mouth pulls tight under my breastbone. Not hunger. Something colder. They were looking for the shape of me.

---

They come again three lows later, and now there are five.

I am up the cliff in the crack that is my home, flat in the dark of it, when they come over the ridge. They move slow and they move together and they carry the long shapes — not seal-spears, longer, with the hooked iron that is made to hold a live thing and not let it run. They fan out across my low ground. They turn the rocks I turn. One of them finds my fish-rack, the split sticks where I dry the catch. He calls the others with a short bark. They come and look, all of them, their backs to me, and I watch from the high black and I do not breathe loud.

They know I am real now. The rack told them. A ghost does not dry fish.

I have a thing they do not. I have every fold of this black under my hands. I know which scree slides and which holds, which crack runs through and which ends in stone, where the tide leaves a road and where it leaves a drowning. They learn it one low at a time, paying for each piece. I was paid in already, in blood, in the long cold lows when learning it was the only thing between me and meat. They will never catch up. But each time they come they take a piece and keep it, and the island that was all mine grows their lines across it like frost creeping the rock, and inside their lines the ground that is mine gets smaller.

---

She warns me. In her way.

She does not come close. She leaves the marks instead — three stones stacked on my line, which she has never done, the top one pointing up-coast toward the smoke of the far village I have smelled and never gone to. She comes at the wrong lows now, off her old pattern, quick and not stopping, her face turned over her shoulder the way a hauled-out seal turns when the orca-shadow is in the water. Her own kind put that turn in her neck. Whatever the hard-soled men are, they are hers and not hers — the way the slaver was a man, but not the kind of man the boy in the hold beside me was.

She knows something I cannot smell yet. The top stone points up-coast. The warning is for me.

---

So I stop watching my own black.

I climb the spine of the island at the failing of the light. I lie on the high cold stone and I look the other way — up-coast, where the smoke stands. Her village. I have never looked at it. I have kept my eyes on my tide, my line, my finger-thin seep of fresh water in the crack, my shelf of wrong things up in the dark of the home where the warm stone sits — on the small kingdom I bled for, because that was all the world that could be mine.

It is not enough now.

I find her among the small fires. I do not know how I know her shape so far off, but I do — the kelp-rope hair, the way she carries grief in her shoulders like a yoke. There are bigger shapes near the fires now that were not there before. Men who stand the way the hard-soled ones stood at my line. They have come up-coast to where she is. The cold rings me on the high stone, that ring of frost that lays itself down around me and never lands, and I lie inside it dry, warm where I should be dying, and I watch the fires of a place that is not mine. The second mouth opens for it.

I have two things to guard now. My black. And the small fire I can pick out of all the others. I cannot stand over both.

---

The lows compress. Watch, hide, count, watch.

Six men, then eight. They come in the gray and they leave in the gray and between they walk my ground in their spread line with the holding-spears. I learn them like I learned the tide. The big one leads; he is the one who crouched. He does not hurry. He does not tire. He turns the right rocks. He is the worst of them because he is patient, and patience is the only thing on this island that has ever been a match for me.

I leave them nothing now. No fish on the rack — I eat where I kill and bury the bones in the scree. No tracks in the soft ash-ground — I move on the hard black and the bare stone. I pull myself thin and quiet and gone, the way I was at the start before there was a rack and a seep and a home to thicken me into a shape. It costs. It costs the small warmth of having a place. I am a ghost again on my own black, by my own choosing, so that their spears close on empty rock low after low.

And every low after they go, I climb the spine and look up-coast and find the small fire and make sure it is still burning.

---

I work it out lying on the high stone in the dark, the way I work out rot-ice from sound-ice — by listening until the shape comes clear.

They are not driving me off. A thing you want gone, you crowd to the cliff and over it. They are not crowding. They are learning me — where I drink, where I sleep, where I cross at low, which way I run when a shape comes over the ridge. The big patient one is filling himself with me the way I filled myself with the tide-tables of this black. He is not hunting an animal to kill it. He is hunting one to take it, alive on the hooked iron, the way the slavers took, the way the hand closed on the back of my neck in the dark.

And something rises behind my gone teeth when the hand-in-the-dark comes back. A sound. Not a word I ever had — the body buried it, in the hold, under the tar and the piss and the dark, and it stayed buried until she laid her own name in my rocks and breathed mine into the wrong warmth of my face and woke it. The sound the slavers made when the hand closed. The shape of that closing hand. I hold it behind the gaps where the three teeth went and I grind it small and I keep it, because it is mine now and not theirs.

I know the shape of them now. I have it whole. They are the hold come walking on two legs across my island to fold over me again.

---

I could go.

Inland there is the deep island, the high black where no road runs and no tide reaches and no spear has ever crouched at a bled line. Caves the smoke-men have never smelled. Cold that kills men. Cold that has never once killed me. I could be gone up into it and they would walk my low ground forever with their hooked iron and close their net on bare stone and old bones and never once touch me. No hand on the back of my neck again, ever. The way the gull is free, the way the tide is free, the way I swore on the first black morning I would stay or die.

I lie on the spine in the full dark and I look up-coast at the one small fire I have learned out of all the fires, and I do not move toward the deep island.

Because she is down there. Because she laid her name in my rocks free and unguarded and I said it back with naked breath and her cold hand sat in the wrong warmth of my face and did not pull away. The second mouth that no meat has ever filled went quiet for the first time when she touched me. I will not climb up into the cold dark and leave the one thing that ever quieted it standing in the firelight with the hooked iron drawing close.

The net is tightening on her too. I can see it from here. The big shapes by her fire. The spears that came up-coast.

I will not be the gull. I come down off the spine in the dark, toward the smoke, toward her — and that is the choice that hands them the only hook that can hold me.
