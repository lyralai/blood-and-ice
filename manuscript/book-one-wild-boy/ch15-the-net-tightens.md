# Chapter 15: The Net Tightens

There are new tracks on my black, and they are not hers.

Three sets. Spread wide, the way men walk when they mean to cover ground and lose nothing between them — not the close, careful step of one body coming alone, but the open fan of bodies that have learned to hunt together, each one set far enough out that nothing can slip the gaps between. Hard soles. Not the wrap of her up-coast feet, the soft skin-and-sinew that leaves a track like a held breath. These bite the black. They press their edges in clean and leave the shape of a thing made to grip, a thing that does not feel the cold of the stone through it. They came at low and they went back at low, reading the tide the way she reads it, and they did not cross my line.

But they stood at it.

I find where they stood. The black is scuffed in a half-ring just short of where I have bled the stone — where the salt of me, the old smoke, the long lying-down all say *mine* to anything with a nose to read it. They came to the edge of that and they stopped. They felt it the way an animal feels the place another animal owns, the prickle that says *not past here*, and they obeyed it, and that is worse than if they had walked straight in. A thing that walks in does not know what it is walking into. A thing that stops at the line has read the line. Has understood that something keeps it.

One of them crouched. The crouch-mark is deep on the toes, the heel come up light — a man folded down low and held there, weight forward, looking. The print of him is pressed long into the black where he settled and did not shift for a long count of breaths. He was looking in. Looking for the shape of the thing that bleeds a line across the stone and calls the cold edge of an island its own.

The second mouth pulls tight under my breastbone. Not hunger — the thing that draws the gut up small and hard, a fist closing in the dark. They were looking for the shape of me. And the half-ring of their standing says they did not find it, and the long crouch says they mean to come back until they do.

---

They come again three lows later, and now there are five.

I am up the cliff in the crack that is my home, poured flat into the dark of it, my back to the warm stone, when they come over the ridge. They do not come the way they came before. They move slow and they move together, holding their spacing the way a net holds its spacing, no man drifting, no gap opening — and they carry the long shapes. Not seal-spears. Longer. The iron on the end is hooked, the barb turned back on itself, and I know that shape in my body before I know it in my eye, because the body remembers iron made to hold a live thing and not let it run. It remembers the bite of the turned barb. A spear for killing wants a clean point that goes in and comes out. This iron is made to go in and stay, to drag, to keep a thing screaming on the line until the hands come.

They fan out across my low ground. They turn the rocks I turn — not all the rocks, the right ones, the loose flat ones a thing might sleep under, the ones with the dry hollow beneath. One of them finds my fish-rack. The split sticks I lashed and stood and dried the catch on, low after low, the small good thing I made with my own hands out of nothing. He stops at it. He calls the others with a short bark, low and flat, and the bark carries across my black to where I lie and the sound of it lands behind my breastbone like a stone dropped in still water. They come, all of them, and they stand around the rack with their backs to the high cliff where I am, and they look at the split sticks and the silver scale dried into the wood. I watch their backs from the high black. I make my breath go small and slow, I let none of it out loud, and none of it smokes — the white that should hang at a mouth in this cold does not hang at mine, and I am glad of that now, the wrongness that hides me, even as I feel the lack of it like a hole.

They know I am real now. The rack told them. A ghost does not lash sticks. A ghost does not dry fish on a frame it built. The rack is a thing hands made, and hands mean a body, and a body means a thing that can be taken.

I have a thing they do not have. I have every fold of this black under my hands. I know which scree slides under a weight and which holds; which crack runs all the way through the spine and which one chokes shut in solid stone three lengths in; where the tide leaves a dry road across the spit and where it leaves a drowning that looks like a road until the water is at your chest. They learn it one low at a time, paying for each piece with a low of cold and a low of walking. I was paid in already — paid in blood, in the long starving lows at the start when learning this island was the only thing standing between me and being meat for the sea. They started late. They will never catch all the way up.

But each time they come they take a piece of it and they keep it. They learn the spit. They learn the loose rocks. They learn the seep, maybe, next, or the low road, and the island that was all mine grows their lines across it the way frost creeps the wet rock at the turn of the cold — fine and pale and spreading, a little further each low, ringing in more and more of what was bare and free. And inside their lines the ground that is mine gets smaller. I feel it shrink under me low by low, the warm small kingdom I bled to make, and there is nothing I can do to push their frost back off it.

---

She warns me. In her way.

She does not come close now. She does not climb to where her hand sat in the wrong warmth of my face. She leaves the marks instead — three stones stacked on my line, one on one on one, which she has never done, never once in all the lows of laying things down. The top stone is set leaning, pointing, the way you point with the whole of a thing and not a finger, and it points up-coast, toward the smoke of the far village I have smelled across the wind low after low and never once gone toward.

She comes at the wrong lows now, off the old pattern I had learned of her the way I learned the tide — not the slack water when she always came, but the broken times, quick, not stopping, her face turned over her shoulder. I know that turn of a neck. I have seen it on the hauled-out seal when the dark shape is in the water under the swell, the head that keeps wrenching back to the place the death will come from, the body that cannot stop watching its own blind side. Her own kind put that turn in her neck. The hard-soled men are hers and not hers — hers the way the slaver was a man, the same shape walking, and not hers the way the boy chained beside me in the hold was a man, the one whose breath I learned in the dark, the one who was warm against my side in a place that had no other warmth in it. The same shape can be the hand that feeds and the hand that closes. She knows which the hard-soled men are. It is in her neck.

She knows something I cannot smell yet. The top stone leans up-coast. The warning is laid down for me, set out open the way she sets everything out, and I take it.

---

So I stop watching my own black.

I climb the spine of the island at the failing of the light, up where no road runs and the wind comes off the cold inland with nothing to break it, and I lie down on the high stone and I look the other way. Up-coast. Where the smoke stands thin and grey against the last of the light. Her village. I have never once looked at it. All the lows of this island I have kept my eyes turned down and in — on my tide, my line, my finger-thin seep of fresh water bleeding out of the crack, my shelf of wrong things up in the dark of the home where the warm stone sits. On the small kingdom I bled for, because looking out at the warm fires of a place that could never be mine was a hunger I could not feed, and a thing you cannot feed you learn not to look at.

It is not enough now. The looking-in is not enough. The thing I have to guard is no longer only down here in the rocks.

I find her among the small fires. I should not be able to — she is a single shape at that distance, one body among the bodies moving against the light. But I know her, the way I know rot-ice from sound-ice without my weight ever going down on it. The kelp-rope hair, half loose. The way she carries the grief in her shoulders like a yoke laid across them, the load she came to my rocks to set down and could not. I find her out of all of them and the finding pulls the second mouth wide.

There are bigger shapes near the fires now that were not there before. They stand the way the hard-soled men stood at my line — squared, weight even, taking up the ground as if it owes them the standing-room. They have come up-coast to where she is. The cold rings me on the high stone as I watch, that ring of frost laying its pale circle down around me a hand's width off my skin, drawing itself out over the stone and never landing, and I lie inside it dry and warm where the cold should be taking me, and I look down at the fires of a place that is not mine and never will be. The second mouth opens for it anyway. It does not care what is mine. It only knows the warm is down there, the many-bodied warm with the small grieving shape inside it, and it aches toward it the way it has ached toward every kindness she ever laid on my line.

I have two things to guard now. My black, behind me, with the hooked iron walking it. And the one small fire I can pick out of all the others. They are at the two ends of the world from each other, and I have one body, and I cannot stand over both.

---

The lows compress. Watch, hide, count, watch.

Six men, then eight. They come in the grey and they leave in the grey and between the greys they walk my ground in their spread line with the holding-spears, and I learn them the way I learned the tide — by lying still and letting them come and come until the shape of them stands clear. The big one leads. He is the one who crouched. He does not hurry, he does not tire, he does not waste himself turning the wrong rocks the way the others do. He turns the right ones. He reads my black the way I read it, slower, paying as he goes, but reading it true. He is the worst of them, and it is not his size that makes him the worst. It is the patience. He is patient the way the tide is patient, the way the cold is patient — willing to come back and come back and come back, taking a hand's width each time, sure of the ending. Patience is the one thing this island has ever set against me that I could not simply outlast, because it is the thing I win with, and he has it too.

I leave them nothing now. No fish on the rack — I tear the rack down and scatter the sticks, I eat where I kill, I push the bones deep into the scree where the scree will swallow them. No tracks in the soft ash-ground where a print would hold — I move on the hard black and the bare wet stone that takes nothing and tells nothing. I pull myself thin and quiet and gone, back to the shape I was at the very start, before there was a rack or a seep or a home, before the island let me thicken into a thing with a place. A ghost again. By my own choosing this time, which is the difference, and the difference does not make it cost less.

It costs the small warmth of having a place. The rack was a thing I made; pulling it down is unmaking a piece of the only making I have done. The buried bones, the chosen bare stone, the careful gone-ness — every low of it peels me back off the shape I had grown into and leaves me thinner, colder, closer to the animal that owns nothing and is owned by nothing and leaves no mark the world can close a hand on. Their spears come down on empty rock, low after low, and that is the win, and the win tastes of going backward.

And every low, after they go and the grey takes them back over the ridge, I climb the spine and I look up-coast and I find the small fire and I make sure it is still burning. That is the other half of the low. The hiding, and then the looking. The being no one, and then the one thing that is still someone, far off, across the wind, still alight.

---

I work it out lying on the high stone in the dark, the way I work out rot-ice from sound-ice — by listening to the shape of the thing until it comes clear and I am sure.

They are not driving me off. A thing you want gone, you crowd. You come loud and you come wide and you push it to the cliff and over the cliff, and you do not care which way it breaks so long as it breaks away from you. They are not crowding. They move slow, they move quiet, they hold the gaps closed. They are not pushing me anywhere. They are learning me. Where I drink. Where I sleep. Where I cross the spit at low and which way I run when a shape comes over the ridge — they have started leaving a man back, I have seen it, one held still up on the high ground to watch which way the ghost bolts. The big patient one is filling himself with me the way I filled myself with the tide of this black, low by low, until he holds the whole of my pattern in him and there is no move left that he has not already seen me make.

He is not hunting an animal to kill it. The hooked iron told me that the first low I saw it. You do not barb the iron for a thing you mean to leave on the stone. You barb it for a thing you mean to drag off the stone alive and keep. He is hunting one to take. Alive on the turned hook, the way the slavers took, the way the hand closed on the back of my neck in the dark of the hold and did not let go and did not care that I was screaming, because a thing that screams is a thing still worth the taking.

And something rises behind my gone teeth when the hand-in-the-dark comes back up out of me. A sound. Not a word I ever chose to keep — the body buried it down in the hold, under the tar-stink and the piss and the dark, packed it away where I would not have to carry it, and it stayed buried low after low on this black until she laid her own name in my rocks free and unguarded and breathed mine back into the wrong warmth of my face and the breaking of my silence cracked the lid off everything, the kept and the buried both. So it came up too. The sound the slavers made when the hand closed. Not a name. A brand — the felt-shape of that closing hand turned into noise, a low thing made behind their teeth that meant *mine, be still, come, you are a thing*. I hold it now behind the gaps where the three teeth went, and I grind it small, and I keep it — not because it is me, it is not me, it was never me — but because it is theirs and I have taken it off them, and a thing I have taken is a thing I can someday hand back across the water to the mouths it belongs in. It is mine to keep only so that I can one day be rid of it. That is the whole of why I hold it.

I know the shape of the hard-soled men now. I have it whole, the way I have the spit and the seep and the loose rocks. They are the hold come walking. Come up out of the dark of memory and onto two legs and across the ridge of my black, with the hooked iron in their hands, to fold over me again the way the hand folded over my neck and made me a thing.

---

I could go.

Inland there is the deep island. The high black where no road runs and no tide reaches and no spear has ever crouched at a bled line — where the stone goes up and in and gets colder the further it goes, into caves the smoke-men have never smelled, into cold that kills men. Cold that has never once killed me. I have walked the edge of it and felt it come for my breath and find nothing, felt the frost ring out around me on that high stone the same as everywhere, drawing its circle and never landing on my skin. The deep island would not take me. It takes them. I could climb up into it and be gone, truly gone, and they could walk my low ground until they are old and close their net on bare wet stone and old buried bone and never once lay the hook in me. No hand on the back of my neck ever again. Free the way the gull is free, the way I swore on the first black morning of this place that I would be free or be dead and nothing between.

I lie on the spine in the full dark and I look up-coast at the one small fire I have learned out of all the fires, and I do not move toward the deep island.

Because she is down there. Because she crossed the high water in the dark and laid her name in my rocks free and unguarded, set it down open between us the way she laid the soft hide open with the seam bared to the sky, and I said it back with naked breath that showed no ghost in the cold and she did not run from the wrongness of that. Because her cold hand sat in the wrong warmth of my face and pressed, a little, and did not pull away. Because the second mouth that no meat has ever filled, the ache behind my breastbone that has answered to no kill and no catch in all the lows of this black, went quiet for the first time when her skin was on me. Quiet. The hunger that is not for meat, gone still under her palm. I have one thing on this whole island that has ever closed that mouth, and it is not up in the cold dark of the deep island. It is down there in the firelight with the hooked iron drawing close around it.

The net is tightening on her too. I can see it from up here, in the dark, the way the cold sees. The big squared shapes by her fire that were not there before. The spears that came up-coast on the same tide that brought the men to my line. Whatever is closing on me is closing on her in the same breath, from the same hands, and she has nowhere up there to climb that is colder than men can follow.

I will not be the gull.

I come down off the spine in the dark, toward the smoke, toward her — down off the one high place where no hand could ever reach me, down toward the warm, the fires, the hooked iron, by my own feet, by my own choosing. And that is the choice that hands them the only hook that can hold me.
