# Chapter 28: The Pit

The water-crossing ends the way the down road ended: with the hand on the back of my neck, and a hole opening to take me, and the body knowing the hole before the head can put a shape to it.

I count the crossing in rolls, not in lows, because there is no light in the hold to cut the dark into days. The floor lifts and falls. I lay my breath under it and keep the count, and somewhere in the long count the water under the hull changes its voice — goes from the warm channel-brown to a deeper grind, the open brine, the long swells that take the made thing and shake it like a brute shaking a small thing in its jaws. Two of the dying ones go quiet up in the light. I hear the quiet come. The cough that rattled them low after low stops between one roll and the next, and the slavers' feet change their weights above me, the dragging gait carrying something over the side, and the splash, and then fewer feet. The green is still in them. It rode their bodies onto the water and it does not stop killing them just because they have left the shore that grew it. I file it where the still place is. *The death is in them and it does not let go.*

Then the brine-voice shallows again. Another channel. Another drowned green, smaller, sourer, the tar-stink of more made things laid close on warm wet air, and over it, riding the planks into the hold, a sound I have not heard since before the shore — the sound of many human throats together. Not naming. Not the girl's slow careful laying of word on the dark. A massed sound, low and constant as surf, constant as a swarm: men, more men than I have ever smelled or heard at once, packed close on a far shore, living their lives in the open the way no man lives long on my green.

We have come to where the humans keep their own dark.

---

They take me up out of the hold and the light comes down on me like a blow.

I have been in the tar-dark so long that the day goes into the eyes like a spear, white and total, and I hang in the nets blind while the body does what the body does — reads the place through the other doors while the eye is shut. The smell first. Smoke, but not green-smoke; the flat reek of many small fires fed on cut wood and dung and the grease of cooked meat. Iron everywhere, the flat metal tang of it laid over everything the way the rot-stink laid over my green. Dung and piss and the sweet-sick of bodies kept too close too long without the rain to wash them. And under it, faint, threading through — the high green rot of caged things. Brutes. Tide-kin. Carrion-kin. The catch of other boats. The green of my whole island, harvested and hauled across the water and held here in the human dark, dying slow in iron the way the dying ones died in the hold.

The eye comes back and I wish it had not.

It is a place of pits. That is the first true thing the eye gives me. The ground is cut open in rings, deep, the walls of them sheer and slick, and around each cut the men stand massed and shouting, and down in the cuts things move that I cannot see from the nets but can read off the men's mouths — the same mouth-shape on all of them, the wide hungry stretch of throats that have come to watch a thing be killed and want it slow. I knew that mouth on the leader's men out wide on the high ground. Here it is on every face, low after low of faces, a whole shore of men whose hunger has learned to feed on the bottom of a pit.

They have built my whole life out of holes that have me. The hold was a hole. The green was a hole I learned to call mine and could not keep. Now this. They take what they own and they put it in a cut in the ground and they stand around the rim and shout while it dies. The body knows it before the head: I am for the pit. The wrong-blooded thing that does not die of the green — they have carried it across the water to find out, in the one place built to find out, how it dies of men.

---

There is a stir at the rim, and the leader comes.

I know his gait before I see the furrow. The drag is worse — the rot I smelled wake in his torn face has gone down into him, the long open from eye to jaw gone tight and shining and sweet, the green riding him too, even him, the patient one. He is dying of my island and he does not stop. He comes to the edge of the nets and crouches and turns my chin up into the white light with his hard fingers, the last time, and he looks at the gap behind my teeth where the three went, and his face does the thing all their faces do — the moves-in-it, the *you are something*, the reading of the wrong-blood he cannot name. He has carried me across the water on that face. He has paid a furrow full of rot for it. And now he is handing me to other men, men with more iron and deeper pits, and I read in the set of him that he is selling the reading along with the body. *This one does not die. Watch. Watch it not die.*

He makes the sound. Low, behind his ruined teeth, the felt-shape of the closing hand turned to noise. The brand. The hold-word. He lays it on me one last time, and it falls off the face that will not take it the way it always falls, and lands behind my breastbone where I file the things to hand back, and lies there small and hard, a third one now, three brands ground down and kept against the one low.

Then they cut the nets off me, and the hands come, many hands, and they walk me to the rim of a pit on legs that are mine but folded, and the white light goes all the way down the cut to a floor I cannot see, and the massed throats around the rim open wider, and they put the hand on the back of my neck.

And they let go.

---

The fall is not long. The body counts it anyway.

I come down the way the soles taught me to come down off a stone, loose, rolling the blow out through the shoulder and the hip, and the floor of the pit is packed earth gone dark and slick, and the smell that comes up off it is the smell that lived under everything else in this place: old blood. Low after low of it, soaked down into the dirt, gone black and sweet and deep, the floor of a hole where things are brought to be killed and have been killed past counting. I am on my hands in it. I am breathing it. My split lip, the one that drew shut clean in the hold, opens again where the fall splits it against the packed earth, and the salt-iron of my own floods my mouth and mixes with the old black blood of the floor, and for one roll of the world I cannot tell which is mine and which is the pit's.

Above me the throats roar. The rim is a ring of dark heads against the white sky, fists going up, the want pouring down the slick walls the way the rot-vapor poured down off the high stones at dusk. They have come to watch. I am the watched thing on the floor of the hole, the small wrong-blooded thing, and they want to see the way it goes.

There is something else in the pit with me.

I read it before I see it — the shift of weight at the far wall, the low cut-breath of a thing kept hungry, the green-rot of it gone close and wild. They have put a thing in the pit to do to me what the green could not. I do not turn to it yet. I am still on my hands in the old blood. I am still finding the count.

Then I turn.

---

It comes off the far wall low, and the body reads it before the head can.

The shoulder first. One shoulder riding high, higher than the other, the line of it wrong the way it has always been wrong. Then the hand — the curled hand, the fingers that never opened, working at the air. Then the gait, that crooked rocking run that the strong hunters laughed at and that I learned, low after low, to set my own pace by. I know that shoulder. I know that hand. I know that rocking, wrong-made run better than I know my own.

Marrow.

They carried two across the water. They knew the worth of one. The runt brute, the wrong-made small one, the leavings the strong crack the bone and leave behind — they threw him in the pit as fodder. Meat to set against the thing that does not die, so the men at the rim could watch the dying thing be torn. They did not know what they had. They never know what they have. They saw a small broken Vorth and they made it bait, and they put it in the hole with me, and it crossed the brine and the brand and the iron and came out the far side still itself, still quick, still mine.

He stops a stride from me. The high shoulder drops. The curled hand goes still.

He makes the sound.

Not a word the men would call a word. A torn thing, hoarse, half breath — the still-sound, the keeping shape I laid on him low after low in the green, given back to me now across the dark of the pit. *I taught him that.* I made that thing with my own mouth and put it into his, and the slavers carried it across the water in him and did not know it was there, and the home that made me is gone and the Kala is gone and the crown is gone, but the thing I *made* came through whole where the home did not.

The warmth comes up behind my breastbone.

---

It starts where the far mouth is, where the second mouth has howled silent and empty since the iron went into the Kala on the stone. But it is not the far mouth. The far mouth is a pull, a want, a hole. This is the other thing — the small wrong secret the body has always kept and never named.

The neck warm under the hand where it should run other than warm. The lip drawing shut while the slavers' wounds went to rot. The fever-air sliding off me, the venom going out fast and leaving no mark, the swarms ringing me and not landing. I have wanted, low after low, to know what that warm thing is. I have reached for it in the dark and come back with nothing, no word, no edge to hold it by. I reach for it now and the same nothing meets me — but the warm is real, and it is rising, and for once it does not slide away under my hand.

The blood in my mouth, mine and the pit's together, lights it the way a coal banked under ash lights when the air finds it. It comes up out of the breastbone and goes down into the hands pressed in the old black blood of the floor. It is not the red rage. I know the red rage — teeth and want and the boy gone thin. This is lower than the rage, under it the way the still place is under the second mouth. It is warm. It is sure. And it answers nothing in the men at the rim. It answers Marrow.

A pulse comes up that is not my heart. Once. Faint. The warm of a rot-hollow rising into me in the dark, except there is no hollow now, there is only the blood, and Marrow a stride from me with his shoulder dropped and his hand gone still. It is a feeling like recognition. Like a hand laid over the back of a young thing's head — *kala*, the keeping sound, the one that woke my name — except no one lays it. It rises from inside, out of the blood, *for* this. For him. The first thing in all the lows since the iron took her that has reached the far mouth from the inside and found it not empty.

I want to know what it is. I press at it the way I have pressed at the gap behind my teeth where the name was buried. And the same wall meets me — no word, no shape, the head reaching and the thing sliding back under. I cannot turn it over. Not for refusing. For having no hand that fits it yet. So I set it down half-held, the deepest wrong thing, the one that lit in the blood at the bottom of the hole and went quiet again the moment I looked straight at it.

The pulse fades. The warm stays low and faint, a flicker, an omen with no use I can name. I do not lean on it. I have never been able to lean on it. I rise.

---

I rise, and Marrow does not come at me, because Marrow was never coming at me. The men at the rim only saw a hungry thing rush a fallen thing. They saw fodder finding meat. What they saw was Marrow crossing the floor to stand at my shoulder, the way he has stood at my shoulder since the low I named him out of the strong tribe's leavings.

The roar above us changes. It pitches up, ragged, the way a sound pitches when men see the thing they came to watch do the thing they did not come to see. The two broken things in the bottom of the hole are not tearing each other apart.

So they will send something real down. I read it already. They will not waste two wrong-blooded things on an empty pit; they will drop a brute in to do the work, and we will meet it on the floor in the old blood. Good. Let it come.

Because the warm in my blood is not a strength. It did not pour into the empty places and fill them. The boy's strength ran out on the stones, ran to mud, and a boy's strength does not come back in a night, and this warm thing does not give it back. It only flickered, and was seen by something in Marrow, and went quiet. It is an omen and a recognition and nothing I can swing.

But I do not need it to be. I never won with strength. I took the apex on my own ground, lows ago, when I learned I was not the strongest thing on the island and won anyway — not with strength, with the still place, with the count, with the wrong-blood that does not break when it should. I have that yet. And now I have Marrow. The quick eyes. The curled hand that sets a snare faster than any whole hand in the tribe. Two of us in the hole who do not break the way the men paid to watch us break.

I do not look up at them. You do not spend the eye on ground that is already gone, and the rim is gone to me; I will not climb it this night. I spend the eye on the floor the foot is in. The floor is dark with old blood, soaked deep with every caught thing they ever brought here to kill, and I am standing in it, and Marrow is beside me, his shoulder high, his breath low and even and waiting on my read.

---

They will haul me out when it is done. They have a use that bleeds and walks back clean, the same use the leader read, the same use the girl read, the same use the seer read off my blood with the old signs. They will keep me. They will bring me back to the rim again and again to find out the thing they carried me across the water to find out, and every time they will lay the hand on the back of my neck, and every time they will make the sound, sure it wraps and holds.

It does not wrap. It never wrapped. The brand falls off and lands behind my breastbone with the other three, kept against the one low.

I kneel in the old black blood with the count going and lay the squared stones in the still place, faced and even, the way the tribe laid its dead on the high stones. The name my mother gave me, woken and whole behind the gap where the three went. *Rook.* Mine. The three brands, ground small, kept to hand back across the water. The road the girl laid, unfinished, the gathering one, still being named in some dark above me by a throat they have not stopped. And her — the great dark eye going out, turned to me with the gentling unbroken, the price, set faced in the still place where it will not pour away. The Kala. The one who carried me. The one who woke the name.

I do not make it a grief and lie down under it. I make it the weight on the line that draws the want tight. The far mouth turned from a hole into a pull in the hold, and here, in the pit, the pull goes from a thing I feel to a thing I aim.

One low the hand will open. It opened once on the moving floor and dropped me into the warm black water, and the water carried me to the shore, and the shore made me. It will open again. And the thing it opens on will not be the small wrong boy it closed on.

I rise in the blood of the pit. The massed throats roar and do not know what they are roaring at. I count the rim. I count the caught. I count the squared stones in the still place. Beside me Marrow drops his high shoulder and goes still, reading my read, waiting on the work.

I am still counting.

And now I am not the only thing in here counting back.
