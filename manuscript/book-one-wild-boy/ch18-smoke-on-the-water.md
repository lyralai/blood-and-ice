# Chapter 18: Smoke on the Water

There are things on the water that sit it wrong.

---

I am up on the seaward stack in the green half-light of the low, where my green runs out and goes to bare wet rock and the channel opens past the last of the mangrove into the wide flat water and the haze. I come up here before a hunt to read the tide — the set of the swell, the dark roads under it where the fat river-pig runs the shallows. The water lies past the pinch the way it always lies, hot and pale and old under the standing haze. And far out on it, where the far reach stands low and drowned-green in the steam, there are shapes.

Long dark shapes that hold their line against the swell.

Marrow is the one who stills first. He is up the rock at my shoulder, where he goes when I go, the high shoulder riding higher than the low one and the curled hand resting against his chest where it rests when it has nothing yet to do. He came for the river-pig. He has stopped looking at the shallows. He is looking out past me at the shapes, and he has gone still — the way a thing goes still when a bigger thing comes into the green and is not yet placed. Every part of him quiet at once, even the breath. The curled hand has started to work, slow, open and shut against the chest, the only moving thing on him. He makes no sound. He does not need to. I have read him a thousand lows, and the working hand has only ever meant one thing.

I read the water myself before I let his stillness in.

I have read it more lows than I have counted. The tide-beast rolls with the swell, gives to it, is the swell. A drift-log does not roll, but there is no will in it; it goes where the brown flood shoves it, dumb. These do neither. They sit up on the water with their will in them and they cut it. The swell lifts them and they come out of it still pointed where they were pointed. A thing that holds its line against the sea has a head. A head chooses.

The word comes up out of me. Up from far down, up from under all the green I have packed over everything that was before the green, and it comes up whole.

*Boats.*

The whole skin of me draws tight at once, the small hairs lifting along the arm, the gut clenching to a fist. The body knows the word before the head can turn it. The body wore the word.

On the boats — small dark uprights. They move. Not the dumb sway of a thing the sea pushes. They bend and pull and bend, all together, the same shape doing the same work down both sides at once. Once I watched the channel pull a thing and had no name for what pulled it. Now I have the name. Hands at oars. Many of them, hauling the dark boats across the pale flat water toward — no. Not toward. Toward the spit of drowned-green where, lows past counting ago, I found the broken ship in the mud and the bones the fevers laid down and the relic with the Eye-and-Thorn bound on it that hangs at my chest this low.

The iron floods my mouth though I have bitten nothing. My own blood, sudden and bright on the back of the tongue without a wound to give it — the body opening some old seam to lay the taste of danger where I will not miss it. Blood without a cut. Fear-blood. I have not tasted it since the hold.

Beside me Marrow drops the high shoulder. All the way down. It is the thing he does when the green has gone bad and he wants to be small in it, and he has never once done it for a thing I could fight.

---

I am off the stack before the head knows the body chose to move. Marrow is off it the same breath, low at my flank, that rocking crooked run of his eating the broken ground as fast as my clean stride — quick where he is wrong-made, always quicker than the strong think a thing built like him can be.

Low. Belly to the wet rock. I go down off the high stone into the broken country I know in the dark of me, the rot-gullies and the root-cages and the mud that takes a foot to the knee if the foot does not know the seat of it. I know the seats. Marrow knows where I put mine. We go fast and silent through the place that is only mine, *my green,* and I do not stop at the crack worn to the shape of my back, and I do not go up to the high stones where the tribe sleeps easy under the new crown. I go to the lookout over the bone-spit, the place I have not walked since I dragged the relics off it, because the thing in me that hungers is already turned that way and I cannot not look.

My heart goes against the slab like a hunted thing in a fist. And it is not the boats my heart runs from. It is what comes up behind the word for them.

The brand. The dark of the hold, the wet wood sweating, the close stink of too many bodies packed where they could not stand. The big hand closing on the back of my neck from behind — slow, sure, owning the whole of me in the one grip — and the low ugly sound made behind the teeth that went with the hand every time, the sound that meant *come,* that meant *be still,* that meant *you are a thing and the thing is mine.* The hand and the sound were one. I feel it now as a weight on the neck, and the skin there crawls and tries to get away from a hand far out on the water and lows past counting gone into the deep.

That is what the shapes mean. That is what hands mean. That is what *many-of-them, together, pointed* means.

I lie flat on the bone-spit lookout in the steam and watch the boats come in. Marrow lies down at my side without being told. He does not look at the water now. He looks at me, then at the high stones behind us, then at me — the curled hand still working, the eyes going back along our trail. He wants off this rock. Every other low of his life he has read the green for me and given me the truth before I asked. This low the truth is in his body, and I am not turning to read it, because the hunger has already turned me the other way.

---

They come like a thing into ground it cannot read — slow, the oars feeling the channel, the dark uprights leaning out over the bows to read the brown water for the bank that will take the keel. They do not know the bank. I know the bank. I know the mud that breathes and the still warm shallows where the Mure rise at low water — pale, wet-skinned, web-fingered — and take a thing down into the brackish before it has the sound out. The boats come in over the Mure's own water and do not know it. The dead ones did not know it either.

The shore makes you pay. These have not learned even the first toll.

They put the boats up on the spit. The dark uprights come over the sides into the shin-deep warm and haul the hulls up the mud, and there are more of them than I have fingers and toes to lay against, more than the tribe is. They go on two legs, all of them. They carry. They set things down and take things up and set them down again, the endless handling of things that only the upright pack does. None are the very small ones that dart and stumble between the legs; these are a pack come without its young — a pack that comes to take and not to live.

And I read them as I read a herd before the hunt — counting, learning where it bunches thick, where it thins and goes soft and a thing could come in along the blind side. The far mouth opens under my breastbone so wide it hurts worse than the belly ever hurt.

Because they are mine. Not the tribe-mine. The other mine. The shape of the thing I had before the hold turns over far down in me, under the green, under all the lows. They go on two legs as I go. They have the working hands, the dark scarred clever hands that turn a rock to see what folds under it. They make the sounds at each other — words thrown mouth to mouth and caught and thrown back, the whole easy road of it that I lost behind the gap where three teeth are torn. They are the shape of the hole in me that the tribe filled with everything but its own exact shape. And the hole knows itself when it sees itself walk up out of a boat. It leans.

Marrow makes a sound at last. Low in the throat, almost nothing, the small distress-note he makes over a wound that will not close. He is not looking at the pack. He is looking at me looking at the pack. He has seen the lean in me before I have owned it, read it off my front as he reads everything, and he does not like it. The curled hand has stopped working. It has closed to a fist.

I have a crown this low. I have the Kala's arm over me in the easy sleep and the bold heart warm on my shoulder and a home with no between in it, the first I have ever had. I have Marrow at my flank, the nearest thing to my own kind the green ever gave me and not my kind at all. The hunger was full. I felt it go full on the king's seat, all the way down. And it leans anyway, toward the pale flat water and the two-legged pack on the bone-spit, toward a warmth shaped exactly like the one torn out of me before the green, and it will not be told it is already fed.

That is the new wrongness. Not the body-wrong, the blood-wrong, the old wrongness I have set down and walked past a thousand lows. This is a head-wrong. A want-wrong. A thing can be full and lean anyway — can open a new seam under the full one, the way the body opens a seam to taste fear, and let in the old human-shaped hunger that no Vorth was ever cut to fill. Not even Marrow. Marrow most of all not.

---

I should go. Every fast part of me says go — up to the high stones, to the crown and the Kala, put the whole broken country between me and the hands. A pack this many will sweep the green like the rains sweep the gullies. I am king of the wild things and the king is still one thing, and I have seen what the many do to the one. I wore it on the neck. The many are how the one is unmade. Marrow knows it too — he is pressed to the rock as small as he can make himself.

But I do not go.

I lie on the wet rock and I watch them set up on the bone-spit where every other human died, and the body reads the thing the head is slow to hold:

They are going to die.

I know it the way I know a tide before the sky shows it. I do not want to know it. I press at it anyway, because not knowing is the worse thing, and the knowing comes whether I will it or not. The green will eat them. Not the teeth first. The green does not need its teeth for the upright pack. It has the fever-air that drops every other body and leaves me clear-headed. It has the bad water and the still rot-pools that look like drink, the snake and the spine and the sting that laid the dead ones down with their charts and their knives. It has the swarms — already I can see them lifting dark off the rot as the boats settle. It has the Mure under the shallows they beached across. It has the killing heat that will turn every scrape these hands take to rot-green and fly-blown in a few lows while mine draw shut clean.

The green eats humans. That is the one law of this place I learned without learning it, because I am the one human it has never been able to eat, and I never knew — until I had no others to measure against — that this was a strange thing to be.

I watch the dark uprights build their fire on the spit. Standing smoke, straight up in the still steam, fire a thing makes on purpose and tends and will not let go out. Smoke on the water. And I lie above it, the thing the green could not kill, and I count, low by low, the deaths the green has already laid out for them and they cannot read.

Marrow nudges my shoulder with his head. Once. Hard. The thing he does when I have stared too long at a trail and the light is going and he means *up, now, away.* I do not move. He does it again. Then he stops, and puts his chin down on the wet rock beside my arm, and waits — because he will not go up without me and I will not go up, and there is nothing in his small wrong body that can argue the hunger out of a grave.

---

There is one comes apart from the rest.

The light is dying and the fed fire is up red on the bone-spit, and one of the dark uprights walks down to the tide-line alone where the boats are drawn up, and stands, and looks out at the channel. Out toward my green. It is big, and it stands the way the strong stand — slow, the way Gull-Marr crossed the bare stone slow, because the slow is its own telling: *I do not have to hurry to the thing I am going to take.* It puts its hands on its hips and turns its head along my shore, reading the dark green wall of it. It does not know what reads it back.

And it makes a sound. Across the water, worn down by the distance to almost nothing, a sound thrown back up the spit to the others. A word. An order. An edge laid on the air. Up by the fire a shape lifts a hand and answers it. The easy road, from the head where the wanting lives, up the throat, out the mouth, laid clean on the air, caught, thrown back. A thing it does not even know it is doing, easy as breath.

Beside me Marrow flinches from the sound. Small. The whole of him drawing in. He has heard the upright pack make their edged sounds across water before — the sound of the thing that came for me, that the seer reads off me, that the tribe took in and could not wholly make Vorth — and his body knows it for a wrong thing even when mine leans toward it. He looks at me, then at the big slow upright on the tide-line, then at me. And he will not look at the water again. He turns his face into the rock and drops the high shoulder as far as it goes. That is the whole of his warning, laid plain on a body that has no words to spend on me, and I read it and I do not heed it.

Something turns over in me so hard the body makes a sound on its own — the rasp, the cough with the growl riding its back, the thing the throat makes now where words used to be made. I clap my hand over my mouth to keep the rest of it in, to keep my shape from giving itself up across the water.

And under the hand I go in behind the teeth, into the gap where three are torn out, the dark hollow where the one true thing has always lived — the name my mother set on me before the ship, before the hands, the name they never knew to reach for and so never took. *Rook.* I go in to find it as I have a thousand lows, to hold it under the tongue where no one can take it, to set it against the brand. I hold the name down hard, and the warm comes up slow and lies down in the hollow around it, and the name is there, and it is mine, and it is the one un-stolen thing.

But I cannot throw it.

That big slow thing throws a word across the water and gets one back. I have a name held under my tongue I have never once said out loud in all the lows of the green, a name that answers nothing — because there is no one on this drowning island I would say it to. No one shaped to catch it. I made one word and laid it on a thing on purpose: I took the runt the tribe meant to cast out and set a sound on him, *Marrow,* the human way, and he learned to turn to it. He is here at my flank with his face in the rock. And even he cannot catch the name, because the road from head to mouth that lays a name clean on the air for another head only the upright pack has, and the upright pack is the hand.

The tribe gave me back a voice — the rasp, the falls, the still-sound I make to the bold heart, the one word I made for Marrow. But it could not give me back the throwing. Only the hand has the throwing. And the hand is on the water.

I take my hand off my mouth. There is no sound to keep in.

---

I lie on the bone-spit lookout until the light is gone and the fed fire is the only thing red on the black water, and I do not go up to the crown. Marrow stays.

I should go up. The Kala will wake to the empty place at her side and make the still-sound into the dark, *be still, you are held, nothing comes,* and the *nothing comes* will hang half-made in the great chest because the king is not at the side where the king sleeps. I should go up and lay the warning into the high stones the only way I have — the body-way, the set of the jaw and the turn toward the water that says *a thing is on the green I have no good sound for.* The seer would read it. The seer reads everything, reads the blood under the Eye-and-Thorn. The seer would know that the thing the water carried in is the thing it has been waiting for since the pale finger first came up.

I do not go up. Not yet.

I lie above the dying pack with my front turned to their fire, and I hold the two things the water gave me this low.

The first is the law. The green eats humans. These are come to walk into the death I survived, and I know it before they take their first bad drink. There is a thing in me — old, low, under the crown — that wants to lie here and count them down into the mud, until the green has eaten the shape of the hole and I can stop leaning at a grave.

The second is the leaning. I lean at the dying pack anyway, because they are the shape of it. The crown did not close it. The Kala did not close it. Marrow did not close it. Nothing on this drowning island closes it but the thing the green is going to eat.

I keep the law behind my teeth, and the leaning beside it, next to the name no one can catch and the brand I will give back someday across the water. The fed fire burns low and red on the bone-spit where every human before me died. The swarms settle on every body but the two lying still above them — the boy and the runt who would not leave him. And somewhere out past the channels there are more shapes that hold their line, because boats come in packs, and this smoke is only the first thread of it.

The crown is the highest thing I have ever stood on. The smoke on the water is the thing the next taking came in on. I knew it would come. It has a fire now. And beside me Marrow has given up nudging and put his curled hand over his eyes — the small wrong body saying the thing words cannot: that I have already started counting the lows between, and that he knows it, and that he will count them with me anyway.
