# Chapter 2: Hunger Law

I stay in the crack three days.

Not by choice. The body will not move. The leg has swollen, the skin around the cut pulled tight and shining, gone a color that is no color a leg should wear — dark under the dark, hot to the back of my hand, pushing back when I press it like there is something under the skin that does not want me touching its work. I press it once to know it. I do not press it again.

Thirst I can answer; the seep is ten steps and a crawl, and the crawl I can pay. Food is another country, on the far side of a sea I have no strength to cross. So I lie in the warm stone and let the days go by me, and the warm stone holds me the way nothing living has held me. I take what it gives. I do not ask after it. There is no asking left in me — only the slow accounting of what the body has, and what the body is spending, and how the one number falls away from the other.

---

The hunger starts on the second day, and it does not start in the belly. It starts in the head. A fog. My thoughts are few to begin with — the cold sees to that, and the hurt, and the long habit of a boy who learned young that thinking too much in a cage only gives the hands more to take — but the fog thins what little there is to almost nothing. It comes up soft, the way the gray came up over the water that first morning, a thing with no edge to it that is suddenly everywhere. Each crawl to the seep is longer than the last. The ten steps grow. The rock blurs at its edges, the black running into the gray, and I have to stop halfway and put my face to the stone and wait for the world to firm up again before I trust my hands to know where the water is.

By the third day the fog is everything, and I cannot feel the cold. That should frighten me. Some old true part of me knows it should — knows that a body that has stopped feeling the cold is a body already half gone over to it, that the not-feeling is not mercy but the cold getting in under the alarm and turning it off. It doesn't frighten me. Nothing does. The fog eats fear along with the rest, the same way it eats the edges of the rock. It is almost kind, the fog. That is exactly what makes it the thing that will kill me, and somewhere far down, under the soft gray, the small still part of me that listens knows that too, and pulls.

---

There are two hungers in me, and I am slow to learn they are not the same.

One is for meat. That one I know. It sits where hunger is supposed to sit, low and plain, a wringing under the ribs, and it speaks in the old language the body has always spoken, the language of *eat or end*. I have carried that one my whole life. In the cage it was the weather; you did not fight it, you only bent under it and waited for the slop that came or didn't.

The other has no belly to sit in. I go looking for it with the inside of myself, the way the tongue goes looking for the gap where a tooth was, and I cannot find where it lives, because it does not live anywhere a hand could point to. It wakes when there is nothing breathing near me — no voice, no other warmth, no body close enough in the dark that I can feel the heat come off it. In the cage that one was always full, and I never knew it for a hunger because I never once felt its floor. There were always bodies. There was always the breath of the others going up in the dark, a hundred small ghosts. There was always the cruelty, and the cruelty was a kind of feeding too — the hands that hurt me at least knew the shape of me, at least turned their faces my way, at least kept the count of me. A thing that is hurt is a thing that is seen. I did not know to be grateful for it. You do not know the floor is there until it is gone and you are still falling.

Here there is no one to fill it at all. It opens in me like a second mouth, low under the first — and it does not close, it will not close. The meat-hunger I can maybe answer; there is meat in the world, if I can get to it. But that other mouth — I am beginning to understand there is nothing on this whole black shore that it eats, and I am beginning to understand it is the one that takes a man last, and surest, long after the belly has made its peace.

I pull myself out of the crack. One mouth at a time. The near one first. The far one I will have to carry, because there is no setting it down.

---

The shore has things on it. I have seen them without seeing — the eyes took them in across three days and filed them somewhere behind the fog, waiting. The hunger makes me look. The hunger is a better teacher than the cold ever was; the cold only told me to hide, and the hunger tells me to *hunt*, and the body wakes to that older word and leans toward it.

Tide pools, ankle-deep, set into the black rock like the world has been pocked by something hot and the pocks have filled with sea. In each one, life. Green slime, slick and bright, furring the stone under the water. Small leggy things that bolt sideways under the ledges the moment my shadow crosses the surface — they feel me before they see me, the dark of me falling over their little sky. Fish the length of a finger, clear as glass, near invisible against the black until they move and the moving gives them away.

I take one with my hands. It costs an hour. I lie on the rock above a pool with my arm in the water to the elbow, the cold of it climbing into my shoulder and setting up house there, and I go still. The hunger makes me patient where the cold never could; the cold made me shake, and the shaking would have scattered them, but the hunger holds me flat and quiet as the stone. I wait until my arm has stopped being mine, until it is only a thing lying in the water that the fish have forgotten to be afraid of. When a clear shape drifts close, I drive my hand down and shut my fingers and hold on, though it thrashes and the cold shoots fresh up the dead arm and my whole grip begs to spring open and let the small cold life go.

I have it. I bring it up streaming and twisting, and I bite into it while it still kicks. The taste is good and wrong at once, cold and slick and salt, the flesh fighting my teeth in the gaps where the teeth are gone. I eat the whole of it. The bones that bend. The skin. The tail flicking against my chin as the head goes down my throat. I do not care. Even before I have swallowed I am reaching for another, the want gone wild now that it has been answered even this little.

Then I am sick.

---

It comes fast, fast as the wave that first wet my face. My stomach knots like a fist and I bring the fish back up onto the black rock, mostly blood and bile and half-chewed bone, the good cold meat turned to a sour mess between my hands. My throat burns with it. My guts wring themselves out and then wring again when there is nothing left to give, twisting on empty, and I lie there with my cheek on the wet stone and shake, and this shaking is a new shaking, not the cold's. It comes from inside, from somewhere deep and wet and low, and it is worse than the cold ever was, because the cold came at me from outside and a body knows how to set itself against an outside thing. This is the body turned against the body. There is no setting yourself against that.

*Ate the fish. Now dying.* That is the whole of what I know, the whole thought, round and plain. There is a thing that lives in certain meat and waits and turns on you, and I have watched it turn on others, and I know the shape of what it does. I think it has turned on me. I am not dying — I will learn that, after, when the morning comes and finds me still here. But I do not know it now, so I lie on the black rock and wait to go, and I hold my mother's name behind my teeth while I wait, because if this is the going then I will go with the one thing of mine still where I put it. And then I do not go. And when I don't, I am only weaker than I was, emptied and scraped and shaking, and both mouths of the hunger are still there, open, asking, no nearer to filled than before I spent the hour and the strength and the fish.

---

So I watch the birds.

Small, dark, quick, they drop onto the pools out of the gray and pick and choose. I am too weak to do much but watch, so I watch hard, the way I once learned to watch the hands — for what they reached toward, for what they left. The birds eat the green slime, tearing strips of it off the stone. They eat the small leggy things, snapping them up from under the ledges where they bolt. They will not touch the clear glass fish. Not one. They drop down beside a clear fish drifting plain in the shallows and they look at it with one flat eye and they leave it, every time, and lift away. And they keep well clear of the dark, still pools where the water sits low and does not move and gives off a smell even my fog-dulled nose can find — a stink of rot under the salt, a green-black breath off water that has forgotten the tide.

I do what the birds do.

The slime tastes of salt and rot and stays down. The leggy things — I crack them on the rock with a stone, pull the legs away, suck out the little wet there is in them — they stay down too. It is not enough. It is not near enough; it is the difference between dying slow and dying slower. But the birds have lived on this shore longer than I have and have not died of it, and I am not too proud to take my schooling from a bird. I follow them two days. When they move along the rock, I move. When they put their heads down to eat, I eat what they ate. They do not know they are teaching me. If they knew, I think they would stop; nothing on this shore gives anything up gladly, and I would not either, in their place.

---

The tide teaches me next, and the tide is a harder master than the birds and a fairer one.

Water in, water out. There is a law in it, under the roar, and the fog cannot quite drown the law. When the water goes, the pools fill from the draining sea and the leggy things and the small life are caught in the low places, trapped in the stone cups until the water comes back for them. When the water comes, the pools vanish into the rising gray and everything that lived in them runs free, back out into the cold beyond my reach. I learn the rhythm of it — not by counting, I have no numbers, the numbers were never mine to begin with — but by the body, the only book I can read. The look of the light laid out on the water. The turn and the smell of the wind when the sea is about to change its mind. A wrongness in the roar, a falling-off, that means the going-out has begun.

Low water is when I eat. I learn to be at the pools when they bare themselves, hand already wet, ready. The rest of the time I spend in the crack with my face to the warm stone, banking what little heat and strength I have, the way I once saw a man in the cage hoard a crust against a hunger he knew was coming — not because he wasn't hungry now, but because he had learned the cost of being caught with nothing.

The best pools are the farthest out, the ones that bare themselves only on the lowest of the low tides, when the sea draws all the way back and shows ground it shows no other day. Those hold bigger things. Darker crabs, slow and heavy in the cold, that I can take two and three at a time before they wake to me. And a fish that is not the clear glass kind but colored — orange-brown along its back, fatter, with a smell to its flesh, a smell of something instead of a smell of nothing. I eat the colored fish, braced for the knotting and the bile, and the knotting does not come. It stays down. It sits warm and real in the belly, and the meat-hunger gives one step backward — only one — but it is the first ground I have taken from anything since the cold woke me on the first morning, and I take it, and I hold it, the way I hold the name. Small and hard and mine.

---

The leg gets worse before it turns.

I do not look at it for two days. There is a kind of not-looking that is its own knowing; the body keeps the count even when the eyes refuse, and the count says the leg is bad. But the food has put a little clear back into my head, enough to do the hard thing, and so I look. The cut has sealed — but the skin around it is still swollen and hot, drum-tight, and at the lips of it the flesh has gone yellow, a thick wet yellow that does not belong to a body that means to live. There is a thing that gets into a wound and makes its home there and feeds, and I have watched it eat a man from a cut no longer than this one, watched it climb up the leg in red roads while the man went gray and then quiet and then gone. I know the leg is hot. I know the leg is wrong. That is enough to act on.

I wash it at the seep. Cold clear water carried in my cupped hands and poured over the hot skin, again and again, the clean cold that only wants down meeting the wrong heat that wants to climb. The water is colder than the air and colder than the sea, a cold with no rot in it, and where it runs the swelling pulls back a little, just a little, the skin easing under the cold the way my shaking eased the first night against the warm stone. So I keep at it. I make the crawl to the seep a dozen times a day for it, spending strength I can ill spend, because the leg is the kind of wrong that does not wait, and the other wrongs can.

On the fourth day the cut is smaller. Not just closed — smaller. The lips of it have drawn in toward each other. The yellow is fading back to the ordinary red of healing flesh. The heat is going out of it, the small fire under the skin burning down to coals and then to nothing.

Too fast.

I go still over the leg, the small still listening thing in me waking and pulling at the back of everything. I have seen wounds. On the ship, on the bodies packed against me in the breathing dark, I watched cuts like this one take weeks to close — watched some of them never close at all, only widen and weep and stink until the body wrapped around them went still in the night and was hauled up in the morning and was gone. I know the slow arithmetic of damage the way I know the warm stone — not in words, in the long evidence of a body that has been hurt and has watched other bodies be hurt and has counted, over and over, how long the hurt is supposed to last. There is a count for a cut like this, and the count is in weeks, and the leg is not keeping it.

The leg is mending in days what should have eaten weeks.

I look at my hands. The gashes the sharp rock tore in my palms that first morning, when I crawled and caught myself and the black stone opened me a dozen places — those are gone. Closed to clean pink lines, the kind a hand carries a week after the hurt, the kind that have stopped being wounds and started being only marks. They are three days old. I turn my palms up in the gray light and look at the week-old healing on the three-day cuts, and the listening thing in me goes very quiet.

I put it where the body puts what it cannot use yet: on a shelf at the back, behind the cold and the hunger and the pain, where a boy in a cage learns to put everything he cannot afford to feel. The breath that goes out warm against my palm and leaves no ghost in the freezing air. The stone that should pull the last heat from my face in the night and instead gives heat back. The frost that rang the hollow where I slept and would not come where my body had been. And now the wounds that close too soon. A small dark shelf of wrong things, all of a kind, all of them facts about this body that the body itself did not agree to. I do not understand them and I do not try to. I will come back to it.

Not now. Now I need to eat.

---

The crabs near the crack run thin. I have cleaned the close pools out, taken everything slow enough to take, and the nearest pool with anything left in it is a long walk down the shore, far enough that the round trip eats most of a low tide and I come back with little to show for the strength I spent going.

So I widen. I watch the shore the way I watched the birds — not for food now but for pattern, for law, for the rules under the gray. Where the sea sets things down when it draws back. What the weed holds when it is thrown up on the rock to rot. I find the eel grass in a calm cove around the turn of the cliff, long tough ribbons of it, salt and tasting of rope, and down in the tangle of its roots the thin dark eels that bolt the moment I tear the grass open and let the light in on them. I take two with my hands, quick, before they can make the deep water. More meat on them than the crabs. More fat, and the fat is what the body cries for, the fat that holds the heat in. No sickness after. The colored fish and the eels and the crabs are the meat that keeps; the clear glass fish and the still dark pools are the meat that turns. I am building the law of this shore one stay-down and one sickness at a time, writing it in the only book that takes ink here, which is the body.

I eat eels and crabs and slime and the colored fish when the low tide bares the deep pools. I eat what the birds eat, and then I eat what the birds never thought to look for. I learn. The shore stops being a blank gray killing-place and starts being a thing with parts, with seasons in a day, with a give to it if you know where to press.

The meat-hunger does not leave. It would be a lie to say it leaves. But it pulls back. It stops being the thing that drives me and becomes a thing I carry instead — present, low, mine, the body's rent paid out daily for the keeping of a life. I can stand under it now. I can think over the top of it.

The other mouth, the second one, does not pull back. Not by a step. Not by a breath. I feed myself. I drink myself full. I wash my own wound, learn the law of the tide — and none of it reaches the far one. Not one scrap of all of it. It sits open in the dark under everything, where no eel or crab or colored fish will ever drop, waiting on a thing the shore does not have to give.

I can feed myself now. I can drink. I can hold the warm dark around me at night and sleep without dreams. By every measure the cage taught me, I am winning.

It is not enough. And I am starting to see it was never going to be.
