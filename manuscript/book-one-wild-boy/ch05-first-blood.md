# Chapter 5: First Blood

The morning the rain breaks, I learn the next law. A den keeps the rot off a body. A body still needs feeding. The green does not feed you for staying still.

The sound from the night-ridge did not come again. I lay in the den-dark with the fire stood up gold and the rain hammering the leaves over the mouth and waited for it. The near mouth had gone quiet. The far one was wide. It did not come. I went under into sleep and woke into the wet green light of dawn with the coals breathing low and the place around me steaming and close and mine. The night-sound I cannot solve. I want to. I turn it in me — too heavy for a swine, too quiet — and find no shape there, no word, nothing but the wanting, so I leave it where it lies and crawl out into the dripping green. The body has its own count, and the count has come due.

The mud-crabs are gone. The fat white grubs from the rot-log are gone. I have spent myself on the den for days, hauling stone and breaking deadfall and weaving the leaf-roof tight, and the spending came out of somewhere, and the somewhere is empty now. The near mouth is awake and loud. Not the other. The other I cannot feed; no meat answers it. This is the true one, the one in the belly that grinds when there is nothing in it, and it grinds now, low and steady, while I stand in the steam coming up off the wet earth and feel how thin I have gone in the building.

---

I go to the channels first, because the channels are what I know.

The tide is out. The mangrove roots stand bared and dripping down to the brown water, arched and tangled like the bones of something half-buried, and the still pockets sit in the hollows of the mud, each a little held world the tide left behind. I have taken crabs from these, pulled grubs from the soft wood. I crouch at the lip of the first with the cage-patience, the one good thing out of all that taking, and wait, and read.

Nothing. The pocket holds black mud and root and my own thin face looking up through the brown. Nothing that moves. The next is the same. The next. I work them down the bared flat, pocket after pocket, and where there was life there is only slow water and the crab-holes empty, and I understand the thing late, with my body, as I understand everything. I have eaten down the easy law of this edge. The pockets fed me until they were empty, and they do not fill again because I am hungry. They gave what they had. Now I have to go further to be fed.

I go further. Out to where the warm brown water still moves, the deep channels the tide does not empty, and I lie on the wet root-knees with my arm in to the shoulder, reading with my fingers what the eyes cannot reach. The water is warm as blood and thick with rot. My fingers find shape in the dark — something hard and ridged gripped to the root, a shell the size of two fists. It is grown to the wood. I brace and pull, and the edge of it opens a line in my finger, and the dark blood threads out into the brown and is gone. I get it loose. One shell. I crack it on a root-knee and swallow the knot of pale flesh whole, and the near mouth barely feels it go down.

One shell. A day of building spent against one shell. The cage-lie rises in me, small and quick, whispering that this is enough, that I should lie still and let it pass — and I drown it. The lie kept me alive in the hold by making me small. Out here it would make me dead. I stand up off the warm roots and look down the long flat to the moving channel. The green has stopped giving. It has started to ask.

---

I find them from the high ground the next day, where a hump of broken stone lifts the trees above the drowned flat, and the near mouth makes a sound in me that is almost the other's.

Down where the channel widens into a brown bay and the mud goes soft and black, dark shapes lie wallowed in the shallows. River-swine. I have no word for them, only the shape — long heavy bodies the color of wet mud, broad-backed and slow, rooting with blunt tusked snouts, grunting low. Now and then one heaves itself a length through the muck and settles. They are fat. That is the thing the eye fixes on and will not leave. They are fat as nothing on this edge is fat, fat as I am not — the crab thin and the grub thin and I thinner than both. They are made all of the fat the green has wrung out of me. The near mouth grinds. Under it the far one stirs and leans toward the dark shapes as it leaned toward the night-sound, and I make myself look off, because that leaning is not a hunger I can trust.

I crouch on the broken stone and learn them. I will not go down there knowing nothing and bleed for it, the same as I bleed for everything. They are heavy in the mud, slow, but not stupid. When a heron lifts off the shallows one swine throws its head up fast, faster than the heavy body says it should, tusks bright, and does not settle until the water is still. They keep near the deep channel, always near, three lengths, four, no more, between the fat bodies and the brown edge where the water takes them into a thing I cannot follow. To take one I have to be the thing standing in the four lengths it needs.

I have nothing. Hands and a body gone thin and the cage-patience. A swine is the most meat I have ever stood near, and the most that can hurt me — those tusks, that weight. Between the two of them the near mouth grinds all night and will not let me sleep deep.

---

I make the thing to do it with, because the hands cannot do it alone.

The deadfall behind the den has hardwood in it, the dense dark wood that does not rot soft like the rest. I take the straightest limb, long as I am, dried at the heart to a thing with no give. The end is broken to a rough point already, and I work it against a flat of the broken stone, grinding, turning, the wood going pale where the dark skin wears away. It eats the day. My hands, barely closed from the den-work, open again — a split across the palm, the blood welling warm and red, the one warm bright thing. By the count it should not close before a moon. It will close in three. Out here in the wet it should go green and foul, and it will not. That is one more thing about me I cannot read, and I have learned what comes of stopping to stare at it while the belly grinds: nothing. So I put the bleeding hand back on the wood.

The point comes up sharp. I test it against my own thumb and it bites. That is the answer. It bites. I have made a tooth. The fire made warmth out of dead wood and the den made a held place out of dead stone, and now the dead wood has made a tooth out of nothing but grinding and blood. The pulling does not stop, the head says. It only finds the next dead thing to pull a living use from. I bind a stone-flake under the point too, lashed with split root-fiber drawn tight to dry hard, an edge to widen what the point opens. I sleep with the spear beside me. The near mouth grinds, and I do not feed it. Tomorrow it eats or it does not.

---

I come down on them in the wet dark before the sun, into the wind.

The wind is the thing. It carries my smell, and a swine's head comes up fast — I learned that on the high stone — so I come into it, my smell going back behind me into the empty green where there is nothing to warn. The mud is soft and black under my bare feet and I read it with the soles, each step set where the foot will not slap and ring and lift the heavy heads. The spear is in both hands. The near mouth is loud. I put it down under the cage-patience and I go.

They are wallowed where I saw them, three lengths from the brown edge, four. I pick the one furthest from its channel, and come around in the wind to put myself between it and the water — low, slow, a thing of mud moving over mud. The body did this in the hold, made itself nothing, crept under the notice of the hands. I can smell it now, thick and rank and rich, mud and dung and the hot animal under it, and the far mouth leans so hard toward it that for a breath I am not the boy creeping with the spear. I am only the leaning. That frightens the body more than the swine does, and I pull myself back into the boy, into the hands, into the point of the wood.

It sees me.

The head comes up out of the mud fast, the small dark eye finds me, and the heavy body heaves up out of the wallow faster than the slowness said it could — straight at the channel, straight through where I stand, tusks down. Because I have done the one thing right. I am between. There is no thinking left, only the body, and the body drops its weight behind the spear and drives the ground-sharp point into the thick of the dark shape as it passes, behind the lowered head, where the fat is, and the wood goes in.

The swine screams.

I did not know it would have a voice like that. A high broken tearing sound, almost a child's sound, almost a sound I have made, and it goes through me and the hands want to let go and run, and I do not let them. I drive my weight onto the wood. The body bucks under it, huge, all muscle and fat and fury, and I go down into the black muck with the spear still in my hands and in the swine. It is stronger than I am by far. For a long stretch it is not a killing, only a holding-on, the swine heaving toward its water and dragging me with it, the tusk raking my thigh in one wild throw, a hot line opening down the muscle. I do not let go.

And then the warmth comes out of it.

It comes out around the wood, dark and fast, far more than wells from my own splits — a flood of it, hot, running over the black mud and over my hands, and the smell is iron, bright iron, the taste of it at the back of my mouth though none has touched it. The same taste my own blood leaves when a tooth-gap fills, only so much more, a whole body of warmth pouring out and steaming in the dawn air. The heaving slows. The screaming thins. The strength that was dragging me to the channel goes out of the body fast and then all at once, and the dark shape sags into the wallow under me and is still, and I am lying on top of it with the spear gripped in my torn hands, shaking, breathing through the gap in my teeth, my whole front soaked in warmth that is not mine.

I have killed it. The near mouth is silent now, struck silent, because here is the answer to it — meat past all the meat I have ever stood near. But the far one opened all the way when the blood came. It leaned toward the running warmth, and it did not close. It is still open over the cooling body, still leaning, still not fed, and I lie in the iron smell and feel the thing I least wanted to feel: that the second mouth took the smell of the killing and wanted more.

---

I drag it up toward the den slow, the same as it dragged me toward the channel — only now I am the one hauling, and it does not fight.

It costs the whole day, hauling the dead weight up off the flat through the root-tangle and over the broken stone, the bark taking skin off my hands and the tusk-line down my thigh weeping. The leg should slow me. By the count of a body it should stiffen and swell and turn the muscle to fire and put me down. It does not. It pulls and weeps and that is all. I cannot read why, and there is no time to try; I keep hauling. I get the swine near the lee mouth and work it where it lies, the same as I learned to work the crab, only there is so much of it — the fat under the hide finger-deep, and under that the dark red meat, the iron smell going into the near mouth and the other both.

I cook it that night in the den the way a creature cooks in its own place.

The fire stands up gold, the rain on the far side of the leaf-wall, and I hold the dark meat to the flame on a green stick and the fat runs and hisses and flares, and when I tear into it it is hot all the way through. I eat slow, chewing on the side where the teeth still are, and the near mouth feeds at last, fills, goes quiet, the grinding gone out of the belly for the first time since the den-work began. The fat is the thing. It sits in me rich and stays, putting back the strength the green wrung out of me. This is the first time the green has put back more than it took.

And the far mouth does not feed.

I knew it would not. No meat answers it. But I had not known it would lean so hard toward the spilling, toward the iron and the running warmth, and then lean still after, over the full belly, as if the swine had only shown it the shape of the thing and not given it. I sit with the fat going into me and the hunger that no meat fills open wider than the meat reaches, and I understand the thing I will carry off this green. I can feed the body past full. And the other will watch me kill to do it, and want the killing more than the meat.

---

I do not look at it straight. I have learned not to. I feed the fire and put my back to the rock, the iron still on my hands, the fat warm in my belly, the tusk-line down my thigh already gone tight and clean at the edges where by the count it should be hot and swelling.

The swarms come for the swine in the night, and something else comes with them.

I wake to feed the coals, the body doing the work half in sleep, and I see them first — the biting cloud, the ants and the carrion-flies, come up to the swine-meat I set on the cool side. They blanket it black and seething, as the swarms blanket every dead thing on this island. They ring the meat and ring the den and do not come onto me. A clean hand's-width of bare skin all around my body where they will not cross, where they have never crossed. I have stopped wondering at it. Not because I have answered it — because I have tried, and the green gives me nothing to answer it with, and tonight I am too tired to fight the green for the meat, let alone for the reason.

Then the swarm lifts.

It lifts off the meat all at once, the black blanket rising in a hum and scattering into the dark, gone. The green outside goes quiet. Not the night-quiet of the rain. The other quiet, the held-breath quiet, the small things gone to ground because something large is reading the dark. I know that quiet. I have made it myself, creeping on the swine. I am the thing the small things go quiet for. And now I am the small thing, and something is making the quiet for me.

Something crosses outside the den.

I do not see it. The fire is low and the leaf-wall is between, and I do not move to lift the flame, because the body has gone still as the stone it lies against. I hear the weight of it, a slow soft fall too heavy for a swine and too quiet, a thing that sets each foot down the way I set mine in the mud, a thing that hunts the way I hunt. I smell it under the iron and the fat. A smell I have no shape for, rank and wrong and old, a green-rot smell with a hot animal hunger laid through it, the smell of a thing that has eaten things like me. It comes to the swine-blood soaked into the ground, and stops there, in the held breath of the whole green. I lie with the spear under my hand and my own warmth gone tight and small in me, and the second mouth that leaned all day toward killing draws back now, all the way back. Here is the killing turned around. Here is a thing the far hunger does not lean toward but cowers under.

It works the blood-ground a long while. Then the weight moves off, slow, soft, back into the green it came out of, and only when the small things start their sound again do I let the breath out of me.

I do not go out. I feed the fire up high and lay every stone across the low mouth and wait for the light.

In the light I look. There is a print in the soft mud where the thing stood and read me through the wall — deep, wide as my chest, the toes long and spread and clawed. No swine. No thing I have a shape for. A thing made to hold the ground and tear it. The green grew quiet for it. The swarms fled it. The whole island made way for the weight of it as nothing makes way for me.

I have read this green for a moon as a hard place — heat and rot and venom and bad water that asks and asks and gives only what it has. I read it wrong. The hardness is only the floor. There is something above me on this green, something the green itself goes quiet under, and the swine I killed and called the most that could hurt me is only the meat the real thing comes for.

I crouch over the print a long time. I want it to tell me what made it. It will not. It is one more wrong thing, sat down beside the fever-air that does not sicken me and the wounds that close too soon and the swarms that will not touch me — and like all of them it gives me its shape and keeps its name. But I do not let this one lie still. I keep my eyes on it. I keep my hand on the spear. The fever and the wounds are wrong things about me, and I can carry them. The print is a wrong thing about the green, and the green is where the body has to feed.

I spilled the most warmth I have ever spilled, and something out in the dark smelled it spill, and came, and read me through the wall, and let me live — this once, for reasons of its own.

The pockets gave what they had, and then they made me go further. Now I know there is a further, and I know what waits in it, and the hunger that wanted more has gone very quiet.
