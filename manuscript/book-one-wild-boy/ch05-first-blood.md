# Chapter 5: First Blood

The pup does not run, and that is its mistake.

It is hauled out alone on the tide flat, the same black flat the fish come off when the cold pools fill — only this is the far end of it, up past where the ridge stops sheltering the cove, where the seep-cliff leans over the stone and lets its one finger-thin thread of fresh water down the rock. The pup is fat and silver-gray, soft as a thing that has never had to be hard. The big seals went off the flats when the wind turned teeth-inland. This one stayed. Too young to read the wind. I know that hunger. I crouch in the lee of a slab and watch it the way I watch everything. Sidelong. Counting.

It rolls. It scratches its belly with a flipper like there is all the warm-time in the world. Behind my ribs the heat wakes, low and red and certain, and for once I do not push it down. For once it wants the same thing my belly wants.

I pick up the rock.

---

Here is the law I have learned with no one to teach it. The fish do not flee. The fish are given. I lower a line into the cold pool and the cold gives. But this thing has eyes, and the eyes will find me, and once they find me it is gone into the surf and I am a boy with a rock and an empty mouth.

So I do not let the eyes find me.

I go low. Belly to the black stone, the cold of it coming up through me, and the cold in my bones settles into that — wants the still dark rock, presses toward it — and I let it have what it wants, because for once the wrong thing and the right thing point the same way. Slow. A slab's length. Wait. The wind is in my face, off the inland fog; it cannot carry me to the pup. The birds taught me that, how they came at the low-tide fish always from downwind. The dead ones taught me by being dead. The living ones taught me by leaving.

Closer. Close enough to smell it: wet fur, fish-breath, the milk-fat reek of something young. My heart is a fist hitting a door. The pup lifts its head.

Now.

I am up and the rock is down before the head is all the way around. The first blow is wrong, off the shoulder, and it screams: a sound like a child, a sound that goes into the gap behind my teeth and sits there. It heaves for the water. I do not hesitate. There is no room in me to hesitate. The second blow lands true, behind the skull where the bone is thin, and the third makes the second a fact.

It stops. The flat is quiet. My breath saws. There is blood on the black stone, dark and steaming where the cold takes the heat out of it. The heat under my ribs rises to meet it, pleased, fed. This blood I made on purpose. This blood is mine to spend.

I do not enjoy it. My hands shake. But I do not look away either, and that is the new thing, and I feel the new thing settle into me like a stone settling into a wall.

---

A pup is not a fish. A fish I split and dry. A pup is a whole country, and I have no blade to open it.

I have my hands, my teeth, and the black stone of the whole shore — and the shore, it turns out, is a kind of blade if you ask it right.

I crack one rock against another until a piece sheds off thin and edged, meaner than anything I was ever given. The first time I strike it I open the meat of my own thumb. A red mouth. It will shut by morning, days where it should be weeks — I do not think about that. I wrap the back of the flake in a strip of hide so it bites the seal and not me, and I open the belly, and the warm comes out of it in a fog, and under the fog is everything. Fat, thick as my hand is wide, pale gold. Meat, dark and wet. And bones. So many bones.

I have only ever been given things to use. The slavers gave me a place to crouch and a cord to not cross. This thing gives me a whole carcass and asks nothing back. And the new thing in me, the one that woke when the rock came down, will not let me set one scrap of it aside.

I keep all of it.

The fat I cut in slabs to carry. The meat the same. The skin I peel whole, scraping it down to the white with the back of the flake, learning the angle as I go, and it comes away a wet warm hide the size of me. And the bones I set aside on a flat rock, apart, because the bones are not food and I do not yet know what they are. But I have learned not to throw away a thing before I know it.

---

I learn the first one by accident, which is how I learn everything.

There is a bone in the flipper, long, smooth, narrow at the middle where it could be gripped. I clean it of meat with my thumb and it sits in my hand like it was grown to sit there. And I see the dead man's toggle — the slaver bone I drove into a throat and snapped between two stones, the thing I only ever used and never understood. I see the thong I cut to bind my fish-bundles, always slipping, always working loose. The two things come together behind my eyes the way one tide slides under another.

The toggle held a thing shut because the bone was wider than the hole. I never knew that. I only knew it killed. Now I know the why under the what.

So I make my own binding. Punch a slit in the hide with the flake's point. Pass the thong through. Set a sliver of flipper-bone across the back of the slit, crosswise, wider than the slit is long — and it cannot pull free. It holds. I make it with the flake and my teeth and a long cold while my fingers go stupid, and when it is done I work it back and forth and it holds.

This one I did not find on a dead man. This one is mine.

Behind my teeth the word turns over. Rook. The boy called Rook understood a thing today that he only ever did by accident before. The second mouth opens while I work — it always opens while I do anything — and says, you make and make and there is no one to show it to. The stone does not look. The water does not look. I set the binding in my palm and I look at it for both of us. There. I have shown it to the only one who keeps me.

---

The bones teach me the rest, one cold day at a time, and the days are shorter cold every time.

I learn that a long bone struck slantwise against the black rock breaks to an edge, and the edge cuts. I learn to wrap its back in hide so it bites the seal-meat and not me. My hands become a ruin of small red mouths that close before they should, and I do not look too long at that either.

And I learn the stone better. I sit through a whole short day in the door of the crack — the crack high in the seep-cliff, up-slope of the cove, that is becoming mine — knocking black on black. Most strikes only jar my arm to the shoulder. Some shed a flake meaner than any bone. The cold in my bones sits still and patient through it, content with the long dark. The heat sits low, fed on seal. For once neither of them is gnawing me, and I work.

This is the part that is new in me. I felt the first piece of it settle when the rock came down on the pup — settle into me like a stone into a wall. Now I am not the wall. I am the one stacking it.

Before, I waited. I crouched and the world gave or it did not. Now I lie awake in the crack in the cliff, walled and fed. I do not wait. I plan. I see the next thing before it comes. I see the long straight standing-dead in the lee that would make a shaft. I see the flake that would be its tooth. I see the seal-sinew that would bind tooth to shaft, drying now on my shelf beside the wrong things — the warm stone that should not be warm, the frost that rings the floor but never touches my body, my own breath that will not smoke. I see, three days out, a thing that does not yet exist: a length of me made longer, an arm made into a reach, a tooth I can put into something from a stride away.

A spear.

I do not have it yet. But I have all its pieces laid out in the dark, apart on the flat stone, and I know now what apart means. It means a thing waiting to be made. The boy who washed up could not see three days out. Could only see the next blow coming, the next hand. This one builds toward.

The mind has grown teeth.

---

I haul the last of the pup up to the crack in its own hide, knotted shut with its own bone. I do not share it. The thought does not even come. There is no one to share it with, and the part of me that once would have flinched at that does not flinch now. It is mine. I made the kill. I read the wind. I kept every part.

The kill changed the heat under my ribs. It does not gnaw like the second mouth. It does not ache. It is calm. It is fed. It looks out of my eyes at the black shore and sees larder, and sees range, and sees what flees and how to be downwind of it, and it does not miss anyone at all.

I sit in the door of the crack with seal-fat shining on my hands, full in the belly, full in that low red place that used to only hurt. Only the one mouth is still open — the second one, small and far under everything, saying the boy is being eaten from the inside by the thing that is keeping him alive.

Behind my teeth I hold the word. Rook. I hold it tighter now, because I can feel the heat pressing up behind it, patient, fed, ready to do the holding itself if I ever let go.

Out past the flats the dark comes down on water I have never crossed, on the long gray distance the slavers' ship came out of and went back into. The heat under my ribs lifts toward it, slow, and it is not afraid.

It wants to know what else is out there to be eaten.
