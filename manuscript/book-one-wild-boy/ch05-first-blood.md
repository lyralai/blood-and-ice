# Chapter 5: First Blood

The morning the sea goes quiet, I learn the next law. A den keeps the wind off a body. A body still needs feeding. The shore does not feed you for staying still.

The sound from the night water did not come again. I lay against the warm inner stone with the fire stood up gold and waited for it, the near mouth gone quiet, the far one wide. It did not come. Somewhere in the waiting I went under into sleep. I woke into gray with the coals breathing low and the place around me cold and still and mine. So the sea-sound goes back onto the dark shelf with the rest, the sound I cannot name, filed not-understood. I crawl out the lee mouth into the wind, because the body has its own count and the count has come due.

The eel-flesh is gone. The crab is gone. I have been spending myself on stone for days, hand over hand, cold dead weight after cold dead weight, and the spending came out of somewhere, and the somewhere is empty now. The near mouth is awake and it is loud. Not the far one — the far one I cannot feed, I have learned that, no meat answers it. This is the other, the true one, the one in the belly that grinds when there is nothing in it, and it grinds now, low and steady, and I stand outside my walled place in the moving cold and feel how thin I have gone in the building of it.

---

I go to the pools first, because the pools are what I know.

The tide is out. The black rock lies bared and weeping all down to the gray water, and the pools sit in the hollows of it, still and clear and cold, each one a little held world the sea left behind. I have taken eels from these. I have taken crabs. I crouch at the lip of the first one with the patience the hands beat into me, the cage-patience, the one good thing out of all that taking, and I wait, and I read.

Nothing. The pool holds weed and stone and my own gray face looking up at me and nothing that moves. The next is the same. The next. I work them down the bared shelf, pool after pool, and where there was life before there is only the weed swaying slow and the small shells shut tight against me, and the near mouth grinds, and I understand, late and with my body the way I understand everything: I have taken this shore. I have eaten down the easy law of it. The pools that fed me fed me until they were empty, and they do not fill again because I am hungry. The shore gives once. Then it makes you go further.

I go further. Out along the bared rock to where the water still moves over it, where the deeper pools are, the ones the tide does not fully empty, and I lie down on the cold wet stone with my arm in the water to the shoulder, feeling under the ledges the way I learned, slow, reading with my fingers what the eyes cannot reach. The cold of the water climbs my arm and sits in the bone. My fingers go stupid with it. There is something far back under a ledge — I feel the shape of it, smooth, hard, a shell the size of two fists, gripped to the rock — and I work my dead fingers around it and pull and it does not come, it is grown to the stone, and I brace and pull and the edge of it opens a line in my finger, sharp, and the dark blood threads out into the water and is gone. I get it loose. I drag it up. One shell. I crack it on the black stone and there is a knot of cold gray flesh inside, salt and sea and small, and I swallow it whole and the near mouth barely feels it go down.

One shell. A day of building spent, and one shell to put against it. The cage-lie rises in me, small and quick, whispering that this is enough, that I should lie still and let it pass — and I drown it. The lie kept me alive in the hold by making me small. Out here it would make me dead. I spit it out into the wet and stand up off the freezing rock and look down the long bared shelf to the gray moving water, and I know the shore has stopped giving and started asking.

---

I see them from the high rocks the next day, and the near mouth makes a sound in me that is almost the far one's sound.

Down on the low flat stones where the shelf goes out into the water, dark shapes lie hauled up out of the sea. Seals. I do not have the word in my mouth, I have the shape only — long bodies the color of wet stone, heavy and slow on the rock, the way a thing is slow when the water is its place and the land is only a place to rest. They lie in the weak gray sun with their faces up, and now and then one humps itself a length along the rock and settles again, and they are fat. That is the thing the eye fixes on and will not leave. They are fat the way nothing on this shore is fat, the way I am not fat, the eel thin and the crab thin and I thinner than both. They are made all of the fat the cold has eaten out of me, a whole body of it lying there in the gray. The near mouth grinds. Under it the far one stirs and does not ease, because it never eases, but it leans, it leans toward the fat dark shapes the way it leans toward the night water, and I make myself look away from that, because the far mouth's leaning is not hunger I can trust.

I crouch on the high black rock with the wind off the water and I watch them, and I learn them, because I will not go down there knowing nothing and bleed for it the way I bleed for everything. They are heavy on land. Slow. But not stupid — when a gull comes down near them one lifts its head fast, faster than the heavy body says it should, and watches, and does not settle again until the gull lifts off. They keep near the water. Always near. Three lengths, four, no more, between the fat bodies and the gray edge where the sea takes them back and the slowness on land becomes a thing in the water I cannot follow. To take one I have to come between it and the sea. I have to be the thing standing in the four lengths it needs.

I have nothing. Hands and a body gone thin and the cage-patience. I go back to the den in the dark and I lie against the warm stone with the fire stood up and I think the way the body thinks when it is working a thing out — slow, turning it, the head watching while the hands remember. I have taken eels with my hands. A seal is not an eel. A seal is the most meat I have ever stood near, and the most that can hurt me, and between those two the near mouth grinds all night and does not let me sleep down deep.

---

I make the thing to do it with, because the hands cannot do it alone.

The drift-spars I leaned over the den mouth — there are more of them up the high tide-line, the long gray bones of old wood the sea throws and forgets. I take the straightest. It is as long as I am and harder than it looks, dried to a thing with no give. The end of it is broken to a rough point already, and I work that point against the black stone the way I worked stone against stone learning the den, grinding, turning, the splinters coming off it, the wood going pale where the gray skin wears away. It is slow. It eats the day. My hands, barely closed from the den-work, open again — a split across the palm where the spar turns under my grip, the dark blood welling, warm and red, the one warm bright thing, and I watch it well and let it pass to the shelf without a name, because by the count it should not close before a moon and it will close in three. I put the bleeding hand back on the wood.

The point comes up sharp. Sharper than the broken end, ground to a real point, a thing that will go in. I test it against the meat of my own thumb and it bites, and I pull back fast, and that is the answer: it bites. I have made a tooth. The fire made warmth out of dead wood and the den made a held place out of dead stone and now the dead wood has made a tooth out of nothing but grinding and blood, and the pulling does not stop, the head says, it only finds the next dead thing to pull a living use from. I sleep that night with the spear laid against the warm inner stone beside me, the point dry and pale in the firelight, and the near mouth grinds, and I do not feed it, because tomorrow it eats or it does not.

---

I come down on them in the gray before the sun, into the wind, the way I learned the wind on the day I found the still pocket.

The wind is the thing. The wind carries my smell and a seal's head comes up fast — I learned that on the high rock — so I come into it, with the cold pushing at my face and my smell going back behind me to the empty land where there is nothing to warn. The black rock is wet under my bare feet and I read it with the soles the way I read the pools with my fingers, slow, each step set where the foot will not slide and ring stone on stone and lift the heavy heads. The spear is in both hands. The near mouth is loud. I put it down under the cage-patience and I go.

They are hauled up where I saw them, dark and fat on the low flat stone, three lengths from the gray edge, four. I pick the one nearest the land, the one furthest from its water, and I come around in the wind to put myself between it and the sea, low, slow, a stone moving, the way the cold creeps and you do not see it cross the floor until it has crossed. The body knows this. The body did this in the hold, made itself nothing, crept under the notice of the hands. I am close. I am closer than I have been to anything that could hurt me since the sea. I can smell it now — a thick smell, warm and rank and oily, fat and salt and wet fur, the smell of all that heat lying there in a skin, and the far mouth leans so hard toward it that for a breath I am not the boy creeping with the spear, I am only the leaning, and that frightens the body more than the seal does, and I pull myself back into the boy, into the hands, into the point of the wood.

It sees me.

The head comes up off the rock fast, the dark wet eye finds me, and the whole heavy body gathers to move — toward the water, toward the gray edge that is three lengths off and behind me now, because I have done the one thing right, I am between. It comes at the gap. It humps the heavy length of itself across the rock faster than the slowness said it could, straight at the sea, straight through where I stand, and there is no thinking left, only the body, and the body drops its weight behind the spear and drives the ground-sharp point into the dark shape as it passes — into the front of it, the thick of it, behind the lifted head, where the fat is — and the wood goes in.

It goes in and the seal screams.

I did not know it would have a voice. It is a high wet broken sound, almost a child's sound, almost a sound I have made, and it goes through me and the hands want to let go and run, and I do not let them, I drive my weight down onto the wood and into the rock and the body bucks under it, huge, all muscle, throwing me, and I go down onto the wet stone with the spear still in my hands and in the seal and the two of us tangled on the cold rock in the gray light. It is so strong. It is stronger than I am by far, a body of warmth and water against a body the cold has eaten thin, and for a long stretch it is not a killing, only a holding-on, my whole weight on the wood and the wood in the wound and the seal screaming and heaving toward its sea and dragging me with it, both of us slick, the rock scraping the skin off my side, the cold edge of the water coming nearer as it hauls us toward it.

And then the warmth comes out of it.

It comes out around the wood, dark, fast, far more than wells from my own splits — a flood of it, hot, running over the cold black rock and over my hands and my arms, and the smell of it is iron, bright iron, the taste of it at the back of my mouth though none has touched my mouth, the same taste my own blood leaves when a tooth-gap fills, only so much more of it, a whole body of warmth pouring out onto the stone and steaming faintly in the cold air. The heaving slows. The screaming thins. The huge strength that was dragging me to the sea goes out of the body the way the heat goes off a fire in the open, fast and then all at once, and the dark shape sags down onto the rock under me and is still, and the warmth keeps running out of it, slowing, pooling, going cold where it spreads, and I am lying on top of it with the spear still gripped in my torn hands, shaking, breathing through the gap in my teeth, my whole front soaked in warmth that is not mine.

I have killed it. The near mouth — the near mouth is silent now, struck silent, because here is the answer to it, here is meat past all the meat I have ever stood near, a body of fat and heat lying under me on the cold stone. And the far mouth — the far mouth opened all the way when the blood came, opened wide, leaned the whole of itself toward the running warmth, and it did not close. It is not closing. The body of the seal is going cold under me and the far mouth is still open over it, still leaning, still not fed, and I lie in the iron smell and the steaming dark and feel the thing I least wanted to feel: that I have spilled the most warmth I have ever spilled, killed the most life I have ever killed, and the second mouth took the smell of it and wanted more.

---

I drag it up to the den slow, the long heavy length of it, the way it dragged me toward the sea — only now I am the one hauling, and it does not fight, and the warmth has gone out of it cold.

It costs the whole day. The body that the cold ate thin against the seal's fat strength now spends itself again hauling the fat strength up the bared rock, stone by stone, length by length, the dead weight of it nothing like the eel and everything like the building-stone, a downward weight with no give and no kindness, and I bleed for it, the rock taking skin off my hands and my side, and I do not stop, because this is meat for days, meat past counting, and the shore does not give twice, and I will not leave it on the flat for the gulls and the gray water to take back. I get it to the lee mouth. I cannot get it through. I work at it where it lies in the wind, the way I learned to work the eel, only there is so much of it — the thick fat under the skin, finger-deep, and under that the dark red meat, and the iron smell rising off all of it and going into the near mouth and the far one both.

I cook it that night in the den the way a creature cooks in its own place.

The fire stands up gold and does not duck, the wind on the far side of the wall, and I hold the dark meat to the steady flame on the green stick and the fat runs and hisses and flares, the smell going up thick and rank and good, crawling along the slab roof, filling the whole walled dark, and when I tear into it the warmth is held in by the held warmth around me, hot all the way through, and I eat slow, chewing on the side where the teeth still are, and the near mouth feeds at last, fills, goes quiet, the grinding gone out of the belly for the first time since the den-work began. The fat is the thing. The fat sits in me warm and stays warm, putting back into the body the heat the cold ate out of it, and I feel myself thicken against the cold even as I sit there, the first time the shore has put back into me more than it took.

And the far mouth does not feed.

I knew it would not. I have learned that no meat answers it. But I had not known it would lean so hard toward the killing and the spilling, toward the iron and the running warmth, and then lean still, after, over the full belly and the quiet near mouth, still open, still wanting, as if the seal had only shown it the shape of what it wants and not given it the thing. I sit in the held warmth with the fat going into me and the second mouth open over all of it, wider than the meat fills, and I understand, low down, the thing I will carry off this rock: that I can feed the body. I can feed it past full. And the other hunger will watch me kill to do it and want the killing more than the meat.

I do not look at it straight. I have learned not to. I feed the fire and lay another stone across the low mouth and put my back to the warm inner rock, the iron still on my hands, the fat warm in my belly, the spear dry and pale beside me with the dark dried on its point.

The cold comes for the blood in the night.

I wake to feed the coals, the body doing the work half in sleep, and I see it — the frost crept out in its wide white ring across the black floor, glittering where the firelight finds it, still and waiting at the edge of the warm. It rings me the way it always rings me, not where my body is, not where the fire burns. But it has come to the seal-meat I set by on the cold side, the dark cut pieces laid on the stone — it has reached them, ringed them, and where it touches them the warmth of them holds. They do not turn. They do not go the way meat goes. The cold sits on them and keeps them, keeps them the way it keeps everything it touches, still and unchanged at the edge of the heat, and I look at the kept meat in the white ring and the heat-wrong and the cold-wrong holding each other off across the small floor of the place I built, and I do not understand it.

I put it on the shelf with the breath that won't smoke and the wounds that close too soon and the warm stone that gives and never asks. The shelf is crowded now. I keep my eyes on the fire, and the iron taste sits at the back of my mouth, and the far mouth stays open in the dark over the full belly and the kept meat and the steady gold flame, leaning still toward a thing none of it answers.

I have spilled the most warmth I have ever spilled, and the cold kept it for me, and the second mouth watched, and wanted more.
