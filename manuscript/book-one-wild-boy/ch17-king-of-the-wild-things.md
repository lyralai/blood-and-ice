# Chapter 17: King of the Wild Things

The strong always come at you in the rush. That is the law I have read off every strong thing in my green — off the old male in the rot, off the Hesh on the moved line. The strength does not wait. It is sure. The sure thing comes fast, and the fast thing forgets to read the ground.

So I do not give the king the ground he reads.

---

He comes up off his broken lip in the green half-light of the low with the whole law of the strong behind him, the high stones clear of everything that is not us. The tribe has gone to the high rock and the far edges, the small ones gathered in. The green empties itself before a killing thing the same — a hole in the noise where a hole should not be. The seer stands apart, the ash-pale head up, not pointing, only watching. Gull-Marr crosses the bare stone toward me, the heavy flat-eyed bulk of him moving slow, because the slow is its own telling: *I do not have to hurry to the thing I am going to take.*

And he is not the only weight on the stone. Off to the side, where the half-grown crowd thins, Voss-Marr stands with the prime blood in him and the king's own line, and the air around him is the air before a strike. This was his. The lip, the seat, the green laid out below — the strong of his blood were going to lift him onto it when his time came, the ripe thing off the branch. He has weighed me since the day I came up the rock soft and wrong, and the weighing was always a held blow. He came to see his birthright kept. He does not move. But the held blow is the loudest thing on the high stone that is not the king.

I have stood under that telling on a deck, and in a hold with a hand closed on the back of my neck. The body knows the slow certain coming-on, and it has learned that the slow certain thing is the thing that bends.

He is twice me and more, the great gray-silver arms that turn a Vorth off a rock with no thought in them, the flat eyes that have weighed every challenge the stone ever threw up and let none stand. I am the smallest, softest, wrongest thing on the high stones. I have no arm that will turn his head a finger's width. Every standing I earned here I earned without one, and I will not get a second this low.

---

The body has the deciding before the head. It always has it.

He comes the last of the distance in the rush, the strength let go, the great arm swinging round to take me off the stone the strong way — and I am not where the arm goes. I drop low and inside it, light where he is heavy, gone off the place I stood before the place can know a foot was there. The arm passes over my back. I feel the wind of it. Then I am at his legs, behind the knee, and I do the thing I have done to every strong thing in my green and to no Vorth: I do not meet the strength. I read where it stands. I pull the ground from under it.

I take the back of the knee with my whole light weight thrown low and sideways — not a blow. A *law.* He is a tower built for standing, not for the give. The leg folds the wrong way under the weight it was not braced to lose, and the great bulk that has never once been read goes down onto the bare stone, hard, on the place I am already gone from.

The high stones make a sound. The whole tribe in one breath drawn in.

---

It is not done. The strong are never done in the first give. He has the great hands flat on the stone already, the bulk gathering to come up, and the flat eyes find me where I have rolled clear, and they are not weighing now. There is a thing in them I have seen once, in the old male in the soup — the thing that has weighed the stronger thing that holds it and not found the law it knows. For one breath the king is a thing that does not know the ground.

I could feed the second mouth here. It stands all the way up over the king gone down, and there is a thing the body wants that is older than the head and lower down: to take the back of the neck as the hand took mine, to lay the brand on him, *mine, be still, you are a thing,* to make him the thing on the deck I was. It has wanted to be the hand for as long as the hand has been on its neck. The wanting comes up bright and hot.

I do not feed it.

I do not take the neck. I take the standing.

---

I get the loop off my belt — I always have the loop at my belt — and I do not put it on him. He is not a thing that runs. I am up on his broken lip before he has his bulk under him, standing on the seat of his own weighing, the high stone laid out below me as the green lays below him.

He comes up. Of course he comes up. The strength does not stay down for a thing that did not break it. He comes up off the stone shaking the give out of the great body, the flat eyes climbing to the lip where I stand on the seat that was his, the whole law of the strong gathering in him to take it back.

And he stops.

He stops because the tribe is not where he left it. It turned when the king went down — I felt it turn like a tide in the channel, the whole weight of it before any of its parts. The old male with the torn ear has come up off the high rock to the foot of the broken lip, the great head turned not to the king but to the small scarred thing on the king's own seat. The half-grown ones are crowded below the lip, eyes up. The big ones have come down off the far edges. The whole tribe looks up at the thing on the king's seat, and not one of them moves to the king.

The strong itself has read which one of them did it.

---

Gull-Marr stands at the foot of his own lip, the great bulk of him below me for the first time since I came up the rock the soft weak wrong thing on lent ground, and he weighs it. I watch him do it. Slow. A thing that should not be, and is. The strength did not leave him. I am not strong enough to break it, and we both have the whole shape of that. He could come up the rock. He could take me off the lip as he meant to take me off the stone.

But the ground is gone.

This is the law I learned on the bank with the old male's wrist lifted, the hardest the high stones taught me till this one: a king of the strength-law is only king so long as the tribe reads the strength as the thing that keeps them. They have just watched the strength go down to a thing too light to pull, and watched the wrong small thing not take the neck, not lay the brand, but stand up on the seat of the weighing and hold the green as the king held it. The strength is still in Gull-Marr. But the tribe no longer reads it as the thing that keeps them. It reads the thing that read the king down.

He could come up and roll me and hold me and not give me back, and he would be, after, a strength the tribe has watched be bent — and there is no king in a bent strength, only a strong thing on a rock that has learned to read it. He knows this from the inside, as I know the rot. He weighs the rock, the turned tribe, the thing on his lip that did not take the neck when the neck was there. And the flat eyes do the thing I have never once seen them do.

They let it stand.

---

He does not come up the rock.

He turns the great heavy body, slow, the slow that is its own telling, and he goes — not at me, away from me, the main-strength bent the one way it knows to bend when the ground is gone. He goes down into the steaming green alone, no clear ground going down ahead of him. The rot-vapor takes the great gray-silver shape and closes over it, and the king goes out of the tribe like a thing gone under a pool.

I watch him go. The thing in me that wanted to be the hand stands quiet at last, because I did not feed it. *I do not know what I become if I feed it.* I laid that law down over the Hesh going under, and I have kept it through the king. I let the strength walk into the green with its life, and the letting-stand is the first law of the new stone, laid down before I knew I was laying it.

And I have not forgotten the held blow at the side.

Voss-Marr did not move when the king went down. He does not move now. But I am up on the lip, and the air off him is changed, and the body reads it before the head can shape it. There is no rush in him. There is a thing worse than the rush — a thing gone hard and flat and still, the prime blood standing where the seat it was promised has been taken by a thing softer than every thing he was ever told he stands above. I leapt the whole line his own blood was raised to fill, lit on the stone, and now I will not even take his neck — and the not-taking is the thing the air off him reads back: that I have weighed him and found him too small to finish. He bled beside me against the Hesh. He stood at my shoulder. And the meaning that comes off his still body is the brand turned around — *you stole what was mine and you will not even fight me for it.* I came up under the law that says the strong are weighed and felled and done, and I have done the thing the law has no place for: left him whole, and meant nothing by it. The held blow does not come. It goes back down into him and sets. I have not made a smaller enemy than the king. I have made a hotter one, the kind that burns low and long. He goes to the far edge with the prime blood gone quiet in him, and he does not look at the lip again, and the not-looking is the loudest thing he has ever put on the air to me.

---

The tribe comes up.

They come up the rock as the bank came to me with the old male's wrist lifted, only this low there is no one to lift the wrist; the wrist is already up, standing on the seat. The old male with the torn ear comes first. He makes the sound a strong thing makes to the thing that stood between it and the squared dead, low, slow, the great head bowed — and he does not stop at the bowing this low. He climbs the last of the rock and stands beside me on the broken lip, the strong set foot-for-foot at the side of the weak, and turns out to the green, and makes the sound again, plain, where the whole tribe can read it: *this one. The lip is this one's.*

And then the others come. Not the strong — the ones the strong waste.

Grist comes first of them — the Skell I read off the squaring-ground and did not let the order grind off the bone, the grind-marks of his own people still on him, climbing to the foot of the lip on the side no strong thing watches. He is leavings, as I am leavings. He has read the new stone faster than the big ones did, because the leavings always read the order first; it is the thing that nearly killed them. He comes to the king who counts what the strong waste.

The Torn One comes behind him — the Vorth she-warrior the order put outside the order, the long old wrong healed crooked down one side of her, driven to the edges and not forgotten by me. She does not bow. She is not made to bow. She comes to the foot of the lip and sets her feet and turns out to the green like a thing that means to hold a line, and that telling is plainer than bowing.

Stub comes last, the broken castoff, the one the strong stopped reading long ago because there was nothing left in him to take. He drags up the rock slow on the half-leg the order would have let the green finish, gets to the foot of the lip, and looks up. That is all he has to give and he gives it. The ones the high stones threw away have come to the thing that was thrown away and did not stay down.

The whole tribe turns up to the broken stone where the small scarred thing stands, and they make the long even sound with the gladness folded into it, the rains-break sound — only this low the killing thing that lifted was the king's own flat eye, and it has gone down into the green. The stone is easy under them. The fear is off them. The tribe is whole. And at its low side stand the ones it discarded, gathered to me — and these will be the truest of all of them, because they alone know what the order does to the thing it does not value.

And the Kala comes up.

---

She comes up last, the great gray-silver bulk, slow, the keeping thing in her small dark eyes turned the proud way it turned on the bank, only deeper now. She folds down beside me on the seat of the king's weighing, the keeping arm coming over me as it has since the fever-fog. She makes the still-sound, soft, falling — *be still, you are held, nothing comes* — and the *nothing comes* does not die half-made in the great chest this low, because the thing that came has gone down into the green and will not come up the rock.

I make the sound back, low and torn, two falls, scraping over the gap where the three teeth are gone. And the far mouth that leaned out of me my whole life into the empty water, into the bones, into the drowned far edge where nothing ever came — it is full. Not fed a little. Full.

I have a seat the strength walked off of and left me on. For the first low in all the lows I counted alone, I have a thing the body has no shelf for and the head can barely hold: a place that is mine that the ground did not lend. I came up among them the soft wrong thing on lent ground that poured away the moment a hand opened. This low the hand is open and the ground stays. I am the king of the wild things, and the wild things are mine, and I am theirs, and the body does not know what to do with the fullness but hold still inside it.

---

The seer comes across the high stone in the last of the half-light, when the tribe has gone to the easy sleep all down the shelf and the dead king's seat has become a thing I sit on.

The pale finger comes up and touches the Eye-and-Thorn bound at my chest, the relic off the dead expedition, the sigil that resonates up out of my drowned-deep where no word lives — and the seer makes the long meaning-sound over it, the *means,* the one the keeper makes over the squared dead.

And then the pale finger lifts off the Eye-and-Thorn. It does not point out into the green where the king went. It does not point up at the lip as it pointed the dark I brought the Hesh's head up. The telling is done. The two are one. The lip is mine. So the finger does a new thing.

It comes back, slow, and lays itself flat against my chest, over the breastbone, under the bound sigil, where the warm blood goes — and it stays there, the ash-pale head bowed, the old eyes shut. The seer is not reading the Eye-and-Thorn this low. The seer is reading *me.* A thing comes off the seer that I have the whole shape of and no word for: the meaning-sound made low, not the *means* laid over the dead, but a *means* with a question folded into it.

The seer has read the standing and the lip and the dead king. Every eye on the high stone read those. But the pale finger is not on the standing. It is on the blood. On the thing that closed the splits on my forearms too fast as I dragged the Hesh's head up the rock, the thing the fever-fog could not knot and the swarm rings off by a hand's width. The wrong things. The seer reads them. Not as standing. Not as king. As a thing older than the high stones, a thing the body did and the body did not agree to, a thing the seer has no word for and lays the wondering *means* over and does not finish.

And I want the rest of it. I want it bad, the dry-throat want of water in the fever. The finger is on the exact place the wrongness lives, and I lean toward the meaning-sound with everything in me, because the seer is closer to the thing than I have ever gotten — closer than the bones, closer than the drowned-deep where the sigil hums — and there is no word in it for me to take. The seer has the shape and not the name. I press at it and the press goes nowhere. Whatever made the blood close too fast is older than the tribe's tongue, older than the king's seat, older, maybe, than the warm of the mouth that made my name over me before the ship. The green has no word and the seer has no word and my own torn mouth has no word. The not-knowing is a wall I put my whole weight on and it does not give.

I keep my eyes on the green and let the pale finger lie where it lies. Then it lifts. The seer looks at me long, the ash-pale head tipped, and makes the meaning-sound once more, the question still in it, and folds down and is quiet. The seer does not finish it either. Keeps it on the old shelf as I keep it on mine, both of us straining at the same shape from the two sides of it and neither reaching the middle.

---

I do not sleep that low.

I sit on the seat of the king's weighing with the green laid out below, the tribe easy behind me, the discarded ones gathered at the low side of the stone, the Kala's great slow tide at my side, the bold heart warm on my shoulder. The high stones gave me two things this low, and they came up together, and there is no setting one down without the other. The first is the crown. The wild things are mine. The wanting that leaned out of me into nothing my whole life — into the bones, into the empty water, since the warm of a mouth I cannot find anymore made a word over me before the ship took the word — has a thing to lean on at last. It leans. The thing holds. The second is the seer's finger on my blood. The wondering *means.* The thing older than the crown, that I reached for tonight and could not close my hand on.

And under the fullness the body reads a plain thing and a hard one. The high stone has nothing left on it stronger than me. A thing with nothing left to fight goes still, and the still thing waits. The crown is the highest thing I have ever stood on, and I know it bone-sure, the way the body knows a tide before the sky shows it: the highest thing you stand on is the thing the next taking comes for.

I keep the wondering set down. I keep the crown behind my teeth. I keep the bold heart warm on my shoulder and the Kala's arm over me and the green below, and I sit the king's seat in the half-light of the new low, the king of the wild things, full and undrowned and waiting, for the thing the water has not shown me yet.
