# Chapter 4: The First Shelter

The light is leaving, and it is not coming all the way back.

I do not count days but I count this. The gray that wakes me comes later and weaker, like a thing that has to climb higher each morning to reach the cove and gets tired sooner of the climb. The dark eats the edges now. Both ends. The day is a coal in the middle of a long cold that is closing its hand.

I taste it in the wind. The wind has turned. It came off the water before, wet and rotten and full of salt. Now it comes down off the high black inland, dry, with teeth in it, and it carries no smell of any living thing. A clean wind. An empty wind.

The cold in my bones leans toward it the way a man leans toward a fire. That is the wrong way to lean. I do not look at it straight.

I taste the turning the way I taste everything here. Sidelong. Hungry. The wet has gone out of the air and the gray has gone thin and the wind has set its mouth to the inland and will not come off it again. Something old in me reads it and goes still. This wind is the front edge of a thing too big to fight, and I have to be small and dug-in before it shuts. I learn the law before anything teaches it, because nothing is going to teach it.

The birds know it before I do. The screaming white ones that fight me for the low-tide fish go up off the cliff one morning all at once, a whole cloud of them, and bend south and do not come back. The ledges go quiet. The quarrel of them was a thing I hated. The quiet is worse, because the quiet means they were right about something and I am still here.

---

The cove will not hold me through this.

I have the lee of a black boulder, a hollow of dead grass, the wind for a wall on three sides and nothing on the fourth. It found me there all the warm-time and I let it. The wind finds the gap and reaches all the way to my spine, and it will keep finding me when the cold's hand shuts, and a sleeping place the wind can walk into is only a longer kind of outside.

So I will make my own. Smaller. Deeper. Harder to come into.

There is a crack in the cliff at the edge of the flats where two black shelves part and a half-starved boy can stand sideways in the dark between them. Too narrow for anything bigger than me. The cold breath of it pushes out against my face when I lean in, old air, dry air, the breath of the stone itself.

It is mine if I make it so. Nothing here is mine yet. Even the knife at my belt is only a thing the sea gave back and no one has come to take.

---

I learn the rest from the seals.

I watched them all the warm-time, hauled up on the black flats, and I thought they were only lazy, only fat. But they bed where the rock breaks the wind. Always. Backs to the stone, faces to the open, the soft parts tucked in where the cold cannot get a grip. They do not lie down in the easy places. They lie down in the right ones.

So. Back to the stone. Face to the open. Soft parts in.

I go deeper into the crack where it narrows. Back here the wind cannot turn the corner. Back here a fire will stand straight instead of guttering sideways into the dark. A thread of fresh water seeps from the rock, one finger of it silver in the corner, sweet, not salt. I put my mouth to it and it does not taste of the sea at all.

The cold in my bones likes it back here. Likes the still and the dark and the way the stone holds without giving anything back. There is a place where the rock is warm under my hand. Not sun-warm. The sun has not reached this deep in a long time, maybe ever. Warm from inside itself, low and steady, like something breathing slow under the stone. I do not like that it likes it. I drink and I move on.

---

Now the mouth of it. The wind's door.

I carry stone. Black stone, all there is, broken slabs off the talus where the cliff has shed itself, and I stack them across the opening the way the tide stacks weed against a rock. Big flat ones low. Smaller fitted into the gaps. My hands split on the edges, two red mouths below the fingers, the old places that keep opening.

They will shut by morning. Faster than they should. Days where it ought to be weeks.

The heat under my ribs wakes at the blood the way it always wakes. It does not care that the blood is only mine. It pushes up, low and red and pleased, and it wants me to stop carrying stone and go find something with a pulse. I keep carrying stone. There is no pulse out here it can have but my own, and the heat will take that too if I let it. So I do not let it. I bleed on the rock and I stack the rock and the heat settles back down under the snow of me, listening, the way it always listens.

I leave a gap at the bottom for the smoke to crawl out low, the way I have watched my own smoke crawl and pool and seek the cold. I leave a gap my own body just fills, so that when I lie down I am the last stone in the wall. Let the wind come to that door. It will find a boy in it, and behind the boy a fire, and it will get neither of us.

---

A wall is only a wall until there is food behind it the dark cannot take back.

I have dried fish. I learned the trick of it without meaning to. A deep-pool fish left on a high rock came back to me days later not rotted but hard, leathered, the cold dry wind having cured it into something that kept. So I make the wind do it on purpose. I split the orange-brown fish down the spine. I lay them open on the high ledges where the dry inland wind scours over. The wet goes out of them. The flesh goes dark and stiff and folds like old hide. They stop smelling of food. That is the whole trick. To make the meat forget it is meat until the day I need it to remember.

I stack the hard fish in the deep dry of the crack, back past the seep where the water cannot reach them. I knock grass-floss from the high cracks and pack it in a dry hollow, fistfuls of it, the dust that catches a spark. I gather the standing-dead from the lee, light in the hand, hollow when I rap it. Never the wet tide-wood again. I know the fire's true food now, and I lay a winter of it against my own wall.

This is the part the second mouth tries to ruin.

It opens under everything while I work. Not the belly. The belly is a thing fish can answer, and fish I have. This is the other one. The one no meat ever shuts. It says why. It says, you stack fish for a boy nobody is coming for. It says, you build a door against the wind, but the wind was never the thing that hollowed you.

I feed the fire another branch and I do not answer it. There is no answer that is meat.

---

And then it is done, and I stand back, and I look at the thing I have made.

A wall of black stone with one boy-shaped gap. A fire that will stand straight in the still air behind it. Dried fish in the dark, water in the corner, tinder dry, fuel laid by.

It is small. It is low. It is mine.

The slavers had cages. The dark in them went where the slavers wanted, and I went where the hand on my neck wanted me. This is not that. I press my hand flat to the floor of it. Mine, under the palm. Every cold inch. The slavers never let me build. There has never in my whole remembered life been one single thing that was mine because I made it so.

Something in my chest pulls tight and high, almost like the fire-laugh, almost. I hold the word behind my teeth, the one the sea did not take. Rook. I have a den now. The boy called Rook has a den. It is the closest the word has come to meaning a thing that is not a hand closing.

---

So I should be full. The belly is answered. The cold is answered. I can last the closing hand of the dark. I have made one spot on all this black indifferent rock that is mine and will keep me alive.

And I am not full.

I learn that tonight too, in the body before the head. I sit in the door of my own home, fed and walled and warm, and I look out past my own firelight to the black where the cove was, where I came up out of the sea with nothing and no one. The second mouth opens anyway. Wider than the cold ever made it. It does not want fish. It does not want the wall. The fire breathes and moves and needs me and is not a voice. The stone at my back holds me up and is not an arm.

The place I built does not belong back. It cannot. It is rock. I gave it my hands and my blood and the cold inch under my palm is mine, but it does not say my word and it never will. One boy. One fire. One on the black stone.

I feed the fire to keep the mouth from swallowing me whole.

And out past the firelight, the warm place in the rock at my back wakes a little wider than it has before. Not sun. Not fire. Something low and slow under the stone, and the cold in my bones turns toward it the way it turned toward the wind. The wrong way to lean. I press my palm to the warm rock. It holds its heat against me, faint, patient, the way it has every night since I crawled in here. I do not have a word for it. I set it on the shelf with the others — the breath that will not smoke, the wounds that close too soon — and I lie down and make myself the last stone in my own wall, and I sleep in the one place on the black shore that is mine.
