# Chapter 4: The First Shelter

The fire taught me a hard thing while I slept: it dies.

I wake in the gray before light and the warmth is gone off my face and I sit up fast, the way the body sits up when a hand has been taken away from it, and where the gold standing flame was there is only a low bed of ash with three red eyes in it, breathing slow, sinking. The wind has come back in the night. It leans over my little fire and steals the heat sideways the moment the flame puts it out, and the dry wood I gathered across two whole days is nearly gone — a few split pieces left, no more, and the long gray tide-bones not yet dried. I feed the coals what I dare. The flame stands back up, small, ducking in the wind. And I understand the next law the way I understand all the laws on this shore: late, and with my body, and only after the lack of it has already cost me.

The fire is not a thing you make once. It is a thing you keep. And it cannot be kept in the open, on a bare shelf of rock, with the wind reaching in to murder it and the rain — there will be rain, the sky has the weight of it — coming down to drown it. The warm stone is one place I cannot carry. Now I have made a second warmth, and it too is one place, and it too the cold wants dead.

I crouch over the small flame with my back to the wind, my body a wall for it, and I think the thought that has run under everything since the sea spat me up: a thing kept alive must be kept somewhere. The eel keeps to the deep pool. The crab keeps under the ledge. The bird keeps its young in the gathered grass up out of the wash. Everything that lives on this shore has a place that holds the wind off it, and I have a crack in the rock that holds off nothing, that the wind walks into after me and lays its cold hand on my neck.

I need a place. Not the crack the shore gave me. A place I make.

---

I go looking the way I learned to look — slow, along the law of the land, reading what is already here before I ask it for anything.

The crack is no good. I knew it in my spine before I knew it in my head. It is a split in the black stone, tall and thin, and the wind that comes off the gray water in the afternoon funnels straight up the length of it as if the rock were built to carry cold. The warm stone is at the back of it, and the warm stone is why I have lived, but the warm stone gives heat up into a draft that pulls it away as fast as it comes. Two warmths I have, the stone's and the fire's, and the crack spends them both into the wind.

So I walk the shore against the wind, into it, to learn where it comes from and where it does not reach. The cold pushes at my face and I push back into it, and where a shoulder of rock stands out the wind breaks against it and goes around, and behind the shoulder there is a stillness — a pocket of air that does not move, that does not pull the heat off my skin. I stand in it. I stand in the moving cold, then step behind the shoulder into the still cold, and the difference is a thing the body knows before the head names it: out there the wind eats me, in here it only sits. Stillness is its own kind of warmth. The shore has been teaching me that since the first fog, and I am only now hearing it.

There is such a pocket not far from the warm stone, where the black rock folds back on itself and a low overhang of it leans out, a slab thrown up in some old breaking of the world and come to rest half over a hollow beneath. The wind comes off the water and strikes the high side of the slab and goes over. Behind it, under it, the air sits quiet. The ground is dry there — dry, on this weeping shore, where nothing is dry — because the overhang has been keeping the rain off it longer than I have been alive. I crawl in under the lean of the rock and the wind's voice drops away to a thing happening somewhere else, and I lie in the dry stillness and feel the whole shape of the place close around me, and the second mouth, low down, does a thing it has not done: it eases. Only a little. Only for a breath. But it eases, here, in the held place, and I file that away on the dark shelf with the rest — that the far hunger answers, faintly, to being held.

The warm stone is a short crawl off. I can feel it from here, the way you feel a fire across a room. Near enough to carry its heat to.

This is the place. The shore made it long before me. I only have to finish it.

---

Finishing it is the work, and the work is stone, and stone does not give.

The overhang keeps the wind off the high side, but the low sides are open — open to the side-wind that curls around the shoulder, open to the wash if a great tide comes, open to whatever walks the night. I have to close them. I have to make walls where the world left gaps, and I have nothing to make walls of but the shore itself, the black stone broken and thrown everywhere along the high line, slabs and blocks and rubble the size of my chest and bigger.

I lift the first one and the cost of it goes straight into me. The stone is cold — cold all the way through, a cold that climbs into the hands and up the arms and sits in the joints — and it is heavy in a way the eels and crabs never taught me, a dead downward weight with no give in it, no kindness, the weight of a thing that does not care whether I live. I stagger it three steps to the low side of the hollow and set it down and the noise it makes is flat and final, stone on stone, the same flat click that gave me no spark, mocking me. My arms shake. The old fog-hunger is still in me, banked low under the eel-meat, and the stone spends it fast.

I go back for the next.

This is the cage-patience again, the patience the hands beat into me, the patience that is the one thing I made out of all that taking. One stone. Then one stone. Then one stone. I learn how they sit — that a flat face wants a flat face under it, that a stone laid wrong rocks and topples and can take a finger when it goes, that the big ones go low and the small ones chink the gaps between. I learn it the way I learn everything, by getting it wrong and bleeding for it. A block turns under my grip and comes down on my hand and I feel the bone go bright with pain and snatch the hand back and crouch over it, breath hissing through the gap in my teeth, the dark blood welling up where the skin split. I watch it well. Warm and red, the one warm bright thing, as always. By the count of days it should not close before a moon turns. It will close in three. I do not let myself wonder at it. I put the hand back on the stone.

The work eats the day. It eats the next. I carry the warm stone's heat in my spine each night and sleep in the half-made hollow and wake stiff and start again, and the wall comes up out of the shore slow, one cold dead weight at a time, a hand's height, then a knee's height, then high enough to crouch behind out of the side-wind. I leave a low mouth on the lee side to crawl through, the way the crab leaves its hole, small enough to close behind me with a last stone dragged across. And over the open front, where the overhang does not reach all the way down, I lean the long gray tide-bones, the drift-spars, butt-end in the rubble and tops against the slab, a leaning fence of old wood with the wind broken on the far side of it. Then the lighter stuff worked into the gaps — knots of root, weed dried stiff, the bark and splinters — until the wind that finds the mouth of the place comes through thin and slow, a draft and not a hunting thing.

It is not warm. I will not lie the cage-lie, small and quick, to get through the night. It is cold in there still — the cold of stone that has never once been warm. But it is still. The wind walks the shore outside and roars on the high face of the slab and goes over, and inside, in the dark, the air sits, and that is the whole of what I have made: a stillness, walled, that the wind cannot reach into and pull the heat off me.

---

The night I close it for the first time is the night I learn what I made.

I bring the fire in. I have kept the coals alive through the building, fed thin, sheltered behind my body and a low ring of stones, three red eyes carried day to day, because I learned the first morning that the fire is a thing you keep and I did not mean to make it again from torn hands and dead wet wood if I never had to. I tip the coals into a pit I have scraped at the lee mouth of the shelter, where the overhang draws and the smoke can crawl up the rock and out and not pool down to choke me — I learn that fast, coughing, my eyes streaming, backing out into the wind to breathe before I read the way the air moves and set the pit where the draft will carry the smoke off me. I feed the coals the dry threads. They take. The small flame stands up gold in the held dark, and it does not duck. It does not gutter. The wind is on the far side of the wall and the flame stands straight up off the black stone, tall and steady, throwing its light onto the close rock all around me — the leaned spars, the chinked wall, the low slab roof a hand above my head — and the light comes back off all of it and fills the small space with a moving gold, and the heat rises and meets the roof and does not fly off into the night. It stays. It pools under the slab. It comes back down on me.

I sit in it and the warmth wraps me the way nothing has wrapped me since before the hold. The fire's heat from the front and the warm stone's heat seeping through from where I have set my back against the inner rock, two warmths now, mine, held under a roof I built, in a stillness I walled out of the wind with my own bleeding hands. The shaking stops. Not the brace-and-lose shaking of the open crack — it stops, fully, the way it has not stopped at night since the sea. The muscles that have braced so long they forgot how to be still let go all at once, and I sag back against the warm inner stone and feel the heat reach the deep places the open fire never could.

I have made warmth. Then I learned the warmth dies in the open. Now I have made a place to hold it. The body did the whole of it, hand over hand, stone over stone, and the head only watched and learned the laws as the body broke itself teaching them.

---

I cook in it that night the way a creature cooks in its own den.

A fish on the green stick, held to the steady flame, the fat running and hissing, the smell going up, crawling along the roof, filling the whole walled dark with the brown good smell of warmth that is mine. No draft to snatch it away. The smell stays, pools, surrounds me, goes down into the near mouth and the far one both. When I tear into the flesh it is hot all the way through and the heat of it is held in by the held warmth around me, so that for the first time the warmth outside me and the warmth inside me are the same warmth, and I eat slow, chewing on the side where the teeth still are, holding the last of the heat in the gap where the three are gone the way you cup a coal, not swallowing until it goes cold against the empty place in my mouth.

The gap. I run my tongue into it the way I do when the body is quiet enough to remember. Three teeth gone, knocked from a child's mouth by a hand that wanted the child quiet, and the slavers thought they took something from me when they took them. They did not know what they left. They left a hiding place. Behind the gap, under the tongue, in the one dark room of me the hands never reached, I keep the only thing that was always mine.

*Rook.*

My mother's word. Her gift, from before — before the ship, before the hold, before the cold that is the whole shape of the world now. Her voice is in it somewhere far down, and her hands, the warmth of her hands that I cannot see anymore but can still, just barely, feel the shape of when I hold the word against the warm. I say it now, in the den I built, in the firelight, behind my teeth — never out loud, the shore is always listening — *Rook, Rook,* and it is warm too, warmer than the fish, the one warm thing that was never taken because it was never found.

They had their own sound for me. The slavers. A low ugly thing made behind their own teeth that meant *property, come-here, shut-your-mouth,* the felt-shape of a hand closing on the back of my neck, and they put it on me the way you put a mark on a beast, and they thought it was what I was. It is not my name. It never was. I left that sound in the sea with the ship and the chains and the cold body that lay next to mine in the hold and was hauled up the ladder one gray morning, whose name I never learned, who never once turned its face to me. That sound I drowned. This one I kept. The shore can take the teeth from around it. It cannot take the word.

---

I lie back in the held warmth, and the body thinks the way a body thinks when it has made the second hard thing and found that the making goes on.

*I made warmth out of dead wood. Now I have made a place to keep the warmth, out of dead stone. First the fire, then the den. One thing taught the next.* Out of the cold I pulled fire, and out of the bare rock I pulled a place. The pulling does not stop.

And under the thought, low down, the far mouth stirs.

Not toward the fire. Not toward the fish. The fire eased it, faintly, the way the held stillness eased it, the way being walled and warm and not flayed by the wind eased it — but eased is not fed. The den holds the warmth. It does not hold the other thing. The second mouth opens under the warmth, wider than the fire fills, wider than the walls reach, and waits there patient, the way the sea waits past the ring of light, and the warmth lies on top of it and does not touch the bottom of it. A den is a place to hold a body. The far hunger is not in the body. It is under it, the way the warm stone is under the shore, and no roof I lean over it will ever quite reach down to where it sits and aches.

I do not look at it straight. I have learned not to. I feed the fire instead, and lay another stone across the low mouth to close the night out, and put my back to the warm inner rock.

The cold answers anyway. In the deep of the night I wake to feed the coals, half in sleep, the body doing the work without me — and I see it has come even here, into the den, the cold-wrong, the frost crept out in its wide white ring across the black floor where I have lain. It rings me the way it always rings me. It glitters where the firelight finds it, still and waiting at the edge of the warm, and it does not come where my body is and it does not come where the fire burns. The heat-wrong and the cold-wrong hold each other off across the small floor of the place I built, the way they held each other off in the open, as if the den changed nothing about the thing I am, only put a roof over it. I do not understand it. I put it on the shelf with the breath that won't smoke and the wounds that close too soon and the stone that gives heat back and never asks. The shelf is getting crowded. I keep my eyes on the fire.

Not now. Now I have a den. The wind is on the far side of the wall, the fire stands straight up and does not duck, the near mouth is quiet and the far one is at least held, and I am not lying in the open with the cold walking in after me to lay its hand on my neck. I have made a place. The first place that was ever mine.

I sit close to the steady flame, fed, warm, walled, lit — and out past the leaned spars, out past the chinked stone, far off on the night water beyond the held stillness, the low sound comes back across the cold that is not the wind and not the sea.

I stop chewing. I listen.

It comes again. Closer.
