# Chapter 14: The Word

She comes at the wrong tide.

Not low. The water is up, chewing the spit black-to-gray, and the dark is coming down behind her — both her limits broken in one crossing, the two things she has never done. She comes fast over the wet rock with the sea already at her ankles, and she is not careful. She does not square anything. She does not stop at my line.

She steps over it.

She is inside mine. The beast wants me up, wants me at her throat — a thing that crosses the bled line is meat — but I am flat in the rocks and I do not move, because her face is wet down both sides. The kelp-ropes of her hair half come loose. She is making the gone-sound, the broken one, over and over, and she has come into my black with the night coming because there is nowhere left up-coast that holds her.

---

She drops down on the cold stone below me and she talks.

Not to the air this time. She turns her face up at the rocks — at me, near me, almost me — and the words come out of her in a flood, low and fast, the gone-sound braided with the sharp up-coast sound. Her hands open toward the black like she is setting down a thing she cannot carry one more low.

I have her shapes now. I have stacked them in the dark of me through low after low. I do not have the words but I have the weather of them, and the weather is this: someone is gone. The one she fed luck for. Gone the way the tide goes and does not turn. I hear it in the gone-sound when it cracks at the top and will not close. I see it in her hands, open and empty to the black.

A ghost in the rocks cannot give her back what the tide took. But she brings it here. She lays it down where I lie and waits. I stay flat. I watch the hand. She has chosen the wrong tide and the dark and a thing in the rocks she has never touched, over whatever is behind her up-coast — and I keep still under the weight of that choosing and let it sit on me.

Then she makes a new sound. She lays her hand flat on her own chest, over the breath, and says it slow, two falls, soft-then-softer. She says it again. And I know — the way I know rot-ice from sound-ice — that this one is hers. Her own. The sound that means her.

She is giving me her name. Into the empty rocks. To no one. She gives it away free, the thing I would die in the hold before I let go of, and she sets it down between us open and unguarded like the seam of the soft hide. The second mouth behind my breastbone opens wide, and this time it does not only widen.

It fills.

---

It comes up my throat before the beast can lock it.

I do not decide. The held thing rises — not the slaver-sound, not Rook, the one behind the gone teeth I would not give back — but the other, the one she has just handed me, her name. It comes up past the place where the throat goes shut under a hand. The beast grabs for it and misses.

I say her name.

One word. Hoarse, cracked, half-shape — a sound that has not been a sound in this mouth since the hold closed on my neck, since before the black, since a hand bigger than mine cupped the back of my skull in the dark and a low voice fell on me twice the way she fell her name twice. It scrapes coming out. It costs skin. But it is hers and it is whole enough, and it lands on her like a stone dropped from the cliff.

The night holds still around it.

---

And my breath does not smoke.

I see it the moment she sees it. Cold like this, a sound from a living throat should hang white in the air between us — hers do, small clouds, gone fast. Mine carries the word out into the dark with nothing on it. No smoke. The word goes naked across the cold, and she watches the place where the white should be and is not, and her wet face changes. She does not understand it. Neither do I. We hold there in the cold, two animals staring at the place where the breath isn't.

The frost is creeping the black around me, a ring of it, the way it always rings me and never lands. Inside the ring, dry stone in the shape of me. She does not look at that. She has only the breath. The breath is enough to make her go still all over.

She does not run.

That is the third time she has not run. The first time it is one of the wrong things she is looking at — and still she stays.

---

She comes up the rocks. Slow. The way I would come on a hauled-out seal that is not yet dead, weight forward, ready for it to turn. She comes the way two wild things come at each other when neither one has decided to be the meal. I watch her hand more than her face. The hand is open, fingers loose, nothing in it, and it comes toward me through the dark and the ringing frost, and I let it.

It touches the side of my face.

Her hand is cold. River-cold, night-cold, alive-cold. And mine — she feels it the instant her skin is on me, I see it jolt up her arm — my face is warm. Too warm. Warm like the stone where I sleep, the heat standing up off me to meet her the way it stood up to meet the hide. Her cold palm sits in it and does not pull back. Her breath catches white. Mine does not catch at all. She is feeling the wrongness with the flat of her hand and she does not let go.

We hold there. Two alone things on the black, and now it is not only me that knows it. Now she knows it too. She is looking at me — has me, finally, the black has stopped hiding me, I have let it stop — and being had does not close on my neck. It does not feel like the hold. It feels like the warm hide under my back in the dark. It feels like a thing put where I sleep and not on the shelf of wrong things.

She says it again, soft, the two falls, and waits, her hand still against my jaw.

---

She wants mine.

I feel it pull up my throat to meet her, the held thing, the last one, the slaver-sound. Rook. It rises behind the gone teeth and stands there. To give it is to be had all the way. To give it is to take the hand off my neck myself and lay it in hers — and the throat that kept it for me through every low waits to see what I will do.

I do not give it.

Not yet. It costs me — it costs the same thing it cost in the cold sitting low, the thing I did not know I still had to spend — but I keep it one more night, because it is the only of me the slavers do not have, and I will not hand it across until I am sure her hand is not their hand wearing a softer skin.

But I have hers now. I have it whole, said back to her once in the dark with naked breath — her two falls, soft-then-softer — and I have it stored in the gap behind my teeth beside the slaver-name. Two sounds where for so long there was one. Her soft one. Their hard one. They will not both stay. I feel the truth of it settle in my chest like cold settling in stone: I will give back the name they made me, the hand on the neck, and keep the one she gave me free. One low soon, when her hand has proved itself her own, I will open the gone-tooth gap and let my own sound out to her.

Her thumb moves once on my cheek, not flinching.

And below us, where the spit was, there is no spit. The tide has come all the way in while we held still, black water where her road home used to be, and the dark is full down now, and she cannot cross back — she is caught on my black in the night, the one thing she swore she would never be, and neither of us has yet looked down to see it.
