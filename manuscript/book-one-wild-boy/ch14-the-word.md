# Chapter 14: The Word

She comes at the wrong tide.

Not low. The water is up, swallowing the spit black-to-grey, the slick road gone under, and the dark is coming down behind her — both her limits broken in the one crossing, the two things she has never once done in all the lows she has come to my line. She has never been caught on my black with the night turning. She has never come when the water was high enough to chew. Now she does both at once, in the same broken step, and the breaking of those two limits tells me more than any sound could: whatever is behind her up-coast is worse than the sea, worse than the dark — worse than the ghost she thinks lives in my rocks. She comes fast over the wet black with the sea already curling at her ankles, hissing white off the stone, and she is not careful. The careful is gone out of her. She does not square anything. She does not lay anything down. She does not stop at my line, the line of round stones I bleed on to make it mine, the line every low until now she has set her toes to and gone no further.

She steps over it.

She is inside mine. The beast comes up hard at that, comes up the back of me like cold water — a thing that crosses the bled line is meat, is trespass, is the world reaching into the one black that is mine and it must be answered, taken, ended. The beast wants me up. It wants me on her, weight and teeth, the way it wants the hare in the half-instant before the hare knows. I feel it strain at the lock. And I do not move. I stay poured down into the stone, flat, because her face is wet down both sides, the water running off her jaw and dropping to the black, and the kelp-ropes of her hair have half come loose and hang wet against her neck. She is making the gone-sound, the broken one, the one I learned the shape of without the word — over and over, low, the sound that means a thing that was and is not, cracking at the top each time so it cannot close. She has come into my black with the night coming down because there is nowhere left up-coast that holds her. The fires hold others; tonight they do not hold her. So she has come to the cold edge, to the ghost, to me. The beast wants meat. I give it stillness instead, and it strains, and it does not win.

---

She drops down on the cold stone below me and she talks.

Not to the air this time. Every low before, she has aimed her sounds out over the water, off to the side of where I am, naming the world to a shape from the fires that cannot name it back. Not now. Now she turns her face up at the rocks, her wet eyes going over the dark stone and not quite finding the dark thing folded into it — and the words come out of her in a flood, low and fast, falling over each other, the gone-sound braided all through them with the sharp-careful up-coast sound, the one that has teeth, the one she lays down like stones across bad ice. Her hands open toward the black. They turn palm-up and empty and push out from her chest, the way you hold out a thing you cannot carry one more step, the way the body tries to set down a weight that has no edges to grip.

I have her shapes now. I have stacked them in the dark of me through low after low, behind the gone teeth, in the warm hollow where I keep things — slik, the water-out, the water-in, the round snail-sound, the careful far-smoke sounds with the teeth in them. I do not have the words. But I have the weather of them, the way I have the weather of the sky long before the rain, and the weather of her tonight is one thing only: someone is gone. The one she fed luck for, maybe. The one she came here against. Gone the way the tide goes when it goes all the way out and does not turn — gone past the line where coming-back lives. I hear it in the gone-sound when it cracks at the top and will not pull shut. I see it in the hands, open and empty, holding the shape of a thing that is no longer in them.

A ghost in the rocks cannot give her back what the tide took. I cannot reach into the gone and bring the thing out. But she has brought it here anyway. She has carried it across the high water in the dark to lay it down where I lie, and she lays it down, and she waits — her empty hands still cupped around the shape of what is no longer in them. Not to be the only one holding it. I stay flat. I watch the hands. And the weight of her choosing comes down on me and sits — she has chosen the wrong tide and the full dark and a thing in the rocks she has never once touched, over everything warm at her back. She broke loose from the fires onto the cold edge where the others will not follow, low after low, and tonight she broke all the way loose. I keep still under the weight of that choosing and let it press on me, because it is the same weight I carry. The one who breaks away. Alone in the middle of the warm. I have been that on this black so long I forgot it had a shape outside of me, and here it is, wet-faced, below my rocks, making the broken sound.

Then she makes a new sound.

She lays her hand flat on her own chest, over the place where the breath sits, and she says it slow. Two falls. Soft, then softer. The second one lower than the first, dropping away under it, the tail of it trailing off into the dark. She says it again — hand flat on the breath, two falls, soft-then-softer — and she holds my eyes while she does it, or holds the place she thinks my eyes are. And I know, the way I know rot-ice from sound-ice before my weight is ever on it, the way the body knows a thing under the word: this one is hers. Her own. Not a name for the weed or the snail or the smoke. The sound that means her, the one carried in the chest and laid on the chest, the sound a mouth made at her once and she made back, that means *me* and not *property*, that was given and never branded.

She is giving me her name.

Into the empty rocks. To no one. To a ghost from the fires that cannot hold a thing or keep a thing or give a thing back. She gives it away free — the one thing I would have died in the hold before I let go of, the one thing the closing hand on my neck never got because it never knew to reach for it — and she sets it down between us open and unguarded, the way she laid the soft hide open with the seam bared to the sky. Not squared like a gift for the dead. Open. Bare. Given. The second mouth behind my breastbone opens wide to it, the way it has opened at every saving and every laid-down kindness, the ache that no meat has ever once answered —

and this time it does not only widen.

It fills.

---

It comes up my throat before the beast can lock it.

I do not decide it. There is no place in me where the deciding happens; the thing simply rises, the way the tide rises, the way the heat rises off the warm stone — up through the chest, up past the breastbone, up to the gate of the throat where the beast keeps its hand. And the thing that rises is not the slaver-sound. It is not the brand lodged behind the gone teeth like a barb in flesh. It is the other — the one she has just this breath handed me, said twice into the dark, soft-then-softer — her name, new in me, and it comes up fast and the beast grabs for it the way it grabbed for me to throw at her throat, and the beast misses. It closes on nothing. The sound goes past the place where the throat shuts under a hand. It gets out.

I say her name.

One word. Hoarse, cracked, half a shape, scraping over the gap where three teeth are torn — a sound that has not been a sound in this mouth since the hold closed on my neck, since before the black, since before the deck and the dark and the closing hand. The throat does not know how to do it anymore. It has been a throat for breathing and for the low warning-noise and for nothing else for so long that the word tears coming out, costs skin, drags raw over the place the cold has worked. But it is hers. It is the right shape, near enough, the two falls, and it lands on her like a stone dropped off the high cliff into still water — I see it go into her, I see it hit. Her wet face stops. Everything in her stops. The gone-sound dies in her chest half-made. She has handed her name to a ghost in the rocks and the rocks have said it back to her in a torn and living voice, and there is no part of her left that can pretend it is luck she came here to feed.

The night holds still around it.

---

And my breath does not smoke.

I see it the moment she sees it. Cold like this, cold enough that her own breath hangs white between us in small torn clouds, gone fast and made again — a sound out of a living throat should carry its ghost with it, the warm of the inside meeting the cold of the outside and standing up white in the air for a breath before it fades. Hers do. I have watched them all night, the little clouds at her mouth with every broken sound. Mine carries the word out into the dark with nothing on it. No white. No ghost. The word goes naked across the cold black between us, the shape of her name riding out on breath that does not show, and she watches the place where the white should hang and is not, the empty air just past my mouth, and her wet face changes again — slower this time, the understanding coming up under the skin of it. She does not know what she is seeing. I do not know either. I have lived inside this wrongness so long I stopped looking at it, and now her eyes drag it back up in front of me, the breath that will not make its ghost, and the two of us hold there in the cold and stare at the place where the white isn't.

The frost is creeping the black around me as we hold. A ring of it, pale and fine, spreading out over the stone the way it always spreads, ringing me, drawing its cold circle a hand's width off my skin — and never landing. Inside the ring, dry stone in the shape of me, a clean dark island in the frost. She does not look at that one. She has only the breath. She has not yet learned the ring or the warmth or any of the rest of the wrong things lodged in this body. The breath alone is enough. It is enough to take the last of the running out of her legs, enough to make her go still all the way down, the kind of still the hare goes when it has stopped believing the grass will save it.

She does not run.

That is the third time she has not run. This time it is one of the wrong things she is looking at — the naked breath, the word that crosses the cold and shows no ghost — and still she stays. The others ran from the shape alone, from the black thing on the black, ran before the fright was finished. She stays for the wrongness itself. She stays into it.

---

She comes up the rocks.

Slow. The way I would come on a hauled-out seal that is not yet dead — weight forward, low, every part of me ready for the thing to turn and become teeth. She comes the way two wild things come at each other across a narrowing distance when neither one has yet decided to be the meal. I watch her hand more than her face. The face can lie; the hand cannot. The hand is open, the fingers loose and spread, nothing held in it and nothing curled to hold — and it comes toward me through the dark and through the ring of frost that does not land, slow, slow, and I feel the beast pull back hard in me, pull toward the flat and the dark and the gone, toward the only safe I have ever had, which is being unseen. The beast wants the black to keep hiding me. I let it stop. I hold the throat of the beast the way it held mine, and I let her hand come.

It touches the side of my face.

Her hand is cold. River-cold, night-cold, the cold of a body that has been out in the high water and the dark — but alive-cold, the cold that has warm under it, not the cold of the stone or the floe or the gone. And mine — she feels it the instant her skin is on me. I see it jolt up her arm, the small shock running back through the wrist to the shoulder. My face is warm. Too warm. Warm the way the stone where I sleep is warm when the rest of the world has gone to ice, the heat standing up off me to meet her cold palm the way it stood up off me to meet the warmth she left in the hide. Her hand sits in that heat and it does not pull back. Her breath catches, a small white tear of it. Mine does not catch at all, does not show at all. She is feeling the wrongness now with the flat of her hand, the warmth that should not be in a body out in this cold, and her fingers do not lift away. They stay. They press, a little. She is learning me through the skin of her palm the way I learned her through low after low of stolen sound, and what she learns is wrong, is warm, is not what a body should be — and she does not let go.

We hold there. Two alone things on the black, in the dark, in the ring of frost — and now it is not only me that knows we are two. Through all the lows it was mine alone to know, because only I could see us both, her on the cold edge and me in the rocks above her. Now she knows it too. She has me, finally. The black has stopped hiding me and I have let it stop, have let myself be a thing that is seen, and the being-seen does not close on the back of my neck. It does not feel like the hand. It does not feel like the hold. It feels like the warm hide under my back in the dark, the even seams pressed along my spine. It feels like a thing carried up the broken country and put where I sleep, and not laid on the cold shelf with the other wrong things.

She says it again, soft, the two falls, and waits, her cold hand still against my jaw, her thumb resting on the bone of my cheek.

---

She wants mine.

I reach for it to meet her. I reach back behind the gone teeth, into the warm hollow where the one sound that was mine used to lie — soft and low and falling, my mother's gift, the sound a hand bigger than mine laid on me in the dark before any of this, the sound that meant *me* and not the thing a hand closes on. I reach for it the way you reach for a thing you have set down a thousand times and never once had to look for, sure it will be under your fingers. I reach to give it to her. To set it down between us open the way she set hers, free, unguarded, the answer that says you are not the only one alone on the water, here is mine, take it, we are two.

And the hollow is warm. And the hollow is empty.

The island took it. Low by low it took it, unmade me down to the animal until I tried once to say it with my own mouth and it came out shapeless, broken of all its shape, and then it was not even there behind the teeth to try again — only the warm where it used to lie. I reach now, with her hand on my face and her name in my mouth and her own waiting in her eyes, and I find the warm and nothing in it. There is nothing of mine to hand across the dark.

There is only the other one.

The ugly one. The low thing they made behind their teeth in the hold, that closed at the end like a hand closing, that meant *mine, be still, come, you are a thing* — that one stayed when the true one left. It is lodged in the warm dark like a barb I have never been able to work out of the flesh, the one sound for me that did not leave. And I will not send it to her. Her palm is open on my face. Her name is in my mouth, said back and landed. And I would rather lay nothing into her open hand than lay the shape of a hand closing — would rather she go home tonight with the ghost having given her nothing than have her carry off the brand, the felt-shape of a fist on the back of the neck, mistaking it for the thing I am. So I keep the throat shut. The beast did not lock it this time; I lock it myself, and that is new, and it costs.

It costs the same thing it cost in the cold of the laying-low, the thing I did not know I still had left to spend — the knowing, whole and bodily, settling through me like cold settling into stone: that the one sound I would have given her is gone, taken, and the only sound left where it lived is the one she must never have.

But I have hers now. I have it whole — said back to her once in the dark on naked breath, her two falls, soft-then-softer — and it is stored behind my teeth, in the warm hollow, lying down beside the brand. Two sounds where for so long there was one. Her soft one. Their hard one. And I feel, holding them both in the same dark behind the same torn teeth, that they will not both stay. The truth of it settles in my chest like the cold going down into the rock: I will give them back their brand someday, the hand on the neck, hand it across the water to the ones it belongs to, and I will keep the one she gave me free. And her soft one is already turning in me, working at the warm place where my own went silent, the way the high water gets under the stone too big for the small tides and rolls it at last. I do not have my own sound yet. But her sound is working toward it. One low — I feel it the way I feel weather coming, bone-sure, before the sky shows it — her name will wake mine back, the one that is mine, the one I lost, and I will open the gap where three teeth are torn and let it out across this same water, into this same mouth, and she will have it.

Her thumb moves once on my cheek, not flinching.

And below us, where the spit was, there is no spit. The tide has come all the way in while we held still — black water now over the slick road home, sheeting and breaking where the dry rock used to bare itself at low — and the dark is full down, no light left in the sky to cross by, and she cannot go back. She is caught on my black in the night, the one thing she swore she would never be, the limit she keeps and keeps hard, broken now with the rest.

And neither of us has yet looked down to see it.
