# Chapter 6: The Wreck

The flood-river carries the smell to me on the third low after the swine, and it is a smell I have a shape for, far down, in the part of me that came before the green.

I am working the high channel where the brown water runs fast off the broken stone, reading the bank with my soles for the soft prints the deer leave, when the wind shifts and lays it across my face. Not the green-smell. Not rot-of-leaf, not rot-of-meat, not the mud-and-dung of the swine or the wrong old hunger of the thing that read me through the wall. This is a dead smell with a hard edge the green does not make — tar, and salt-eaten wood, and iron gone to rust, and under all of it bone. The near mouth does not stir for it. The far mouth lifts its head.

I should turn away. The body knows that much. Every law the green has taught me says a strange smell is a thing to circle wide and read from upwind before you put your skin near it. But the smell goes down past the laws, down to the drowned-deep place, where the hands live and the ship lives and the low ugly sound the men made behind their teeth lives, and it pulls. It is the smell of the world I came out of before the sea spat me up onto the green. It is a human smell.

I follow it.

---

I follow it down the flood-river all that low and into the next, where the water spreads and slows and the mangrove takes the banks again, and the smell thickens until I taste the rust at the back of my throat.

The river bends and opens onto a wide brown reach. There, run up onto a bar of black mud and broken to its bones, is the thing the smell comes from.

A ship.

I have no word in my mouth for it and I do not need one. The body knows it as it knew the hands. It is the shape that carried me — long and curved and made of wood worked by hands, a hollow thing built to ride the water and hold a hold inside it, a thing with men in it. This one will never ride again. It lies canted on the bar with its spine snapped and its belly stove in, the planks sprung apart and gone green-black with the years of wet, furred with weed and barnacle to the waterline and bleached bone-pale above. Vines have come down off the mangrove and into the broken hull and made it part of the green. A tree grows up through the deck. The mast is down half in the water, and on the mast there is cloth, gone to rotted rags, hanging.

I stand in the shallows a long while and do not go up.

The hungry part of me leans the whole of itself toward the broken ship, leans the way it leaned toward the held-breath dark where the largeness stood and read me through the wall. *Men were here.* The body says it under the head. Men were here, in my green, men with hands and mouths and the sounds that make words, men who are mine as the swine and the thing that walks above me are not mine, men of the kind that took me and the kind I came from, all the same kind. I want to go up into the broken ship and find them. I want it so hard the wanting frightens the body, because the wanting does not stop to ask what killed the ship. A thing that does not stop to ask gets killed.

So I make it stop. I make it ask. I read the reach as I read everything.

And the reach is wrong. Not the held-breath wrong of the den, not the largeness this time, not the thing the swarms flee. The other wrong. The wrong of a place where many things died at once and the green has not finished taking them.

I see it once I let the eyes go slow. The bones.

---

They are everywhere on the bar, and I did not see them because the green has half-made them part of itself, as it has half-made the ship.

Long bones in the mud, pale and washed. A cage of ribs sunk in the silt with a young tree standing up through it. A skull, mud to the eye-holes, the jaw fallen open and gone, that grins up at the green gloom with the wide grin the dead all wear. Another. Another. They lie up and down the bar and up the bank into the mangrove roots, scattered and pulled apart by the swarms and the tide and the carrion-kin over the moons, no order in them, no two together, a whole — I have no word for it, the head reaches and finds only *many*. Many men. Men past my count, dead here on this bar and left to the green.

I go up onto the bar slow, the cage-patience over the wanting, the spear in both hands, and I read them as I read the swine-bones, with the body, crouching, my jaw near the dead jaws, asking.

These men did not die as the swine died. There is no wound in them I can read, no spear-bite, no tooth-mark in the bone but the small worryings the carrion-kin left after. The skulls are whole. The long bones are whole. They are not killed-bones, the bones of a thing run down and torn. They are the other kind. They are the bones of things that lay down where they fell and did not get up — the way the old swine-boar lies down in the wallow when the moons have eaten his strength and does not get up, and the swarms come, and the green takes him slow.

Something laid all these men down at once and put no mark on them. I want to know what. I crouch over the grinning skull with the wanting gone tight and small in me and I ask it, *what came for you*, and the skull does the thing the bones always do. It says nothing. It grins up at the green dark and lets the question fall through it into the black mud.

But I have read this green for a moon now. I know its ways the swine did not teach me. I look at the brown water standing in the low pockets of the bar — the still water with the green skin on it, the water that knots the belly of every other thing that drinks it and slides off me. I look at the rot-haze hanging in the mangrove, the fever-air that lies over every drowned reach, thick and breathless and warm, the air I have slept in a moon of nights and woken whole. I look at the swarms. The biting cloud is here too, thick over the bones and the brown pools, the ants in the eye-holes, the carrion-flies in a hum over the whole bar.

And the body understands it before the head. *The green killed them.* Not the tooth. Not the largeness in the dark. The green itself — the water, the air, the rot, the small biting things — the green that asks and asks laid these men down where they stood and put no mark on them and took them slow. They had no laws for it. They sickened, and they died.

I press at it. I want the next thing, the thing under the thing — the why of it, why them and not me, when their air is my air. The head reaches for the gap and cannot close it. There is no word in me for what would close it. I stand on the bar full of dead men, breathing the air that killed them, and the knowing hangs just under the brown water and will not surface. I only file what I cannot yet hold, and go on.

---

I go up into the ship.

I should not. The wanting takes me up over the sprung planks and into the broken belly of it, and the body lets the wanting lead this once, because the pull is so deep — the smell of the world before the green.

Inside it is dark and close and stinking of wet wood and old rust and the dead, and the tree that grows up through the deck lets a green-stained light down onto the slime and the wrack. There are more bones in here, in the dark of the hold, and these lie in a kind of order — men who crawled into the hollow of their broken ship to die together out of the rain, as I crawl into the den. The deep part of me does not want to read them. It wants only to be near them, the men of my kind, even dead, even bone. I stand in the stinking dark among them and feel the thing the swine could not feed, fed a little, just by the nearness — and then not fed, leaning still, because bones do not turn and see me. Bones are not an eye that is not my eye.

There are things in the hold the green did not make.

I find them with my hands in the slime and the dark, reading by feel what the eye cannot reach. Hard things. Worked things. Things with edges the green does not put on a thing — the straight edge, the true round, the flat plane, the edges that say *hands made me.* I pull them out of the muck one by one and the body knows each one before the head, with a deep slow shock of knowing, because these are the things from before.

A pot of iron, gone to rust-lace, that crumbles when I lift it.

A length of chain.

The chain stops the body still. I hold it up in the green-stained light and the body fills all at once with the drowned-deep thing — the hold, the dark, the weight on the wrist and the neck, the felt-shape of the hand closing, the low ugly sound behind the teeth that means *come*, means *be silent*, means *you are a thing.* The brand. I drop the chain in the muck and stand over it shaking, the gap behind my teeth aching where the three are gone, and for a long breath I am not on the bar. I am in the hold of the other ship, small, made nothing, the hands closing. Then the green-rot smell comes back and the green light comes back and I am here, on my green, master of it, and the chain is only rust in the mud and the men who held it are bones. I do not touch it again. But I do not forget it is there. The brand was here too. The men of my kind brought the brand onto my green, and the green ate them anyway, brand and all.

---

I find the rest in a chest gone soft as wet bread, up under the canted deck where the wet came slower.

Skins. Worked thin, scraped pale, marked all over in dark lines and small shapes — rows of them, ordered, the way nothing in the green is ordered. The body knows these too, far down. *Marks that mean.* I cannot read them. The marks make a buzzing low in the drowned-deep place, almost a shape, almost a sound, and then the buzzing fades and the marks are only marks, dark scratches on dead skin, and I cannot make them speak. But I know they are made to speak. I press at them. I drag my thumb along a row of the small shapes and wait for it to break open, and it does not break open. There is a way of reading them that I had once and do not have now, and I cannot reach back far enough to find it. I roll the skins careful and set them by. The men are dead but the marks are not dead. The marks are a thing the men could do that the green cannot do. I will keep them, and maybe the way of reading them will come back, the way I keep waiting for the name to come back.

A knife.

It is under the skins, wrapped in a rag gone to paste, and when I clear the rag the iron of it is dark with rust but the rust has not eaten through, because it was wrapped, because a man wrapped it careful before he lay down to die. I work the rust off the edge against a flat of wet plank, grinding as I ground the spear-point, and under the rust the iron comes up bare and hard and true. An edge no grinding of stone on wood will ever make. An edge that will go through hide and root and bone like the green going through a dead man. I have made a tooth out of hardwood and blood. This is a tooth the men made out of the dark iron, and it is past my tooth as the swine is past the crab. I hold it in my torn hand and the hand knows it. The hand has held a thing like this before. The grip of it sits in the palm like a thing remembered, and I push after the memory, hard, after where the palm learned it — and the push goes down into the drowned place and finds only brown water, and no when, and no whose.

I keep the knife. The green did not give it. The men gave it, dying, as the swine gave the fat, and I take it, because the dead give once and you carry off what you can carry.

---

And then, last, under everything, in the soft-rotted bottom of the chest, my fingers find the thing that stops the whole of me.

It is small and hard and heavy in a way the iron is not, a worked thing, round, a disc of dark metal on a perished cord, and when I lift it into the green light from the tree and rub the muck off it with my thumb the body does not file it. The body comes up out of itself at it.

There is a mark on the disc. Raised, worked into the metal by hands, the true round and the true line, a shape made to mean. An eye. An open eye, ringed, looking. And through the eye, crossing it, driven into it, a thorn — a long curved barb, a thing made to pierce, laid through the looking eye so the eye looks out around the thing that pierces it.

I have seen this.

The knowing goes through me the way the wrong-heat went through me on the day the seam closed in my palm, a long wave with no bottom. I have seen this mark. Not on the green — there is no this on the green, no eye, no thorn-shape worked in metal. I have seen it *before*, in the drowned-deep place, before the ship and the hands, before the sea, in the part of me the slavers could not reach and the green could not starve, the part where the name lived before it banked too low. The eye and the thorn. The body knows it the way it knew the chain and the knife and the smell of men, only deeper, from further down, from under the floor of everything I can reach.

I crouch in the broken belly of the dead ship with the disc in my torn hand and I reach down after the knowing, hard, the way I reach behind my teeth after the name, and it is the same — there is the shape, there is the certainty, *I know this, this is mine* — and there is no word in it, no when, no whose, no how. Only the eye, looking. Only the thorn, through it. Only the buzzing low in the drowned place, a thing trying to surface and never breaking the brown water.

I will it to surface. I hold the disc up to my eye in the green-stained dark and I stare into the worked eye and I ask it everything the body is screaming to ask — *whose are you, where did I hold you, what am I that I carry your mark under the floor of me* — and the disc gives me nothing back. It only looks. The thorn only pierces. The meaning is in there, I know it is in there, and I have no way down to it.

The far mouth stands all the way up. Wider than the men's bones woke it. Wider than the smell. Because the bones are men of my kind and the marks are a thing men do, but this — this is *mine*, particular, a thing that means *me*, a thing from the inside of where I come from, and it is here, in my green, in my hand, and I cannot read it any more than I can read the marks on the skins or say the name behind my teeth. It is one more thing that is mine and gone. One more shape with the meaning starved out of it, and I want it back so hard the wanting shakes the hand that holds it.

I loop the perished cord around my neck where the chain once sat, the worked disc against my chest over the breastbone, over the place the hunger lives, and the metal is the one part of me that is not the green's. The eye looks out from my chest now. The thorn goes through it. I will carry it until the way down to it comes back. It will come back. I tell the body it will come back.

---

I go out of the dead ship in the failing green-dark, the knife at my hip, the rolled skins under my arm, the disc on my chest, and I stand on the bar among the bones of all the men the green ate, and the body understands the last thing, the thing under the thing, and it is so large I cannot hold the whole of it at once.

The men came onto my green. Men of my kind, with their ship and their iron and their marks and their brand and their eye-and-thorn. They came onto the green I made myself master of, and the green did to them the one thing it has never done to me. It laid them down where they stood and put no mark on them and took them slow — with the water and the air and the rot and the small biting things, the water I drink and the air I sleep in and the rot I den in and the swarms that part around my skin. Every one of them died of the green I live in. Every one.

And I am standing in the middle of their bones, breathing.

I do not have the head-shape for it yet. The head reaches and finds only the bones and the standing and the breathing, the men dead all around and the boy alive in the middle, and it cannot close the gap between the two. I want it closed. I press at it standing there in the dark, *why them and not me, what is in my blood that the fever cannot find* — and the press goes down to the deepest place and finds no floor, only the brown water and the buzzing under it, the same buzzing as the eye-and-thorn, as if it is all one drowned thing down there and I have no breath to dive that deep. So I lay it down on a shelf I did not know I had, the deepest one, the one under all the others — *the green kills my kind and does not kill me* — and I do not turn it over, not because I will not but because I cannot, not this low, standing on a bar of dead men with the wanting open wide and nothing alive to answer it.

The far mouth leans out over the reach in the dying light, and it finds what it always finds. The bones do not turn. The marks do not speak. The eye-and-thorn looks out from my own chest and tells me nothing.

I take the river back up toward the den in the dark, slow, the dead-iron tooth at my hip and the looking eye against my breastbone, and I lean out behind my teeth into the rot-haze, out and out toward the before, toward anything at all that might turn and see me and make me a thing worth a sound.

And the bar behind me grins up at the dark with all its many mouths and says nothing, the way the bones always say nothing, the way my own name says nothing now in the gap where the three teeth were torn.

But the wanting does not believe the nothing. It never has.

I carry the eye out of the place of dead men, and it looks out from my chest into my green, and I go on.
