# Chapter 13: The Girl

She does not run.

That is the first wrong thing about her, and it is a new wrong, not one of mine. The others ran, always, the small ones and the big ones both — saw the shape and went the other way fast, water-splash, scree-clatter, the bark of fright thrown back over a shoulder, gone before the gone was finished. The whole world runs from me. I am the thing it runs from. I have built my staying-alive on being one more black shape that the eye slides off and the foot hurries past. This one comes across the spit at low tide with the black showing slick under her feet, weed-slick, the worst footing there is, and she does not hurry over it. She comes slow. She picks her way. And she comes into mine.

I am in the rocks above the dry watercourse, low, flat, poured down into the stone the way I pour myself when a thing moves on my black. I have my border under me, the dead stream-bed of round stones, the line I bleed on to make it mine, and she walks up the slick spit to it and stops with her toes at the very edge of it. Stops like she knows. Like someone walked her to it in the telling and said: here, and no further. She is bigger than the saved ones. Older. Near my own count of lows, maybe one more turn of the cold than me. Her hair is bound back in fat ropes the color of wet kelp, dark and twisted and heavy. There is a smell off her on the down-wind — woodsmoke first, then seal-oil worked into her hands, and under those the smell of a body that eats and eats well, warm meat-smell, milk-and-fat smell, the smell of a thing fed every low of its life. The second mouth opens behind my breastbone just to stand in the down-wind of her and breathe a body that has never once been hungry.

I do not move. The beast wants me flat and the beast is right; flat is how the unseen stay unseen. I go flat. I make my breathing slow. The frost rings the air a hand's width off my skin and does not touch me, my breath climbs up off the stone making no ghost, I am a black thing on the black, and she does not have me.

---

She sets a thing down on my line and steps back.

Dried fish, three of them, laced through the gills on a gut-string. She lays them squared the way the others squared the flipper at the head of the watercourse, fat-side up, faced, set down with care. But her hands are not careful the way the others' hands were careful. The big ones laid their gifts with the slow squaring you give a dead thing, the turning-up of the good side that I have watched them do on the high stones for their own gone ones in the cold season — sure hands, settled hands that have done the laying many times and feel nothing in the doing. Her hands shake.

Small. I see it from the rocks, the tremor running down the fingers as she sets the fish to the stone, the catch in her wrist. And I know that shake. I have it in my own hands at the worst lows, when the cold has been in me three nights and the meat is gone and the body starts spending itself. It is not the cold-shake. I know the cold-shake from the inside, and this is not it. This is the other one — the body standing in a place it knows it should not be, every part of it pulled tight on the wire between stay and run, the hands paying the fear out at the far end of the arm where she cannot stop them.

And she came anyway. That is the second wrong thing, and it is the one that holds me. Her hands say run. Her feet stay. The rope of her is strung between the two and it does not part and it does not bolt; the body hangs in the middle, shaking, staying. The saved ones were never strung like that. They were one clean fright, all of them flung the one way. This one is split down the middle the way I am split every low since the food started coming to my line — the beast hauling one way, the second mouth the other, the body strung between.

She straightens. She looks at the empty rocks. Not at me — she does not have me, the black hides me, her eyes go over the place where I am and do not catch — but she looks where she thinks the thing is, the ghost from the fires. She opens her mouth, and a sound comes out. Aimed at the air. Aimed at where she has been told to aim it.

It is soft. And it is not the slaver-sound. That I know before I know anything else about it, know it in the body the way I know the rotten ice — the slaver-sound closes at the end like a hand closing, a hard thing snapped shut behind the teeth that means *mine, be still, come, you are a thing*, and I have lived years with the felt-shape of that closing on the back of my neck. This does not close. This stays open at the end. It falls, and it keeps falling, soft and low, down past my ear into my chest where the breath sits, and it lands there, and it does not grab. I hold still under it and let it land. Nothing in me has made a sound land like that since before the hold. I had forgotten the air could carry a thing toward a body that it did not mean to close on.

---

She comes back at the next low. And the one after.

I learn her the way I learn weather, the way I learn ice — by watching the same thing happen enough times that the shape of it comes clear. She comes when the tide pulls back and bares the spit, the slick black road between her shore and mine, and never when the water is high, and never in the dark. That is a limit she keeps and keeps hard. She will not be caught on my black with the dark coming and the tide turning to chew the spit out from under her. She reads the water the way I read it. I respect that limit the way I respect the grey rot in the floe, because it is the same kind of knowing — the knowing that the cold does not forgive a body that lingers.

She leaves a thing each low. A hide, once, scraped soft and stitched down one long edge with sinew in small even bites, each stitch the same length as the last, a patience in it I can feel with my eyes. A sliver of bone, ground to a point and an eye bored through the fat end of it — a thing a hand made on purpose, by working the bone against stone a long time until it became what the hand wanted. I carry it to the crack and I put it on the shelf of wrong things, beside the stone that should be cold and stays warm, beside the things the world does not do by itself. The world does not bore an eye through bone. A hand does. And a hand at my line is the slavers, is the hold, is the closing — except this hand shakes when it lays the gift, this hand stitches small even bites into a hide for no reason the belly can name, and the shelf of wrong things is the only place in my whole black that has ever held something a hand made without wanting to close.

And each time, she talks.

---

She talks to the empty air, and the empty air is me.

She thinks she is feeding a ghost, naming the world to a shape from the fires, and so she talks easy, the way you talk to a thing that cannot talk back. She points at the grey weed in the tide-wrack and makes a sound — *slik* — short, soft at the front, the tongue tapping it out. She points at the purple weed, the deep wine-dark fronds, and makes another. The round black snails she pries off the low rocks and eats raw, another sound for those, round in the mouth like the snail is round. She walks my border and does not cross it, never crosses it, and names the going-out of the water and the coming-in of it with two sounds that lean against each other — one low and settling, one rising at the tail. She points across the wide grey water at the far smoke, the up-coast smoke, the strange packs whose fires I have only ever smelled on a turned wind, and the sounds she makes there change. They go sharp. They go careful, set down one at a time like she is laying stones across bad ice, like the naming itself has teeth in it and she does not want to be bitten.

I take every sound into me. I hold them behind the gone teeth, in the gap where three were torn, the warm hollow where I keep things. They stack up in the dark of me — *slik*, the water-out sound, the water-in sound, the round snail-sound, the careful sharp far-smoke sounds — and they sit there, and something turns over. Slow. Like a stone too big for the small tides, that lies in the wrack moon on moon, until one high water finally gets under it and rolls it, and the wet underside comes up to the air for the first time in a long time, pale and strange and alive with the small things that lived in the dark of it. Something in me like that. I had sounds once. This is the thing the turning stone shows me. Before the black, before the hold, before the deck and the closing hand, there was a mouth that made sounds at me, low and soft and falling, and I made them back, and the making-back meant we were two and not alone. I do not have that mouth anymore. I lost it. I lost the sound it gave me, the one that was mine, that lived behind my teeth where the warm hollow is — I have reached for it low after low and found only the warm and nothing to send. But I have hers now. Hers, sent into the air for a ghost, and I am the ghost, and I am stealing them one low at a time, building back a thing I lost the shape of, with another body's names.

I never answer. The beast keeps my throat shut and I let it keep it; a sound from the rocks would turn her face the wrong color, would set her feet to the running at last. So I stay shut. But I am not empty up there in the stone. I am full to the back of the teeth with her words. And the second mouth, the one no fish has ever filled, the one that has only ever widened at every saving and every gift and every laid-down kindness — it takes the words too. And for the first time, taking them, it does not only widen. It holds something. The words go into it and do not fall straight through. Not enough. Never enough. But the empty closes a little around them, the way a hand closes on a thing it means to keep and not on a thing it means to crush, and that is new, and I lie in the rocks and feel it be new.

---

One low she does not bring food.

She comes empty-handed across the bared spit and she does not lay anything down. She sits. She comes to just short of my line and folds down onto the cold black, pulls her knees up against her chest, wraps her arms around them, and stays. And she talks longer than she has ever talked, and her voice does a thing it has not done before.

It cracks.

It goes wet in the middle of a sound and the sound breaks in half. She is looking out at the water, not even pretending the ghost now. The words come fast and low, broken over each other, the sharp-careful up-coast sound in them, over and over, the far-smoke sound, the one with teeth. Threaded all through it is another sound I do not have yet but will come to know, a sound that means *gone*, that means a thing that was and is not, and her shoulders climb up around her ears as she makes it, the way a body folds itself smaller around a hurt to keep it from spilling out.

I understand none of the words. I have all of the shape. I have lived inside that shape; I know it the way I know the hare-stillness from the inside, having been the hare. She is not feeding a ghost for luck. She has come here from her own fires against something — one body breaking loose from the warm of them onto the cold edge where the others will not follow. She is the one who breaks away. There is a hand at her back that does not want her crossing to the black, and she crosses anyway, low after low. She is alone in her own pack, alone in the middle of the warm and the fed and the close, alone the way I knew the tide long before I could name a single one of its parts — in the body, in the bone, before the word.

We are two things alone on two ends of the water. And only I know it, because only I can see us both — her on the cold black with her shoulders up and her voice broken, and me in the rocks above her, full of her stolen words, holding still so I do not lose her. She has half of it. I have all of it, and I cannot give her the half she is missing, because to give it I would have to make a sound, and the sound would end us.

---

I almost make a sound anyway. It comes up my throat toward her — wanting to land on her the way her soft falling sound landed on me, wanting to be the answer that tells her she is not the only one alone on the water.

And there is nothing there to send.

That is the cost, and I feel it open under me like the floe going thin. I reach back into the gap where three were torn, into the warm hollow that should hold the one sound that is mine — the sound the mouth-before-the-hold gave me, soft and low and falling, the sound that meant *me* and not *property*, that was given and not branded — and the hollow is warm and empty. I lost it. On the island that unmade me low by low, I tried once to make it with my own mouth and it came out animal, a thing with no shape, and then it was not even behind my teeth to try again. Only the warm. Only the place where it used to lie.

There is one sound still in there, and it is not mine. The ugly one. The slaver-sound, the low thing they made behind their teeth in the hold that closed like a hand, that meant *mine, be still, come, you are a thing* — that one stayed. The brand stays. I never wanted it and I cannot get rid of it; it is lodged in the warm hollow like a barb in flesh, the one sound for me that did not leave when the true one left. And I will not send that to her. I would rather send nothing across the water than send her the shape of a hand closing. So I send nothing. The beast locks my throat and for once I am glad of the lock, because the only sound I have to give is the one I would never give.

It cost me to keep silent. It cost me something I did not know I still had to spend — the knowing, sharp and bodily, that I have lost the thing I would have given her, and that the only thing left where it used to be is the thing she must never have.

---

She leaves the hide at my border and goes before the tide turns.

The stitched one. The soft one, scraped pale and worked supple, the seam running straight down its edge in the small even bites. She does not set it squared and fat-up like a gift for a dead thing. She drapes it over the flat border-stone, open, spread wide, the seam laid bare to the grey sky, pulled tight as gull-skin over the stone — the way you lay a hide for a body to lie down on. For a body. Not an offering. A bed. Then she stands and looks one last time at the empty rocks where I am not, where I am, and she makes the soft sound again, the one that falls and does not finish, and she turns and crosses back over the spit with the water already come up to chew at her heels.

I wait until she is small on the far black, a dark upright thing dwindling toward the warm. I wait until she is gone off the spit and onto her own shore. I wait until she is smoke-smell only, a thread of her on the turned wind.

Then I go down to my line.

I have never picked up a thing while it was still warm from a hand. The flipper was tide-cold when I cracked it. The fish were cold. Everything has come to me cold and squared and left, gone-cold from the hand that set it down. This is warm. Her warmth is still in the fur where her arms held it against her chest crossing the spit, and the smell of her is worked all through it — her sweat, the fire-smoke off her fires, the seal-oil from her hands. And when I lift it, the wrongness stands up off me to meet it. My own heat rising the way it rises off the stone in the deep cold, the close skin of warmth that stands the frost back a hand's width and will not let it down onto me — it rises now into the warmth she left in the hide. They reach for each other, hers and mine. For one breath they are one warmth; I cannot find the seam between them. I hold her heat and my own wrongness in my two hands and they are the same temperature, the same close living warm, and the second mouth opens so wide I have to stand still on my line and breathe through it before I can move.

My hand is on the fur. The old white mark sits across the knuckles, where the bone-scrape closed in days when it should have taken a moon — the wrong thing that lives in my skin, the healing that comes too fast, the mark pale and strange against the dark. I would not have let her see it. I would not have let her see any of it, the warmth or the frost-ring or the breath that makes no ghost. But there is nothing on me now that she has ever been close enough to flinch from. She has only ever had the empty rocks and the shape from the fires. She has never had the hand.

It is the first thing another hand has made that I have taken into my own hands since the hold closed on my neck. I carry it up the broken country to the crack, the soft warm weight of it against my chest. And I do not put it on the shelf of wrong things, beside the warm stone, beside the bored bone.

I put it where I sleep.

And in the night I find my own back has learned the shape of her even seams in the dark, the small ridges of the sinew bites pressed along my spine, and that I have brought the sound into the crack with me — the soft one, the falling one, the one that does not close like a hand. I am holding it behind my teeth, in the warm hollow, in the gap where three were torn. It lies down in there next to the only other sound that lives there, the ugly one, the brand, the slaver-sound I would not give back and could never be rid of —

and there are two sounds behind my teeth now, and I do not know yet that the soft one will wake a third, the one that is mine, the one I lost; I do not know yet that I will be given it back across this same water, in this same mouth, and that on the day they come with the net I will hold it whole behind my teeth while they brand me again with the other.
