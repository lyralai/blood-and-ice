# Chapter 13: The Girl

She does not run.

That is the first wrong thing about her, and it is a new wrong, not one of mine. The others ran, always, the small ones and the big ones both — saw the shape and went the other way fast, water-splash, scree-clatter, gone. This one comes across the spit at low tide with the black showing slick under her feet, and she comes slow, and she comes into mine.

I am in the rocks above the dry watercourse. I have my border under me, the line I bleed on, and she walks up to it and stops with her toes at the edge of it like she knows. Like someone told her where my skin ends. She is bigger than the saved ones. Older. Near my own count of lows, maybe one more. Her hair is bound back in fat ropes the color of wet kelp, and there is a smell off her of woodsmoke and seal-oil and, under that, the smell of a body that eats well, warm, alive, and the second mouth opens behind my breastbone just to take it in.

I do not move. The beast wants me flat. I go flat.

---

She sets a thing down on my line and steps back.

Dried fish, three of them, laced on a gut-string. She lays them squared the way the others squared the flipper, fat-side up, faced, careful. But her hands are not careful the way the others' hands were careful — not the slow squaring you give a dead thing. Her hands shake. Small. I see it from the rocks, the tremor in the fingers as she sets the fish, and I know that shake, I have it in my own hands at the worst lows. It is not cold. It is the body knowing it should not be here.

And she came anyway. She does not run. Two things at once, the way the saved ones never were. The shake says go and the feet stay.

She straightens. She looks at the empty rocks — not at me, she does not have me, the black hides me — and she opens her mouth, and a sound comes out, aimed at the air where she thinks the ghost is.

It is soft, and it is not the slaver-sound: it does not close like a hand, it stays open at the end and falls and keeps falling, low against my chest where the breath sits. I hold still and let it land on me.

---

She comes back at the next low. And the one after.

I learn her the way I learn weather. She comes when the tide pulls back and bares the spit, never when it is high, never in the dark — she will not be caught on my black in the dark, that is a limit she keeps, and I respect it the way I respect the rotten ice. She leaves a thing each time. A hide, once, scraped soft and stitched down one edge with sinew in small even bites. A sliver of bone, ground to a point, an eye bored through the fat end — a hand made it to do a thing, and I put it on the shelf of wrong things in my crack because it is a thing the world does not do by itself.

And each time, she talks.

---

She talks to the empty air, and the empty air is me.

She does not know it is me. She points at the gray weed in the tide-wrack and makes a sound — slik — and points at the purple weed and makes another, and the round black snails she pries and eats raw, another. She walks my border, not over it, never over it, and she names the going-out of the water and the coming-in with two different sounds, low and rising. She points across the wide water at the far smoke, the up-coast smoke, the strange packs, and the sounds she makes go sharp and careful there, like she is naming things with teeth.

I take every sound into me. I hold them behind the gone teeth with the other one I keep there. They stack up in the dark of me, slik, the water-out sound, the water-in sound, and something in me that has been shut a long time begins to turn over, like a stone the tide finally moves. I had sounds once. Before the black, before the hold, there was a mouth that made them at me and I made them back. I do not have that mouth anymore. I have hers, sent into the air, and I am stealing them one low at a time.

I never answer. The beast keeps my throat shut and I let it. But I am not empty up there in the rocks. I am full to bursting with her words, and the second mouth, that no fish ever fills, takes them too, and for the first time it does not only widen. It holds something. Not enough. Something.

---

One low she does not bring food.

She comes empty-handed and sits down on the cold black just short of my line and pulls her knees up and stays there. And she talks longer than she has ever talked, and her voice does a thing it has not done. It cracks. It goes wet. She is looking out at the water and the words come fast and low and broken, and there is the sharp-careful up-coast sound in them again, over and over, and a sound that I will come to know means gone, and her shoulders go up around her ears.

I understand none of the words. I have all of the shape. She is not feeding a ghost for luck. She has come here from her own fires, against something, defying some hand that does not want her crossing to the black — I have felt that, the way the far fires pull close and then a body breaks from them and comes alone where the others will not. She is the one who breaks away. She is alone too, in her own pack, alone the way I know the tide before I can name its parts.

We are two things alone on two ends of the water, and only I know it, because only I can see us both.

I almost make a sound. It comes up my throat to her, the held thing, the one behind my teeth. Rook. The slaver-sound, the one they made at me in the hold, the one I took off them and would not give back. I do not let it out. If she has me, the slavers can have me. To give the name away is the hold closing on my neck again, the throat going shut under a hand — I keep it, and my throat keeps it for me, locked the way the beast locks it. But it cost me to keep it. It cost me something I did not know I still had to spend.

---

She leaves the hide at my border and goes before the tide turns.

The stitched one, the soft one. She does not set it squared and fat-up like an offering to a dead thing. She drapes it over the flat border-stone, open, spread, the seam laid bare, tight as gull-skin, the way you lay a skin for a body to lie on. Then she stands and looks one last time at the empty rocks where I am not, and makes the soft sound again, the one that falls and does not finish, and she crosses back over the spit with the water already chewing at her heels.

I wait until she is small on the far black. I wait until she is smoke-smell only.

Then I go down to my line.

I have never picked up a thing while it was still warm from a hand. The flipper was tide-cold, the fish were cold, everything has been cold and squared and left. This is warm. Her warmth is in the hide where her arms held it, the smell of her worked deep into the fur — her sweat, the fire-smoke off her fires, the seal-oil from her hands — and when I lift it the wrongness stands up off me to meet it, my own heat rising the way it rises off the stone — and for one breath the two warmths are one warmth and I cannot tell the made thing from myself.

My hand is on the fur, the old white mark across the knuckles where the bone-scrape closed in days when it should have taken a moon — I would not have let her see it, but there is nothing on me she has ever been close enough to flinch from.

It is the first thing another hand has made that I have taken into my hands since the hold closed on my neck. I carry it to the crack. I do not put it on the shelf of wrong things.

I put it where I sleep. And in the night I find that my own back has learned the shape of her even seams in the dark, and that I have brought the sound into the crack with me, the soft one that does not close like a hand, and I am holding it in the gone-tooth gap next to the slaver-sound, next to the name they made and I would not return —

and there are two sounds behind my teeth now, and I do not know yet that I will trade one for the other.
