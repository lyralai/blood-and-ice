# Chapter 8: Taken In

The rot-thicket takes everything that lies down in it. It took the dead men in the wreck, who lay down in the green and let it have them and never got up. It comes for me the same, the same patient mouth opening slow.

It does not take me.

---

Six lows I count the young one down to the channel and back, the swamp pulling off the same stones on the same string as the tide, the young one coming down to glean and climbing back to the high fire — and on the seventh the green decides to kill me. It decides to kill everything, sooner or later. I have learned that letting it decide is how I live. The thing that fights the green dies fighting. The thing that lies still under it sometimes wakes whole. So I let it.

There is a still pool in the deep rot under the fallen giant. Scum-skinned, brown, the water that lies where the green has died and gone to soup, and it breathes its fever-air up slow and thick all night against my back where I curl into the seam worn to my shape. I have slept against worse. But this low I wake with the throat shut and the head gone to wet stone, the gut turning, the legs gone to rope — the shaking-sickness on me at last, the heat rising off my own skin, the bad air worked all the way in. The death that lays everything down in the green. It has come for me as it comes for all the rest.

I have burned before. Once after the clear-glass fish. Once after a thing struck me in the dark and the leg went black and hot to the hip. Each time the green leaned in close and opened its mouth on me, and each time I woke whole — the black going out of the leg, the heat going out of the head, fast, faster than a thing should mend, faster than the dead men mended, who did not mend at all.

I have wanted to know why. I have lain in the dark and pressed at it like a print I cannot read, turning the why over until the head goes raw — *why me, why not them, what is in me that the green cannot eat* — and there is never a bottom to it, only the buzzing where the answer should be, and no word in me to lay against it. So I do the only thing the question lets me do. I set it down. I lie still and I let the green try.

I drink from the rainwater caught in the great cupped leaf I keep wedged in the roots, and I wait for the wrongness in me to win the way it always wins.

I do not know they are watching.

---

The young one finds me on the second burning low.

I am out of the hollow by then. The fever drives me out — it always does, the body wanting open air and running water when the heat is on it — and I crawl down through my own green to the channel-edge where the swamp pulls back and bares its stones, to lie in the wet mud and drink and shake. Too sick to keep my shape. Too sick to be nothing. A man-shape laid plain on the bared stones in the first light, burning, and I do not care, because the green has decided, and a thing the green has decided does not waste its last strength hiding.

And the young one comes down to glean, and there I am on its stones.

It stops a long way off. I feel it stop the way I feel a herd turn — the green changing shape around a will in it. I lift the head off the mud. Across the bared stones it stands, big-armed, young, the dark hide slick with the wet, and it looks at me with the small head tipped, and it does not come and it does not go. The lip wants to peel off my teeth. The old low thing wants to start in my chest — *hands, hold, the weight on the neck* — but the fever has burned it thin, and all I can do is lie in the mud and look back, two things on the same stones, neither one moving.

Then it makes a sound at me.

Low. Up and down. No edges in it. The gentling sound — the one I have heard a big one lay on a small one, soft, when they scoop the small one up off the killing ground against the warm. It makes that sound at me, at the man-shape burning in the mud, and then it turns and goes quick and crooked back up the green toward the high stones, the small-one's gait, and I think: *it has gone to bring the many.*

The fast part of me, what is left of it, says crawl. Says go to ground, go deep, die in the dark where the hands cannot close on you. I get the knees under me. The green tips and swings. I get one hand to a root and the root is far away at the end of a long burning arm, and I go down again in the mud, and the last clear thing I have is the thought the body has carried since the hold, flat and certain: *better the green than the hand. Better eaten than owned.*

Then the dark comes down loud, and I am gone into the fever, down under the brown water of myself where the buzzing lives.

---

I wake against a warm that is not the rot.

This is the thing I cannot make sit right, after, when I turn it over and over and cannot wear it smooth. The rot is warm. The stone holds my back warm. The coal in its fist of fiber breathes its warm. I know every warm in my green, and I made my peace long ago with each one being a dead warm, a thing-warm, a warm that does not know it holds me.

This warm knows.

It moves. It breathes — a great slow tide of breath, in and out, under me and around me — and it has a smell, rank and close and living, matted hide and old meat and a deep musk under it, and there is a heartbeat in it, vast and slow, going against my ear where my ear is laid to it. I am held. I am folded in against a great living thing the way the small ones are folded in, scooped up off the killing ground, and the second mouth — the far one, the one no meat answers, the one that has leaned and leaned across all that green and found nothing to lean on —

it goes still.

It has never gone still. Not once in all the dark on this island. It is the hole under the breastbone that the kill cannot fill and the water cannot fill, the wanting-to-be-known that eats me from the inside while the green eats me from the out. And here, with the great slow heart going through me and the great arm folded over me, it lies down in the dark under my ribs and is quiet, and the quiet is so strange and so deep that the body shakes harder than the fever ever shook it, and water comes up out of my eyes that is not sweat, and I have no word for the thing happening to me. I only have the shape of the hole, going full.

I should run. Every fast part of me, when it climbs back up through the fever, says *hands,* says *hold,* says the many take the one and unmake it. The arm is huge. It could close. It could break me as I break a thing's neck without thought. I am held as a thing is held that cannot get away.

But it is not the closing. I know the closing. I wore the closing on the back of the neck through a whole dark sea — the slow sure grip that owned the whole of me, and the ugly sound that came with it, the brand, the hold-word made behind the teeth that meant *mine, be still, come, you are a thing.* I have carried that hand my whole life. I know its weight to the ounce.

This is not that hand.

This hand holds the small one. Not to own. To keep. The arm is folded over me to keep the rot off, to keep the warm in, to keep the burning shape from crawling back to die in the bad water — and the great head bends down over me in the dark and makes the sound, low, up and down, no edges, *the gentling sound,* over and over against the top of my head. And the body knows the difference before the head can find it. The body wore the other hand. The body knows this one is not it.

I do not run. I am too sick to run, and I do not want to run, and the not-wanting is a stranger wrongness than any I have ever set down, and I do not set this one anywhere. I lie in it. I burn against the warm, and the warm holds, and somewhere in the burning I understand that I have been carried — up out of my green, up the bare rock, up to the high stones where the kept fire holds. That I am in among the many now, the very thing I fled. And that I am not dead.

---

I learn her in pieces, the way the fever lets me.

She is big even among the big. Heavy through the chest, long in the arm, the dark hide gone gray-silver at the muzzle and along the great ridged back, the gray of a thing that has carried many lows. She is past her strength, and she is not near the king; she sits to the side of the high stones with the still ones, the laid-out dead, and tends them. She is the one who keeps. I will learn the word the tribe lays on her later, when the words come, when I steal them off the others' tongues one low at a time. But the first thing I have of her is the warm and the slow heart and the gentling sound, and the second thing I have is this:

she lost one.

I read it in pieces, over the slow lows of mending. On the cleanest high shelf, up where the sun strikes first, among the squared and faced dead, there is a small one. Small as the ones that ride the backs are small. Laid out faced like the rest, with the same care all down its little length, and she goes to it. She crouches by it and lays a hand on it and takes the hand away and lays it back, the way I saw a big one do across all that green before I had a word for any of it, and she makes a sound over it that is not the gentling sound — lower, with a tear in it, a thing that comes up out of the great chest and goes nowhere, that the high stones swallow.

A small one that does not ride her back anymore. A warm she made and tended that went out.

And then she comes back down off the high shelf to where they have laid me, in the warm dark under the lean of the great trees against the rock, and she folds the great arm over me and makes the gentling sound, the keeping one, and I understand the thing the body understood before the head, as it always does. There was a place in her shaped to hold a small thing against the killing ground. The green took the thing that filled it. And out of all the green she could have leaned her want toward, she leaned it toward the burning man-shape on the channel-stones that the green tried to kill and could not.

The far mouth in me has a hole in the exact shape of a thing taken. So does she. We lie down in each other's hole, the two of us, and neither one of us has a word for it, and we do not need one.

---

There is a thing they saw that I did not know they saw.

It comes to me slow, over the mending lows, in how the others move around me. The young one that found me. The big ones that pass and look. They do not look at me as a thing looks at meat. They do not look as the many look at the one before they sweep it. They look at a thing that should not be.

And on a high morning when the fever has gone out of me at last — gone fast, gone clean, the head come back to itself, the gut quiet, the legs under me again, days before a thing should mend and the dead men never mending at all — one of them comes to me and crouches and does not touch me, and makes a long sound at the others, and the heads come up, all of them, and tip toward me. And it goes down through me sudden and bright, what they witnessed.

They watched the green try to kill me. They carried up a burning man-shape laid out plain on the channel-stones, the shaking-sickness thick on it, the fever-air worked all the way in — the death that lays everything down, the death that put no mark on the dead men in the wreck — and they laid it in the warm to go out. The way the small one went out. The way they have watched it take their own, low after low, since before any of them was big. And it did not take me. I climbed back up out of the brown water of myself whole and fast, the heat going out of me and leaving no mark, while every other thing the green decides to kill stays decided.

This is the wrong thing in me. The buzzing thing I cannot read. I did it where the many could see.

And the many do not read it as I read it. I read it as one more wrong thing I cannot get to the bottom of. They read it as a sign. A thing the green could not eat. The longest tooth on the whole low country, carried up burning to die, and not dying — and the heads tip toward it, and the line bends around it, the small wrong way the line bends around a thing that means something the tribe has no word for either. Only the hole where the word would go.

---

The old one comes last.

It is the oldest of them I have seen, grayer than the keeper, the great body sunk in on itself, the eyes gone milk-pale at the edges but still moving, still reading. It comes to where I lie healing and it crouches a long while and it reads me as I read the green — slow, taking the whole shape of a thing before it goes down into it. And then it does a thing that turns the gut over hard.

It reaches one slow gray finger and lays it, not on me, on the thing at my chest.

The disc. The hard worked thing from the wreck, the one with the mark on it — the open eye, ringed, looking, and through the eye, crossing it, driven into it, the long curved barb, the thorn laid through the looking so the eye looks out around the thing that pierces it. I have carried it bound at my chest since the broken belly of the dead ship, because the body would not set it down, because there is a buzzing low in the drowned place of me when I hold it, a knowing with no word in it — *I know this, this is mine* — and no when, and no whose.

The old one lays its gray finger on the eye and the thorn, and it goes very still. And then it makes a sound — low, and long, and with a thing in it I have not heard from any of them, a thing like the tear over the dead small one but turned the other way, turned toward, turned up. It looks from the mark to my face and back to the mark, and it knows the mark. I see it know the mark.

And I lean in. I cannot help it. The whole of me leans toward the gray old thing the way a starving thing leans toward smoke, because it has what I have wanted my whole dark life — it knows. It knows the mark. It carries the answer to the buzzing in its milk-pale eyes, and I am inside a single arm's reach of it, and I strain at it. I push the head down into the drowned place of me where the buzzing lifts and tries the brown water — *tell me, old one, what is the eye, whose is the thorn, what am I* — and the brown water does not break. The buzzing lifts and falls back. There are no words in me to ask with, and none in it to answer, and the knowing sits in the gray head a hand's breadth from mine and will not cross. I am closer to it than I have ever been to my own name, and it might as well be across the whole green sea.

The old one draws its finger back and bows the great gray head, slow, the way the keeper bowed hers over the small one, and makes the sound again, and the others draw back from me a half-step, all of them — drawing back from a thing they did not understand and now half-do.

I am left with the deep slow shock of *known.* The disc means something here. To them. The eye-and-thorn means a thing the old gray one carries in its blood the way I carry the empty gap behind my teeth, and it has read it on me, and it has bowed its head. And I cannot get one finger's width closer to what it is. I lie down with the wanting still in me, hot and unanswered. I do not set it down. I have not finished reaching for it.

---

The keeper comes for me at the dark.

The kept fire is up on the high stone, red and low and fed, the way it has been every dark I counted from across the green, the held light in all the black that a thing makes and tends and does not let go out. And I am up among it now. I am inside the warm I leaned toward across all that drowning green with nothing to lean on. The small ones run between the legs of the big ones, and one of them — dark and quick, no fear in it at all — comes and looks at me and goes away again. The heads are tipped one way. The king sits high on the bare stone and has looked at me once, long, and looked away. And I am inside it.

The keeper folds down beside me in the firelight, the great gray-silver bulk of her, and lays the arm over me, and makes the gentling sound, the keeping one. And I lie against the warm that knows it holds me, with the slow vast heart going through my ear, inside the high stones, inside the many, the very thing I fled.

And I do the old thing. I cannot help it. I go in behind the teeth, into the gap where three are torn out, the dark warm hollow where the name my mother set on me used to live, before the ship, before the hands — to find a sound to set against this, to send back into the warm the one thing that means *me.* Not the brand. Not the thing they made of me. The true thing, mine — *this is who is held.* I go in to find it.

And the gap is empty, the way it is always empty. Warm, dark, shaped like nothing. The water lies down full in the print the name left and is nothing like the name.

But for the first time, behind the teeth, in the empty place, I do not stop. The keeper makes her sound over me, low, up and down, no edges, and I take it in through the ear and down into the chest where the wanting lives, and I lay it against the gap where the name used to be — not the name, not mine, but a sound, a true one, a sound that came down out of a warm that chose to hold me — and the gap takes it. The way the rot takes a thing laid down in it. The way the warm takes me.

I have no name to send. But I have a sound to keep now, laid in the place the name was torn from, and a warm to keep it for. And the far mouth, full at last, does the only thing it has ever known how to do with a thing it cannot hold.

It begins to count the lights it has, instead of the lights between.
