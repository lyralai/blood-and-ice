# Chapter 23: The Two Hungers at War

I have two mouths, and for the first time in my life they hunger in two directions.

---

I learn it on the spit, low after low, the body knowing it before the head will hold it. I am two roads now. Each low I walk both. Each low they pull further apart.

There is the up road. At the bare of the dark I go up off the bone-spit into my green, up the slick rot and the broken stone, to the high shelves where the Kala lies vast and slow with the small ones heaped warm against her and the home with no between in it waits. I lie down in the kept warm with my ear to the great tide of her. The near hunger is fed there. The far hunger was full there once, full all the way down. The body knows that road like the soles know the runs — it is mine, the only crown I have ever worn.

And there is the down road. At the heavy of the next low, when the owning-fire goes thick with grease, I pour myself down off the high stones, down the rot I just climbed, down to the drowned roots over the spit, and lie in the mud and watch the upright pack at its fires. The down road is new — a handful of lows old against a whole life of the up. But the second mouth leans down it now and will not come up, because at the bottom of it there is the man-shaped hole, the warm of fires that are not mine, and the owned girl naming the world one stone at a time to the dark she thinks cannot answer.

Two roads. One body. The body cannot lie on both ends of the spit at once.

---

Marrow lies down the down road with me, and he hates it.

He comes because I go. That is the whole of it. He folds his small wrong body into the roots a little above mine, where the run is good if a thing has to leave fast, and stays the whole long watch. The high shoulder rides up and does not drop. The curled hand works at the mud, opening, closing, opening — the thing it does when the green has laid a wrong smell on the wind and the head has not yet found it.

He will not look down the spit at the fires. That is how I know how bad it is. Marrow looks at everything; it is what he is for, the quick eye that reads the run while I read the kill. But he will not turn his face to the owning-camp. He looks at me instead, the long sidelong look, and he goes still — his only big word — and the still says what his hands cannot: *that is not your warm. That is the thing that grinds the bone and keeps the marrow. Come up.*

He does not say it. There are no sounds in him for it and none in me to answer. But I have read Marrow's body since the low I gave him a name, and I read this whole: he is the one true thing the green ever made that loves me, and he has followed me down into the lit mouth of a trap he can smell and cannot stop. That is the worst of his loyalty. He cannot turn me. So he comes down to lie in the same roots, watching the king he made lean toward the hand.

---

The down road has changed since the keeper read my mark.

I read the change off the camp like a turn in the weather. Before, it leaned only at its work — the hauling, the cutting, the dragging and lashing of the cages. Now it leans up the spit. At the green. At me. The captain has set watchers where the spit comes in, bodies posted to read the dark for the thing that wears the Eye-and-Thorn. The fires burn longer. The owning ones go nowhere without the iron near the hand. They are looking out for me.

But the camp does more than watch. It calls, low after low, since the keeper first set the warm thing at my line. A thing goes out from the fires every low, aimed at the green — not the closing brand-sound, not the *come* that means *mine, be still, you are a thing.* The other thing. Meat set out warm at my line and left, bright kept-water beside it, welcome laid out in the only road the camp has to lay it. They make the fires bright and let the grease-smoke roll up the spit into my green, and on the smoke comes the smell of the upright pack at its ease — fed and dry and many and warm. The smell of the warm I lost.

The keeper baits me still. He cannot find my shape in the dark, so he lays out the thing the want wants and waits for it to walk the body down onto the open ground where the iron can reach. He does not know the dull-water folded in the welcome slides off me low after low. He is wrong about the body, right about the want.

And here is the thing I cannot file away and be done with: I know it is bait, and I lean at it anyway. I want to lay the knowing down on the shelf where the other torn things lie and walk away from it clean. I cannot. The wanting will not let the knowing settle. They lie side by side in me, both true.

---

I have read bait all my life. It is the first law of the green — the still pool too clear, the easy meat laid too plain, the trail that runs too good toward the place the ground will not hold. *A thing the world lays out easy is a thing the world means to close on.* I read the captain's bait whole, and the green part of me, the part that made itself a king, says the law plain behind my teeth: *do not go down. Take the tribe deep, into the fever-fog and the high broken stone where men die and you do not, where no boat reaches and no brand burns.*

The legs say it. The soles say it, dug into the mud. The whole green I bled into being says it. Marrow's still body says it loudest of all.

And I do not go up.

I lie in the streaming roots and I do not go up. The want holds me down by force, and I understand at last — the body first, the head long after — that the two hungers were one road my whole life. The near and the far wanted the same thing. To live. To be fed. To be kept. The green gave both at the high stones, the belly and the belonging in the one warm. But the keeper has cut them apart. He has laid the far hunger's exact food down where the near one's death is. And now they pull against each other inside the one body — one hauling up the green toward life, one hauling down toward the warm. The rope between them is me, and it is fraying.

This is the war. Not the iron and the green. The war is in the breastbone, and there is no eating that ends it.

---

She comes down still.

That is the thing that holds me past all the law: the keeper shut the road — kept her behind his fires, set the welcome out in his own hand — and still she comes. She slips the thornwood at the bare of the low, comes down the killing spit under the watcher's eye, stands at my line, and lays me the road one stone at a time.

She knows now that she is watched. I read it in her — the strung shape, the looking-back, the carrying-hand kept low against the mud where the watcher at the stumps cannot see it. The keeper does not haul her back. He has read that the dark moves for her and not for him, so he lets her go, the way a man lets the hook down with the bait on it, to see what rises. She knows it. She comes anyway. And while she makes her easy talking-to-the-dark sounds for the watcher to hear — the weed, the snail, the two leaning water-sounds — her low hand against the wet mud lays me the other road. The hand-road, the one I have read all my life when the mouth-road was shut to me. The eye and the thorn. The flying-apart. The closing hand pointed back at the camp. *The hand is coming. The mark has set them moving.*

She is the mirror, and I will not lie to myself about which she is. She is not the Kala, not the home with no between. She is the owned thing on the killing ground, the road-carrier with the lifting hand over her bent neck — a thing I look in and see my own neck. And a man can look in a mirror and not be looking at a home. I know which is the home. The home is up the green with the small ones heaped against the Kala's arm.

But the want does not know mirror from home. It only knows that no thing has come down onto my killing ground under a watcher's eye to warn me since the Kala — and it leans at her, hard. Every low I go down to take her warning is a low the watcher reads the green a little better, a low the keeper's hook sinks a little deeper, a low the brand walks a step nearer toward the warm hollow where my name lies.

Marrow watches her from the roots above, and he does not trust her. The curled hand works the whole time she is at my line. To him she is a thing of the down road, and the down road is the thing taking me, and so he weighs her like a snare half-buried — a thing that might be true and might be the bait of a truer thing behind it. I cannot tell him she is real. I am not sure I could read the difference if she were not. So we both watch her from the mud, the runt and the king — he watching for the snare, I leaning at the warm — the whole of the war in two bodies side by side.

---

The want is the door the brand walks through. I knew it the low I let her see the green move, and I keep standing in the door anyway. Only the man-shaped thing in me leans into the lit mouth of a trap, because there is a body inside it that has been hungry as I have been hungry, naming snails to the dark.

And there is a thing I have started to read off the camp, and I press at it because I cannot make it mean what it ought to mean.

The fires call me down. But the camp behind them is going wrong. Slow. The hauling is slower than it was. Bodies that do not come out from the lean-tos at all now, and bodies that come out and bend and do not straighten right. The captain drives them harder for it, and the failing fall further behind. I watch a big owning one go down at the cage-line. Just go down. The others do not run to him; they step wide of him, the way the tribe steps wide of a fevered thing. I press at the why, and it slides. It is the green doing it to them — the green I do not feel, that I walk through wet to the teeth and come out clean. Something in the wet is in them and not in me. I cannot turn the thought all the way to its name. But I file it true: the warm at the bottom of the down road is, low by low, beginning to die of the very green that keeps me alive. The hand reaching up the spit for me is a sickening hand. It does not yet know it. I only know the smoke smells of the warm I lost, and under the warm, faint, the smell of the rot-fevers I cannot catch.

---

I go up to the Kala wrong now.

That is how I know the war is real and not a thing the head made up to chew. I go up the green at the bare of the dark and lie down in the kept warm, and the Kala lifts her great gray head off the sleep and lays the small dark patient eye on me, and reads me as she did the first low, before there was a single sound between us. And what she reads now is the down road on me. The grease-smoke in my hair. The man-shape come back up the rot smelling of the fires of the upright pack.

She makes a sound low in the vast slow tide of her chest. Not the asking-sound, the rising *where, where.* A lower one, a settling one with a question folded under it — the sound she makes over a small one that has strayed too near the rot-thickets and come back smelling of the soft ground that does not hold. *Where do you go. What is the smell of the thing that is not us.* She lays the great arm over me as she has a thousand lows, the home with no between — and for the first low in all my counting there is a between. I put it there. I carried it up the green on my skin, and now it lies between her tide and my ear, thin and new and wrong.

I have never lied to the Kala. There were no words to lie with. And now I lie to her with my whole body every low, and she reads the lie plain, in the small patient eye, and has no sounds to ask me whole, and I have none to tell her. The between sits in her arm and grows.

This is the cost of the war. Not the iron yet. The cost so far is the between in the Kala's arm where there was never a between, and it is the worst thing I have paid since the hold, and I pay it every low for the leaning of the want at a fire not mine.

---

The seer reads it before the Kala can name it.

It comes up in the heavy of a low when the rains have thinned — the old gray one with the slow finger, keeper of the tribe's old signs, the one that bowed its head over the blood in my throat a hundred lows ago and named me *kept.* It crouches down where I lie in the Kala's between-arm and lays the gray finger to my chest. Not to the throat this time. To the hard flat thing on the gut-cord — the Eye-and-Thorn, the shape the dead men died wearing, the shape the keeper wears on his iron and the owned girl draws on the wet air.

The seer goes into the half-seeing stillness. Its old finger rests on the eye and the thorn, its breath goes long and even, and it makes the meaning-sound it made the first time, the low one for the kept and the come-from-before. Then it makes a new one under it. A wrong one. A leaning-away one. Its small old eyes come up off the relic and go down the green, down the rot toward the spit, toward the fires it cannot see and somehow reads off my skin — and the new sound is the one the tribe makes at the edge of the rot-thicket where the ground will not hold. The danger-sound. The *here, and no further.*

I want to know what the seer knows. I lean at it the same as I lean at the camp's sickening — I press, I reach, I try to turn the old one's two sounds into one shape I can hold — and it slides, because the seer has no more sounds for it than I do. It reads the eye and the thorn as kin-omen, a thing of the kept, of before. And it reads, off the smoke in my hair, that the kin-omen has a hand reaching for it up the spit — the thing from across the water that takes the kept and carries it off into a hold. It lays its gray finger on the mark that says *owned* and makes the danger-sound at the green below, and I understand it whole, the body first: *the thing you wear is reaching for you, and the reach comes from below.*

It knows what the Kala only feels — that the down road ends at the brand, that the want is walking me toward the one death the green could never deal me. It lays the finger down, bows the old head, and goes back into the steam, and leaves me with the danger-sound ringing under the eye and the thorn.

---

There is unrest up the green too, and it is not only mine.

I read it off the strong ones like weather — Voss-Marr's side, the prime-blood, the ones who never bent the knee easy to a wrong-blood king. They have caught the smell on the spit and seen the watch-fires burn long. And they have done the count the strong always do: the iron came hunting a thing that wears the Eye-and-Thorn, and the thing that wears it is the human king. The hand reaches for *me*, and the tribe is the green it will trample to reach. I feel them lean away from me at the gather. The looking-off. The drawing-tight of their own among their own. No one strikes. No one makes the challenge-noise. It is quieter and worse than that — the slow turning of a body's weight off a thing it has decided not to die for.

I file it true: when the iron comes up the spit, I do not know who climbs the green behind me. I am most of the way to knowing it will not be them.

---

I lie between the two roads in the full dark and I hold the things behind my teeth and I add no last one, because the last one has no shape yet — only a pull.

The name that is mine and answers nothing. The brand that means *you are a thing.* The road the owned girl lays me one stone at a time. The warning — the eye flying apart, the camp, the closing hand. The sickening warm at the bottom of the spit that I cannot turn all the way over to a name. And over all of it now the danger-sound the seer made, the *here, and no further,* aimed at the very road I will not stop walking. And beside me in the dark, Marrow, still as the green before the thing comes out of it, the high shoulder up, the curled hand working, watching me lean.

I am most human here, and I know it in the body. All the long climb up the green I was going the other way — going beast, going deep, paring the man down until there was barely a man left to take. And here I am more a man than I have been since the deck. The man is the soft thing. The man is the thing the keeper can take.

The two hungers are at war, and I am the ground they fight on, and I cannot feed both without losing one. Go up and stay up — take the tribe deep, never lay my shape where the weighing-look can cut it from the dark — and the near hunger lives while the far one starves at the door it will not leave. Go down the spit and keep going, and the hand closes on the back of my neck, and I am cargo, and the between in the Kala's arm becomes the whole of the distance: water and a hold and the dark.

I do not choose this low. The body will not let the head choose yet; it only lies between the two ends of the spit and bleeds toward both. But I feel the choosing coming like a tide turning, slow and sure and from below, and I feel which way the want leans. And I am afraid. Not of the iron. Not of the green. Afraid of nothing I can lay a finger to — afraid the way a thing is afraid that already knows it will walk down the lit road anyway, into the warm, into the closing hand.

I have been a king of the wild things. I have been a beast the green could not eat. And here at the bottom of my own crown, torn in two, I am only what I have always been — a thing the world keeps reaching for, leaning toward the one warm it should run from, with my own self holding the door for it. And the one body that would follow me through that door is the small wrong one beside me in the mud, who never wanted the down road and comes.
