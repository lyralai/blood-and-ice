# Chapter 1: Black Shore

Cold wakes me.

Not all at once. In pieces. The fingers first — wrong, stiff, far away, like they belong to some other boy's hands. Then the face. Wind on wet skin. Salt in the cracks of my lips, and under the salt, the iron taste of me.

I open my eyes.

Sky, gray and low and moving. Clouds run fast, like they are afraid of what is behind them.

I close my eyes. The cold comes for the rest of me, and takes its time.

---

Rock under me. Black rock. Not sand, not deck, not the straw of a cage. Rock broken and frozen and broken again until every edge is a blade. It bites through what I am wearing, through the skin, down to the bone.

I tell my left hand to close. It closes. I tell my right. Nothing. I lie there and ask it again, and at last it answers, one finger at a time, slow as a thing waking from the dead, and the pain of it is enormous, and I am glad of the pain, because the pain is mine.

My tongue finds the gaps in my teeth. Three. Two below, one at the side. The holes are fresh — blood and socket and a whistle of air where teeth used to be. I spit. Red on black.

I do not ask *where am I*. There is no use in it, and no word for the asking, not yet. The body knows this work. Hurt, cold, wrong, wake, move. Move or be meat.

I move.

---

The rock runs down to the sea. Gray-green water, heavy, thick with ice — not a sheet of it but pieces, floating, as if the sea has tried to freeze and lost its nerve.

I breathe out. My breath should smoke in air this cold. It doesn't. The air takes it and shows nothing, the way still water shows nothing of a stone dropped through it. I watch the place where the smoke should be and it isn't there, and somewhere under the hurt a small thing in me goes quiet and listens.

Then a wave breaks on the rock and the spray hits my face and I flinch — not from the cold, from the sound. The sound is the whole world, top to bottom, and none of it is for me. The water does not care. The rock does not care. The sky, least of all. I have been dropped here and the world will take me apart and never once look up from its work.

*Good*, I think — not in words, in the set of my jaw. *Let it try.*

---

Thirst comes before hunger.

I crawl toward the low places where water has gathered. The first pool is salt; it burns the gaps in my teeth and I spit it out. I follow the foot of the cliff, where the wind goes quiet and the world draws in close and small, and there I find it — a crack thin as a finger, weeping water, a thread of it running down into a hollow worn in the stone the size of my palm. Clear.

I drink.

The cold of it is a clean cold, a different thing than the cold outside. It draws a line down my throat and into my chest, and it tastes of rock and of nothing, and nothing has never tasted so good. I drink the hollow dry and wait for it to fill and drink it dry again. Four times.

Then I sit with my back to the black cliff and shake, and the shaking will not stop.

---

There is one thing the cold cannot reach.

When the slavers wanted me they made a sound, low, behind the teeth, and the sound meant *me* — meant *property*, meant *come here and shut your mouth*. I do not have their words. I have that one. I keep it the way I keep the gaps in my jaw: a small hard place where something used to be.

*Rook.*

It is not a name. It is the shape of a hand closing on the back of a neck. But it is mine now — the only thing I carried off that ship — and in the dark I hold it behind my teeth where they cannot reach it, and it is not warmth, and it is not enough, and it is something.

---

Night comes, and the cold turns cruel enough to make the daylight cold seem like a kindness I should have thanked.

I find a crack in the cliff. Not a cave — a split, a gap I can pull into sideways, rock at my chest and rock at my back, tight and dark. *Good.* The wind cannot reach in here to strip the heat off me. I fold my knees to my chest and put my face to the stone.

The stone is warm.

That is wrong. I know it is wrong the way the body knows things before the head can catch them. Black rock, frozen place, deep night — it should be cold enough to kill. It is warm. Not hot. Not kind. But warmer than the air by enough that I feel it, and the warmth soaks into my chest where the stone meets me, and for the first time since I woke, the shaking stops.

I do not ask why. I press into the warm dark and let it hold me, and I do not let go of the one word, and I sleep.

---

I dream in shards.

Hands. Big hands, rough, dragging me by the arm. A floor that moves — wet wood, the reek of tar and piss. Bars of rope and bone. A weight on my collarbone, cold, biting. A voice low above me in no words I own, and the voice means *down* and means *still* and means *you are not a person, you are a thing that cost money.*

The floor tilting. Water coming in. The hands letting go.

I wake.

---

Dawn is gray. The wind has dropped. I pull myself out of the crack and everything in me complains. I look at my leg for the first time: a gash down the outside of the thigh, long, crusted with dried blood and salt, the skin around it red and tight and hot.

I drink at the seep. I walk where the cliff lets me, and the cliff turns, and the shore opens, and I see the rest of it.

Black rock. Gray water. White ice. Nothing else. No smoke. No boats. No walls. No other breathing thing that walks on two legs.

I stand where the rock meets the sea, and the wind finds me again and leans its weight on me, and I understand the thing the body has been trying to tell me since I woke.

This is a different alone.

The cage was alone — but the cage had walls, other bodies close, the knowledge that hands would come. To hurt, but to come. This has no walls. This is the whole gray world with one boy in it and nothing in it that knows his one word, or would answer it if he spoke.

Frost has formed on the rock where I slept. A clean white furring of it, all around the hollow my body pressed — and inside that hollow, where I lay, none. Bare black stone in the shape of me. I look at it a moment. Then the wind cuts through and my leg is on fire and my mouth tastes of iron, and the moment lets me go.

I turn back toward the crack and the warm stone and the thread of water. I walk careful. The rock is sharp.

I am alive.

I don't know yet whether that is mercy, or only the long way around to dying.
