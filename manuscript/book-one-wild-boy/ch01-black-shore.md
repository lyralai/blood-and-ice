# Chapter 1: Black Shore

The cold woke him.

Not all at once. In pieces. First the fingers — they were wrong. Stiff and thick and far away, like they belonged to someone else. Then the face. Wind on wet skin. Salt. His lips were split and the salt found every crack and he tasted iron.

He opened his eyes.

Sky. Gray and low and moving. Clouds running fast like they were afraid of something behind them.

He closed his eyes.

The cold came for the rest of him.

---

He was on rock. Black rock. Not sand, not wood, not the floor of a cage. Rock that had been broken and frozen and broken again until the surface was all edges. It cut through whatever he was wearing — he could feel it through the cloth, through the skin, against the bone.

He tried to move his hands. The left one answered. The right one didn't. Not at first. He lay there and told the hand to close and the hand ignored him and then it closed, slow, one finger at a time, and the pain of it was enormous.

Something was wrong with his mouth. He ran his tongue across his teeth and found gaps. Three of them. Two on the bottom, one on the side. The holes were fresh — blood and socket and air where tooth used to be. He spat. Red on black rock.

He did not think *where am I.* He did not think anything with words. The body knew what to do. It had done this before. Hurt and cold and wrong and wake up and move. Move or die.

He moved.

---

The rock went down to the water. Black rock all broken and sharp and then the sea, gray-green, heavy, full of ice. Not solid ice — chunks. Pieces floating like the water was trying to freeze and couldn't quite commit.

The waves came in and broke against the rock and the spray hit his face and he flinched. Not from the cold. From the sound. The sound was enormous. It filled the whole world, top to bottom, and it didn't care about him.

He understood that. The water did not care. The rock did not care. The sky did not care. He was a thing that had been dropped here and the world would take him apart piece by piece and it would not notice.

He sat on the black rock and the wind pushed him and he bled from his mouth and his hands and somewhere on his leg that he hadn't looked at yet and the sea came in and went out.

---

Thirst came before hunger.

He had no word for it. His body knew — the throat, the dry, the need. He crawled along the rock toward where the water pooled in the low places. But the pools were salt. He tasted one and spat and the salt burned the gaps in his teeth and he pulled back.

He followed the rock. Crawled at first, then on hands and knees when the hands started working better, then upright against the wind because his knees hurt too much to bend. The rock face went up to his left — a cliff of black stone, wet, streaked with white. Above the streaks, ice.

He walked along the bottom of the cliff. The wind was quieter here. The rock made a wall and the world got smaller.

He found the seep.

A crack in the cliff face, thin as a finger, with water coming through. Not much. A thread of water running down the rock and pooling in a groove someone — something — had worn in the stone. The pool was the size of his palm. The water was clear.

He drank.

The water was cold. Deeper than cold. It went into his throat and his chest and he could feel it all the way down, a line of cold that was different from the cold outside. This cold was clean. It tasted like rock and nothing.

He drank until the pool was empty and then he sat and waited for it to fill again. It took a long time. He drank again. He did this four times.

Then he sat with his back against the black cliff and the wind above him and the sea behind him and he shivered.

He shivered and the shivering wouldn't stop.

---

Night came and the cold got worse. So much worse that the daytime cold had been a joke, a thing he would later remember as almost warm.

He found a crack in the cliff. Not a cave — a crack. A place where the rock had split and left a gap wide enough to pull himself into if he turned sideways. He pulled himself into it. The rock pressed against his chest and his back. Tight. Dark.

Good.

The rock held the day's warmth. Not much. A ghost of it. But his body heat stayed in the crack instead of being ripped away by the wind. He pressed his knees to his chest and his arms around his knees and he put his face against the stone.

The stone was warm.

That was wrong. He knew it was wrong the way the body knows things before the mind catches up. The stone should have been the same temperature as the air. It was black rock in a frozen place at night. It should have been killing him.

It was warm.

Not hot. Not comfortable. But warmer than the air by enough that he could feel the difference. He pressed against it and the warmth soaked into his chest where the stone touched and he stopped shivering for the first time since he woke up.

He didn't think about why. He didn't have words for why. He pressed against the stone and the stone held him like the crack was a mouth and the warmth was the breath of something old and asleep.

He slept.

---

He dreamed. Shards.

Hands. Large hands, rough, pulling him by the arm. The floor moving under him — wood, wet wood, the smell of tar and piss. A cage. Bars made of rope and bone. Something around his neck. Weight on his collarbone.

A voice. Low. Words he didn't understand. Not his language. Not any language he could find in his head. Just sound, just tone, and the tone said *property* and *shut up* and *move.*

The ship. The floor tilting. The water coming in. The hands letting go.

He woke up.

---

Dawn was gray. The wind had dropped. The sea was calmer — the ice chunks floated in patterns that almost looked deliberate.

He pulled himself out of the crack. Everything hurt. His leg — he looked at it now — was cut bad. A gash on the outside of his thigh, long but not deep. Dried blood and salt crusted over it. The skin around the cut was swollen and red.

He found the seep and drank. He walked along the cliff. The cliff went on. The rock went on. The island — if it was an island — went on.

He walked until the cliff turned and he could see more of the shore. Black rock and gray water and white ice and nothing else. No structures. No fires. No boats. No people.

He stood on the black shore where the rock met the sea and the wind found him again and pushed at him.

He was alone.

He didn't have a word for that either. But the body knew. The body had been alone before — in the cage, in the dark, in the hold of the ship. But that aloneness had walls around it. Other bodies nearby. Other breathing. The knowledge that hands would come.

This was different. This was open. This was the whole gray world and nothing in it but him and the rock and the water and the wind.

He stood there for a long time. The wind pushed. He didn't move.

Something in him — not a thought, not a word, something deeper, something in the blood — said *this is where you were supposed to come.* Not the island. The rock. This specific black stone under his feet. As if the stone had been waiting. As if the warmth was an answer to a question his blood had been carrying without knowing it.

The feeling lasted two breaths. Then the wind cut through it and he was cold and his leg hurt and his mouth tasted like iron and salt.

He turned back toward the crack and the seep. He walked carefully. The rock was sharp.

He didn't know his name. He didn't know the name of the island or the sea or the stone. He didn't know if the ship had sunk or if he'd been thrown. He didn't know if anyone was looking for him.

Nobody was looking for him.

He knew that the way he knew the stone was warm. In the blood. Without words.

He pulled himself back into the crack and pressed against the black stone and the stone held its warmth against his chest and he breathed.

He was alive.

He didn't know if that was good.
