# Chapter 1: Black Shore

Cold wakes me.

Not all at once. In pieces. The fingers first — wrong, stiff, far away, like they belong to some other boy's hands and have been loaned to me badly. Then the face. Wind on wet skin. Salt in the cracks of my lips, and under the salt, the iron taste of me, the taste of the inside of my own mouth gone out into the air where it should not be.

I open my eyes.

Sky, gray and low and moving. Clouds run fast, fast as a thing running, like they are afraid of what is behind them. They tear past and do not slow and do not look down. There is no sun in them. There is no warmth in them. Only the running.

I close my eyes. The cold comes for the rest of me, and takes its time, the way a careful hand takes everything off a table one piece at a time so nothing falls and makes a sound.

---

Rock under me. Black rock. Not sand, not deck, not the straw of a cage. Rock broken and frozen and broken again until every edge is a blade laid up to meet me. It bites through what I am wearing, through the thin wet cloth of it, through the skin, down to the bone, and it holds me there pinned to the cold of the world.

I tell my left hand to close. It closes. I tell my right. Nothing. I lie there in the wet and ask it again, and at last it answers, one finger at a time, slow as a thing waking from the dead, the knuckles grinding like the joints are full of grit. The pain of it comes up the arm and into my teeth. It is enormous. I am glad of it the way I have never been glad of anything. The pain is mine. The slavers did not give it to me and they cannot take it back. It is the first thing in a long time that is only mine.

My tongue finds the gaps in my teeth. Three. Two below, one at the side. The holes are fresh — blood and socket and a whistle of air where teeth used to be, the edges of the gum soft and torn, and when I press them the hurt is bright and far down in the jaw. I do not remember them going. I remember a hand. I remember the floor coming up. The rest the sea has taken and will not give back. I spit. Red on black. The red sits on the stone a moment, bright, the only color in the whole gray morning, and then the wind drinks it dull.

I do not ask *where am I*. There is no use in it. The body knows this work and the body does not wait for words. Hurt, cold, wrong, wake, move. Move or be meat. That is the whole of it. That is everything I have been taught and everything I am.

I move.

---

The rock runs down to the sea in long black steps, slick with weed and old ice. Gray-green water below, heavy, the kind of water that looks like it would not splash but break, thick with ice — not a sheet of it but pieces, slabs and shards, floating and grinding, as if the sea has tried to freeze and lost its nerve and now cannot decide what it is.

I get my knees under me. I get my feet. The world swings once, hard, and I put a hand to the stone and wait for it to hold still, and it does, slowly, the way the floor of the ship used to come back level between the rolls.

I breathe out. My breath should smoke in air this cold. The breath of the sailors smoked. The breath of the other bodies in the cage smoked, a hundred little ghosts going up together in the dark, the one warm thing in that place. Mine doesn't. The air takes it and shows nothing, the way still water shows nothing of a stone dropped through it. I breathe out again, slow, watching, close. Nothing. I put my hand in front of my mouth and feel the warm of the breath on my palm — so it is warm, it goes out warm — and still the air will not catch it, will not make the ghost. I watch the place where the smoke should be and it isn't there, and somewhere under the hurt a small thing in me goes still and quiet and listens, the way an animal goes still at a sound it does not have a shape for yet.

Then a wave breaks on the rock below and the spray comes up and hits my face, and I flinch — not from the cold of it, from the sound. The sound is the whole world at once, top to bottom, a great gray roar with no edge to it and no end, and none of it is for me. The water does not care. The rock does not care. The sky, least of all, the sky that will not even hold my breath. I have been dropped here like a thing dropped, and the world will take me apart slow and never once look up from its work.

*Good*, I think — not in words, in the set of my jaw, in the way the back teeth come together. *Let it try.* I have been taken apart by hands that meant it. The world does not even mean it. The world is only weather. I have outlasted worse than weather.

---

Thirst comes before hunger. The hunger is there — it is always there, low, a second mouth somewhere under the first one — but the thirst is louder, a dry rasp pulling at the back of my throat, and the body will be heard in its order.

I crawl toward the low places where water has gathered in the lap of the stone. The first pool is salt; it goes into the gaps in my teeth and burns down into the raw of them and I gag and spit it out, and the spitting hurts too. I follow the foot of the cliff on hands and knees, where the wind goes quiet and the world draws in close and small and the roar of the sea falls back to something I can think over. And there I find it — a crack thin as a finger in the black face of the rock, weeping water, a slow thread of it running down and gathering in a hollow worn in the stone the size of my palm. Clear. Not the gray of the sea. Clear all the way to the black bottom of the little cup.

I drink.

I put my mouth to the stone like an animal and I drink. The cold of it is a clean cold, a different thing than the cold outside, the cold that wants me dead. This cold only wants down. It draws a line down my throat and into my chest, cold all the way, I can feel the shape of my own insides by the cold going through them. It tastes of rock and of nothing, and nothing has never tasted so good. I drink the hollow dry and then I crouch over it and wait, shaking, while it fills again drop by slow drop, and I drink it dry again. Four times. By the fourth the dry rasp in my throat has gone and the second mouth, the low one, has not. The water does not reach the second mouth. Nothing reaches the second mouth. I am beginning, even now, to understand that.

Then I sit with my back to the black cliff and shake, and the shaking will not stop, and I let it shake me. There is no one to see it. There is no one to make the low sound at me for it. I shake until the shaking is done with me, and the gray light does not change, and the sea does not stop, and I am still here.

---

There is one thing the cold cannot reach.

I had a name once. Before the hands, before the floor that moved, before the cage and the dark and the tar-stink. My mother gave it to me. I do not have her face anymore — the sea took that too, or the years did, all but a warmth and a closeness and a voice low over me in the dark — but I have the name. She put it in my mouth when I was small and I have kept it there. The slavers never got it. They never knew to reach for it. They took the teeth and they could not take the thing hidden behind where the teeth had been.

*Rook.*

I hold it behind my teeth, in the soft gap where they are gone, and I do not say it, because saying it would be giving it to the air, and the air here does not even keep my breath. I only hold it. It is small and it is hard and it is warm in a way the stone is not, warm the way a coal kept covered through a long night is warm. It is mine. It is the one thing on this whole black shore that is wholly mine, that I carried off that ship in the only place they never thought to look — under the tongue, behind the gap, in the dark of my own mouth.

The slavers had their own sound for me. Low, made behind the teeth, ugly, a sound that is not a word so much as the felt-shape of a hand closing on the back of my neck. It meant *property*. It meant *come here*. It meant *shut your mouth*. They put that sound on me a thousand times and I never once answered it as a name, only as a man answers the lash, with the back and not the name. That sound is not mine and it is not me. I left it in the sea. I would have left my teeth for it if the sea had asked. The name my mother gave me is the other thing, the opposite thing, and it is not warmth enough to live on, and it is not enough to fill the second mouth, and it is something. In all this gray it is the one something.

---

Night comes, and the cold turns cruel enough to make the daylight cold seem like a kindness I should have thanked while I had it.

I do not see the dark come down so much as feel the warmth, what little there was, lifted off the world like a skin pulled back. The wind changes. It comes in off the ice now with teeth in it, and it goes through the wet cloth I wear as if the cloth is nothing, as if I am nothing, and it goes for the wet of me, for the water still in my hair and on my skin, and turns each drop to a small biting stone. I cannot stay in the open. The open will kill me before the morning. The body knows it, and pulls me up off the cold ground and sets me looking, looking along the black face of the cliff, low to the rock, the way a hurt thing looks for a hole.

I find a crack in the cliff. Not a cave — a split, a gap I can pull into sideways, rock at my chest and rock at my back, tight and dark, tighter than I want, tight as the cage and then tighter. I do not like the tight. The body remembers the tight. But the wind cannot reach in here to strip the heat off me, and the wanting-to-live is stronger than the not-liking, and I fold myself in. Knees to my chest. Arms around the knees. Face to the stone.

The stone is warm.

That is wrong. I know it is wrong the way the body knows things before the head can catch them, a cold dropping feeling under everything that should not be cold. Black rock. Frozen place. Deep night, the deepest cold the world has shown me yet. The stone should be colder than the air. It should pull the last of the heat out of me through my own face and leave me a frozen thing curled in a crack come morning. It is not cold. It is warm. Not hot — not a fire, not a body, nothing kind. But warmer than the air by enough that I feel it plain against my cheek and my brow. The warmth soaks in slow where the stone meets me, into the bone of my face, down into my chest, and the shaking, which has been with me all the long gray day, eases. And then it stops. For the first time since the cold woke me, my body is still.

I do not ask why. There is no answer to be had, and the head that would ask is tired past all asking. I press into the warm dark and let it hold me, the only thing that has held me since the hands let go. I keep my mother's name behind my teeth. I sleep.

---

I dream in shards.

Hands. Big hands, rough, the skin like the rope, dragging me by the arm so the shoulder screams. A floor that moves — wet wood under me, the reek of tar and too many bodies, the world always tilting, so that even now in the dream I cannot find which way is down. Bars of rope and bone, lashed tight, a cage that breathes when the ship breathes. A voice low above me in no words I own, and the voice makes the sound, the ugly behind-the-teeth sound, and the sound means *down* and means *still* and means *you are a thing, and the thing may yet be thrown back to the water.*

The floor tilting past all tilting. A sound of wood giving. Water, cold, coming in across the moving floor, climbing, and the bodies in the dark all moving at once, all the breath-ghosts gone. The hands above me. The hands letting go.

I wake.

---

Dawn is gray. The wind has dropped to a low murmur along the rock. I pull myself out of the crack and everything in me complains at once, the joints, the back, the gaps in my jaw, a whole body's worth of small voices all saying the same thing. I unfold slow. I look at my leg for the first time in the light: a gash down the outside of the thigh, long as my hand, crusted thick with dried blood and salt, the lips of it dark, the skin around it red and tight and hot to the touch — hotter than the rest of me, a small fire lit under the skin. I have seen wounds go this way. I have seen what comes after, in the cage, the red climbing, the heat spreading, the body of the one it was in going quiet and then gone. I press the skin beside it and it gives back pain, clean and deep. I let it be.

I drink at the seep until the cup is dry and dry again. Then I walk where the cliff lets me, slow, a hand on the stone, and the cliff turns, and the shore opens out below me, and I see the rest of it.

Black rock. Gray water. White ice. Nothing else. No smoke standing up off a far hill. No boat dark against the gray. No walls, no roofs, no field, no smudge of anything made by hands. No other breathing thing that walks on two legs anywhere in all that gray and black and white, as far as the eye can throw itself before the world goes to mist.

I stand where the rock meets the sea, and the wind finds me again and leans its whole weight on me, patient, like it has all the time there is, and at last I understand the thing the body has been trying to tell me since the cold woke me on the first morning.

This is a different alone.

The cage was alone — but the cage had walls. It had other bodies close in the dark, breathing, warm, their breath going up in ghosts. It had the knowledge that hands would come. To hurt — but to come. To take notice. To know I was there. There is a kind of company even in the hands that hurt you; they have at least learned the shape of you, they at least look. This has no walls. This is the whole gray world laid out empty with one boy standing in it, and nothing in it that knows his one word, nothing that would turn its head if he spoke it, nothing that would answer. The second mouth opens wider at the thought of it. The low hunger that no water reached, that no meat would reach. It wants the thing that is not here. It wants to be known, and there is no one, and it will not close.

Frost has formed on the rock where I slept. I see it when I turn back. A clean white furring of it, spread all around the hollow my body pressed into the cold ground in the open before I found the crack — frost rung all around the place in a ring. And inside that ring, where I lay, where the shape of me is pressed into the black, none. No frost. Bare black stone in the exact shape of a curled boy, dry and clear, while all the world around it goes white. I stand and look at it a long moment. The frost stops where I was, as if it knows me, as if it will not come where I have been. Something cold drops through my chest, deeper than the wind put there. I only feel the small still thing in me go quiet again and listen.

Then the wind cuts through and my leg is on fire and my mouth fills with the iron taste, and the moment lets me go.

I turn back toward the crack, and the warm stone, and the thin thread of water. I walk careful. The rock is sharp, and there is no one to carry me if it opens me.

I am alive.

I don't know yet whether that is mercy, or only the long way around to dying.
