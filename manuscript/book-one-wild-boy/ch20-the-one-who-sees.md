# Chapter 20: The One Who Sees

There is one in the camp that does not own.

---

I have lain three lows above the bone-spit and learned the camp the way I learn a herd. I know where it bunches and where it thins. I know the fire they feed and never let die, the wall of cut thornwood dragged up against the green, the staked hide they crawl under against the rain and the swarms. And the swarms do not go off them. The dark cloud lifts off the rot at every low and rides every neck red and welted, and they slap and curse and do not understand that the green has already started to eat them through the skin. The cloud rings my own den and will not land on me. It lands on them. That is the first thing the green tells me about these men: it has them, and it does not have me.

Marrow lies the den behind me and does not like the camp. He has not liked it since the smoke. He keeps the high shoulder turned to it and the curled hand working slow against his thigh, and when I go up to the lip to read the owners he comes only so far and stops, and will not bring his eyes down onto the men. I read it off him plain. The smoke is wrong, the fire that never dies is wrong, and I am leaning toward all of it. His whole crooked body says *come back up the green.* I do not. He stays anyway. That is the shape of him.

I know which ones own. They walk slow and stand with the hands on the hips like the big one on the tide-line stood, like Gull-Marr crossed the bare stone. They carry nothing. The owning is in the not-carrying. And the owned carry everything, bent under it the way I went bent under the hold-weight before the green, the easy road shut in their mouths as it was shut in mine.

And there is one that is neither.

She carries. So she is owned; I read that first, the bend of her under the bundle, the rope-mark worn into the meat of her shoulder. But she is not chained to the others that carry, not roped neck to neck in the shuffling line, and the owners do not lay the hand on her like they lay it on the rest. They keep her near the owning-fire. They feed her from the owning-pot. She is owned and worth keeping, like a thing is kept that does a thing the owner cannot. A sound goes from an owning-mouth, an edge laid on the air, and she turns it over and lays it down again changed, slower, shaped at the owned, and the owned lift their heads. She stands between the two roads and carries the sounds across like she carries the bundle, bent under it.

A thing that carries the sounds across. The far mouth opens at her so wide and so sudden I press my front flat to the wet rock and breathe through it. There is no Vorth shaped to do that thing, no Kala, no seer — only her, owned and bent on the bone-spit, doing the one thing the tribe could never give back: moving meaning from one head into another, on the air, alive.

---

She slips the camp at the bare of the low.

I almost lose her in it. The light is going gray and thick, the steam coming up off the cooling mud, and she goes out past the thornwood wall where the owned go to make their water, and she does not come back to the owned-fire. She comes the other way. Down the spit, slow, over the weed-slick where the worst footing is, picking her way — and she comes toward the green. Toward the dark wall of mine.

Behind me Marrow goes still. The curled hand stops.

She stops at the tide-line. Just short of where the dry mud goes to wet, just short of the first of my mangrove, and she stands with the water coming up slow behind her and she looks into the green. Into the dark of it. Into the place where I am, though she does not have me — her eyes go over the root-cage where I lie poured into the mud and slide off it as every eye slides off me.

She is near my own count of lows, a little more maybe, hard to read under the wear of the carrying. Her hair is bound back in fat ropes the color of wet root, heavy with the damp. There is a smell off her on the down-wind, under the grease-smoke and the fear-sweat of the owned line — a smell I know. A body that has been hungry. Not the milk-and-fat smell of a thing fed every low of its life; the other one, the thin sour under-smell of a body spent down to the bone and fed back up just enough to carry the bundle one more low. She has known the near hunger, the one that scrapes the bottom. I read it off her on the wind, and the empty in me widens at that too, at a body hungry as I have been hungry, standing owned at the edge of my green.

She does not come into mine. She stops at the line as if someone walked her to it and said *here, and no further* — but no one walked her here. She came down the killing spit alone to stand at the wall of the green that eats humans, and she does not bolt, and her shoulders are up around her ears like a body folds itself small around a hurt to keep it from spilling.

I name her in my own head before I have a sound for her. Yara. It is not a word; it is the shape the wind makes coming off her, the falling open shape of the sound she lays on the dark. I take it for her name because she needs one in me. I will not call her *the owned thing* in the place where I keep her.

She is the one who breaks away. I knew the shape before anything else of her — one body coming loose from the warm of the pack onto the bare edge where the others will not follow. I have been that shape my whole life. I am that shape now, lying above a crown I will not climb back to, leaning at a fire that is not mine.

We are two things alone at the two ends of the tide-line, and only I can see us both.

---

I should kill her, or leave her.

That is the law and I know the law. She is the hand. She is the shape the brand comes in, and a thing of theirs walking alone into the dark of mine is a thing I could take down into the mud before it had a sound out — like the Mure take a thing, like the green takes them all in the end. The beast wants it. It lies flat and ready and counts the steps between us and finds them few.

But she has come empty-handed onto the killing ground, and stood at my line and not crossed it, and she has the smell of hunger on her and the shoulders of the one who breaks away, and the empty in me will not let the beast move.

Behind me Marrow lets out a low breath, the sound he makes when I lean toward a thing he will not follow me to. I do not turn. I do not have to. The high shoulder is down; he has dropped it like he drops it before a snake. He does not see a hungry girl. He sees the camp on legs, walked down the spit into our dark, and his whole body says *this is theirs and theirs is not ours.* He is right. I lean anyway.

So I do the thing I have never done.

I let her see the green move.

Not me. Not the shape, not the hand, not the wrong skin and the pale mark on the knuckles where the bone closed in days that should have taken a moon. I move a frond. One root-arm of mangrove, low, slow, the way a tide-beast rolls and gives — I lean my weight into it and let it sway against the still air where no wind is, a wrongness in the stillness, a thing that moves when nothing should move it.

Her head comes up.

She goes still the way the hunted go still, and I know that stillness from the inside, having been the hare. Her eyes fix on the moving frond. And then she does the wrong thing, the new wrong, the one that holds me: she does not run.

She stays. The water comes up another hand behind her heels and she does not turn for the camp. She stands strung on the wire between stay and run, the whole rope of her pulled tight and not parting, and she looks at the place where the green moved, and she opens her mouth.

A sound comes out.

It is soft. It is aimed low, aimed at the dark, aimed at the thing she thinks she saw — and it is not the slaver-sound. That I know before anything else of it, in the body, like I know the rot-pool from the drink. The slaver-sound closes at the end like a hand closing, the hard thing snapped shut behind the teeth that means *mine, be still, come, you are a thing,* and I have worn that closing on the back of my neck through more lows than I can lay finger to. This does not close. It stays open. It falls, and keeps falling, soft and low, down past the root-cage into my chest where the breath sits, and it lands, and it does not grab.

I hold still under it and let it land. Nothing has made a sound land on me like that since before the hold. I had forgotten the air could carry a thing toward a body that it did not mean to close on.

---

She comes back at the next low. And the one after.

I learn her the way I learn weather — by watching the same thing happen enough times that the shape comes clear. She slips the camp at the same hour, when the owning-fire is full of grease and the owners are heavy and slow with the eating. She comes down the spit and stands at my line and does not cross it. She will not be caught in the dark of my green with the tide turning to chew the spit out from under her; that is a limit she keeps hard, and I respect it like I respect a thing's reading of the water — that the green does not forgive a body that lingers.

And she talks.

She talks to the moving green, and the moving green is me. She thinks she is talking to a thing that lives in the dark and cannot talk back — a beast, a haunt, the green itself — and so she talks easy. She points at the gray-green weed in the tide-wrack and makes a sound, short, soft at the front, the tongue tapping it out. She points at the black snails on the low rocks and makes another, round in the mouth like the snail is round. She lays her hand flat on the wet mud and names the going-out of the water and the coming-in of it, two sounds that lean against each other, one settling, one rising at the tail. She points back at the camp, at the thornwood wall and the owning-fire, and the sounds change. They go sharp, careful, set down one at a time like she is laying stones across bad mud, like the naming itself has teeth and she does not want to be bitten.

I take every sound into me.

I hold them behind the gone teeth, in the gap where three were torn, the warm hollow where I keep things. They stack up in the dark of me and something turns over. Slow. Like a root-buried stone that lies in the wrack moon on moon until one high water finally gets under it and rolls it, and the wet underside comes up to the air, pale and strange. Something in me like that.

I had this once. The turning stone shows me. Before the green, before the hold, before the deck and the closing hand, there was a mouth that made sounds at me and I made them back, and the making-back meant we were two and not alone. The tribe gave me a voice — the rasp, the falls, the still-sound, the two-falls I make to the Kala — but it could not give me back the easy road, the one that lays a name clean on the air for another head to catch and throw back. Only the upright pack has the road. And now one of them lays it down at my feet, one stone at a low, naming the world to a thing she thinks cannot answer. I am stealing the stones, building back across the gap in my teeth a thing I lost the shape of, with another body's names.

Up in the dry Marrow watches me steal them. He does not know what I am taking, only that I lie at the lip every low now with my whole front turned to a thing of theirs. He brings me a snail once, in the curled hand, set down by my elbow without a sound — a thing he does when he wants me back in the den and has no words to say so. I eat it to ease him. Then I turn back to the girl. He goes still behind me, the long still, and I feel it on my neck the whole low: his body saying the thing his mouth cannot, that I am leaning out over water that will not hold me.

---

I do not answer. The beast keeps my throat shut and I let it keep it. A sound from the green would turn her face the wrong color, set her feet to the running at last, call the owners down the spit with their fire and their hands. So I stay shut, and I am full to the back of the teeth with her words, and the empty in me — the one no fish ever filled, the one the tribe filled with everything but its own exact shape — takes the words too, and holds them, and does not let them fall straight through. Not enough. Never enough. But it closes a little around them, and that is new.

She is the shape of the hole, and the hole leans.

I have a crown three lows behind me and the Kala's arm in the easy sleep and a home with no between in it, and the empty was full — I felt it go full on the king's seat, all the way down. And it leans anyway, leans at the owned girl naming snails to the dark, because she has the road, and the road is the exact shape torn out of me before the green. Nothing on all my green closes it but the thing the green is going to eat.

---

She finds the relic.

It is the worst low and the best one. She comes at the bare of the low as always, and the rain comes early, the fat warm rain that falls straight down through the still air and turns the spit to running mud, and she does not go back. She crouches at my line with her root-rope hair gone black and streaming, and she does not name anything; she only looks into the green where the frond moves, closer than she has been, near enough that the down-wind brings me the rain off her skin.

And I lean out.

I do not mean to. The empty leans the body with it, out of the root-cage, up onto the haunches in the streaming dark, and the Eye-and-Thorn swings forward off my chest on its gut-cord — the relic I dragged off the dead ones in the mud, the open eye and the curved thorn crossing it. It swings out into the gray light at the edge of the green, wet, catching what little the dying low gives it.

She sees it.

I feel her see it the way I feel a tide turn. Her body fixes on it like it fixed on the moving frond that first low — gone still, strung tight — but this is a different fix. The first was fear. This is the other thing. Her breath goes out of her in a sound with no shape, and she does not look at the dark around the relic, at the thing that wears it. She looks at the relic. Only at the relic. And her hand comes up off the mud, slow, and her fingers draw the shape on the wet air between us — the eye, and the thorn crossing it — and then she lays a sound on it, set careful and low, the way you set a name on a thing you know.

She knows it.

The owned girl knows the shape on the relic the dead ones died wearing, the shape that lives in my drowned-deep below all the green, and she draws it on the air and names it, and her face is not the beast-fear and not the easy talking-to-the-dark. It is the face of a thing finding a piece of its own gone world in the mouth of the green that eats it.

And I want it. Hard, all at once, harder than I have wanted anything since the hold. I want the sound she just laid on the shape. I want to know what it says. She has the answer in her mouth — a clean name for the means-wrong thing that turns in my drowned-deep — and it is three of my body-lengths off across the rain and I cannot reach for it. I have no mouth to ask with. No word to throw back to draw the rest out of her. The beast holds my throat and the green holds my shape, and the one head on all the drowning world that carries the answer crouches at my line not knowing she carries it. I strain at the gap between us and it does not close. The shape I cannot file away and leave turned face-down has a sound now, her sound, laid careful on the air, and I still cannot get under it to roll it over.

I draw the relic back against my chest. The beast hauls me down into the root-cage and I let it. My heart goes against the mud like a hunted thing.

There are two of us who know the shape, on all the drowning world. The one who wears it and cannot say it, and the one who can say it and does not know who wears it.

---

She does not come the next low.

The owners keep her in. I lie above the camp and watch through the gray, and she is at the owning-fire with the big one standing over her, his mouth going, the edge laid hard on the air, and twice the hand comes up and twice it does not fall, holds over her like the hand held over me in the hold before it closed. The owning. The keeping-close. She did a thing the owner did not send her to do, and the owner has found it out, and she stands owned under the lifting hand and takes the edge of the sounds on her bent neck.

And the thing I lie still and feel be new is this: I have a name for what she is now, and I did not learn it from her. I learned it from my own neck. She went out under the lifting hand toward a green that could take her down into the mud in a breath, to name snails to the dark — because she is alone in the middle of the owned and the owning both. Alone as I knew the tide long before I could name a single part of it. In the body, in the bone, before the word.

She is owned, and she is the road, and she is the one who breaks away, and she carries on her bent neck the thing the green tore out of me and gave back in her mouth. She is not the Kala. The Kala filled the hollow full; the Kala is the home with no between. Yara is not home. Yara is the mirror — the owned thing on the killing ground, naming the world to the dark — and a man can look in a mirror and not be looking at a home.

Behind me, all this while, Marrow has not slept. I feel him at the den-mouth in the streaming gray, the high shoulder down, watching me watch the camp. He knows only that I lie at the lip of a thing that eats us, leaning, and that his warning has run out of body to make. There is nothing left in him to drop, no shoulder, no hand. He just keeps the watch with me, over a fire that is not ours, on a spit that wants us dead, because I will not leave it and he will not leave me. He is not the shape of the hole. He is only the thing that loves me, a different keeping, and tonight I am too full of her stolen stones to feel how much it is.

The far mouth does not know mirror from home. It only knows shape. And she is the shape. I lie above the camp in the streaming gray, the king of the wild things who will not go up to his crown, and I hold her stolen words behind my teeth in the gap where three were torn — the weed-sound, the snail-sound, the two leaning water-sounds, the careful sharp camp-sounds with the teeth in them — and I lay them down beside the name no one can catch and the brand I will give back someday across the water, and the warm comes up slow and lies down around all of it together.

There are three things behind my teeth now. The name that is mine and answers nothing. The brand that is theirs and means *you are a thing.* And the new one, the gathering one, the road laid down one stone at a low by an owned girl who does not know she is teaching me to throw the name at last —

and I do not know yet that the shape she named on the air will be the thing the owners come to take me for, nor that the road she is laying me will run, in the end, straight to the net. Marrow knows. Marrow has known since the smoke. He cannot say it, and I will not read it, and that is the whole of what is wrong with me, lying happy at the lip of the trap with my mouth filling up.
