# Chapter 26: Property Again

They have me down on the high stones under the nets, and the Kala is still alive when they bring the iron out for her, and that is the part they meant me to see.

---

I am in the mesh. Three nets over me now, stone-weighted at the edges so the more I surge the more it gathers. A net is built to gather. The fight is already behind me — the torn strand, the tar-reek flooding my mouth, the furrow I opened down the leader's face that did not move him one stride. It bought nothing. They came one animal with many backs, the way they always meant to, and when the first net filled with me they brought the second, and over that the third, and then the rope.

A loop on the wrist. A loop on the ankle, the leg dragged straight and lashed. A loop drawn cruel around the throat so my own pulling chokes me, so the fight is the thing that takes the air. They lean their whole weight down the lines. The high stone comes up to meet my face and the blood floods my mouth where the rock splits my lip, warm, all of it, mine and theirs, iron-warm and quick where my own should run thin and slow and does not.

And the hand closes on the back of my neck.

The hand. The hand in the dark. The hold. It is here, it is now, it is real and broad through the sweat-grease of the leather, the spread of the fingers and the heel of the palm bearing my cheek down into the gray stone, and it is the exact shape, the exact weight, the exact closing I carried buried under the tar all those lows in the wreck of myself — and the body knows it before the head can say the word for it, and the scream tears out of me whole, every low of the hold come up the throat at once. The hand on the back of the boy's neck, folding him down, making him a thing. They came all this long way up my green to put it back exactly the way it was.

I am folded. I am held. I turn my head the one finger's width the hand allows, the cheek grinding on the stone, because the body will not let me not look.

I look at the Kala.

---

She has stopped making the torn sound.

That is the first wrong thing, read in the gut before the head. The high stones are quiet where her voice was, and a thing gone quiet under two irons is not a thing at rest. She is on her side on the cleanest high shelf, the one where the sun strikes first, where her small one is laid faced among the squared dead, and the net is still over the great gray bulk of her, the hide gone dull in the heat-light, the dark blood stopped welling thick and started welling slow. The long arm I have lain under in the fever-dark is not fighting the mesh anymore. It lies open on the stone. The great hand is half-curled, the fingers I once watched fold a wrong man-shape in against the warm of her chest gone loose now, gone to the slack a hand goes to when the thing under it lets go.

But she is alive. I read it off the great ribs, the slow heave of them, the breath coming long and dragged and far apart — alive the way the tide-beast is alive a long count after the spear is in it, the body still working the bellows out of habit while the thing that was in it leaves by the holes. Alive, and looking at me. The great dark eye is turned to me across the stone, low, level, finding me through the net and the rope and the men, and there is no fear in it for herself. I have learned that eye low after low. I know its weathers. It is not afraid of the iron in its shoulder or the iron in its haunch or the slow dark pool spreading into the seams of the stone. It is looking at the small held thing in the nets, the wrong man-shape it came down off its crown to fold into the warm, and what is in the eye is the thing it laid over me in the fever — the keeping thing, the gentling thing, *I have you,* turned outward now and finding me across the stone with nothing left to back it but the looking.

She knows what I am seeing. She made the torn sound at the sky for a long count to bring me, not knowing she was bringing me, the way the staked thing screams and does not know it is the bait — and now I am here, caught, and the great eye finds me caught, and the gentling does not go out of it. Not the irons. Not the hand on my neck. This: that she is dying with the iron in her and the only thing in the great dark eye is for the thing in the net.

A slaver steps to her with the long hooked iron.

---

I scream. There are no words in it. The words went out of me lows ago in the wreck-night and what came back came back through the tribe, through the Kala's own throat laying the keeping sound over my head until the lost sound that is mine woke under it — and now even those are gone, the green ones, the kin-words, all of it torn out of me, and there is only the open throat of the thing and the red coming up behind it, hot, flooding, every edge of me gone to teeth and want.

I surge against the nets and the nets gather and the loop bites my air and the hand bears down on my neck, even and sure, and I do not move the great dark bulk of her one finger's width closer to me. I cannot. There is a boy's strength in my arms and it ran out fast, all at once, the deep well of it draining to mud while the red still screams for more. That is the thing they counted. That is the thing the patient one read out of me before the others ever crouched at my line — that the shape in the green is wrong in the body, slides the dull-water, eats the open hand and walks back clear, heals the split lip while they drag him — but is, under all the wrongness, one boy, with a boy's arms, and a boy's arms end.

The slaver sets the hooked iron behind the great shoulder where the heart is. He knows where the heart is. The iron always knows.

He looks at the patient one first. I see it. He does not drive the iron home off his own want; he turns the half-question of his face up the stone to the one whose hand is in all of this, and the patient one — I cannot see him from under the hand but I feel the still center of him up there, the green-killer who walked up my green and did not die of it — the patient one gives the small nod. A man setting down a weight. The bait has done its work. The thing the screaming was set to bring has come, and is in the net, and the screaming thing is no longer worth the meat it would take to keep it. She was never the catch. She was the leaning stone, set out pointing. And a leaning stone, once the road is walked, is kicked loose.

The iron goes in behind the shoulder.

The great body knows it. The slow bellows catch. The long arm draws in off the open stone, the loose fingers closing on nothing, on air, the way a hand closes when the last of the thing goes out of it — and the great dark eye does not leave me. It holds me through the whole of it, the iron and the catch and the long shudder going down the gray hide, and the gentling does not break and does not turn to fear and does not turn away. It stays on me. It is on me when it goes out. The light in it does not run out fast and visible the way blood runs; it is there, and then it is the eye of a thing on the stone, open, gray-filming, turned to me, holding nothing.

The high stones are quiet.

---

I do not make a sound now.

That is the thing I cannot account for and do not try to. The scream that tore out of me for the net and the hand and the tar — gone. The red that came up flooding behind it — banked, pulled down into a place lower than the red, lower than the second mouth, a place I have no name for and do not reach for one. The body goes still under the hand. Not the stillness of a thing giving up. The other one. The stillness of the green when the screaming thing has been taken and the bigger thing crouches over the dark with the whole weight of the world gathered behind its teeth and does not yet move.

They take the quiet for breaking. I feel it run through the hands on me, the small loosening, the breath let go — *the animal is done, the fight is out of it.* The hand on my neck does not loosen, the patient one's law in it even now, but the others let a little air into their grips, and one of them laughs, short and low, the laugh of a thing got that has been hunted long, a weight set down.

I file it. Low. Hard. No turning.

Beside me in the mesh there is another body. They netted Marrow with me — the runt, the wrong-made thing, worth nothing to them but the trouble of the rope, taken in the same surge that took me. He is under the same weight, one shoulder riding high against the stone where it always rides high, the curled hand working at the strand, slow, the way it works when there are no words in him for a thing. He is not screaming either. He is looking at me, and then at her, and then at me, and his high shoulder drops, the way it drops when the worst of a thing has already come and there is nothing in the body left to brace against. He does not make a sound. He has read what is on the stone. He knew this green-road for what it was before I would let myself, and he came down it anyway, behind me, because I went.

---

I look at her across the stone. The great gray bulk gone still under the net, the irons standing up out of the shoulder and the haunch and the heart, the small one laid faced beside her among the squared dead, the kept fire scattered to ash. She came down off the high shelf in the fever-dark and folded the great arm over the burning thing the green tried to kill and could not, and made the keeping sound, low, up and down, no edges, over and over, when every fast part of me screamed *hands, hold, the weight on the neck,* and she was not the hand. She was never the hand. The far mouth that no kill ever filled and no water ever filled, the hole under the breastbone that ate me from inside while the green ate me from out — it went still against her warm. The only time in all my life I can reach that it went still. Lay down quiet under my ribs and was full.

She is the price. I knew it crouched in the shadow with the trap drawn whole in the gut, and I came down anyway, by my own feet, eyes wide, because the thing on the end of the hook was the one thing the world ever set warm against the hole, and a thing that has filled that hole once is a thing you do not leave on the stone. I knew it would cost her. I did not let myself reach all the way to what it would cost — the great eye going out turned to me, holding nothing, the gentling still in it. The body has no head for this. Only the still gathered weight of its teeth.

---

They get the irons under me to lift me, and that is when I learn the last shape of it.

The leader crouches into my face, the long furrow I opened from his eye to his jaw welling dark, crusting already, and he is looking at me the way a man looks at a thing he has paid too much for and means to make the price back. He turns my chin in his hard fingers, up, into what is left of the light, and he looks at the gap behind my teeth where the three went, and something moves in his face that is not the hunting-want and is not the laugh. It is the thing the owned girl's face did when she first read the relic I carry, the Eye-and-Thorn worn smooth, her hands going still, her eyes coming up to me wrong, *you are something.* It is the thing the seer did up on the high stones with the old signs, the great slow knowing turned on my blood.

He does not have the word for it. None of them did. But he has read what they read. He has watched the green try to kill me low after low — the fever-air that lays his own men down shaking, the bad water, the rot that should be green in my split lip and is already drawing shut clean — and it has not killed me, and he was patient enough to learn which way I bolt, which means he was patient enough to learn the one thing under the body that does not bolt and does not slide and does not break.

And here is the thing I want to know and cannot reach the bottom of, even now, even with his fingers in my jaw and the answer standing right behind his eyes: *what* I am. He has seen it. The girl saw it, the seer saw it, the smooth disc carries it — and I am the only one who cannot turn it up into the light and look. I press at it. I have pressed at it since the wreck, and it gives nothing back but his face reading me, sure of a thing I am locked out of. They all know what I am worth carrying across the water. I am the only one who does not.

He has read enough. Not an animal — he has animals, the Vorth and the Mure and the Skell, brutes and tide-things and carrion-kin to chain and sell. He could have taken the Kala dead off the stone and sold her hide and her great squared bones. He put the iron in her live and held me to watch instead, and that is the tell I read too late, lower even than the bait: she was never the cargo. *I* am the cargo. He staked her out to bring me because the one thing he crossed the water for, walking into the death he could not survive, was the one thing on this island that survives it.

The leader stands. He looks down at me in the nets and the rope, and out of his mouth comes the sound — low, made behind his own teeth, the felt-shape of the closing hand turned to noise. *Mine. Be still. Come. You are a thing.* The brand. The hold-word, the only word they ever had for me, because they never found the other. He throws it down at me sure it will wrap and hold.

It does not.

---

There is one of them they leave behind.

Up the slope, past the squared dead, the proud one of the tribe stands at the head of what is left — Voss-Marr, Gull-Marr's blood, the one raised to take the high stones I leapt over him to take. His people did not come down to the nets. They hung at the green's edge while the iron walked up onto the high stones, and they hang there now, and the air around him does not move toward the road and does not move toward the Kala on the shelf. He looks down at me dragged in the mesh and there is no kin in it. A thing settles in the set of his shoulders, slow, like a stone finding its bed. The slavers came up the green for the human king. They are leaving with the human king. And the broken tribe, the scattered fire, the squared dead and the great gray bulk going small on the shelf — that is left to him. He did not want it like this. He wanted it clean, by the neck, in front of all of them. He has it the wrong way now, and the wrong way will sit in him and rot toward something. I read it off him the way I read a sky going hard at the edge. He is already counting.

I am hauled past, and his eye holds mine for one stride, and then I am below him, and the high stones are his.

---

They have made me property again.

The hand on the back of the neck. The rope at the wrist and the ankle and the throat. The brand flung down. The iron getting under me, lifting, and the high stones starting to slide away beneath me backward — my high stones, the ground the tribe bled into being and I bled to keep, the squared dead and the scattered fire and the still bulk of her going small as they haul me down the road, all of it pouring away the way the scree poured between my fingers in the wreck-night, every grain running, not one stone staying in my hand. It was never mine to keep. It was only ever borrowed back from the green a low at a time, and the moment the hand opens it pours.

But they are wrong about the brand, and wrong about the quiet, and wrong about what they have taken.

They think they have taken an animal. A wrong-blooded animal that does not die, worth iron and the long water-crossing, but an animal — a thing the hold-word wraps, a thing the hand on the neck makes still. They have not. I hold the name they never found. My mother's. The one she gave me before the slavers, before the ship, before the dark of the hold — kept low and safe under my tongue, pressed flat like a coin behind the gap where the three teeth went, where no hand has ever reached and no hand reaches now. *Rook.* The green wore it down to an animal sound and then to no sound at all, and the Kala woke it back, laying the keeping sound over my head until the lost thing that is mine rose under it — and here it is, behind my teeth, whole and woken, while the brand breaks against my face and falls off and holds nothing. They can have the body in the net. They can drag it down my green to their iron and their water and their hold. They cannot have that. It is not in the body. It is under the tongue, behind the gone teeth, and it is mine, the last and only thing that has always been mine.

And Marrow is dragged beside me, one shoulder high, the curled hand still working the strand, and he does not break either. They reckoned the worth of one of us and threw the other in for the trouble it would have cost to leave him. They reckoned wrong about that too, though they will not learn it for a long count.

And I hold her. The great dark eye going out and never leaving me, the gentling still in it, holding nothing — I take it down into the place lower than the red, the still gathered place that has no name, and I set it there beside the name, and I do not let it go. She filled the hole once. The hole is open again, torn wider than the green ever tore it, howling silent into the dark where her warm was. But the having of it once is in me, and the having does not pour away with the scree. They drag the body and the body bleeds and the lip draws shut clean and the brand falls off the face that will not wrap, and down under all of it, in the place the body goes when the body is taken, I carry three things they cannot reach: the name my mother gave me, woken and whole. The brand, ground small behind my gone teeth, kept only so that one low I can hand it back across the water to the mouths it came from. And her — the warm that knew it held me, gone out with the gentling unbroken, the only thing the world ever set against the hole.

I learned to care, and it caught me, and her face going out is the price, and I would pay it again.

The high stones slide away. The road goes down into the green. The hand stays on the back of my neck. Marrow's eyes find mine in the mesh, and hold.

I begin, somewhere low under the red, where the still place is, to count.
