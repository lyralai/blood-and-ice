# Chapter 25: He Goes Anyway

The bait is the home. That is the thing the keeper learned that no man before him learned, because no man before him stayed long enough on my green to watch where I lean.

He could not draw my shape out of the still. He set the meat at the thornwood and I did not come, because a fed beast does not walk onto open ground for a haunch. So he stopped baiting the meat. He went up my green and took the Kala.

A bait is a thing you set where the animal already goes. There was only ever one place I went.

---

The trap is whole in me, every tooth of it, and the having does not turn me back. I do the thing the bait was set for.

I go down toward her.

The first law of the green is in my legs the whole way, the law I learned the first moon and have never once forgotten, screaming up through my soles as I pour myself down off the safe high edge into the scattered fire and the owning shapes. *A thing the world lays out easy is a thing the world means to close on.* The Kala on the cleanest high shelf with two irons in her is the easiest thing the world has ever laid for me. The whole green I bled into being screams the law up into me. *Do not go down onto her. Take the small ones and go up, into the fever-fog and the broken stone where the men die and you do not. The home is already on the hook. Going to it only puts you on it beside her.*

That is the law. It has kept me alive on an island that keeps no one.

I go anyway.

The pull is bigger than the law and always was. It has its teeth in me now, dragging me down off the one edge where no hand could reach me, toward the net and the hooked iron and the patient one waiting in the green below. No part of me turns back. Not fooled. Never fooled. I come down the open stone toward the closing hand with my eyes wide and the choosing made, because the thing in the net is the one warm that ever filled the second mouth full, and a body does not leave that on the stone. Not for any law the green ever taught.

---

I am not the only one who comes down.

Marrow is at my shoulder. He has been at my shoulder since the high edge, the high uneven shoulder of him riding up by my arm, the curled hand working, the rocking gait carrying him down the broken stone quicker than a whole creature has any right to go. He read the trap before I did. Back on the safe edge he went still and the curled hand worked and he would not look where I was looking, and that is the whole of the warning he had words for, and I went anyway, and so he comes.

This is the thing about the runt the rich crack the bone and leave. He does not weigh it. The strong weigh it. The strong stayed up on the high edge.

They are up there now. The band I bled into a tribe, the spears I called down off the crown — they stand on the safe high ground in a long dark line and do not come. Voss-Marr at the head of them, the prime blood, magnificent and still, and the air around him says it plain as a struck drum: *the human king brought this down the water. Let him walk into it.* He will not spend Vorth lives for a wrong-blood king and a great gray beast. Some of the line break and run for the fog. The rest watch.

So it is two of us that go down. The boy and the runt.

---

She sees us come.

She lifts the great gray head off the bloodied stone as far as the net will let it and lays the small dark patient eye on me, and she reads me the way she has read me since the first low. What she reads is *come.* What she reads is the trap. Both at once. The sound she makes is the lowest I have ever heard out of her. Not the asking-sound. The one she makes over a small one straying too near the rot-thicket where the ground will not hold.

*Go back. Go up. Do not come down onto the soft ground after me.*

She is in the net with the irons in her shoulder and her haunch and she is telling me to leave her in it. To take the between I carried up the green on my skin all those lows back up the broken stone, and live.

I go to her.

---

I come fast and low across the scattered fire and the first one never hears me.

I take him at the back of the knee with the killing-stone in my fist. The joint goes sideways and he folds with a sound like green wood breaking. His holding-spear rings on the rock. My hand is already out for it, the long iron, the barb wet and gleaming for the length of one heartbeat in my palm —

No. The net comes out of the dark behind me.

It is weighted at the edges with stone and it is on me before the spear is anything but a weight in my hand. The mesh drops over my head and shoulders and the stones swing in and wrap. The more I surge the more it takes. That is what a net is built to do. I am down. The spear is gone, ringing away across the stone. I roll. I bite. I get a fist of the mesh in my teeth and pull and a strand parts and the taste of it floods my mouth — tar. Hold-tar. The black seam-stink that lived in the dark of the ship low after low under the bodies, and the body knows it before the head can shape it. The buried sound comes up screaming behind my gone teeth at last. The hand. The closing. The brand. It rises out of the place I packed it down and fills my whole skull, and the red comes up all the way behind it, hot, flooding, every edge of me gone to teeth and want.

Good. Let the net try.

I bite through a man's wrist to the cord under it. He howls, high, a sound that does not belong to a thing that carries hooked iron, and the blood that comes is warm in my mouth, alive, singing all the way down into the gut. Nothing like meat. Nothing like the still deep green. All rush, all want, all the living heat of him. The ache under my breastbone yawns wide for it. It does not want the wrist. It wants all of him, the whole warm living shape of him taken into the place no meat has ever filled, and for one black breath I let the rage and the want run together and there is no boy left in it, only the open throat of the thing.

Then there are four on the net at once.

---

Beside me Marrow takes one apart at the eyes.

He is small and wrong-made and quick past all of it. He goes in low under a spear-thrust like water under a stone, the curled hand fast at the man's face, the high shoulder driving, and the man reels back screaming with both hands to his eyes. Marrow is on the next before the first is down. He does not fight square and proud, the way the strong fight. He fights the way I taught the deadfall — small, sideways, taking the joint and the soft place and the thing the big creature thinks is safe. For one breath I think the two of us might cut a road back up the stone after all.

Then they bring the second net, and it is over him, not me.

I see it close. The stones swing in and wrap the high shoulder and the curled hand and pin the quick out of him, and he goes down into the same dark mesh they have already half-folded over me, the tar-stink rising off both of us at once. He does not howl. He gets his teeth in the cord the way I did. A strand parts under him. It is not enough. It was never going to be enough. They counted us long ago, lying out on the high ground learning which way we bolt and how long the heat lasts — and they did not count one. They counted two, and brought net enough for two, and here it is.

We are in the same nets. That is the thing the high country will remember, if it remembers anything. They took the king and the runt in one closing, and the strong watched from the safe edge and did not come down.

---

Let the high stones keep the count: three of them out of the fight before they fold me, and Marrow's share beside it, the blinded one and the two he hamstrung. The one at the knee. The one whose wrist runs red into the seams of the rock. A third I take across the throat with the edge of my hand and he goes down whooping for air that will not come fast enough. A thing that hunts me carries the marks of having hunted me. That is mine. They cannot take it off me.

I open the leader's face from the eye to the jaw with my nails before they pin the arm, a long furrow welling dark — and he does not cry out. That is the worst of him in the end. Not the size. That he takes the wound and does not break stride.

But here is the other worst of him, and I read it even folded in the mesh. He is dying on his feet. The hand that bears down on me shakes. The skin under the furrow I opened is not a clean man's skin — it is grayed, gone slack and damp, the fever-sheen on it that I have watched climb up the camp low after low while they climbed my green. He bore the home off its crown and bought a length of rot for every step. They all did. Half the line of them sways where it stands, slick with the green sickness that does not touch me, eyes too bright, breath coming wet. They are winning. They are also dying, and they do not yet know how much. The fever I do not feel is in every one of them. They climbed into it to come for me and it took them on the way.

It does not slow the hand enough. A dying man's full weight still folds a boy. He bears down harder, even and sure, patient with his own face running blood, patient the way the rot is patient with a fallen thing. He has been sure of this ending since the low he first crouched at my line, and a torn face does not move a man who is sure, and a fever in the blood does not either, not yet.

I am one boy. There is a boy's strength in my arms and no more, and it runs out fast, all at once, the deep well of it draining to mud while the red still screams for more. They do not come one at a time the way beasts come, each waiting its turn at the kill. They come together, one animal with many backs and many hands, and when the first net is full of me they bring the next, and over that another, weighting me down under layer on layer of stone-hung mesh. Then they bring the rope.

A loop closes on my wrist. I twist and bite the hand that set it and taste salt and grease and get a curse for it, but the loop holds. Another loop. My ankle, dragged straight and lashed. They lean their whole weight down the lines and the stone comes up to meet my face and the salt-iron floods my mouth where the rock splits my lip.

And the Kala heaves.

She comes up under her own mountain of net, surging once against all the stone they swung into her and the two irons turning in her, and the high shelf groans under it. For one breath I think the home will stand up out of the trap and bring the whole sky down on them — and they turn the iron on her. Not to kill. I read it even folded in the nets. They do not drive the long iron into the deep of her where the life is. They work it shallow and cruel along the great shoulder where the barb already sits, driving her back down into the stone by the pain of it the way a man drives a beast he means to keep. Her heave breaks. She goes down. The torn sound comes out of her, and it is the worst sound on the whole of this island.

I scream into the mesh and I cannot reach her. I am many hands deep in the rope, and the between I carried in her arm is the whole of the distance now.

---

They have made me a thing in the net.

Rope at the wrist. Rope at the ankle. A loop drawn cruel around my throat so the more I pull the less the air comes, so my own fighting chokes me. Blood in my eyes, mine and theirs, all of it iron, all of it warm where my wounds should run thin and slow and do not. They get the weight of three nets and four men down onto me and the stone bears up under my cheek, and I claw at it, at my own high country, the ground the tribe bled into being and I bled to keep. I get a fistful of grit and the loose scatter of the kicked-out fire and I clamp down and pull, and it pours out between my fingers, not one grain staying. That is the whole truth of this island in one fist of running ash. It does not hold what I cannot hold. It was never mine to keep — only borrowed back from the killing green a low at a time, and the moment my hand opens it pours away.

I snarl, bitten and bleeding, more beast than boy left in the rope, the words gone, the self gone thin, only teeth and the red and the choke of the loop. The rope does not care that I am a beast. The stone does not care. The patient one, somewhere up the shelf with the whole still weight of him gathered, does not care.

A hand's-width from my face Marrow goes still in his own mesh. He has stopped fighting the cord. Not broken — counting. The curled hand lies flat against the stone and the small dark eye finds mine through the gap in the nets and holds it, and I read off his body the one thing he has no human word for and never needed one: *not yet. Not here. We are not done.* He went down the stone after me knowing the end of it, and he lies in the rope beside me now choosing it again, and that is a thing my own kind on the safe high edge did not do. The monster came down. The men did not.

And here, even here, the body keeps its wrong small secret. The wounds the nets and the stones tore down my arms and side are already drawing their edges in while they haul me flat, knitting faster than a cut has any right to, here on the high stones where every cut on Saqarro festers and goes rot-green and fly-blown in a low. The biting swarms that crawl in a black blanket over the bleeding men do not land on me — a clean hand's-width of air rings my skin and they will not cross it. They cross onto the slavers freely, into the fever-damp of them, feeding where the rot has already opened the door. The brand is in my skull and the wrong small thing in my blood goes on keeping its account in the corner where it always has, asking nothing, agreed to by nothing, while they make me cargo. I do not turn it over. There is no turning anything over with a loop on my throat. But I strain at it — I always strain at it — *what walks the swarm off my skin and walks it onto theirs, what knits me while it rots them.* The fever has them and not me, and I do not have the word for why, and the loop draws tight before the wanting can do anything but ache.

---

I see her once more before they turn me.

The Kala, pinned in her mountain of net, the irons standing up out of her shoulder and her haunch, the small dark patient eye finding me across the wall of men. She does not heave again. She lies down into it. The eye that lays on me is not afraid for herself. No fear in it turned inward, no care for her own opened side. It is wide and dark and it breaks on me, and I understand at last what she has been reading off me since I came down the stone.

She read the down road on my skin and the smoke in my hair and went still around me holding it. She could not say it whole and I could not hear it whole, and she did the one thing a great slow thing can do — folded the between into her arm and waited. And it came down to this anyway, because the one thing none of it could do was make me leave her on the stone.

The ache behind my breastbone went still under her once. That is the thing. In all the lows of this green, the one time it lay down and was at peace was in her warm, the small ones heaped against her arm and her great slow tide under my ear and no between in it anywhere. She gave me that with no word and no asking, the only crown I ever wore that was not bled out of the green by my own hands. She made me, the way the deck unmade me. And the hook took the home for bait, and the home is down in the net with the irons in it, and the boy is in the rope, and there was never any other end to it from the low I learned to care.

---

Then the hand closes on the back of my neck.

The hand. The hand in the dark. The hold.

It is here. It is now. It is real and warm through the leather, the broad spread of fingers bearing my cheek into the stone, and it is the exact shape, the exact weight, the exact closing I carried buried under the tar all those lows in the wreck of myself — and the scream tears out of me at last, every low of the hold come up my throat at once.

They made this. They wove the tar-nets and climbed my green in the dark, paying a length of fever and a length of rot for every step, dying slow the whole way up, and dragged the home off its crown — and it was all only ever the long slow making of this one closing. The hand on the back of the boy's neck, folding him down, making him a thing. They came walking across the water onto my green to put it back where it was. And they are rotting even as they do it. The hand on my neck is a dying man's hand. It does not matter. It is closing all the same.

I hold the name they have not found, pressed flat behind the gap where the three teeth went, where no hand has ever reached and no hand reaches now. *Rook.* Whole in me, woken back through a long buried tide and a warm with no between in it. They can have the body in the net. They cannot have that. It is not in the body. It is under the tongue, behind the gone teeth.

They think they have taken an animal. Two animals. They fold me down into the stone and the hand bears even and sure on the back of my neck, the patient one's whole long work come down to this one weight, and somewhere up the shelf a man laughs, short and low, the laugh of a thing got that has been hunted long. It lands behind my breastbone and stays. A hand's-width away, Marrow does not laugh and does not scream. He counts.

I learned to care, and it caught me, and she is the price, and crouched here under the closing hand with the trap shut whole around me, I know the last clear thing before the red heat closes back over it.

I would do it again.
