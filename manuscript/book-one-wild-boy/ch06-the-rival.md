# Chapter 6: The Rival

I smell it before I see it. Wet fur, but bigger than any fur I know — a reek of meat-on-the-breath, of a thing that eats and is not eaten.

I am up past the seep-cliff, hunting the long straight standing-dead for my shaft, three days of wanting it sharp in my hands. The shaft is why I do not watch the ground. The shaft is why I am stupid. I come over the lip of black rock into a place where the snow lies different — packed, trodden, a road of pads pressed into it going one way and back — and the smell falls on me like cold water, and I stop.

Too late to stop.

It rises out of the white where it was not white. Pale, near as pale as the snow, so that it was only ever a shape until it moved. Long in the body, low in the head, and the head swings toward me on a neck thick as my whole chest. A cat. The eyes are the color of old ice with the sun behind it.

The shaft is forgotten. My hand has the bone-edge in it, the broken flipper-bone I carry now, and against this it is a splinter.

I have walked into its larder. I am standing in the road of its pads. And the heat under my ribs, the calm fed heat — it does not look out and see range now. It looks out and sees the one thing on this shore older and harder than us.

---

It does not roar. That is the worst of it.

It comes low and silent, no warning, no sound but the snow giving under the weight, and I am already turning, already gone, because the body knows this work before I do. Run or be meat. I run.

The black rock saves me. It knows the rock no better than I do, but it is heavy and I am light, and where it must go around the broken slabs I go over, hands and feet, a scuttling thing. I feel the wind of it at my back. I feel the heat of it — a real heat, breath-heat, a furnace of meat — and then claws take my shoulder.

Not deep. A swipe, a backhand, the way you cuff a fly you cannot be troubled to kill. It opens four red mouths down my back. Throws me off the slab edge. The gully comes up and hits the breath out of me.

I do not stop. There is no room in me to stop. I am down the gully on my belly, into the crack between two leaning stones too narrow for the great pale shoulders, and I wedge there shaking while it screams above me at last — and the scream is not a child's, like the pup's. It is the sound the whole island would make if the island had a throat. It paces the lip. It cannot reach. The four red mouths in my back run hot and the cold tries to come into them and I press to the stone and hold, hold, hold.

It leaves. Slow. Telling me, in the slowness, that it is leaving because it chooses to, not because I am safe.

I lie in the dark of the stone a long time. The back closes wrong-fast, the way I do not look at, days where it should be weeks — and even fast is not fast enough to stop the shaking. Behind my teeth I find the word and clamp it. Rook. I found the edge of my range today. The edge has teeth longer than mine.

---

Here is what I do instead of dying. I watch.

I cannot kill it. I knew that the moment its head swung. So I do the other thing — I learn it the way I learned the birds, the wind, the pup. Sidelong. Counting.

It walks its road twice a day. Out along the high flats when the light is least, back when the light is most. It is white on white and it is silent, but it is heavy, and heavy leaves a road. I find the road. I lie above it in the rocks with the wind in my face so it cannot drink me out of the air, and I count.

It favors the left fore. Old hurt, maybe, or only old. It hunts the seal-flats I hunt, and the seals it takes are the slow ones, the young, the ones too stupid to read the wind — it takes the pups I would take. That is the war. Not that it would eat me. That it eats my larder. Two mouths on one shore, and the shore feeds one.

Its eyes are bad in the most light and good in the least, backwards from mine, which is why it sleeps when I would wake. It will not cross open water; at the tide-channel it stops, paws the edge, turns back, and I lie there learning that the channel is a wall it built itself and does not know it built. Its weight is its strength, and its weight is the place to put a knife.

If I had a knife.

The second mouth opens while I lie there in the cold counting a thing that wants to eat me. You make a war, it says, because a war is a kind of company. You watch it the way you'd watch a face. There is no one else to watch.

I do not answer it. But I do not say it is wrong.

---

The spear gets made. Cold day by cold day, in the door of the crack that is mine, the standing-dead becomes a shaft, the flake becomes a tooth, the seal-sinew binds tooth to wood and dries hard on the shelf beside the wrong things — the warm stone, the frost that rings the floor and will not touch me, my breath that will not smoke. I do not look at the wrong things. I look at the spear.

When it is done I hold it and the reach of me is longer by an arm and a half. A tooth I can put into a thing from a stride away.

I go to the road and I wait downwind through a whole low-light day, and the cold in my bones lies still and patient, content with the long dark, and for once I let it. The pale shape comes back along its road favoring the left fore. It does not see me. Its eyes are bad in the least light, and the least light is mine.

I do not kill it. I am not a fool twice. But when it passes below me I put the whole weight of the drop and the spear into the slow left shoulder, and the tooth goes in.

---

It screams — the island's-throat scream — and this time there is a thing in it that was not there before. Surprise. The spear tears free and there is blood on the black snow that is not mine — dark, steaming, the cold pulling the heat out of it — and the heat under my ribs rises up to meet it, pleased, fed in that low red place, hot as the blood steaming on the snow, my gut clenching tight and glad around it.

Then it is coming up the rock at me faster than a hurt thing should, and I am running again, and the running is not the same as the first running. The first time I ran from a thing that owned the shore. This time I run from a thing I have hurt, and a thing you have hurt knows your smell forever.

I make the crack. I wedge in the dark. It does not scream at the lip this time. It is quiet, and the quiet is worse, and I hear it breathing out there, wet, ragged, the breath of a thing tasting its own dark blood and deciding what I am.

We both bleed in the dark, the two of us, a stone apart. My back. Its shoulder. I have learned the shape of it and it has learned the shape of me, and there is no unlearning either now.

The shore is not big enough for two mouths.

One of us will have to teach the other that it is gone. And the pale thing has owned this place since before I washed up on it, and I have owned my crack and my warm stone and my finger-thin thread of water for a handful of cold short days — and behind my teeth, where three are missing, I hold the name, and my hands are not shaking now.

I am counting.
