# Chapter 6: The Rival

The morning the sea goes quiet, I learn the next law. A den keeps the wind off a body. A body still needs feeding. The shore does not feed you for staying still.

The sound from the night water did not come again. I lay against the warm inner stone with the fire stood up gold and waited for it, the near mouth gone quiet, the far one wide. It did not come. Somewhere in the waiting I went under into sleep. I woke into gray with the coals breathing low and the place around me cold and still and mine. So the sea-sound goes back onto the dark shelf with the rest, the thing I cannot place, filed not-understood. I crawl out the lee mouth into the wind, because the body has its own count and the count has come due.

The eel-flesh is gone. The crab is gone. I have been spending myself on stone for days, hand over hand, cold dead weight after cold dead weight, and the spending came out of somewhere, and the somewhere is empty now. The near mouth is awake and it is loud. Not the far one — the far one I cannot feed, I have learned that, no meat answers it. This is the other, the true one, the one in the belly that grinds when there is nothing in it, and it grinds now, low and steady, a fist opening and closing on nothing. I stand outside my walled place in the moving cold and feel how thin I have gone in the building of it. The wrists narrower than I remember them. The skin gone close over the ribs. I have poured myself into the stone, and the stone keeps what I poured, and the body is the cup it came out of, and the cup is near empty.

---

I go to the pools first, because the pools are what I know.

The tide is out. The black rock lies bared and weeping all down to the gray water, slick and shining where the sea pulled off it, and the pools sit in the hollows of it, still and clear and cold, each one a little held world the sea left behind. I have taken eels from these. I have taken crabs. I crouch at the lip of the first one with the patience the hands beat into me, the cage-patience, the one good thing out of all that taking, and I wait, and I read.

Nothing. The pool holds weed and stone and my own gray face looking up at me out of the still water and nothing that moves. The next is the same. The next. I work them down the bared shelf, pool after pool, the cold coming up through the soles, and where there was life before there is only the weed swaying slow and the small shells shut tight against me, drawn in hard behind their lips the way I draw the name in behind my teeth. The near mouth grinds. And I understand, late and with my body the way I understand everything, not in the head first but in the gut and then the head: I have taken this shore. I have eaten down the easy law of it. The pools that fed me fed me until they were empty, and they do not fill again because I am hungry. The shore gives once. Then it makes you go further.

I go further. Out along the bared rock to where the water still moves over it, where the deeper pools are, the ones the tide does not fully empty, and I lie down on the cold wet stone with my arm in the water to the shoulder, feeling under the ledges the way I learned, slow, reading with my fingers what the eyes cannot reach. The cold of the water climbs my arm and sits in the bone and stays there past where the arm comes out, a second arm of cold inside the first. My fingers go stupid with it, far away, mine but not answering. There is something far back under a ledge — I feel the shape of it, smooth, hard, a shell the size of two fists, gripped to the rock the way the small ones grip — and I work my dead fingers around it and pull and it does not come, it is grown to the stone, and I brace and pull and the edge of it opens a line in my finger, sharp and clean, and the dark blood threads out into the water and is gone, taken, no trace. I get it loose. I drag it up. One shell. I crack it on the black stone and there is a knot of cold gray flesh inside, salt and sea and small, and I swallow it whole and the near mouth barely feels it go down. A pebble dropped down a well that has no bottom.

One shell. A day of building spent, and one shell to put against it. The cage-lie rises in me then, small and quick, the old voice that is not a voice, whispering that this is enough, that I should lie down on the warm stone and let it pass, that stillness is the kindness — and I drown it. I know that lie. It kept me alive in the hold by making me small, by making the body want less than it needed so it would not break against the wanting. Out here the same lie is a different thing. Out here it would lie me down warm and full of nothing and let the cold come and take me gentle. I spit it out into the wet, real spit, the body answering the not-voice the only way it can, and I stand up off the freezing rock and look down the long bared shelf to the gray moving water, and I know it the way I know the wind now: the shore has stopped giving and started asking.

---

I see them from the high rocks the next day, and the near mouth makes a sound in me that is almost the far one's sound.

Down on the low flat stones where the shelf goes out into the water, dark shapes lie hauled up out of the sea. I have no name for them in my mouth, only the shape — long bodies the color of wet stone, heavy and slow on the rock, slow the way a thing is slow when the water is its place and the land is only somewhere to rest between. They lie in the weak gray sun with their faces turned up to it, and now and then one humps itself a length along the rock and settles again, sighing the heavy body down, and they are fat. That is the thing the eye fixes on and will not leave. They are fat the way nothing on this shore is fat, the way I am not fat, the eel thin and the crab thin and I thinner than both of them now. They are made all of the fat the cold has eaten out of me, a whole body of it lying there in the gray, the very thing the wind has been pulling off my bones and pouring into the stone, kept here in a skin and breathing. The near mouth grinds, and grinds, and the wanting climbs up out of the belly into the back of the throat.

Under it the far one stirs. It does not ease — it never eases — but it leans, the way it leaned toward the night water, leans toward the fat dark shapes, and I make myself look off them, because the far mouth's leaning is not a hunger I can trust. It does not lean at the meat. I have learned that much. It leans at something the meat is only standing near.

I crouch on the high black rock with the wind off the water and I watch them, and I learn them, because I will not go down there knowing nothing and bleed for it the way I have bled for everything else. They are heavy on land. Slow. But not stupid — when a gull comes down near them one lifts its head fast, faster than the heavy body has any right to, and holds it up, the dark wet eye fixed, and does not settle again until the gull lifts off. They keep near the water. Always near. Three lengths, four, no more, between the fat bodies and the gray edge where the sea takes them back and the slowness on land becomes a thing in the water I cannot follow and will never follow. So I read it the way the hands taught me to read a wall I had to get over: to take one, I have to come between it and the sea. I have to be the thing standing in the four lengths it needs. I have to make myself, for the length of one killing, the wall it cannot get through.

I have nothing to do it with. Hands, and a body gone thin, and the cage-patience. I go back to the den in the dark and I lie against the warm stone with the fire stood up and I turn it over the way the body turns a thing it has to solve — slow, the head watching while the hands remember what they know. I have taken eels with my hands. A seal is not an eel. A seal is the most meat I have ever stood near, and the most that can hurt me, and between those two the near mouth grinds all night and does not let me sleep down deep, only skim along the top of sleep, surfacing, going under, the dark shapes hauling themselves across the back of my shut eyes toward a sea I keep standing in the way of.

---

I make the thing to do it with, because the hands cannot do it alone.

The drift-spars I leaned over the den mouth — there are more of them up the high tide-line, the long gray bones of old wood the sea throws and forgets, bleached pale as the dead. I take the straightest. It is as long as I am and harder than it looks, dried to a thing with no give left in it. The end is broken to a rough point already, and I work that point against the black stone the way I worked stone against stone learning the den, grinding, turning, the splinters lifting off it, the wood going pale where the gray skin wears away, a wet bright wood under the dead skin like the meat under the seal's hide. It is slow. It eats the whole of the day. My hands, barely closed from the den-work, the splits in them only just gone shut, open again — a new line tears across the palm where the spar turns under my grip, the dark blood welling, warm and red, the one warm bright thing in all that gray grinding, and I watch it well and let it pass to the shelf without a name. By the count of the body I knew before this place, that split should not close before a moon. It will close in three days. I know it now the way I know the tide. I do not look at the knowing. I put the bleeding hand back on the wood and grind.

The point comes up sharp. Sharper than the broken end ever was, ground to a real point, a thing made to go in and not to push and tear. I test it against the meat of my own thumb, a bead of dark coming up, and I pull back fast. A tooth. I have made a tooth out of dead wood and grinding and the blood off my own hands, the same as the fire made warmth out of dead wood and the den made a held place out of dead stone — the pulling of a living use out of one dead thing after the next, never stopping, only finding the next dead thing and pulling again. I sleep that night with the spear laid against the warm inner stone beside me, the point dry and pale in the firelight, and I keep my mother's name held in tight behind the gap where the three teeth are gone, the way I keep it every night, the one thing those hands never found because they never knew to reach for it. The near mouth grinds, and I do not feed it, because tomorrow it eats or it does not.

---

I come down on them in the gray before the sun, into the wind, the way I learned the wind on the day I found the still pocket.

The wind is the thing. The wind carries my smell, and a seal's head comes up fast — I learned that on the high rock, watched it learn me — so I come into the wind, with the cold pushing flat against my face and my smell going back behind me to the empty land where there is nothing left alive to warn. The black rock is wet under my bare feet and I read it with the soles the way I read the pools with my fingers, slow, each step set where the foot will not slide and ring stone on stone and lift the heavy heads. The spear is in both hands, cold and dry. The near mouth is loud, loud enough to hear over the wind. I push it down under the cage-patience and I go.

They are hauled up where I saw them, dark and fat on the low flat stone, three lengths from the gray edge, four. I pick the one nearest the land, the one furthest from its water, the one I have the best chance of being the wall to. I come around in the wind to put myself between it and the sea — low, slow, a stone that moves, the way the cold creeps and you do not see it cross the floor until you feel it has already crossed and is sitting against your back. The body knows this creeping. The body did this in the hold, made itself nothing, crept under the notice of the hands, learned to be a thing not worth the turning of a head. I am close. I am closer than I have been to anything that could hurt me since the sea took me and gave me to this rock. I can smell it now — a thick smell, warm and rank and oily, fat and salt and wet fur all in one, the smell of all that held heat lying there breathing in a skin — and the far mouth leans so hard toward it that for the length of one breath I am not the boy creeping with the spear at all, I am only the leaning, a thing pointed at the warmth with nothing of me left over, and that empties me out worse than the seal's strength does, and I haul myself back into the boy, back into the hands, back into the cold point of the wood, holding the name in tight behind my teeth like a stone to bite down on.

It sees me.

The head comes up off the rock fast, the dark wet eye finds me and fixes, and the whole heavy body gathers itself to move — toward the water, toward the gray edge that is three lengths off and behind me now, because I have done the one thing right, I am between, I am the wall. It comes at the gap. It humps the heavy length of itself across the rock faster than the slowness ever said it could, straight at the sea, straight through where I stand, and there is no thinking left in me, none, only the body, and the body drops its whole thin weight down behind the spear and drives the ground-sharp point into the dark shape as it passes — into the front of it, the thick of it, behind the lifted head, where the fat lies deep — and the wood goes in.

It goes in and the seal screams.

I did not know it would have a voice. Nothing told me. It is a high wet broken sound, almost a child's sound, almost a sound I have made myself, behind a hand, in a dark place — and it goes straight through me and the hands want to fly open and let go and run, the whole body wants it, and I do not let them. I drive my weight down onto the wood and into the rock under it and the body bucks against me, huge, all muscle under the fat, throwing me like the sea threw me, and I go down onto the wet stone with the spear still gripped in my torn hands and still in the seal, the two of us tangled together on the cold rock in the gray light. It is so strong. It is stronger than I am by far, a body of warmth and water against a body the cold has eaten down to the wrists and the ribs, and for a long stretch it is not a killing at all, it is only a holding-on, my whole weight thrown on the wood and the wood in the wound and the seal screaming and heaving toward its sea and dragging the two of us with it, both slick, the rock peeling the skin off my side as we go, the cold edge of the water coming nearer each time it hauls. I hold. I do not have its strength so I have only the holding, the cage-patience turned the other way at last, a thing that took and now a thing that will not let go.

And then the warmth comes out of it.

It comes out around the wood, dark, fast, far more than ever wells from my own splits — a flood of it, hot, running over the cold black rock, over my hands, up my forearms. The smell of it is iron, bright iron, and the taste of it is at the back of my mouth though not a drop has touched my mouth, the same taste my own blood leaves when a tooth-gap fills, only so much more of it, a whole body of warmth pouring out onto the stone and steaming faint in the cold air where it spreads. The heaving slows. The scream thins to a wet thread and then to nothing. The huge strength that was dragging me to the sea goes out of the body the way heat goes off a fire left in the open, fast and then all at once, gone between one breath and the next, and the dark shape sags down under me onto the rock and is still. The warmth keeps running out of it, slowing, pooling, going cold at the edges where it spreads thin, and I lie on top of it with the spear still gripped in my torn hands, shaking, breathing hard through the gap in my teeth where the name is held, my whole front soaked in a warmth that is not mine.

I have killed it. The near mouth is silent now, struck silent — here is the answer to it, here is meat past all the meat I have ever stood near, a body of fat and held heat lying under me on the cold stone. And the far mouth — the far mouth opened all the way when the blood came, opened wide, leaned the whole of itself toward the running warmth, and it did not close on it. It is not closing. The body of the seal is going cold under me and the far mouth is still open over it, still leaning, still not fed. It leaned hardest not when the seal lay dead and fat but when the warmth was running and the scream was in the air. Not the meat. The spilling. I lie in the iron smell and the steaming dark and I put my eyes anywhere but on it.

---

I drag it up to the den slow, the long heavy length of it, the way it dragged me toward the sea — only now I am the one hauling, and it does not fight, and the warmth has gone out of it cold.

It costs the whole of the day. The body that the cold ate thin, the body I threw against the seal's fat strength and barely held, now spends itself again hauling that strength up the bared rock, stone by stone, length by length, the dead weight of it nothing like the eel and everything like the building-stone, a downward weight with no give in it and no kindness, the weight of a thing that wants only to lie still and be the sea's again. I bleed for it. The rock takes more skin off the hands and the side. I do not stop. This is meat for days, meat past counting, the shore does not give twice, and I will not leave a body of warmth on the flat for the gulls and the gray water to come and take back. I get it to the lee mouth. I cannot get the whole of it through. I work at it where it lies in the wind, the way I learned to work the eel, only there is so much of it — the thick fat under the skin, finger-deep, pale and dense, and under the fat the dark red meat, the iron rising off all of it and going into the near mouth and the far one both at once, the two hungers standing side by side over the open body, one that will be fed and one that will not.

I cook it that night in the den the way a creature cooks in its own place.

The fire stands up gold and does not duck, the wind shut out beyond the wall, and I hold the dark meat to the steady flame on the green stick and the fat runs, hisses, flares, the smell going up thick and rank and good, crawling along the slab roof, filling the whole walled dark with itself. When I tear into it the warmth is held in by the held warmth all around me, hot the whole way through, and I eat slow, chewing on the side where the teeth still are, careful of the gap, careful of the name behind it. The near mouth feeds at last. Fills. Goes quiet. The grinding goes out of the belly for the first time since the den-work began, the fist down there opening and staying open, easy, and I had near forgotten the body could feel that. The fat is the thing. The fat sits in me warm and stays warm, putting back into the body the very heat the cold ate out of it, and I feel myself thicken against the cold even as I sit there chewing, slow, a little, the first time the shore has put back into me more than it took.

And the far mouth does not feed.

I knew it would not. I have learned no meat answers it. But here is the thing I had not known: that a full belly does not quiet it the way a full belly quiets the other. The near mouth is shut and easy and forgetting, and the far one stays open over all of it, over the warm thick belly and the quiet, still leaning, still wanting, as if the seal had only shown it the shape of the thing it wants and not given it the thing itself. I sit in the held warmth with the fat going into me and the second mouth open over all of it, open wider than any meat will ever fill, and I understand it low down where I understand everything, the thing I will carry off this rock whenever the rock lets me go: that I can feed the body. I can feed it past full, thicken it, keep it. And the other mouth will watch me kill to do it, and want the killing more than the meat, and lean toward the warmth running over the stone, and never once close.

I do not look at it straight. I have learned not to look at that one straight. I feed the fire and lay another stone across the low mouth and put my back to the warm inner rock, the iron still drying brown on my hands, the fat warm in my belly, the spear dry and pale beside me with the dark dried hard on its point.

The cold comes for the blood in the night.

I wake to feed the coals, the body doing the work half in sleep, and I see it — the frost crept out in its wide white ring across the black floor, glittering where the firelight finds it, still and waiting at the edge of the warm. It rings me the way it always rings me, never where my body is, never where the fire burns, holding off from the heat of me by a hand's width all the way around. But it has reached the seal-meat I set by on the cold side, the dark cut pieces laid out on the bare stone, and where it touches them the warmth of them holds. They do not turn. They do not soften or go the way meat goes when it sits. The cold sits down on them and keeps them, keeps them the way it keeps everything it touches, still and unchanged at the edge of the heat, the killing held in them exactly as it was, the spilling stopped and kept. I look at the kept meat in the white ring and the heat-wrong of me and the cold-wrong of the floor holding each other off across the small place I built, and I do not understand it.

I put it on the shelf with the breath that won't smoke and the wounds that close too soon and the warm stone that gives and never asks. The shelf is crowded now, four wrong things standing on it in a row, and I do not take any of them down and turn it over, because turning them over is no use, they do not come apart in the hands. I keep my eyes on the fire. The iron taste sits at the back of my mouth where the name is held, the two of them close together in the dark behind my teeth, the name I keep and the taste I cannot spit out. And the far mouth stays open over the full belly and the kept meat and the steady gold flame, leaning still toward a thing none of it answers, none of it ever answers.

I have spilled the most warmth I have ever spilled, and the cold kept it for me, and the second mouth watched, and wanted more.
