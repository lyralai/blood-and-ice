# Chapter 8: Mastery

I taste the storm before I see it.

It comes on the back of my tongue at the low-light, a metal under the salt, the way blood sits behind a loose tooth before it pulls. The water out past the channel has gone the colour of old smoke. I stand on my high rock with the wind combing my hair flat and I read the whole shore the way I read my own hands, and I know exactly how the next day will go, every step of it, before I take one.

I have three days of meat in the cold-cache under the slab. The seep is running clean, finger-thin off the black. The brand-coal sleeps in its fist of kelp. I am fourteen winters of something, or near it; I stopped trusting the count. And there is nothing on this black rock that can put a tooth in me I did not choose to let in.

So. A storm. Good. Let it try.

---

I work the flats while the light holds.

The seals come in ahead of weather, hauling out fat and stupid on the low rocks, and I do not even need the spear for this. I know the one channel where the haul-out spills, the blind side where the wind carries my scent away over the water and not down onto them. I come along the wet stone with my weight in my heels, slow, in the spaces between their looking-up. A bull as long as I am tall. I take him at the neck before the herd has finished its first bark, and the heat comes out over the black rock, steaming, and the gut-hunger lifts its head and eats.

This is the easy mouth to feed. I have known that a long time.

I do not waste him. The fat goes for the brand and for the long burning. The stomach makes a bucket, the way it made the bucket I poured to glass the channel water that froze and held its shape till morning. I gut and quarter on the slab with my hands moving on their own, no thought in them, and I am up the rocks with the haul cached and weighted before the herd has gone back to sleep. The whole of it takes less time than it takes the light to slide one finger down the cliff.

There was a season this work would have killed me. I can feel the boy who could not do it, still in here somewhere, far down and cold. I have grown a long way past his reach.

---

The storm comes in at full dark and I am already home.

The crack takes me the way it has always taken me, my own shape worn into the black. I have packed the low end with turf and seal-hide against the wind, and I lie in the dry of it with the weather screaming a hand's breadth from my face and not touching me. The warm stone is at my back. Still warm. In a cold that is stripping the frost off the cliff in sheets and flinging it past the mouth of the crack, the stone holds its heat against my spine like a thing with blood in it, and I have long stopped asking it why. It is mine. It is part of the home. That is all the why I need.

I lie and listen to the island fight the sea. I know every voice in it. The boom under the pinch where the channel fills. The high reed-whistle off the broken rock to the north. The slabs taking the wind and shrugging it. Nothing in that whole black chorus is a thing I do not know the name and shape of, and I lie inside it warm and undrowned, the longest tooth on the shore, and I should be — what. Full. I should be full.

I breathe out into the cold of the crack mouth.

There is no smoke of me on the air.

I notice it the way you notice a stone in a known path that was not there yesterday. The frost comes in past the turf and it beads white on the hide, on the stone, on everything but the bare skin of my arm where it should bead thickest, and it rings clear of me, a hand's-width of black wet stone around my body where the white will not come. My arm lies bare in the one warm circle on a freezing shore. The cold files it away in me, quick, with the rest. The place I keep them is fuller than it was.

A thing happens in the meat of me and there is no.

So I close my eyes, and the storm screams, and I do not even need to keep one ear open. Nothing is coming. Nothing ever comes anymore.

---

In the grey after, the shore is washed new and the wrack is heavy on the tide-line, and I walk my whole kingdom because the body wants to walk it.

Down past the pinch, where the cat's bones lie scattered and clean now, gulled and salt-bleached. I crouch by the long jaw a while the way I crouch there sometimes. The still cold part of me bows its head. Then I get up and go on.

I have made a road of it. The same road, every low-light, my pads the only pads on it. I know it so well I could walk it blind, and some days I do, eyes shut, hand on the cold rock, the whole circuit drink and cache and brand and back, just to feel whether the island has moved under me in the night.

It never has.

---

The cut comes from nothing. A wrack-slick rock, a slip, a barnacle's edge laid open down the meat of my palm to the white. It should lay me up. A hand is everything out here.

I look at it bleed. I bind it with hide and I go on, because there is no one to bind it for me and no reason to lie still, and in the morning I take the binding off to look at the rot starting, the way it starts.

There is no rot. There is a seam. Where the meat was open to the bone there is a dark line, closed, the cold sealing it at the edges the way it sealed my arm under the cat, days where it should be a moon's turning, and I flex the hand and it answers, whole. I stand on my black stone in my grey light and I open and close the new-whole hand and I feel the wrongness of it go all through me, cold, and there is no one to show it to.

That is the thing. That is the thing under all of it.

I have closed a hole in my own hand in three days and the only witness on the whole black shore is me. If I died on this rock the rock would not know it had held the thing that does not smoke, that does not frost, that does not rot. The seals would not know. The clean white bones in the pinch would not know. The wrongness wants to be seen the way the cut wanted to be bound, and there is no eye on the shore to see it but my own, and my own is not enough. It was never the kind of hunger an eye of my own could feed.

---

I climb to the high lookout in the last of the light, the way I climb most nights now, though there is nothing left to watch for.

I have killed the thing that owned this place. I have learned its every stone. I walk its road blind and it answers under my pads — mine, all the way to the waterline and past it. I stand on my high rock over all of it with my healed hand on the cold stone and I look out.

I look out the way I have looked out every night since the war went quiet. Out past the channel. Out past the smoke-coloured water. Out at the long grey edge where the sea stands up and becomes the sky, where nothing has ever come, where nothing ever will.

The second mouth opens under my ribs into all that empty and I let it. I am done telling it there is nothing out there. I hold the name behind my teeth in the gap where three are gone, Rook, the only word I own, and I say it to the water with no sound, the way you say a thing you have stopped expecting an answer to.

And the water answers.

Far out on the smoke-grey, where the sea meets the sky, where nothing has ever come — a shape. Small. Wrong. Made by hands.

A sail.
