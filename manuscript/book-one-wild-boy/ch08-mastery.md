# Chapter 8: Mastery

I taste the storm before I see it.

It comes on the back of my tongue at the low-light, a metal under the salt, the way blood sits behind a loose tooth before it pulls. The water out past the channel has gone the colour of old smoke. I stand on my high rock with the wind combing my hair flat and I read the whole shore the way I read my own hands, every cove and slab and runnel of it laid out under me, and I know exactly how the next day will go, every step of it, before I take one. There is a thing the body does now that it did not do when the sea first gave me up onto the black. It looks at the weather and it is not afraid of the weather. It counts the weather the way I count meat.

I have three days of meat in the cold-cache under the slab, cut and weighted and held in the white. The seep is running clean, finger-thin off the black, and I have widened its lip with a chip of stone so it pools before it spills. The brand-coal sleeps in its fist of kelp at the back of the crack, banked low, breathing slow, never let to die — I have not struck fresh fire in more turnings than I can hold, because I have not had to. I am fourteen winters of something, or near it; I stopped trusting the count a long way back, when the counting stopped mattering. And there is nothing on this black rock that can put a tooth in me I did not choose to let in.

So. A storm. Good. Let it try.

---

I work the flats while the light holds.

The seals come in ahead of weather, hauling out fat and stupid on the low rocks, packing the haul-out three deep where the channel breaks the swell, and I do not even need the spear for this. I have the spear. I made it well, the head knapped down off a long flake and lashed with gut to a shaft I straightened over the coal across a whole season of patience. But I do not need it. I know the one channel where the haul-out spills, the blind side where the wind carries my scent away over the water and not down onto them. I come along the wet stone with my weight in my heels, slow, in the spaces between their looking-up, reading the herd's looking the way I read the tide — there is a rhythm in a herd, a rise and settle. You move in the settle. You are stone in the rise.

A bull as long as I am tall. I take him at the neck before the herd has finished its first bark, the whole weight of me come down behind it, and the heat comes out over the black rock, steaming in the cold, and the gut-hunger lifts its head out of the dark under my ribs and eats. The iron rises to the back of my mouth where the gap is. The near mouth feeds and is glad and is simple about it.

This is the easy mouth to feed. I have known that a long time. There was a season, far down and cold in me, when the spilling was a thing I had to set my teeth against, when the warmth coming out over my hands leaned on something in me that I would not let it have. I do not feel that set of the teeth now. The hand comes down. The heat comes out. The mouth eats, and there is no argument left in the doing of it. The bull is meat. I take the meat.

I do not waste him. The fat goes for the brand and for the long burning, rendered down and kept. The stomach makes a bucket, the way it made the bucket I poured to glass the channel water that froze and held its shape till morning. I gut and quarter on the slab with my hands moving on their own, no thought in them, the knowing gone all the way down into the meat of the hands where I do not have to call it up — and I am up the rocks with the haul cached and weighted, the offal off the slab and into the tide for the gulls, before the herd has gone back to sleep. The whole of it takes less time than it takes the light to slide one finger down the cliff.

There was a season this work would have killed me. I can feel the boy who could not do it still in here somewhere, far down and cold, the soft thing the sea spat up that did not know a haul-out from a hunger. I have grown a long way past his reach. I do not always know if that is a thing the body won, or a thing the island took.

---

The storm comes in at full dark and I am already home.

The crack takes me the way it has always taken me, my own shape worn into the black, the stone curved to my back and my shoulder where I have lain a thousand nights until the cold rock remembers the boy. I have packed the low end with turf and seal-hide against the wind, layered and weighted, and I lie in the dry of it with the weather screaming a hand's breadth from my face and not touching me. The warm stone is at my back. Still warm. In a cold that is stripping the frost off the cliff in sheets and flinging it past the mouth of the crack, the stone holds its heat against my spine like a thing with blood in it, steady, giving and giving and never once asking back, and I have long stopped asking it why. It is mine. It is part of the home. That is all the why I need, and the not-asking is its own kind of skill, learned slow, the way I learned the haul-out: you set the wrong thing down and you stop turning it in the hands and after enough turnings of not-turning it grows quiet on its shelf.

I lie and listen to the island fight the sea. I know every voice in it. The boom under the pinch where the channel fills and the swell drives into the dead end of it and has nowhere to go. The high reed-whistle off the broken rock to the north. The slabs taking the wind and shrugging it off, the long low grind of water working stone it cannot move. Nothing in that whole black chorus is a thing I do not know the name and shape of. I made myself its master one voice at a time, learned each sound until it could not wake me, and now I lie inside the storm the way a tooth lies in a jaw — held, fitted, part of the bite. The longest tooth on the shore. Warm and undrowned in the heart of the thing that tried to drown me.

And I should be — what. Full. I have meat and fire and a roof of black stone and the storm cannot reach me and nothing on the island can. By every count the body keeps I should be full.

I breathe out into the cold of the crack mouth.

There is no smoke of me on the air.

I notice it the way you notice a stone in a known path that was not there yesterday — the foot finds it before the eye does. The frost comes in past the turf and it beads white on the hide, on the stone, on the very lashing of the spear where it leans, on everything but the bare skin of my arm where it should bead thickest, where the warmth of me should pull it in fastest, and it rings clear of me. A hand's-width of black wet stone around my body where the white will not come, a dark moat in all that climbing white, the shape of me cut out of the frost and left bare. My arm lies in the one warm circle on a freezing shore and the frost crowds to the edge of it and stops. It simply stops.

The cold files it away in me, quick, with the rest. The shelf where I keep the wrong things is fuller than it was when I was a boy at the waterline. The breath that will not smoke. The frost that rings me and spares my body. The warm stone. The wounds that close in days. I do not take any of them down and turn them over. They do not come apart in the hands. I have made a master's peace with not knowing them, the way I have made a master's peace with everything on this rock — by knowing it so well it cannot wake me.

A thing happens in the meat of me then and there is no name for it, no eye to lay it against, no other body anywhere on the black to nod and say yes, that, I have felt that too. It opens and there is nothing on the shelf to set beside it and nothing in the storm to answer it.

So I close my eyes, and the storm screams, and I do not even need to keep one ear open. Nothing is coming. Nothing ever comes anymore.

---

In the grey after, the shore is washed new and the wrack is heavy on the tide-line, weed and broken shell and the pale wood the sea brings from nowhere, and I walk my whole kingdom because the body wants to walk it.

Down past the pinch, where the cat's bones lie scattered and clean now, gulled and salt-bleached, the long jaw and the cage of the ribs and the wide pan of the skull where the eye was that watched me the night I learned what I was strong enough to do. I crouch by the long jaw a while the way I crouch there sometimes. It was the thing that owned this place. It would have had me, soft and salt-sick and new, if I had been one degree less than I made myself. Now it is white sticks in the wrack and I am the longest tooth on the shore, and the still cold part of me bows its head over the bones — not in sorrow, not in winning. The head bows of its own weight. My jaw answers the dead jaw across the wrack, tooth to emptied tooth. The bones do not answer. They are the closest thing to company the island keeps. Then I get up and go on.

I have made a road of it. The same road, every low-light, my pads the only pads on it, worn pale into the black where my weight goes down. I know it so well I could walk it blind, and some days I do, eyes shut, hand on the cold rock, the whole circuit drink and cache and brand and back, just to feel whether the island has moved under me in the night, whether one stone has shifted, whether the world has done one thing I did not already know it would do.

It never has.

---

The cut comes from nothing. A wrack-slick rock, a slip, a barnacle's edge laid open down the meat of my palm to the white. It should lay me up. A hand is everything out here — a hand is the spear and the climb and the kill and the fire, a hand is the whole difference between the thing that eats and the thing in the wrack that gets eaten.

I look at it bleed. The heat comes up bright out of the white of it, the iron rising, and I bind it with hide and I go on, because there is no one to bind it for me and no reason to lie still, and in the morning I take the binding off to look at the rot starting, the way it starts, the way every deep cut starts when there is no help for it — the angry edges, the swelling heat that is the wrong heat, the slow turn toward losing the hand or losing more than the hand.

There is no rot. There is a seam. Where the meat was open to the bone there is a dark line, closed, the cold sealing it at the edges the way it sealed my arm under the cat, days where it should be a moon's turning and more. I flex the hand and it answers, whole. I stand on my black stone in my grey light and I open and close the new-whole hand, open and close, watching the seam hold, and I feel the wrongness of it go all through me, a cold deeper than the storm's cold, and there is no one to show it to.

That is the thing. That is the thing under all of it, under the mastery and the meat and the warm stone and the storm I sleep through. That is the second mouth, and no kill has ever been shaped right to feed it.

---

I have closed a hole in my own hand in three days and the only witness on the whole black shore is me.

If I died on this rock the rock would not know it had held the thing that does not smoke, that does not frost, that does not rot. The seals would not know; they would haul out fat and stupid over my bones the way they haul out over the cat's. The clean white bones in the pinch would not know. The storm would scream the same scream over an empty crack and never feel the lack of the one body it could not freeze. I am the only eye on the whole shore, and the wrongness in me wants to be seen the way the cut wanted to be bound, the way the storm wants the slabs to take it and answer back. It leans out of me into all that grey and finds nothing to lean on.

There is no eye on the shore to see it but my own, and my own is not enough. It was never the kind of hunger an eye of my own could feed. I learned the haul-out and the road and the storm and the cat. I learned to bank the coal and glass the water and read the weather off the back of my tongue. There is one thing on this whole black rock I have not learned how to master, and it is the wanting to be known, and it grinds in me the way the channel grinds the stone it cannot move, low and endless, under everything I have won.

---

I climb to the high lookout in the last of the light, the way I climb most nights now, though there is nothing left to watch for.

I have killed the thing that owned this place. I have learned its every stone. I walk its road blind and it answers under my pads — mine, all the way to the waterline and past it, mine to the limit of where the body can go and not one finger further. I stand on my high rock over all of it with my healed hand on the cold stone and I look out, and the whole of my kingdom lies under me, every voice of it known, every law of it learned, and I am its master, and its master is alone in it the way a tooth is alone in a jaw the sea has emptied of everything but the one tooth.

I look out the way I have looked out every night since the war with the island went quiet, since there was nothing left to fight and nothing left to fear and nothing left to learn. Out past the channel. Out past the smoke-coloured water. Out at the long grey edge where the sea stands up and becomes the sky, where nothing has ever come, where nothing ever will.

The second mouth opens under my ribs into all that empty and I let it. I am done telling it there is nothing out there. I have told it that lie a thousand nights and it has never once believed me, and tonight I am too full of mastery and too empty of everything else to keep lying. I hold the name behind my teeth, in the gap where three are gone, the dark hollow where my tongue has guarded it through every taking — Rook, the name my mother gave me before the ship, before the hands, the one word those hands never found because they never knew to reach for it, the last un-stolen piece of who I am. I hold it the way I have held it through every kill and every cut and every frost that rings me. And I say it to the water with no sound, the way you say a thing you have stopped expecting an answer to.

And the water answers.

Far out on the smoke-grey, where the sea meets the sky, where nothing has ever come — a shape. Small. Wrong. Made by hands.

A sail.
