# Chapter 4: The Rot and the Den

The fire taught me the first hard law of keeping: a thing kept alive must be kept somewhere. The rain teaches me the second.

It comes on the fourth low after the fire, and it does not come like any rain I have a memory of. It comes all at once and from the whole sky, a falling so thick the green drowns to a smear behind it, and it does not fall on me so much as it stands on me, a warm weight pouring down through the leaves with no edge and no end. My little fire — the fire I bled for, the fire I carried in three red eyes behind the wall of my own body — dies under it in the time it takes me to throw my arms over the coals. The dying is a small hiss and then nothing. Then only the rain, hammering the black ash flat. I crouch over the dead pit in the streaming dark and I understand the law late, and in the body first, and only after the lack of it has already cost me.

The den-crack does not hold the rain off. The water comes up the floor of it warm and brown and full of moving things, and it comes down the back of it where the seep is, and there is no part of the crack the green does not pour into. The crack is a place the shore gave me. It holds off nothing. There is a part of the old days in me that knows what it is to be cornered in a small place with the dark coming in — that waits, even now, for the hand to find the back of my neck — and the green here does its own thing instead, which is worse, which is to climb in after me wet and warm and alive and begin to eat.

I need a place. Not the crack the green gave me. A place I make.

---

In the steaming after the rain, when the whole green smokes white off the leaves and the standing water breathes its rot back up into the air, I go looking the way I have learned to look — slow, along the law of the land, reading what is already here before I ask it for anything.

The crack is no good and I knew it in my spine before I knew it in my head. It sits low, where the land folds down toward the standing water, and the low places here are where the green pours its rot. The floor is never dry. By the second low after a rain it goes soft and sour, a film comes up on the standing water in it, and the smell climbs into the back of my throat and stays. And the swarms find it. The crack is a thing with one mouth and no draft, and the biting clouds come up off the swamp-edge at dusk and pour into it after my heat with nowhere to go. The dark in there is a living dark, a dark that crawls, and I lie in it and give my blood up a bite at a time until the light comes back.

So I climb. I go up, away from the water, into the broken stone the rain showed me — for the rain that drowned my fire showed me one other thing, which is where the land sheds it and where the land holds it. I read the green now the way I once read a tide I cannot rightly remember, with the soles and the fingers and the back of the tongue, and the soles tell me up. Up out of the rot-bottom. Up where the old stone breaks pale and sun-bleached through the green and the water runs off it and does not stand. I climb until the swamp-stink thins behind me and the air loses some of its weight. Somewhere in the climbing the far hunger, low down, does a thing it has not done since the fire: it eases. Only a little. Only for a breath. The body that has spent its whole life being kept low knows the worth of high without the head telling it, and the deep want answers, faintly, to the climb. I would press at that if I had the strength to spare. I do not. I climb.

I find the place where the ridge has broken in some old breaking of the world. A great slab of the pale stone leans out from the broken face and rests its top against the rock above, and under the lean of it the ground is dry — dry, on this weeping green, where nothing is dry — because the slab has been shedding the rain off this hollow longer than I have been alive. I crawl in under it. The roof is a hand and a half above my head, the back solid stone, the floor bare grit and old leaf-fall gone to powder, and when I press my palm to it, it is dry to the wrist. The rain that drummed the whole green flat last night never once touched this ground.

I lie in the dry dark of it and feel the shape of the place close around me. The deep want eases again, wider this time, like that first night in the light of the fire, and I lie still and let it. A held place. The body has wanted a held place since the sea spat it up. This is the place. The green made it long before me. I only have to finish it.

---

Finishing it is the work, and the work is against the green, and the green does not give. It only takes, and waits, and takes again.

The slab keeps the rain off the high side, but the low sides stand open. Open to the rain that drives in sideways when the wind turns. Open to the things that walk the night. Open to the swarms that will find the heat of me wherever I lie down. I have to close them. I have to make walls where the world left gaps, and I have what the broken ridge gives me, which is stone — the pale rubble thrown everywhere along the high line, blocks the size of my chest and bigger. And I have what the green gives me, which is everything, more than I can ever use, all of it wet, all of it rotting, all of it wanting to climb into a wall and pull the wall back down into the ground.

I lift the first stone and the cost of it goes straight into me. It is heavy in a way the fish and the root-pools never taught me, a dead downward weight with no give in it and no kindness, the weight of a thing that does not care whether I live. And it is hot — sun-hot on its top face, slick with green slime on its under face where it sat in the wet — so it slides in my grip and fights me two ways at once. I stagger it three steps to the low side of the hollow and set it down and the noise it makes is flat and final, stone on stone. My arms shake. The near mouth, the plain belly-hunger, is never quiet here, and the stone spends it fast.

I go back for the next.

This is the cage-patience again, the patience the hands beat into me, the one thing I made out of all that taking. One stone. Then one stone. Then one stone. I learn how they sit — that a flat face wants a flat face under it, that a stone laid wrong rocks and topples and can take a finger when it goes, that the big ones go low and the small ones chink the gaps. I learn it by getting it wrong and bleeding for it. A block turns under my grip and comes down across my hand and I feel the bone go bright with pain and snatch the hand back and crouch over it, the breath hissing through the gap in my teeth, the dark blood welling up where the skin split.

I watch it well. Warm and red, the one warm bright thing, as always. By the count of lows it should not close before a moon turns, and here in the wet it should do worse than not close. Every cut on this green goes the green's way — swells and shines and weeps and turns the dark rot-color the leg turned in the crack, the color that means a thing under the skin has begun its own work. The green gets into every open hurt here. It does not get into me. I watch the lips of the split draw toward each other while I crouch, the blood already slowing, the edges already going pale and quiet the way no hurt on this green has any right to go, and it will be shut clean in three lows and gone in five, and the green will not have touched it.

I want to know why. I press my thumb to the closing edge and I hold the wrongness up to the light of my own head and ask it: *why you, why not the green's way, why are you not like every other live thing here.* But there is no word in me for the asking, and no word in the green that answers, and my arms are shaking and the wall is half a wall and the dark is coming. The question slides off me the way the green slides off me. I put the wrong thing on the shelf with the fever-air that does not sicken me and the bad water that does not knot me — not because I do not want it, but because I cannot hold it and the stone both, and the stone is what keeps me alive tonight. I put the hand back on the stone.

The walls of stone are only half the wall. The other half is the green, and the green I weave. Where the rubble runs out I cut the long tough vine and the broad stiff leaves and the cane that grows in stands by the high water, and I lash and lean and chink until the gaps between the stones go thick with it, butt-ends in the rubble and tops against the slab, the lighter stuff worked in behind. I leave a low mouth on the downhill side to crawl through, small as a crab's hole, small enough to close behind me with a last stone dragged across. And I learn, working, the second thing the green teaches about its own gifts: the cut vine and the cut leaf begin to rot the hour I cut them. The wall I weave green will be a wall of slime and stink and crawling life in three lows if I let it, a thing that feeds the swarms instead of keeping them off. So I cut more than I need and lay it to dry on the hot stone through the white middle of the lows, and I tear the rotted out and weave the dried in, and the wall becomes a thing I do not finish but keep, the way the fire is a thing I do not make but keep. Everything here is keeping. The green takes back what you do not keep, fast, and leaves no mark that you were ever there.

---

The work eats the lows. I sleep in the half-made hollow and wake stiff and start again, and the den comes up out of the broken ridge slow, one dead weight and one dried weave at a time, a hand's height, then a knee's height, then high enough to crouch behind and be hidden. And the night I close it for the first time is the night I learn what I made.

I have made the fire again. I made it the slow way, the torn way, with the bow and the spindle and the dry pith, because the first fire taught me the fire dies and I did not yet know how to carry it through the building. The small flame stands up gold in the held dark and it does not duck and it does not gutter, because the rain is on the far side of the slab and the wall breaks the night wind to a thread, and the smoke crawls up the back stone and out a gap I have left high for it and does not pool down to choke me. The light comes off the close rock all around — the leaned green, the chinked stone, the low pale roof a hand above my head — and fills the small space with a moving gold, and the heat rises and meets the slab and does not fly off into the night. It stays. It pools under the roof. It comes back down on me, and I keep it.

And then I learn the thing the den was truly for, and it is not the fire and it is not the rain.

It is the dusk-hour, when the white weight lifts off the green and the rot-warm dark fills with the things that own this place, and they come up off the swamp-edge for the live heat of me as they have come every low of my life here — the long whining clouds of the biting flies, the crawling cords of the ants that follow blood through the black, the soft wet things that drop from the leaves and feel for a place to bite in. They come up the ridge after my heat. I hear them find the den. I have braced for this, the giving-up of blood a bite at a time that is the price of every dark here, and I have built the walls half against it, to thin the clouds, to slow them. But the walls are not what stops them.

What stops them is the thing on the shelf.

I sit in the firelight and I watch the swarm reach the mouth of the den and pour in, and I watch it not land. The cloud comes to the edge of where my body is and it stays there, a hand's width off my skin and no nearer, a living wall of them held back from a clean wall of air that is the shape of me. They settle on the stone. They settle on the dried green of the weave and the wet green that has crept back into it. They crawl on the carrion the den's own warmth has begun to draw — a fish-spine I did not clean out, a knot of gut — and the carrion seethes with them, black and moving, while a hand's width off it my skin stays bare. The ants run their cords up the wall and along the roof and around me, around, and the soft things drop from the leaves at the mouth and feel along the floor and do not feel toward me.

I hold my hand out to it. The cloud bends away from my hand. There is no will to it, no fear in it, only the going-around, and where my hand passes the air is clean, and where it passes from the air closes thick with them again.

I sit very still and watch the green seethe and crawl over everything I have made and everything that dies in the den, and not me. Never me. A clean hand's-width all around the live heat of me, walled off from the whole crawling dark by nothing I built.

I want to understand it more than I have ever wanted to understand anything. I turn it in the firelight — the fever-air that leaves me clear, the bad water and the tainted fish that slide off me, the hurts that draw shut clean and fast, and now this, the green itself parting off my skin. There is a shape to it. All of it points one way, toward one thing in me, and I lean toward the thing the way I lean toward a sound in the dark — straining, reaching — and I cannot make it out. There is a wrongness in me the green keeps trying to name and cannot, and I cannot name it either. The head is too young and the green gave me nothing to think it with. So I keep my eyes on the fire and let the reaching go slack, because I am tired, and because it is enough, tonight, that I am — for no reason it will let itself know — not bitten. The low animal glad of a body that has been bitten its whole life and is, tonight, whole.

---

I cook in the den that night the way a creature cooks in its own den, a fish on the green stick held to the steady flame, the fat running and hissing, the brown good smell going up and crawling along the roof and pooling, mine, with no rain to drown it and no draft to snatch it away. And when I tear into the hot flesh I chew it on the side where the teeth still are, and I hold the last of the heat in the gap where the three are gone, the way you cup a coal, before I let it go down.

The gap. I run my tongue into it the way I do when the body is quiet enough to remember. Three teeth gone, knocked from a child's mouth by a hand that wanted the child still, and the slavers thought they took something from me when they took them. They did not know what they left. They left a hiding place. Behind the gap, under the tongue, in the one dark room of me the hands never reached, I keep the only thing that was always mine.

*Rook.*

My mother's word. Her gift, from before — before the ship, before the hold, before this green that is the whole shape of the world now. Her voice is in it somewhere far down, and her hands, the warmth of her hands that I cannot see anymore but can still, just barely, feel the shape of when I hold the word against the warm. I say it now, in the den I built, in the firelight, behind my teeth — never out loud, the green is always listening — *Rook, Rook,* and it is warm, the one warm thing that was never taken because it was never found.

They had their own sound for me. The slavers. A low ugly thing made behind their own teeth that meant *property, come-here, be-still*, the felt-shape of a hand closing on the back of my neck, and they put it on me the way you put a mark on a beast, and they thought it was what I was. It is not my name. It never was. That sound I left in the sea with the ship and the chains. This one I kept. The green can climb into everything I make and rot it back into the ground. It cannot find the word.

---

I lie back in the held warmth and the body thinks the way a body thinks when it has made the third hard thing and found the making does not stop.

*I pulled fire out of dead wood. I pulled a place out of dead stone and rotting green. First the fire. Then the den. One thing taught the next, and the pulling does not end.* This is the den's law, and it is the law under the green: a thing is never made here, only kept, borrowed back from the rot a low at a time. I have borrowed a dry place out of the wet world. The day the hands stop, the green will pour back in and pour it away, and it will be as if I was never here. I know this. I lie in the dry dark anyway, and I keep it.

And under the thought, low down, the deep want stirs. Not toward the fire. Not toward the fish. The den eased it — the climb eased it, the wall eased it, the being-held and not-flayed eased it — but eased is not fed. The den holds the heat. It does not hold the other thing. The far hunger opens under it, wider than the fire fills, wider than the walls reach, and waits there patient, the way the dark water waits past the ring of light. A den is a place to hold a body. This thing is not in the body. It is under it, like the rot is under the green, and no roof I lean over it will ever quite reach down to where it sits and aches.

I do not understand it. I would, if I could find the bottom of it, but I cannot, and I am too tired tonight to go down after it again. I feed the fire and lay the last stone across the low mouth to close the night out, and the swarm seethes on the far side of the wall and rings the clean shape of me on this side, and I put my back to the dry stone — fed, warm, walled, lit, the first place that was ever mine. And out past the leaned green, out past the chinked stone, far off down the night-ridge below the held stillness, the low sound comes up out of the dark that is not the rain and not the swarm.

I go still over the fire. I listen.

It comes again. Closer this time.
