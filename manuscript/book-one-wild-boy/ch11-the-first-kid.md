# Chapter 11: The First Kid

The young one comes back to the water the way the tide comes back. Both of them on the same string. I have learned the string by now.

---

Two low tides I waste pretending I will not go up to watch. Both times the body goes anyway, up the broken country to the lookout, low, belly to the black, the way I would not waste on a seal that is not there. There is no seal. There is the far shore and the standing smoke and the small uprights moving among the dens, and down at the tide-line, every time the water pulls back and bares the same wet stones it always bares, the young one.

It comes alone. That is the thing I keep turning in the mouth like a stone too smooth to crack. A whole pack up the shore, warm, close, lifting its small ones against itself — and it lets this one come down to the cold edge by itself, to work the rocks. Not cast out. I know cast out; I am cast out; I would smell it across the water if it were. This is the other thing. This is sent, or let, or trusted. Down it goes to the bared stones with the dens at its back and the fires red behind it, and it crouches where I crouch, and it turns the rock.

I lie still and I learn it the way I learn a herd.

It is not strong. The arms are thin. When it heaves a big stone off its seat it has to set its whole small weight against it, brace and shove and let the stone roll, and twice the stone wins and the young one sits back hard on the black with its hands empty. But it does not quit. It comes back at the same stone. It works the line of the tide the way I worked it for years before I had the years to count — start high where the easy soft things are, the weed, the limpets. Then down. Down into the colder bared country as the water goes on pulling back, into where the better things hide, the things that fold and dart, the things worth the cold. It reads which stone hides a crab and which hides only mud. It reaches quick into the black hollow under a turned rock, quick, before the thing there can think to move, the one true way to do it, the way the cold taught my own hand with no one there to teach it.

No one taught it either. Or someone did, up the shore, in the warm. But the crouch is the same crouch and the patience is the same patience and watching it is like watching my own body from outside, from across a strip of dead water, doing the work of staying alive — and not alone in the dark with it. In the open. In the last grey light. With a pack at its back and a fire that will be there, fed, when it climbs back up the cold to it.

That is the part the far mouth cannot leave. Not the gleaning. The going back.

---

I make a thing.

I do not choose to make it. The hands begin and I watch them, the way I watched the word boats come up out of me whole. They have decided before I have. Down in the broken country, in the grey between tides, I am sitting with my back to the warm stone and my hands are working a piece of driftwood, a pale weathered length the sea gave up, hard at the core and soft at the skin, and they are working it the way they work a thing they mean to keep.

I do not know what it is until it is most of the way to being it.

It is a seal. Small, the length of my forearm, the wood pared down with the black flake of stone I keep edged for the skinning. The blunt head, the long taper, the line of the back that a seal makes when it rolls the swell and gives to it. My hands know a seal better than they know anything; I have opened more of them than I have fingers and toes twice over; the shape lives in the hand the way the name once lived behind the teeth. So the hand makes a seal. Pares the wood thin and thinner. Rubs it smooth on the rough black till the skin of it goes warm under the working, till it sits in the palm like a thing that was always going to sit there.

And the whole time the hands work I am thinking a thing I have no business thinking. I am thinking: for the young one.

The thought is so strange the body laughs — except the body has no laugh left either, only the rasp, the cough with the growl on its back, and I shut it behind my hand on the cold stone. To give a thing. Not to take. Not the long tooth coming in along the blind side to take the meat off the herd. To set a thing down on the black and step back from it and let another come and lift it up. I have never once done that. There has been no one to do it to. There has been the stone that holds my back and does not bend, the coal in its fist of kelp, the gulls that follow me to steal, the sea that gives and takes and means nothing by either. I have been the only hands on this island so long that the idea of a second pair of hands closing on a thing I made opens a place under the breastbone, wide, hollow, and I have to stop working and press the flat of my arm against my chest and wait.

It does not ease. It is the second mouth, the one no meat answers. It only learns to be carried.

I finish the seal. I rub it one last time warm against my thigh and I hold it up in the grey and it is good. It is the truest thing I have made with my hands that was not made to kill or to keep me from dying. And holding it I understand what I mean to do with it, and the understanding turns the gut cold all the way down — because to give it I have to go to the water. To the open. To the bared stones the young one works. Across, or near across. Where hands could reach, where the many could pour down off their shore and sweep the one black tooth into the net the way they swept me, the hand on the neck, the ugly sound made behind the teeth that meant mine, mine, be still, you are a thing.

I hold the small wood seal and I shake. The body remembers the hold even as the hands hold the gift. Both at once. My hands close on the wood, holding to give. My feet have already turned for the dark behind me, for the crack, for the going-to-ground. The one skin pulled two ways and hauling.

---

It is not the young one I find at the water.

I come down in the dead grey before the lowest tide, down the blind drops I know in the dark, the wood seal warm in my fist from being held, around the long way, around the pinch where the channel narrows and the two shores come near enough that a strong swimmer in the warm months might dare it. I do not dare it. I come to my own side of the narrows, to a flat bared shelf the low tide gives me, close enough that across the strip I can see the far stones plain, the wet black backs of them coming up out of the falling water, the place the young one works.

But it is not the young one at the stones yet. Lower down, nearer the channel, on a rock half in the water, there is a thing I know better than I know my own face.

A pup. A seal pup, white-coated still, hauled up alone on the wet black where the swell can reach it, calling. The thin high call of a pup that has lost the herd, that the herd has left or the swell has cut off, the call that brings the long tooth down off the rocks. I have answered that call a hundred times. It means meat with no fight in it. It means the easy kill, the white coat parting under the black flake, the hot iron flood of it, the red mouth — the belly-hunger, the one that lives low and that meat does answer — opening wide and glad.

The belly-hunger opens now. It comes up red and certain. It says: down, take it, the throat is soft, the meat is fat, you are the longest tooth and there it lies. My feet have already started. The flake is already in the other hand. The body is already low and going, the old true going, the going that has kept me on the black all these winters while everything else in me was unmade.

And then up the far shore, thin, worn down by the dead water to almost nothing, a voice. The young one's voice. Calling — coming down to the tide as it always comes, picking its way down the cold to the stones, and not yet seeing the pup, but coming, coming to the very water where the pup lies calling and where the long tooth waits with the flake out and the red mouth open.

I stop.

I do not stop because I choose to. I stop because the two things in me lock. The belly-hunger says take the pup. The far mouth — the second mouth, the one no meat answers — says the young one comes, the young one comes to this water, and if the long tooth is on this water with the red mouth open and the white coat parting under its hands, then the young one will see what I am. Will see the thing on the black that takes the soft and the small and floods its mouth with iron. Will see a tooth and not a hand. And will never come down to the water again.

I stand on the bared shelf with the flake in one fist and the small wood seal in the other and I am torn so wide I think I will come apart down the middle and spill whichever mouth is under.

The pup calls. The young one comes. And the red mouth, the belly-hunger, the one that has fed me through every dark on this island — I make it close. I make it close with everything I have, the way I made the legs stay in the crack when the far mouth wanted them up and going toward the boats. I put the flake away. My hand shakes putting it away. The belly cramps, furious, a fist of want low down, the body certain I have just let the easy meat go and the body is right, I have, I will be hungrier for it, the cold will cost more tonight for the meat I did not take.

I let the pup live. For the young one's eyes. That is the first time the far mouth ever beat the red one. It costs the body. It costs it in the gut and in the cold to come. The second mouth does not feed the body; it eats it.

---

The young one reaches the stones.

It does not see me. I am low on my own shelf across the narrows, one black shape folded into the black, the way I have learned to be nothing. It works down the line the way it always works, turning the rock, reaching quick. And it comes to the pup.

It stops. It crouches a little way off and it looks at the white-coated thing calling on the wet black, and it does not raise a hand against it. It does not have a flake. It does not have the red mouth. It only looks, the small head tilted, and then it does a thing that opens the second mouth so wide it is all I am — it makes a sound at the pup. Soft. A low soft sound, up and down, no edges in it, the kind of sound the big ones must make to the small ones when they lift them up against the warm. A gentling sound. To a seal pup. To meat. It does not see meat. It sees a small lost thing calling for its herd, and it answers it soft, and then it steps wide around it and leaves it to its calling and goes on working the lower stones.

I have killed a hundred of those. It gentles one.

The strip of dead water between us has never been so wide and never been so thin. I want to go across it. The whole weight of me leans across it, warm toward warm, the way the blood leans toward the warm stone, the way the far mouth has leaned and leaned and found nothing to lean on. Across there is a young one that gentles meat and works the tide alone and goes back to a fire that is fed. Here is the long tooth that let the meat live so a child would not see its hands run red.

I set the wood seal down.

I do not throw it; I cannot reach across; the channel is too wide even here and the body will not, will not go into that open water toward the hands. I set it on the black shelf, on a flat high stone the next tide will not take, the small smooth seal my hands made, facing the far shore, facing the young one. I set it where, if ever the water drew the young one to this side, to my stones, it could not miss it. A thing given. A thing set down and stepped back from. The first such thing I have ever made or done.

And then the old habit, the deepest one, the want under all the other wants — to send a sound across with it. To call the way the young one calls up its shore. To lay one sound on the dead water that means me, this is mine, I made it, I am here, I am like you. I go in behind the teeth, into the gap where three were torn out, into the dark warm hollow where the one true thing used to live, the name my mother set on me before the ship and the hands, the name they never knew to reach for and so never took —

and it is empty.

Warm. Dark. Shaped like nothing. The tongue runs the scarred edge of the gum, the healed ridges of the slavers' work, into the smooth hollow where the word lay ten thousand nights, and brings up only the warmth that rises slow from the deep of me and lies down full in the print the name left, the way water lies down in the hollow a lifted stone leaves — the shape of the gone thing, and nothing like it.

I have a seal of wood to give and no name to send with it. I can make a thing with my hands. I cannot make the one sound that says whose hands made it. The island took that. Not the men. The men never reached it. I take my hand from my mouth. There is nothing to keep in. I have none to throw.

---

The light dies. Up the far shore the kept fires come red on the black. Low. Steady. Fed. The small uprights draw in toward them; the young one gathers its cold gleaning to its chest and climbs the cold back up to the warm that waits for it, fed, that does not go out.

It does not look at my shelf. It is too far, and the light is dead, and the wood seal is one small shape among the black shapes of the stones.

But the water will bare these same stones at the same hour. And the young one comes on the same string as the tide, down the cold to the gleaning, every low water, the way it has come and come. One day the tide may pull it wide, across toward the narrows, toward my side, to the stones it has not worked yet. And there on the flat high stone the next tide will not take, the small wood seal will be waiting, facing the shore, where it cannot miss it.

I let the pup live. I set the seal down. I have learned, on this one grey lowering tide, that the far mouth can beat the red one, and what it costs the body to let it.

And the far mouth, the one with no name left behind it to feed it, the one that leans and leans across the dead water and has, for the first time, set something of itself down on the far side of the lean — it lies down in the dark under my ribs and it does the only thing it knows how to do.

It begins to count the lights between.
