# Chapter 11: The First Kid

I am low on the spit of black, the long tongue of stone the tide swallows twice a light. I am there for crab. Belly to the wet rock, hand under the cold ledges, reading the haul-out across the channel out of the corner of me the way I always do now — watching the far shore, the kept fires, the small uprights that I cannot stop watching, the second mouth wide and counting.

And one of the small ones is on my side of the water.

Not far down the far shore. Here. On the near rocks, the seal-rocks, where the channel pinches thin enough to wade at the low — it must have waded, the young thing, must have come crab-gleaning along its own tide-line and not stopped where its pack stops, come out along the stones the way I would, head down, hands working, not looking up.

The way I would. That is the thing that goes cold in me. It hunts the low exactly like me, and the low is turning.

---

The tide is coming back.

I feel it before I see it, the way I feel everything on this rock now — the pull in the channel changing its mind, the water sucking back to gather itself, the smell of the deep coming up. The pinch fills fast. I know this. I learned it on my own skin, learned it caught and clinging with the black going to white water under me. The road the small one waded out on is already gone under. It does not know. Its head is down. Its hands are in the rock.

A name comes thin across the water — high, up the far shore, a big one calling, the road from head to mouth open and easy, the call going out for a young thing that does not call back because it is here, on my rocks, with its ears full of its own work.

The water takes the low stones. The small one looks up at last and the looking-up is the wrong shape. Quick. Stiff. The shape a thing makes when the ground it stood on is suddenly sea.

---

I do not decide.

There is no road from my head to my legs that I walk down. The legs go. The thing that survived the hold, the thing that lived under the ice of me all this time and never asked to be fed — it does not ask now. It throws me off the spit and into the channel.

The cold white water closes over my chest. It rings me, files me sharp all around. The blood-warmth comes up easy and does not feel it. It comes up and stays.

I go through the pinch in three strokes, four, feet finding the black I have crossed blind a hundred lows. The small one is going under. The current has it by the legs, turning it, and its hands beat the water the way hands beat the dark of a hold, and its mouth is open and the sound coming out of it is the high one, the one with no words left in it.

I get under it. I get my arm across its chest, the small ribs going like a hunted thing against my arm, and I plant my feet on the black I cannot see and I lift.

---

And for that breath the cold lets go.

Both of us. The water is still around us, still pulling, still white — but the ring of it, the file of it, the sharp stillness that wraps a body in this sea — it is not on the child. I feel it not be there. The small one stops fighting in my arm, goes loose, not from drowning, from the cold not being in it, from the warmth standing up around the two of us like a held breath, the way it stands up off the warm stone in my crack at home.

And for one breath I am not afraid of it. For one breath I am only a thing with strong hands in a killing sea, holding a smaller thing up out of the dark, and the dark cannot have either of us. There it is. The bone-thin pride of it goes up through me hot as a swallowed coal. My hands did that. My hands.

Then there is no time. The body knows the rest the way the body knows the iron taste — a wrong thing, a thing the world does not do, happening through my arm and into the child.

I lift it up onto the high black, above the line the water will reach, and I set it down on the dry stone, and it coughs the sea up out of itself, alive, ribs working, alive.

And then it turns its head and it sees my face.

---

I have not seen my own face in a long time. I do not know what is on it. But I see it land in the small one's eyes — I see the eyes go wide and white-ringed and fix on me, on the salt-cracked thing crouched over it, the bone, the hair, the wrong shore-smell of me, seal-fat, old smoke, blood. I see it see me. Up the head, down the mouth, the road open — it is going to make the sound, the one that names a thing and throws it up the shore for the whole pack to hear.

The fast part takes me again.

I am off the rock and into the broken country before the sound is all the way out of it, low, gone, into the pinches and the shelves where a thing that does not know them dies. I do not look back. I press my spine to the warm stone in my crack and the warmth leans into me and my heart goes and goes and goes against the black.

It saw my face.

That is the thing under all the other things, the thing the warmth lays over and cannot melt. The seal does not know my face. The cold does not know it. But the boy — the part of me with the face, the part that the legs threw into the water without asking — that part can be seen. That part can be named. Thrown up a shore. Caught. The longest tooth on the shore and only a tooth — and it lived, it would not stay down, it threw itself into the sea for a stranger's child.

It is not dead. The boy is not dead. That is the wrong thing I cannot set on the shelf. I felt him decide.

---

Across the water, after, there is a noise on the far shore that there has not been before.

A big one has come down to the near rocks at the low — waded the pinch the small one waded, come for it, found it dry and coughing and alive on the high black with nobody there. I lie in my crack. I do not go up. I do not have to. I know the shape of a thing finding its young not-dead. I know the shape of arms closing on a small one and lifting it up against the body, warm to warm, the way nothing on this island has ever closed on me.

And I know — because I am the thing that reads this rock — what the big one finds on the stone where I stood.

Frost. A ring of it, white on the black, all around the place my feet were planted in the channel-edge. Frost where no frost should stand at a turning tide, in the wet, in the spray. A cold mark in the shape of a thing that is gone.

And on the child — none. The big one runs its hands over the small one, down the wet of it, feeling for the cold that should be in a thing the sea has had, the killing cold, and it is not there. The child is warm. The child is warm and the stone is white and there was a face, the small one is saying it now, I can feel the saying go up the shore, a face, a wild thing, a boy on the black that lifted me out of the water and the cold would not touch me —

and the big ones look out across the pinch, out at my dark shore, where there is nothing to see.

There is a story on the far shore now that was not there at the last low tide. It has my face in it. And the second mouth leans toward that shore harder than ever — wanting the arms, the warm-to-warm, the being lifted — and the beast in me flattens and bares the gone teeth, because now they will be looking, because now the wild boy on the black shore is a thing they know is there.

I held a name behind my teeth once, the sound the slavers made, in the gap where three are gone. The gap is warm and empty now. But up the far shore tonight, in a small one's mouth, in the firelight, I am being given a new one — not behind anyone's teeth, out loud, thrown up the black for the whole pack to hear.

I do not know yet what it will be. I only know the small one is saying it, and the big ones are leaning in to hear.
