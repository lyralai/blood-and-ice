# Chapter 9: Among Them

I learn the high stones the way I learned my green. Soles first. Fingers. Teeth. The head comes after. It always does, dragging behind the body.

---

The first thing I learn is where I may put my weight.

There is a country up here that is not the country I see. The bare sun-bleached shelf looks all one thing to the eye, all heat and stone. It is not one thing to the tribe. There is the high clean ledge where the sun strikes first, for the still ones, the laid-out dead, squared and faced, no living foot going up onto it but the keeper's. There is the wide lower shelf where the kept fire holds, banked and fed and never let die. And there is the broken lip where the king sits, a clear bare stone all around him, like the clean hand's-width the swarm leaves around me. I learn that stone with the body before the head. I go to cross it on a mending low, and a great arm comes down across my chest, not hard, only sure, and turns me. The bare stone is the king's. I learn it the way I learned the rot-pool was death — by being stopped at the edge of it.

The king is Gull-Marr. I do not have the sound yet on that first low. I have only the shape of him sitting highest, heavy past all the others, the old silver come into the dark of his hide along the great ridged shoulders, and the eyes that go over me and do not warm. His is the one head on the whole shelf that does not tip toward me. The rest have started to. He looks at me the way I look at a wrong thing — a thing that should not be, and is, and has not yet been turned over to see what is under it.

I file it. *The king has not chosen me.* The keeper has chosen me, and the old silver one has bowed to the mark at my chest, but the king has only looked, and looked away. A thing the king has not chosen is a thing on borrowed stone — the ground that pours away the moment the hand that lent it opens. I have lived my whole life on it.

---

The second thing I learn is that they have sounds for everything, and the sounds are a country too.

The keeper makes the gentling sound at me, low, up and down, no edges, and I had thought it was one sound. It is not. I lie against her warm through the mending lows with my ear to the great slow heart, and I learn the gentling has many shapes, each a different thing. The one she lays on me when the firelight is low — soft, falling, the tail of it trailing off — means *be still, you are held, nothing comes.* A shorter one, two beats, hard at the front, that she throws when a small one wanders toward the lip, snaps it back like a jerked line snaps a hooked thing: *no, back, the edge.* And one that rises at the tail, asking, that the big ones throw to each other and answer, *where, here, where, here,* the line of sound between them saying *you are still ours.*

I take them all in. I hold them behind the gone teeth, in the gap where three were torn, the warm hollow where I keep things. They stack up in the dark of me, and something turns over. Slow. I had sounds once. There was a mouth that made sounds at me, before the black, low and soft and falling, and I made them back, and the making-back meant we were two and not alone. I lost it. But I have theirs now. I steal them one low at a time, building back a thing I lost the shape of, with another body's names.

---

The small ones teach me fastest, because the small ones are not afraid.

The big ones still move around me with the half-step of a thing drawing back from a thing it half-understands. But the small ones have not learned what should not be, so nothing about me is wrong to them. They climb me like they climb the big ones, and make their sounds in my face, close, and wait. This is how a sound is given. Close. In the face. The mouth shaping it slow so the eye reads it while the ear takes it.

There is one I mark before I have its name, and the body marks it first, the way the body marks all the surest things. It is small even among the small. Wrong-made. One shoulder rides high, hauled up past the other and held there, and the hand on that side is curled in on itself, a knot of fingers that will not open. It does not walk so much as rock — a crooked, swaying gait, the high shoulder leading, the bad leg swinging out and round. And it is quick. That is what the eye snags on. A thing made that wrong has no business being quick, and this one is quicker than the straight-made ones, darting between the big legs, taking the gap before it closes.

I know it before I know anything else about it. It is a runt — the one the strong meant to leave on the rock. I have lived that. I know the shape of a thing supposed to be culled and not culled, that survives in the narrow space the strong do not bother to fill, on quickness and wit. I read it off its body the same as I read the king off his, and what I read is *mine, near as a brand.*

It comes to me with no fear at all on the first low, the boldest of them. It rides my chest in the firelight and points its small curled hand and makes a sound and waits, giving the world its names. The kept fire — a sound low in the throat, round at the back. The water in the rock-hollow — another, wet at the front. The high green, the bare stone, the dark coming down, each with its sound, the runt laying them on me one at a time, watching to see if I take them.

I never answer. The beast keeps my throat shut and I let it; a torn sound out of me would turn the small face the wrong way. But I am not empty. I am full to the back of the teeth with their sounds. And the runt does not need me to answer. It needs me to take, and I take, and the curled hand points again.

This is a wrongness I cannot file with the body's. The body's wrong things sit easy — the fever that walks through me and leaves me clear-headed, the swarm that spares me, the wound that draws shut clean and fast where every other body's goes to rot in the wet. But this — a small wrong-made thing laying the world's names on me, free, asking nothing back — this has no shelf to sit on. The far mouth takes it instead. The names go down into it and do not fall straight through. Not enough. Never enough. But the empty closes a little around them, like a hand closing on a thing it means to keep and not crush, and I lie under the runt's finger and feel it new.

---

The keeper has a sound I learn before all the others, because it is the one the tribe lays on her.

It comes out of the big ones' mouths when they call across the shelf, when a small one needs the keeping. Two falls. Soft, then softer, the second dropping away under the first. *Ka-la.* They throw it up at her where she tends the still ones, and she lifts the great head and answers, and comes. I hear it thrown low after low until the shape is worn into me. I learn what it points at. Her.

The Kala.

I take it like I take her warm. It is not a name the way the name behind my teeth was a name — given by one mouth in the dark, mine and no one's else. This is a name the many made and the many throw. But it is hers. It points at her and at nothing else in all the high stones, and when I have it whole behind my teeth I find I have stopped taking her as *the warm.* I take her as the Kala. I did not decide it, no more than I decided to be held.

---

There is one low the king comes down off his bare stone.

He does not come to me. He comes to the fire, heavy and slow, the clear bare stone moving with him like the swarm's clean line moves with me. But this low his eyes are on me as he comes, and the gentling goes out of the shelf. The runt slips off my chest and away between the big legs, quick, the high shoulder leading. The keeper goes still beside me, her great arm tightening over me, not hard, only sure.

Gull-Marr stops at the edge of his bare stone and makes a sound. Not the gentling. Low and flat, careful, laid down with teeth in it. *What is this. Why is this on my stone.* And the keeper answers, soft and long, the keeping in it. *Mine. This is a kept thing. I keep it.* And the seer comes slow into the firelight on the king's far side and lifts one finger and points — not at me, at the disc bound at my chest, the eye, ringed, looking, the thorn driven through the looking — and the king's flat eyes go to the mark, and hold.

I do not have the words. I have all of the weather. The king does not want me on his stone. The Kala says I am hers to keep. The seer says I am marked — the eye-and-thorn carries a thing the old blood knows; I am not only a wrong thing but a *known* one. The king looks from the mark to my face and back, and he does not choose me, and he does not unmake me. He lets the borrowed stone stay lent. Kept by one. Marked to another. Borne on sufferance.

I file the king's danger where his bare stone is already filed, and let the warm of the rest have me — because three of them have spoken *about me,* and a thing spoken about is a thing that *is,* and I have not been a thing in any tribe's mouth since the hold made me a thing in the worst one.

---

The sound comes on a dark when I am not reaching for it.

That is the thing about it, after, when I turn it over and cannot wear it smooth. Every low I went in behind the teeth, into the gap where three were torn, reaching to find a thing to send back into the warm — and every low the hollow was warm and empty, my own sound gone down into the deep with everything from before, only the brand left, the ugly hold-word that closed like a hand. I reached and reached and found nothing of mine. So this dark I do not reach. I only lie against the Kala in the low firelight with her slow vast heart going through my ear and the runt come back to ride my chest, and the keeper makes her still-sound over me, soft, falling, *be still, you are held* — and the runt makes a sound back at her, a clumsy shape of it, learning as I am learning, throwing it up at the warm —

and I make it too.

I do not decide it. There is no place in me where the deciding happens. The thing rises like heat off the firestones into the wet dark, up through the chest, up to the gate of the throat where the beast keeps its hand. The beast grabs for it the way it grabs to throw me flat. And the beast misses. The sound gets out.

I make the still-sound. Low, up and down, no edges. Back at the Kala.

It is hoarse. Cracked, half a shape, scraping over the gap where three teeth are torn — a sound that has not been a sound in this mouth since the hold closed on my neck, since before the black. The throat does not know how to do it anymore. It has been a throat for breathing and the low warning-rasp so long that the sound tears coming out, costs skin. But it is the right shape, near enough, and it lands on her.

I feel it land. The great head comes up off the stone. The slow heart against my ear does not change, but the breath does, the vast tide of it catching once, and the eyes that did not warm on the first low come down to my face, close, and read me slow. I have made her sound back at her. I have laid the gentling on the one who gentles, handed it back in a torn and living voice, and there is no part of her left that takes me only as the burning man-shape carried up to die.

She makes the still-sound again. Soft, then softer. And waits.

And I make it back. Better the second time, the throat learning it on the way out, the tear not so deep. And I understand what I have done. I have not found my own sound; the hollow is still empty of the one that is mine. But the throat still works. A sound can go out of me toward a warm and land and not close. I am not only the place where a sound was torn out. I am a place a sound can still be made.

---

The seer hears it.

It is across the fire, low and folded into itself, the milk-pale eyes half shut, and at the torn sound out of me they open, and come around, and find me. It looks from my mouth to the disc and back. A thing that should not make a sound is making a sound. A known wrong thing, and now a voice where no voice was. The seer reads it as it read the mark, and bows the great head, slow, the firelight running red along the silver.

I want to know what it reads. I press at it — the mark, the bow, the milk eyes finding the disc as if the disc were the loud thing and I only the wrapping. I press, and the body has no head for it, only the deep slow shock of *known,* and no words in me yet to hold the question still long enough to turn it. It sits in me unturned. Not because I will not look. Because I cannot reach the underside of it. One low I will have the words. I mean to.

---

I make their sounds back, after that, low after low. Not many. Not well. The throat is slow to remember a thing it forgot so deep, and the words come torn and half-shaped and cost skin each time. But they come. I make the edge-sound at a small one wandering toward the lip, and it snaps back at the torn shape of my voice as it snaps from hers — and the doing of it, the throwing of a sound that *moves a thing,* opens a place in me I did not know was shut.

The runt learns my torn voice as it learns the big ones'. It throws the rising asking-sound across the firelight, *where, where,* and I throw the answer back, hoarse, cracked, *here* — and it comes quick and crooked over the stone, the high shoulder leading, the curled hand reaching, and climbs my chest, because the line of sound ran out from me and it followed it home. A warm thing came up a line of sound to me. Nothing in all my green ever did. The green never answered. The black water took the mouth that answered, and I have lived in a country that throws no sound back ever since.

---

Then comes the low I choose one.

The runt has a name in the tribe's mouth and it is not a name. It is a noise the big ones make at it, short, flat, thrown down and not up — the sound you make at a thing you stepped near and would step around. A dismissal worn smooth by use. They do not call it up, to bring it, the way they call the Kala. They make the noise to be rid of it, and the runt rocks clear of the big feet and does not lift its head as if anything were owed it, because nothing is. The leavings — what is left on the rock the strong did not bother to finish.

And I lie there with the tribe's torn sounds fresh and live in my mouth for the first time since the black, and I look at the small wrong-made thing on my chest — the high shoulder, the knotted hand, the quick dark eye that read me with no fear when every grown thing here still half-drew-back — and I want to throw a sound *up* at it. Not their noise. A sound that brings, not one that rids.

This one I do reach for. This is not the still-sound rising on its own past the beast's hand while I lay still and let it. This I choose. I go down into the deep where my own sound is drowned, where I will not yet bring up the thing that is mine, and I find instead a word from the other country — the man-country, the tongue that made me before any tribe did. Marrow. The rich thing inside the bone. What the strong crack the bone for and then leave — the deep soft worth the cracking did not get. Like me. Like the runt. Too small to be wanted, too good in the deep to die.

I lay it on the throat slow, the shaping before the sound, the way the small ones taught me. I send it up — torn, cracked, scraping the gap where three teeth are gone. But this one I aimed. This one I *meant.*

"*Marrow.*"

The dark eye comes up. The curled hand stills on my chest. The runt does not know the sound, cannot, it is from no country it has ever heard — but it knows the *shape* of the throwing, up and not down, a sound that reaches for a thing to bring it and not be rid of it. No mouth in the high stones has ever thrown a sound up at this one. I watch the small wrong body take it as I took the Kala — not deciding, only taken — and something in the runt settles, the high shoulder dropping a finger's width: a thing that is, for the first low, called and not dismissed.

I made that. The still-sound got out of me; this one I let out. I went down with my will and brought a word up out of the drowned country and laid it on a living thing on purpose, and it stuck. The first time I have taken a sound, chosen what it would mean, and set it on the world to hold. There is a name behind my teeth that a hand closed on once. One low I will open the gap and bring it up. This is the small practice for that great freeing — my own hand learning the choosing the closing hand once did to me, but laid up, not down. Given, not shut.

Marrow. I throw it again, surer, and the runt rocks up the line of it and tucks under my jaw, the knotted hand at my throat where the torn sounds live.

Marrow. Mine. The first thing I have named and not been named by.

---

I do not have my own sound yet. I reach for it once, this dark, sure for half a breath that the making of all these others will have shaken mine loose. The hollow is warm, and empty, and there is only the brand in it. I will not send that. But the others are working at the warm place where mine went silent — I feel it like weather coming, bone-sure, before the sky shows it. One low the hollow will give my own sound back, and I will open the gap and let it into the high stones, and they will have it. I have learned the hand can choose. I named Marrow. The rest is only finding the courage of the deep.

The Kala makes the still-sound over me. I make it back, low and torn, soft-then-softer, two falls. And Marrow, half asleep now in the red light, makes it after us both — clumsy, the wrong shape, learning — and the three of us lie in the kept warm above the drowning green and throw the one soft sound back and forth, around and around, *be still, you are held, nothing comes.* No part of it closes like a hand. And the far mouth, for the first low in all my counting, holds it whole.
