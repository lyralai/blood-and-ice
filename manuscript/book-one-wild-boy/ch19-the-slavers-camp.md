# Chapter 19: The Slavers' Camp

They have built where the dead built.

I know it before I am close enough to read a single body. The green here is wrong like a wound is wrong, the canopy hacked back off the old high-water flat where the Wreck lies rotting in the mangrove, the rot-thicket cut and dragged and the bare mud opened to the sky. No thing of my green opens the canopy. The green closes. It grows down over every hole as the tide comes back over a print, and leaves no scar. Nothing stays opened. Only men open a thing and hold it open. I read the held-open from a long way off, downwind, low in the drowned roots, and the second mouth pulls up tight and hard under my breastbone.

Marrow is at my shoulder. He has come this far and no further, and the body of him says it.

He is small against the root, one shoulder riding high. The curled hand is drawn in tight against his chest. He has gone still — not the still of a hunter holding a line, the other still, the one that means a thing has read death ahead and will not walk into it. The high shoulder drops, slow. That is how he means *no* when he has no sound big enough to lay over the *no.* He will not look at the cut clearing. He looks at me, and then at the green behind us, the way back, and then at me again. The curled hand works against his chest, opening, closing, opening. That is the whole of what he has to say, and he says it loud as a kill-cry: not this. Not toward this.

I read it. I read it whole. And I go on anyway, low under the cut stumps, and he does not.

---

They have landed where the last men died.

They have cut a clearing in the rot that ate the others and they have not smelled the dying still on it. Men have no nose for the thing that takes them. I have read the bones of the dead expedition under the mangrove, the ones the fever took, the ones I do not feel — and the living have set their fires on the same ground, in the same fever-air, and do not know they are already meat.

The green does not hurry. It has them, and has had them since the first foot came off the boat. I go in close on the low, and as I cross the warm shallows where the Mure rise I read a thing the men cannot: the air over the cut mud has the seep in it. The slow rot-breath, the same wet sweetness that hung off the dead expedition's bones. It is in their water. They drink it down and laugh and do not taste it. I tasted it on the Wreck and did not sicken. They will taste nothing until it is in the blood. Then it comes all at once, late and sudden, the way it came for the dead ones — a clearing full of strong men, and the green eating them from the inside while they think they have won.

I lay that against the shelf and go on, into the smell of them.

---

The smell lands behind my teeth before my eye finds the first shape, and the smell is the hold.

Tar. Smoke that is fed and tended and does not go out. The hot iron-stink of a forge kept small and close. And under all of it, threaded through all of it, the close packed reek of bodies held where they cannot move, the piss and the sour breath and the open sores of the kept — the smell of a thing that is owned. I have not had that smell in my nose since the dark of the hold, since the wet wood sweating salt and the boy warm against my side. The body wears it again now in one breath, and the skin on the back of my neck crawls and tries to get away from a hand that is not there, the slow sure grip that owned the whole of me in the one hold. I lie in the mud under a cut stump and I make the crawling stop, and I look.

---

There are more of them than the tribe.

I count the haul of them low and sure, learning where it bunches thick and where it thins and goes soft. Many tens. They go on two legs, all of them, and they handle things — the endless handling that only men do, setting down and taking up and setting down, the boxes, the lashed bundles, the long hooked iron stacked in racks. They have made their dens out of cut hide stretched over poles, set close, all turned in to the kept fires in the middle, the red glowing low in the seams.

A pack. A herd of men, gathered, warm, tending its own.

The far mouth opens for it. It does not care what they are. It leans me up out of the mud toward the many-bodied warm, toward a warm that chose to hold me — and in the same breath the near part of me draws back flat and the lip wants to peel off the teeth. Because hands. Because hold. Because the smell is the smell of the thing that made me a thing. Toward in the chest. Away in the legs. The two of them in the one skin hauling at each other until the pull sings.

I have a tribe now. I have a home with no between in it and a seat the strength walked off of and left me on. I tell the leaning that. *You are full. You do not need the fires. You have the high stones.*

The leaning does not hear me. It leans anyway. Because the warm down there is the warm of my own kind, the same shape walking, and there is a hole in me shaped like that warm that the high stones cannot fill, that the Kala's arm over me cannot fill — a man-shaped hole worn smooth by a thing that used to sit there and sits there no longer. The tribe filled the far mouth. It did not fill this. It is the worst thing I have learned since the gap behind my teeth came up empty: that I had two holes in me and only knew the one.

And up the slope behind me, in the dark of the drowned roots, Marrow has not moved. I feel the *no* of him laid against my back like a hand, steady, refusing to come down. He does not know the words I am full of. He knows only that I am crawling toward men, and that men are the thing that owns. The runt the strong meant to cull is the one thing on this island that will not leave me to the fires — and the fires are my own kind, and he is not. I lie in the mud and cannot get that to sit right side up in my chest.

---

They are not here for the green.

A pack that wanted the green would take meat and water and go, as the dead expedition took and could not keep. These take the wrong things. I watch them haul a shape up the mud from the channel-mouth on poles, gray-silver and gone slack, and the gut drops out of me because for one breath I read it as Vorth — but it is a drowned-one, a Mure, a big one, web-fingered hands lashed and the pale wet skin going dull. They have taken it alive off the warm shallows and broken it and they cage it. Not kill it. Cage it. The hooked iron is for taking a live thing and not letting it run, and I have the whole shape of it now: they hunt the green not to eat it but to keep it — to carry it off across the water in the dark of a hold, as they carried me.

I see the cages then, set back where the smell is thickest. Wood and lashed iron, low, too low for the things in them to stand. The lashed Mure in one, its breath whistling wet and wrong through the web of its throat. A Skell in another, jackal-coding broken, lying on its side in its own waste, the lean ribs going and coming too fast. I know the Mure off the warm shallows and the Skell off the scree — read both alive and free and dangerous — and now they lie in the iron going dull, and the dull is the same dull. And there are men in the cages too. Some of the kept are men.

I lie very still when I read that. Men keeping men. The same shape on both sides of the iron, the hand that closes and the neck the hand closes on, and only the iron between them saying which is which — and I know which the iron made me. I wore the inside of it. I have never once, in all the lows since the sea spat me up, let myself think the men who held me were men. I made them a weather without a face. And here is the face. Tens of faces around the kept fires, warm, tending their own — and the men in the warm do not turn their heads to the men in the iron.

---

The leader I read last.

He does not handle things. He stands and the handling moves around him. He is not the biggest — it is never the biggest — but the others bend toward him without being struck, the way a tribe leans to the king before the king has done a thing. He has the still of a thing that does not have to hurry to what it is going to take. I have stood under that still on a deck and in a hold. The body knows the slow certain coming-on, and it knows this man is made of it.

He wears a mark.

It is on the breast of him, worked into the hide he wears, and on the iron of the long blade at his hip, and burned into the wood of the cages — the same mark in all the places, the mark that says *mine* to anything that can read a mark. And I can read it. It comes up out of my drowned-deep where no word lives, and the whole skin of me draws tight: it is the Eye-and-Thorn. The sigil off the relic I took from the Wreck, the one the seer laid the meaning-sound over, the one that lives in me from a place older than the island and older than the hold — the eye, and the thorn through it, the watching and the keeping-by-pain bound into one shape. The dead expedition bore it. I have it bound at my own chest this low, under the Kala's keeping and the crown I won. And the living men who landed where the dead men died wear it burned into the iron of their cages.

The dead bore my sigil. The living bear it. And they wear it to mean *owned.*

I press at it. I want it open. I lie in the mud and try to make the head close on the one thing I most need to know — how the mark the seer read as kin-omen over my blood can be the same mark burned on the cages these men will drag across the water; how it can be mine and theirs at once. I reach for it like I reach for a print going faint in the mud, straining to lift the next thing out of it. And it will not come. The head has no purchase. The fever-air and the dark and the no-word-for-it crowd in and shut me out, and I am left holding only the wanting-to-know, laid bare and aching. I set the shape of it down only because I cannot lift it, and keep my eyes on the leader of the men.

---

He comes to the Mure-cage, and the pack makes a path for him, and he looks at the drowned-one as the seer looks at the squared dead — reading it, weighing it, laying it into some order I cannot see. He says a thing. The sound carries to me low and flat and edged, sounds set against sounds, a word, maybe a name — and a smaller man near him answers with the quick bend of a body that answers because it must.

And the man-shaped hole leans so hard at the sound of men making words to each other that it hurts worse than the gut ever hurt. Because that is the thing I lost. Not meat. The easy road of it — from down where the wanting lives, up the throat, out, laid clean on the air, thrown to a body that will catch it and throw one back. Marrow gives me an answer even now, all in the body, the high shoulder and the working hand throwing me *no* across the dark. But it is not this. It is not men speaking to men in the shapes my mother spoke over me before the ship. I dig my pads into the mud and hold my own shape down by force against the want that pulls me toward the very men who would burn the Eye-and-Thorn on the cage they bent me into.

The leader turns from the cage, and his still face passes across the green, across the cut stumps, across the dark drowned roots where I lie — and it does not stop on me. It cannot. I am one dark shape in a green of dark shapes and the wind holds true. But the weighing look passes over me, the slow certain reading, and the back of my neck crawls under it as it crawled under the hand: he is the hold come walking. Come across the water onto two legs into my green, with the hooked iron and the burned brand, to fold over the world as the hand folded over my neck and made me a thing.

His name will be a thing I learn. The kept will give it to me, low, behind the teeth, in the dark — Drevan Sool, the captain, the still one whose hand all the other hands answer to. But I do not have it yet. This low he is only the shape, and the shape is enough.

---

I should go.

Every near part of me says it. The whole body of Marrow up the slope says it. A thing this many, this armed, that walks into the death I survive and cages what it does not kill — it will burn the brand on everything that can be dragged to a boat. I have a tribe to guard. A Kala. A bold heart that rides my shoulder. A high stone full of small ones. The men are not here for them yet; they are here for the easy green, the channel-mouth, the warm shallows. But the channel is a hand's reach from the high stones, and a thing that holds the green open does not stop at the line a beast bleeds across the stone. It learns the line, and then it crosses it. I read it off the spread fan of them already fanning out from the fires, the cutting-parties with the hooked iron going into the rot-thicket.

I should take the tribe deep, up into the high broken stone and the fever-fog where men die and I do not, where no boat reaches and no brand burns. The seep is in their water already. I could go up the stone and wait, and the fever would do the whole of it for me, and I would never once lay my shape where the captain's weighing-look can cut it from the green.

I do not go.

I lie in the warm mud with the smell of the hold in my nose and the Eye-and-Thorn burning on the cages, and I do not pull back into the deep green. Because the men are my own shape, and the captain wears my own sigil on his iron, and I have to know. What the mark means and whose it is and why it lives in me and in him both. A thing that learns a new hole in itself cannot stop leaning toward the only thing shaped to fill it, even when the thing is the hand and the brand.

---

I stay until the light goes.

I watch the kept fires come up red on the cut mud when the green half-light dies, fat and fed and tended, warm in the killing green. I watch the men throw their shapes at each other, the easy road, the call caught and thrown back, and the man-shaped hole aches at every sound. I watch the cages where the kept do not get the fire — the Mure going dull, the broken Skell on its side, the men in the iron who are the same shape as the warm.

And I watch one of the warm ones stand up from a fire and sway. A big man, strong, well-fed. He puts a hand to his head, and another laughs at him, and he sits back down. It is nothing to them. I have read this on bones; I am reading it now on a living man who does not yet know he is meat. The count starts under my ribs, the slow sure count the green keeps and the men cannot hear: so many lows, and then the swaying, and then the cages full of the dull, and then the warm ones dull too.

I lay the whole night of it down on the shelf and behind my teeth, beside the brand I keep only so that I can one day be rid of it.

And under the hand I clap over my own mouth in the dark — the old clap, learned long lows ago, to keep the rasp in — I want a sound to set against the men's far calls. One sound. Mine. The name my mother set on me before the ship, the one un-stolen thing, the one the slavers never knew to reach for and so never took. I go in behind the teeth to find it and throw it across the cut mud at the fires of my own kind, as the young one threw its name up the shore.

It is there. It is always there, held down hard in the gap where three teeth went, and the warmth comes up slow and lies down in the hollow around it, and the name is mine and whole. But the throwing of it is the thing they tore out. The hands that owned me took the road from the head to the mouth and left me the name and no way to lay it on the air — the name held full in the dark behind my teeth, and no one on the whole drowning island shaped to catch it who is not also the hand.

I take my hand off my mouth. There is no sound to keep in. I have the name, and no one to throw it to.

---

I go back up the slope in the full dark, and Marrow is where I left him.

He has not moved the whole night. He has waited. The high shoulder is still down, still saying *no,* and when he sees me come clear of the cut stumps the curled hand opens once and closes, and that is all — no kill-cry, no relief thrown up loud. Just the body easing a hair, the small ease of a body when the thing it dreaded has not, this low, come to be. He does not crow that he was right about the fires. He turns, quick on the rocking gait, and goes up ahead of me into the green, putting himself between me and the way down, and I understand the whole of what his body has been telling me all night and could not say in any tongue: your own kind is not your kin. The kin is the runt going up the rocks ahead of you, the one who would not leave you to the fires of the men who are your shape.

I go up after him, toward the Kala and the bold heart and the home that is mine. And the man-shaped hole leans behind me the whole way, back toward the red fires, toward the brand burned on the iron — full of the tribe, and leaning anyway. A body that has everything still leans toward the one thing that almost killed it.

I know, the way the body knows a tide before the sky shows it, that I will go back down to the fires. Not this low. But I will go, down to the brand and the cages and the still captain who wears my own mark on his iron — and the green will be at its work the whole time, counting the men down while the men count their haul. The man-shaped hole has learned the road to the only thing shaped to fill it, and a hole like that does not unlearn a road. It only counts the lows until it can walk it. And behind me on the rocks Marrow's body says, as plain as a body can say a thing, that he already knows I will, and that when I do he will be at my shoulder, and that he is afraid.
