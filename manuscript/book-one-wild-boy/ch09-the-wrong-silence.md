# Chapter 9: The Wrong Silence

I wake to a quiet that is shaped wrong.

For a heartbeat I cannot find what is missing. The body wakes the way it always wakes, the eye open before the thought, the ear out ahead of both, casting over the shore for the one wrong thing the way it has cast a thousand mornings. And it finds the wrong thing by its absence. The shore is too still. Not the stillness after a storm, washed and ringing, that has a fullness in it. This is a stillness with a hole at the center, and the hole is the sail.

The crack is cold at my back where the warm stone holds steady, and the grey is coming up over the channel the way it comes up every low-light, and out past the smoke-coloured water the long edge where the sea stands into the sky is bare again. Empty. The way it has always been empty. The way it was empty every night I told the second mouth there was nothing out there.

But I saw it. The body does not lose a thing it has seen. I held my mother's name to the water with no sound, and the water gave back a shape, and the shape was made by hands.

And now there is nothing.

I lie in the cold and I do not move, and the not-moving is its own kind of listening. Maybe the sea took it back. Maybe the storm I slept through after, the grey one that came up behind the first, drove it off the edge of the world and into the deep where everything goes. Maybe I wanted it so hard out of the empty that the wanting put a shape on the water where no shape was, the way hunger puts the smell of seal-fat in a cold wind. The mouth that wants to be known would lie to me. I know that. It is the one part of me I have not made my master.

I get up. The body wants to walk the road, and I let it, because the road is the one thing I trust to tell me the truth of the shore.

---

The road tells me nothing.

Drink, and the seep is running clean, finger-thin off the black. Cache, and the meat lies cold and weighted in the white. Brand, and the coal sleeps in its fist of kelp, breathing slow. Every stone is where I set it. Every voice of the island is the voice I know. I walk it the way I walk it every low-light, my pale-worn pads in the black, and the island does the one thing it has always done, which is exactly what I knew it would do, and the knowing sits in me like a stone I have swallowed.

I crouch by the cat's bones in the pinch where I always crouch. The long jaw. The cage of ribs. The wide pan of the skull. I bow my head over them the way the head bows of its own weight, my jaw to the dead jaw, tooth to emptied tooth, and they do not answer, because they are bones, because they have never answered, because the closest thing to company the island keeps is a thing the gulls cleaned of every soft part a long time ago.

I want to ask the bones if they saw it too. The wanting comes up sudden and hot, the near mouth and the far one both, and I crouch there with the question in my throat and nowhere to put it. A sail. Out on the smoke-grey. You saw it. Tell me I did not make it.

The bones say nothing. They have a way of saying nothing that is wider than the storm's saying nothing. The storm at least pushes back on the slabs. The bones just lie there in the wrack and let the question fall through them into the black.

I go on. I do not look at the edge of the sea. I make a thing of not looking at it, the way you make a thing of not turning the wrong stone on the shelf, and the not-looking is heavier than looking would be, and I carry it all the way around the road and back.

---

The mouth grinds in me all that low-light, low and endless.

It was bad before the sail. I knew it was bad. It ground in me like the channel grinds the stone it cannot move, and I had made a kind of peace with the grinding, a master's peace, the way I make peace with everything I cannot kill. The grinding was a weather I had learned to sleep through.

But the sail moved the edge of the thing. For one night the second mouth got the smell of meat it has hungered for since the sea spat me up — not seal-meat, not bull-fat, the other meat, the one no kill has ever been shaped to feed. An eye that is not my eye. A thing on the shore that could turn and see me and know that the seam in my palm closed in three days, that the frost rings me and spares my body, that I am the longest tooth on an emptied jaw. For one night the smell came in off the water.

And now the wind has shifted and the smell is gone. You cannot un-feed a hunger its first true scent. I learned that with the seals, far back — the first warm spilling over the hands woke the near mouth and it never slept whole again. The sail has woken the far mouth the same way, and it stands up in me, wide, hungry, ringing.

The body fills with the wrong heat. I stand on my high rock and I want to go down to the channel and spear the grey flat of it, drive the head into the smoke-colour and rip it the way I rip the offal, make it bleed, make it give back the shape it showed me and took away. The hand closes on the spear-shaft hard enough to whiten. A herd of seals hauls out fat and stupid on the low rocks, three deep, and I do not go down to them. I have meat. The near mouth is fed. It is the far one that starves, and there is no haul-out for it, no channel where it spills, no blind side to come along slow.

---

So I do the thing. I do not know I am going to do it until it is half done.

I open my mouth to the empty water, the way I opened it the night before, the night the water gave back a shape. I think — if I say it again. If I hold the name to the grey again, the way I held it, the one un-stolen word, the thing my mother gave me before the ship and the hands and the low ugly sound the men made behind their teeth that meant come, that meant be silent, that meant the felt-shape of a hand closing on the back of my neck — if I say my own true name to the water one more time, maybe the water answers one more time. Maybe the shape comes back. Maybe the saying of it is what called the sail.

And this time I do not say it with no sound.

This time I make the sound.

I have not made the sound in — I cannot find the count. I have not said a word aloud to anything since the sea gave me up. There has been nothing to say it to. The bones do not answer and the seals do not answer and the storm does not answer and so the mouth that makes words went quiet, the way the coal banks low when there is no need of fire, breathing slow, never let to die but never struck up either. I open the gap behind my teeth where my tongue has guarded the name through every taking, and I reach for the shape of it, and I push the breath up out of my chest to lay the shape on the air —

and what comes out is not a word.

It comes out a sound. Low. Torn. A thing made in the throat under the place where words are made, a thing with no edges to it, a hauled-out grunt, a seal-bark, the noise the bull makes when the weight comes down behind his neck. It scrapes out of me into the cold and the cold takes it and it is not mine, it is not the name, it is not anything that ever meant a boy. It is the sound a thing makes. Not a word. A throat working.

I shut my mouth on it.

The body has gone cold all the way down, a cold deeper than the storm's, the cold I felt watching the seam close in my palm. I stand on the high rock with the wrong sound still ringing in the air around me and I cannot believe it came out of me. I try again. I push the breath up careful this time, slow, the way I straighten a shaft over the coal, and I shape my mouth around the name, and again — the throat closes wrong, the air comes out shapeless, the sound that is not a word, the sound a thing makes.

The mouth that makes words is gone. It banked too low. It is not there to strike up.

---

I stop trying to say it out loud.

I go back to the way I have always held it. Behind the teeth, in the gap where three are gone, the dark hollow, silent, the way you hold a thing too precious to set on the air where the cold can get it. I do not need to say it aloud. I never needed to. It lived behind my teeth through every taking and the men never found it because they never knew to reach for it, and I have held it there through every kill and every cut and every frost that rings me. I close my eyes and I reach my tongue back into the gap to find its shape and hold it the way I have held it ten thousand nights.

And the shape is not there.

I reach for it the way the foot reaches for the known stone in the dark path. And the path is bare. There is the gap, the dark hollow, the place where three teeth were torn out, the place where the word has lived — and the word is not in it. I run my tongue along the empty edges of the gum where the slavers' work scarred over hard, and there is only the gap. Only the hollow. The name that lived in it is not there.

The cold goes through me in a long wave that has no bottom.

I reach again. I make the body reach, the way I make it walk the road, the way I make it do the thing it does not want to do. I go back behind the teeth and I cast for the shape of it the way the ear casts the shore for the one wrong thing, and there is nothing wrong because there is nothing there, the whole gap is one smooth absence, and the name — the name my mother gave me, the sound of her mouth making it over me before the ship, the warmth of it, the un-stolen piece, the last thing that was mine that the hands never reached — I cannot find it. I cannot find its shape. I cannot find the start of it or the weight of it on the tongue or the way it sat in the gap all those years like a coal banked in kelp.

It banked too low. There was no one to say it to. The mouth that made words went quiet, and the name lived in the quiet, and the quiet got so wide and so long that the name went quiet too, the way the coal goes quiet, breathing slower and slower, never let to die but never struck up either, until one cold morning you reach into the kelp to wake it and your fingers find only ash that holds the shape of a fist and falls when you touch it.

I have lost it. The island took it.

Not the men. The men never got it. They took three teeth trying for everything else. They took the body, the count of the winters, the soft thing the sea spat up. And the one thing they never reached, the name behind the gap, I carried out past their reach and onto the black, whole, mine. And the island that I made myself master of, the island whose every stone I learned and whose every voice I broke to sleep through, the island took the thing the men could not. It took it the slow way. It took it by being so empty for so long that the word starved in me the way the far mouth starves, with no eye to say it to, no other mouth to make it back, no answer ever, until the saying of it withered and the holding of it withered and one grey morning there was only the gap, and the gap was empty, and I was a thing on a shore with no name behind its teeth and no word in its throat and only the sound the bull makes when the weight comes down.

---

I do not climb to the lookout that night.

I go into the crack and I lie with my back to the warm stone that gives and gives and never asks, and I keep my mouth shut. The jaw stays shut of its own weight, and I hold the breath so I will not hear it come out shapeless again. The frost comes in past the turf and beads on the hide and on the stone and on the lashing of the spear, and it rings clear of my arm in the one warm circle, the dark moat in all that white, the shape of me cut out and left bare, and I look at the bare wet black where the frost will not come and I think: that is what I am now. A shape cut out of the world and left with nothing in it. The frost knows me better than I know me. It at least knows where my edges are.

I reach behind my teeth one more time before the dark takes me. Slow. The way you go back to the cold-cache in the night to be sure the meat is still weighted. I go into the gap and I cast for the name.

Ash. The shape of a fist. It falls when I touch it.

And then — far down, under the ash, under the cold, under all the empty the island poured into me — something turns. Not the name. Not the shape of it. Something below the place the word used to live, a heat with no edges, the near mouth and the far one both, lifting their heads together out of the dark under my ribs. Not the name. The thing the name was for. The wanting of the mouth that made her make it over me, the warmth of being a thing she set a word on, a thing worth a word, a boy and not a sound a throat works out into the cold.

It is still in there. The name is gone but the place it lived is not gone. The hollow is empty but the hollow is still mine, and it is still shaped like wanting, and the wanting will not bank low. It stands up in me wide and ringing and it leans out of the dark crack into all that frozen black and it finds nothing to lean on, nothing — the bones, the seals, the storm, the smoke-grey edge where the shape came and went. I lie there with no name and no word and only the wanting, and the wanting is the last un-stolen thing, because they cannot take a hollow, they cannot net an emptiness, they cannot brand a hole.

I shut my eyes. The warm stone holds my spine. The frost rings me and spares my body. Somewhere out past the channel the sea stands up into the sky, bare, the way it has always been bare.

I do not say my name to the water. I have no name to say, and no mouth to say it with.

But the wanting says itself, with no word, the way it has said itself ten thousand nights — out, and out, and out, into the grey, toward anything at all that might turn and see me and make me back into a thing worth a sound.

And out on the smoke-grey, where the sea meets the sky, where the shape came once and went —

nothing.

Not yet.

But the wanting does not believe the nothing. It never has. It stands up in the dark behind my teeth where the name used to be, and it watches the edge of the world, and it waits.
