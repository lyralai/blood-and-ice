# Chapter 9: The Wrong Silence

I go to say my own name and a dog answers.

---

It is nothing at first. A grey low-light like the others. I am at the seep with my mouth to the black, taking the finger-thin water the way I take it every morning, cold running down the inside of me, and something in the work of it — the throat moving, the swallowing — wakes a want in me to make a sound.

I do not know where the want comes from. It just lifts its head.

So I let a sound out, easy, the way you let breath out.

And it is wrong.

It comes up out of me low and broken, a cough with a growl on its back, scraped, no shape to it. It hits the black rock and the rock does not know it for anything but weather. I shut my mouth on it fast. The seep runs. The sea works the pinch. I crouch there with the water still on my chin and a cold goes through me that is not the water.

That was not a boy's sound.

---

I try again. I cannot remember the last time I made a sound on purpose that was not for the kill — the bark to freeze a seal, the hiss through my teeth at the gulls. Sounds that do work. This is not work. This is only to hear it.

I open my mouth. I push the air. I try to put a word on it. Any word.

What comes is a rasp. A throat full of old smoke. The shape of the word is in my head — I can feel it sitting there, whole, a thing with edges — but the road from the head to the mouth has gone over to weather and wrack, and what crawls up it is animal. I try a second time and a third and each time it is worse, because now I am listening, and a thing you listen for that hard goes shy.

My own voice. I have not heard it. I count back and I cannot count it, and that is the trouble with not trusting the tally — I have lost the place where I last spoke. A moon. More. Long enough that the mouth forgot it was a mouth and learned to be a maw.

---

I sit back on the wet black and I make myself go slow.

I had words once. I know I had them. They lived in me thick as the herd on the haul-out. I reach for them now and they are there but they are far down and cold, like the boy who could not do the killing, like the fingers waking in pieces that first morning. *Water.* I have the thing the word means — it is on my chin, in my throat — but the sound of it slides off when I grab for it. *Cold.* *Rock.* *Hand.* They are stones under ice. I can see their shapes through the ice. I cannot get my fingers around them.

And the cage comes up at me out of nowhere.

The dark of the hold. The wet wood. Too many bodies, the salt of them. A big hand closing on the back of my neck, and the low sound behind the teeth that meant me. The iron of the chains. The rock of the deck. The boy in the dark holding his one word behind his teeth so they could not have it.

It does not come like mine. It comes like a thing I was told, a shore I stood near once and watched. I reach for that boy and he is as far down and cold as the words are. The island took him. There was no day it happened. I crossed some pinch in the dark, hand on the cold rock the way I cross my own road blind, and came out the far side already this — already the long tooth, already the maw — and no eye on the shore to see it but mine, and mine had gone over with the rest of me.

---

I have one word that is mine. I have kept it. It is the one I swore on.

I close my eyes on the grey and I go in behind my teeth, into the gap where three are gone, to where I keep it. The shape of a hand closing on the back of a neck. The sound the slavers made. The only thing I carried off the ship that they did not put on me. I have said it to the dark and to the sea a thousand low-lights. I go to say it now, out loud, in air, just to prove the road is not all weather — just to put one true sound on the black and have it be a word and not a growl.

I open my mouth.

*Rook.*

What comes out is a noise.

I do it again. I bear down on it, I shape my mouth the way the mouth wants to go, I drive the air — and it breaks in my throat into that low scraped cough, that thing with the growl on its back. The rock takes it for weather. The gulls do not even lift. I cannot say my name. I cannot say the one word I am. It is behind my teeth still, whole, I can feel it sitting there — but the throat has silted up like a pinch the tide closed over, and the word is locked in the warm dark of me with no way out that does not come up animal.

And then — because the gulls did not lift, because they sit there fat and smug on the pinch like they own the noise I made — I bare my teeth and I throw the rasp at them, hard, the hunting hiss, the one sound my mouth still keeps whole. They go up screaming. The black is mine again, and empty, and I am still the thing that cleared it. There is that, at least. There is still that.

I put my hand over my own mouth. To keep the rest of it in.

---

And in the silence after — the held-breath silence — a warmth moves in me.

Not the gut-hunger. Not the second mouth. Lower than both, in the blood itself, in the deep red work of me that should only ever beat. A heat where there is no fire. It surges up slow, then steadies, then rises again, while I sit there mute with my hand over my mouth.

When I take my hand away and try the sound once more — bear down, drive the air, break it — the warmth slides back down and goes quiet. The noise drives it under. I go still again. It comes up again. Silence, and the heat lifts; sound, and it sinks. I do it twice more to be sure, and twice more it is so. It comes up into the place where the words used to be, into the empty road, and it lies down there easy. The mouth that cannot make the name. The throat gone to weather. It settles into the wordless the way I settle into the worn black of the crack, the way a thing settles into its own shape at last, at home.

The cold of the island files at my bare arm, ringing the skin, sharp and still. The warm thing in my blood does not feel the cold. It feels nothing of the cold at all. It comes up in the quiet and it stays.

I take my hand off my mouth. I do not try the sound again.

---

I go home before the light is gone.

The crack takes me, my own shape worn into the black, the turf, the hide. The warm stone meets my spine and holds its heat there like a thing with blood in it, and the warmth in me leans toward it, warm to warm, two heats in the one cold dark, and I do not ask either of them why. I am too tired to ask. I am tired in the throat, of all the strange places, from pushing all day at a road that is closed.

I lie in the worn black and I do the thing I have done every dark since the ship. I go in behind my teeth to hold the word. The one true thing. The shape of a hand on the back of a neck, the sound they made, the name that is not a name but is mine —

— and it is not there.

I reach into the gap where three are gone and the warm dark is warm and dark and there is nothing in it shaped like the word. I had it this morning at the seep. I had it a thousand low-lights. I push my tongue into the gap as if the shape will be there for the touching, and there is only the gum, the salt, the iron seam of old blood. No word. No hand. No neck. No sound — only the warmth coming up slow from the deep of me, lying down in the place where the name used to be, full and slow, taking the room the word kept, until there is no room left.

I lie in my den on my black rock, the master of all of it, the longest tooth on the shore, and I cannot remember the shape of my own name.

Out past the channel, drawn up on the smoke-grey water through the dark, the sail is closer.
