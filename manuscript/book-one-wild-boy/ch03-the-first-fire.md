# Chapter 3: The First Fire

I have seen fire. I have never owned it.

On the ship it lived in cages of iron, swinging from the low roof, and in the slavers' hands it sat curled in little clay cups, orange, alive, breathing without lungs. They carried it where they wanted and the dark went out of their way. I watched it the way a starving thing watches a full mouth chew. I knew what it was. I did not know how it came.

Now I crouch on the black stone in the gray light with two sticks and a notch I have scraped, and I make it the only way I have guessed at — by spinning, by friction, by working one stick against the other until the wood weeps a little smoke and my hands weep a little blood. And nothing else.

---

The first wood is wrong.

It is the wood that comes up on the tide, pale and swollen and patient with seawater. I work it until my arms shake and the seal-fat in my shoulders burns out and there is nothing left to spin with. The notch goes dark. The notch goes warm. The notch goes nowhere. Wet wood holds its smoke the way the deep pool holds its cold — all the way down, no matter what the day does to the top of it.

I throw it into the sea. I watch it float. The sea does not care that I am beaten. The sky, least of all.

I go back for more wood.

---

Days. I do not count them in numbers, I count them in skin.

The hands split first across the soft part below the fingers, then deeper, into the meat, two red mouths that open every time I close my grip. Then they stop bleeding, faster than they should — I feel them knit while I sleep, the gashes drawing shut to pink lines in a day, in a night. I reach back without looking and set it on the shelf with the cold breath and the warm stone, where I keep the things I do not let myself turn over in the light. The wanting is all for fire now. It crowds the front of me and takes up all the room there is, and behind it the second mouth waits, patient, the way the sea waits.

---

I learn by watching what burns by accident.

Lightning, once, far off on the water, and a smell after of old smoke that turns me around like a hand on my chin. And the dead grass. The dead grass on the high ledges, gone gold and brittle, that the wind tears loose and packs into the cracks of the cliff. I take a fistful and crush it and it falls to dust and floss in my palm, dry as bone, dry as the inside of my own mouth. Tinder. The wood is the slow thing. This is the thing that catches.

And the wood I want is not the wet wood and not the green wood. It is the standing-dead branch high in the lee of the cliff where the salt does not reach, light in the hand, hollow-sounding when I knock it. When I scrape it the dust comes off dry. I have been feeding the fire the wrong food. No wonder it would not eat.

---

I build the small bed of grass-floss. I notch the dry wood. I spin.

My breath does not smoke in the cold air. But the wood begins to, a thin gray thread climbing where my breath will not, and something in my chest goes very still and leans toward it. Spin. The thread thickens. Spin. The notch goes black and a smell comes up, sharp, good, the smell of the thing almost happening. Spin. My hands are screaming. I do not hear them. There is a little dark grain in the notch now, a crumb of char, and it glows. It glows. A small red eye opening in the wood, looking at me.

I tip it into the grass. I bend down low, my face in the floss, close as a kiss, and I give it the one thing I have that will not smoke — I give it my breath.

The grass takes it. A flame stands up out of nothing the length of my smallest finger, pale, almost colorless in the gray day, and it is the most alive thing I have seen since the sea spat me out.

I feed it. Slow. Twig, then twig, the way you would gentle meat into a small animal so it does not bolt. It eats. It grows. It climbs into the dry wood, gets a grip, roars up bright and orange. There it is. There it is, mine, sitting on the black stone in front of me — breathing without lungs, alive without blood, the slavers' thing, in my own two ruined hands.

I laugh. It comes out wrong, cracked, a sound I have not made in a long time and barely know. The fire does not mind how it sounds. It pops and answers in its own tongue, and for the first time on this whole black shore something has answered me.

---

I do everything at once, like a glutton, like a boy.

The orange-brown fish I caught at low tide — I hold it to the flame on a green stick and the fat hisses and runs and the smell of it goes up into my nose and down into the second mouth both. When I tear into it it is hot, hot all the way through, and I have never in my life until now eaten a thing that was warm. Cooked. The flesh comes apart soft. It does not turn my belly the way the raw clear fish did. My eyes run water and it is not the smoke. I chew on the side where the teeth still are. The hole where the three are gone holds the warmth like a cup.

The dark comes down and the dark does not win. I have a ring of light. I can see my own hands, the black stone, the wet shine of the cliff, the mouth of the crack where the warm stone is, where I sleep, where the frost rings me but never touches the shape of me. I press my hand flat to the black floor of it and it is mine under my palm, every cold-and-warm inch. The seep gives its finger of fresh water in the corner and it catches the firelight and runs silver. The fire makes it home by showing it to me.

I sit close. The warmth on my front is a different warmth than the stone's at my back — the stone's is strange, quiet, a held thing, a thing that does not move; the fire's leaps and bites and shrinks and has to be fed or it dies. Two warmths, like my two hungers, and I sit in the seam between them and let them have me from both sides.

---

The meat-hunger is full. The fire is company — it breathes, it moves, it needs me, and a thing that needs you is the next nearest thing to a thing that loves you.

But I know its trick now. When the wood burns down the second mouth opens under the warmth and reminds me that fire is not a hand, is not a voice, will not say my one word back to me. I hold the word behind my teeth where the gaps are. Rook. The fire ticks and settles. It cannot say it. It does not even know I am here.

I feed it another branch anyway. I am not ready to watch it go out and be alone in the true dark again — and that, the wanting it to stay, is the first thing on this island I have chosen instead of only endured.

I am not prey tonight. I sit by my fire on the black stone, alone, warm, fed, lit — and somewhere out past the ring of light, far off on the night water, a low sound comes back to me across the cold that is not the wind and not the sea.

I stop chewing. I listen.

It comes again.
