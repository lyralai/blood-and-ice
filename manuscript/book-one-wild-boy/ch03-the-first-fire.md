# Chapter 3: The First Fire

The dark comes down like the tide going out, all at once and from every side, and it brings the green to life.

For days I have made my peace with the light hours. The sun is a killing thing here, a white weight that lays itself on the back of the neck and does not lift, and the heat in the breathless under-green is a wet hand pressed over the mouth — but I have learned its law. I keep to the shade in the worst of it. I drink before I thirst. The day I can carry.

The night I cannot carry.

The night the green wakes up. When the white weight lifts, the rot-warm dark fills with the things that own this place, and they come for the live heat of me. Biting clouds come up off the standing water. Cords of ants follow a smell of blood through the black. Soft wet things drop from the leaves and feel for a place to bite in. And the fever-air rises thick off the swamp-edge, a breath out of the drowned green that hangs low and warm and rotten — a man breathes it and the shaking-sickness gets into him. The body knows it before the head does. This air kills. It pours up out of the ground at night, and against it the law of the shade and the law of the water sit in my chest light and useless as ash.

The first nights I lay in the den-crack and let the dark have me, and woke whole. No sickness in me from the fever-breath. No mark where the swarm should have fed. That is the thing I cannot look at straight — and I try to. I press at it in the dark. Why this body and not another. Why the rot goes around me. But I have no words for the shape of it, and the not-knowing slides off and leaves me only the fact. I set it down. The small dark shelf of things this body does that the body never agreed to, and I do not turn it over.

But not-being-touched is not the same as not being afraid. The dark holds teeth I cannot read. Things move in it that I have only heard — a heavy something far off in the high green, a wet sliding at the channel-edge, a cough that is not a bird. Night is when the green hunts. I lie in my crack in the black with my eyes open and my heart slamming, holding it off with nothing but listening. A thing that holds the dark off with only listening does not sleep. It waits.

I need a thing that pushes the dark back. A thing the green is afraid of.

---

I have seen fire.

In the cage there was fire — far off, up where the men were, a glow at the top of the ladder-hole and a smell coming down through the dark. The smell I remember better than the light. Old smoke, and meat cooking on the smoke, and under it the men's warmth and the men's voices, all of it up there, none of it down where we were. We got the black. They got the fire. That was the shape of the whole world as I first learned it: fire is a thing other people have, behind the hands, and you smell it and you do not get it.

But I watched. A boy in a cage watches everything, because watching is the only thing the hands cannot take. I watched the fire for the law under it. I saw a man strike a stone on a steel and the spark jump. I saw him feed the small light dry stuff first, threads and dust, and only then the bigger wood. And I saw that if he fed it big too soon the small light drowned and went out, and he had to start again and curse. There is a law in it. There is a law in everything. I did not know I was keeping the fire-law against a day I would need it. I kept it anyway. I keep everything.

So now I go looking for what fire eats.

---

The green gives me wood the same as it gives me everything: drowned, and crawling, and not where I first look for it.

There is no want of wood here. That is not the trouble. The trouble is that every stick on the floor of this place is wet to the heart, soft with rot, ringed with the white threads of the thing that eats wood. I lay my hand on a fallen limb thick as my leg and it gives like rotten meat. A cord of pale ants pours up over my wrist. I throw it down. Wet wood does not take a spark — I know this from the cage, from the man who cursed. And the wet is in everything here. The rain comes most afternoons in a roar that turns the ground to soup, and a thing left out is green-furred by morning.

So I learn the next hard law. I do not look on the ground. I look up.

There are dead limbs still hanging in the high green, snags caught in the crooks of the living trees, lifted off the rot-floor and turned in the wind and the sun, dry to the heart where the ground-wood never is. I climb for them. I learn to read a snag for the white-thread rot before I trust my weight to it, and twice I read wrong and come down hard in the leaf-wet and lie there with the breath knocked out, listening for what the noise has woken. I tear the high wood loose and carry it back armful by armful to the lip of my den-crack, out of reach of the next rain. I split the pieces and find them dry inside — the first dry thing I have held since the sea.

I gather more than I need. I gather against the rain like I gather against the hunger, both mouths open the whole while.

It costs me two days. Two days of work eaten, climbing and dropping and splitting and stacking, the sweat running off me faster than I can drink it back, the biting clouds finding the wet of my skin and settling all around me and not on me. There is a fly-cloud over the rot-limb at my feet thick as smoke. There is a clean hand's-width of air around my own skin where they do not come. I want to understand it. I crouch there in the middle of the work and look hard at the bare ring, and the wanting goes nowhere — there is no thread in me to pull, no name for what holds them off, only the wood waiting and the light going. I have wood to gather. I gather it.

---

The spark is the next law, and it is the law that breaks me.

I have no steel. The man in the cage struck stone on steel and the spark jumped. I have stone — the ridge above the swamp is nothing but stone — but no steel, no scrap of worked metal anywhere in all this drowning place. I do what the body does when it has no answer. I try everything. I strike stone on stone, hard, again and again, until my hands ache and the gashes that closed too fast threaten to open. I get nothing. A dull click. A smell of struck rock, flat and mineral. No spark. Not one.

I try faster. I find a stone with a glassy shine to it, dark and sharp where it has broken, and strike that, sure this time, and it only chips, and a flake of it bites into my palm. I bleed. The blood wells up warm and red and bright in the green dark, the one clean color in all the rot, and I watch the small cut begin already to draw its edges shut faster than a cut should. I want to know why. I press my thumb to it and watch it close and I lean toward the wrongness of it the way you lean toward a half-heard word — and it tells me nothing. Even my own blood mocks me. I have heat in me. I cannot get it out where the wood is.

I work until the light goes under the canopy, which is early, the green swallowing the sun long before the sky is done with it. I work the next day. The dry wood lies stacked and ready and I cannot light it, and the not-being-able is a new kind of hunger, worse in its way than the belly ever was. The belly I learned to feed. This I cannot. This I do everything right for and it gives me nothing — a flat click and a smell of rock and the dark coming down thick with its teeth.

I want to break the wood. I want to throw it in the swamp. The wanting comes up in me hot and stupid, the cage-rage, the rage of a thing that has done everything asked of it and still gets the dark, still gets the back of the hand. I hold it behind my teeth, in the gap, because I learned young that rage spent out loud only gives the hands more to take. I swallow it. I crouch over the useless wood and make myself go quiet and watch. Watching is the thing that has never once failed me when everything else has.

---

The friction teaches me. The green did not teach it. The body did, out of the long memory of the cage.

I am turning a split stick in my hands, only turning it, the way the fingers worry at a thing when the head has given up — and the place where it rubs the wood under it goes warm. I feel it before I know it. A small heat under the work, no bigger than the heat of my own palm. I go very still over my own hands.

Wood on wood. Not stone on stone. The spark does not jump out of the rock here. The heat comes up out of the wood itself, if you push it hard enough and long enough and never let it cool.

I remember, far off and dim, a thing half-seen. A man on the deck once, before the dark of the hold, spinning a stick between his palms over a board, fast, his whole weight bent down into it, smoke rising thin off the place where they met. I did not know I had kept it. I keep everything. I find a flat soft piece of the dry wood and a harder stick. I cut a small pit in the flat piece with the bitten edge of my glassy stone, set the stick in the pit, and spin it down between my palms — the body remembering it without my asking.

It is the hardest work I have done since the sea.

My palms burn. The skin tears where it has only just closed and the blood makes the stick slick and I wipe it and spin again. My arms shake — not a dark-shake, a work-shake, the muscle giving out, the breath sawing. The heat lies on me thick and wet and steals the strength out of the work. I have to stop, gasping, the green swimming muddy at the edges of my eyes. And then I start again. Because under my hands the wood is hot now. A smell is coming off it, faint and brown and good — old smoke, the smell from the top of the ladder, the smell of the warmth that was always other people's — and the smell is mine this time, coming up out of my own hands, and I will spin until my arms come off before I let it cool.

---

It comes on the third try of the day, when the light is going and the night is rising up out of the ground and I have nothing left.

A wisp of smoke, thin as a hair, lifting from the black pit. Then more. Then, when I tip the board and breathe on the dark place — soft, soft, not too much — a thing catches in the dust I have ground there, a small live red eye, no bigger than a seed, glowing and fading as my breath finds it.

I have made an ember.

I cup my hands around it against the night air and tip it into the nest I have ready — the finest shavings, the dust, the pale fiber I stripped and dried from a split vine. I breathe. The threads take, a small flame standing up gold in the green-black, and I feed it the way the law says. Threads first. Then shavings. Then the small split pieces, careful, careful, not too big, not too soon, or I drown the small light and start again and curse.

I do not curse. I feed it right.

And then it is burning. A fire. My fire, standing up off the dark stone in the falling dark. Gold and orange and moving, throwing its light onto the wet leaves, onto my hands, onto my face, and its heat comes with the light. And the green pulls back from it. The biting clouds lift and thin where the smoke goes. The wet things on the leaves do not drop. The dark that I have held off every night with only my eyes and my ears takes one step back from the ring of the light, and inside that ring I am — not safe, there is no safe here — but held. The fire stands between me and the green and says *not here*, and the green, for the first time, listens.

I have never in my life had a thing stand between me and the dark.

---

I do everything at once, like a glutton, like the boy I have not let myself be.

A fish from the channel — one of the clear-glass ones I would not eat raw, that knotted my belly when I tried, though the knot came loose far faster than it should and left me clean — I hold to the flame on a green stick, the way I saw meat held at the top of the ladder. The fat hisses and runs and the smell goes up into my nose. When I tear into it it is hot. Hot all the way through. The flesh comes apart soft and goes down easy, the fire eating the wrongness out of the meat before the meat can come into me. The gap where the three teeth are gone holds the warmth like a cup, and I hold it there, that small heat against it, and do not swallow until it goes.

---

I sit by the fire a long time.

I feed it, and learn its law as I go — how it wants air under it, how it sinks and reddens and needs more before it dies, how the high dead wood feeds it clean. I am careful with the wood, because it cost me two days and the rain will come and take what I leave out. But I let it burn brighter than I need anyway, because of the thing it is doing to the other mouth.

The far hunger. Low under the first, the one no fish or crab or root has ever once dropped into. I feel it move.

Not fed. I will not lie to myself the small quick way the cage taught me to lie, to get through. Nothing is fed; the fire is not a body and does not breathe and does not know the count of me. But it leaps and falls and changes. It answers my breath when I bend close. It does a thing back when I do a thing to it, and that is more than the green has ever given — the green only eats, only closes over, only takes its dead back into itself and never once turns its face to me. So the far hunger, the one I had begun to believe nothing on this whole drowning island could so much as touch, leans toward the fire. A starving man leans like that toward a smell that is not food but is at least the memory of food. I leaned like that once, in the dark of the hold, toward the warmth of the body chained beside mine — that never turned its face to me and was gone one morning, hauled up the ladder, and I never knew its name.

I have a name. I hold it now, behind my teeth, in the gap where the three are gone — Rook, my mother's word, the one thing the slavers never found because they never thought to look under a child's tongue for the only thing he owned. I say it now in the firelight. Not out loud — never out loud, the green is listening — but behind the teeth, against the warm of the flame. *Rook, Rook.* My mother's voice in it somewhere far down, her hands in it. The word the last warm thing before the hold, before the hands, before all of this.

The fire does not answer to the name. Nothing here does. Not yet. But it leaps and falls and is not the green, and for the first time since the sea spat me up I am not alone with only the dark and the two hungers and an island that takes and takes and never asks me anything.

---

I lie back in its light, and the body thinks the way a body thinks when it has won a thing it did not think it could win: *I made fire. Out of dead wood and my own hands. If I can make fire, maybe there is something else on this island I can make. Maybe the far hunger is not a thing I only carry. Maybe it is a thing I can someday feed.*

And under the warmth, as if the thought rang a bell low down in me, that hunger stirs. Not toward the fire now. Toward nothing I can see. It opens wider than the fire can fill and waits there, patient, the way the swamp waits at low water, and the warmth lies on top of it and does not reach the bottom.

In the deep of the night I wake once and the flame has sunk to coals, red eyes breathing in the black, and I feed it half-asleep, the body doing the work without me. And as I lay the wood I see the other wrong thing, the crawling one, ringing me in the dark as it always does where I sleep. A thing has died somewhere close in the wet, and the long cords of ants have found it, and the swarm covers it — a living crawling skin over the dead, the fly-cloud hanging thick and humming above it in the firelight. It comes to the very edge of where I have slept. And it stops. A clean ring of bare warm ground around my body where the swarm does not cross, where the live heat of me holds them off like the fire holds off the dark. Two rings around the little ground I have borrowed from the island for one night. I want, fierce and wordless, to know what I am that the rot won't have me. I reach for it. There is nothing to take hold of — the fever-air that does not sicken me, the bite that leaves no mark, the cuts that close too soon, all of it facts and no shape, a question with no edges. I set it on the shelf with the rest, not because I do not want it but because there is nowhere yet in me to put it.

Not now. Now I have a fire. The near mouth is quiet, the dark is held off at the ring of light, and I am not all the way alone.

I sit close, fed, lit, the smoke keeping the night-cloud thin — and somewhere out past the ring of light, far off in the high black green, a sound comes across the dark. Low. Long. Not the wind and not the water and not any beast I have learned. A made sound. A sound with a shape to it, the way a word has a shape before you know the word, and it goes down past the near mouth and touches the deep one — touches the place where no sound has ever reached — and the far hunger, which leaned toward the fire and was not filled, turns now in the dark toward the sound the way a head turns to a name.

I stop chewing. I listen.

It comes again.
