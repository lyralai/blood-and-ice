# Chapter 3: The First Fire

The cold comes back like a tide turning.

For days I have not felt it — the fog ate it, the meat held it off, the warm stone gave heat back into my spine through the long nights. But the weather on this shore does not ask what I can carry. A wind comes up out of the gray water in the afternoon, low and steady and with teeth in it, and it does not stop when the light goes. It hunts along the rock. It finds the wet of me, the salt damp that never dries, and pulls the heat off the skin faster than the warm stone can put it back. By the dark of that night I am shaking again, the old shaking, the from-outside shaking that a body knows how to brace against and brace against and still lose.

I crawl deep into the crack. I press myself to the stone. The stone gives what it has, and it is not enough. The wind reaches in after me, around the corners of the rock, finding the gaps, and where it touches I go numb in patches — a knee, an ear, the back of one hand — and the numb spreads slow inward, the cold doing its quiet patient work of turning the alarms off one by one.

I have fed myself. I have washed the wound. I have read the tide and the birds and built the law of the shore. And the cold can still kill me in a single night if it wants to, and it wants to, and against that the whole weight of what I have learned sits in my chest light and useless as ash.

The warm stone keeps me alive until morning. Only just. I wake stiff and slow, the numb places aching as the feeling crawls back into them, and I understand a thing I did not let myself understand before: the warm stone is one place. I cannot carry it. The day I have to leave this crack — and the meat is already telling me that day is close, the close pools cleaned out, the eels and crabs always one cove farther — the cold comes with me and the warm stone does not.

I need a warmth I can carry. I need heat that is mine and not the rock's.

---

I have seen fire.

In the cage there was fire — far off, up where the men were, a glow at the top of the ladder-hole and a smell coming down through the dark. The smell I remember better than the light. Old smoke, and meat cooking on the smoke, and under it the men's warmth and the men's voices, all of it up there where the warmth was, none of it down here where we were. We got the cold. They got the fire. That was the shape of the whole world as I first learned it: warmth is a thing other people have, up the ladder, behind the hands, and you smell it and you do not get it.

But I watched. A boy in a cage watches everything, because watching is the only thing the hands cannot take, and I watched the fire the way I watched everything else — for the law under it. I saw a man strike a stone on a steel and saw the spark jump. I saw him feed the small light dry stuff first, threads and dust and shavings, and only then the bigger wood, and I saw that if he fed it wrong, fed it big too soon, the small light drowned and went out and he had to start again and curse. There is a law in it. There is a law in everything. I did not know I was keeping the fire-law against a day I would need it. I kept it anyway. I keep everything.

So now I go looking for what fire eats.

---

The shore gives me wood the way it gives me everything: grudgingly, and salted, and not where I first look for it.

The tide throws things up. I have seen them and filed them — pale spars worn smooth, knots of root, long gray ribs of something that was a tree once, a whole life ago and a whole sea away, drifted here and bleached and thrown down on the black rock to rot. I gather them. It costs me a morning, the wood scattered thin along the high line where the biggest tides reach, and I carry it back armful by armful to a shelf of rock near the crack, out of the wind, and I learn the first hard thing about the fire-law on this shore.

The wood is wet. All of it. Wet to the heart, soaked through with sea and the gray weeping air, and wet wood does not take a spark — I know this from the cage, from the man who cursed. I lay my hand on the smooth pale spar and it is cold and damp as a dead fish and I know it will not burn. So I split the small pieces against the rock and find them wet inside too, dark and heavy with water all the way down. The sea has gotten into the bones of it.

I lay the wood out on the warm stone to dry.

That is the warm stone teaching me again, the way it has taught me every quiet thing on this shore. If it dries me, it will dry the wood. I spread the pieces over the heat where I sleep, the small split shavings first, turning them through the day the way I turned the leg to the cold water, patient because the hunger taught me patient.

It takes two days. Two days of low tides eaten by the work. Gathering. Splitting. Turning. Waiting, both mouths of the hunger open the whole while. The wind never stops. And every night the cold comes back to remind me what the work is for.

---

The spark is the next law, and it is the law that breaks me.

I have no steel. The man in the cage struck stone on steel and the spark jumped, and I have stone — the shore is nothing but stone — but I have no steel, no scrap of worked metal anywhere in this drifted gray world. I do what the body does when it has no answer: I try everything. I strike stone on stone. I strike the black rock on a paler rock, hard, again and again, until my hands ache and the gashes that healed too fast threaten to open again, and I get nothing. A dull click. A smell of struck rock, flat and mineral. No spark. Not one.

I try faster. I try harder. I find two stones with a glassy shine to them and strike those, sure this time, and they only chip and dull and a sharp flake bites into my palm. I bleed. The blood is warm against the cold stone and the cold day, and for a moment I only crouch there and watch it well up, red and alive, the one warm bright thing in all the gray. Even my own blood mocks me. I have heat in me. I cannot get it out where the wood is.

I work until the light goes. I work the next day. The wood lies dry and ready on the warm stone, and I cannot light it, and the not-being-able is a new kind of hunger, sharp and particular, worse in its way than the belly ever was — because the belly I learned to feed and this I cannot, this I do everything right for and it gives me nothing, a flat click and a smell of rock and the cold coming back down the night.

I want to break the wood. I want to throw it in the sea. The wanting comes up in me hot and stupid, the cage-rage, the rage of a thing that has done everything asked of it and still gets the cold, still gets the back of the hand, and I hold it the way I hold the name, behind my teeth, in the gap, because I learned young that the rage spent out loud only gives the hands more to take. I swallow it. I crouch over the dry useless wood and I make myself go quiet and watch, the way I watched the birds, the way I watched the hands, because watching is the thing that has never once failed me on this shore even when everything else has.

---

The friction teaches me. The shore did not teach it; the body did, out of the long memory of the cage.

I am turning a split stick in my hands, only turning it, the way the fingers worry at a thing when the head has given up — and the place where it rubs against the wood under it goes warm. I feel it before I know it. A small heat under the work, no bigger than the heat of my own palm, and the body wakes to it the way it woke to the older word *hunt*, and I go very still over my own hands.

Wood on wood. Not stone on stone. The spark does not jump out of the rock here; the heat comes up out of the wood itself if you push it hard enough and long enough and never let it cool.

I remember, far off and dim, a thing half-seen — a man on the deck once, before the dark of the hold, spinning a stick between his palms over a board, fast, his whole weight bent down into it, smoke rising thin off the place where they met. I did not know I had kept it. I keep everything. I find a flat soft piece of the drift, dry, and a harder stick, and I cut a small pit in the flat piece with the bitten edge of my glassy stone, and I set the stick in the pit and spin it between my palms, down and down, my weight on it, the way the half-seen man did, the way the body remembers without my asking.

It is the hardest work I have done since the sea.

My palms burn. The skin tears where it has barely healed and the blood makes the stick slick and I wipe it and spin again. My arms shake — not the cold-shake, the work-shake, the muscle giving out, the breath sawing. The fog of the long hunger is still in me and the work spends what little the eels and crabs have banked, spends it fast, and I have to stop, gasping, my heart slamming, the world swimming gray at its edges. And then I start again. Because under my hands the wood is hot now. Hot and dark in the pit. And a smell is coming off it, faint and brown and good — old smoke, the smell from the top of the ladder, the smell of the warmth that was always other people's — and the smell is mine this time, coming up out of my own hands, and I will spin until my arms come off before I let it cool.

---

It comes on the third try of the day, when the light is going and the wind is rising and I have nothing left.

A wisp of smoke, thin as a hair, lifting from the black pit. Then more. Then, when I tip the board and breathe on the dark place the way the man in the cage fed his small light — soft, soft, not too much — a thing catches in the dust I have ground there, a small live red eye, no bigger than a seed, glowing and fading and glowing as my breath finds it.

I have made an ember.

I cup my hands around it against the wind, and here is one of the wrong things waiting for me even now, even here: my breath goes out warm against the dust and leaves no ghost in the freezing air, no smoke of its own, only the wood's smoke rising through it. I do not stop to wonder at it. I have wood-smoke of my own to tend; I have no breath to waste on the old wrongness. I tip the ember into the nest of dry threads I have ready — the finest shavings, the dust, the pale hair of split root — and I breathe, and the threads take, a small flame standing up gold in the gray, and I feed it the way the law says. Threads first. Then shavings. Then the small split pieces, careful, careful, not too big, not too soon, or I drown the small light and start again and curse.

I do not curse. I feed it right.

And then it is burning. A fire. My fire, standing up off the black stone in the blue dark. Gold and orange and moving, throwing its light onto the rock, onto my hands, onto my face, and its heat comes with the light. The heat reaches me. It reaches me from a thing I made. Not the rock's borrowed warmth that I take and do not ask after — this is heat I called up out of dead wet wood with my own torn hands and my own remembering, and it crosses the little space and lays itself on my face and chest like a hand that means me no harm.

I have never in my life felt a hand that meant me no harm.

---

I do everything at once, like a glutton, like the boy I have not let myself be.

One of the colored fish from the low pools — I hold it to the flame on a green stick, the way I saw meat held at the top of the ladder, and the fat hisses and runs and the smell goes up into my nose and down into the second mouth both. When I tear into it it is hot. Hot all the way through. I have never in my life until now eaten a thing that was warm. The flesh comes apart soft and does not turn my belly the way the cold raw flesh did, and my eyes run water and it is not the smoke. I chew on the side where the teeth still are. The hole where the three are gone holds the warmth like a cup, and I hold it there, that small heat against the gap, and do not swallow until it goes.

---

I sit by the fire a long time.

I feed it. I learn its own law as I go — how close to lay the next piece, how it wants air under it, how it sinks and reddens and needs more before it dies. I am careful with the wood because the wood cost me two days and I do not know where more dry wood comes from once this is gone. But I let it burn bright a while anyway, brighter than I need, because of the thing it is doing to the other mouth.

The far hunger. The second one, low under the first, the one no eel or crab or colored fish has ever once dropped into. I feel it move.

Not fed. I will not lie to myself the way the cage taught me to lie, small and quick, to get through. Nothing is fed; the fire is not a body and does not breathe and does not keep the count of me. But it leaps and falls and changes. It answers my breath when I bend close. It answers the wood I give it. It does a thing back when I do a thing to it, and that is more than the gray dead stillness of the rock has ever done. And the second mouth, the one I had begun to believe nothing on this whole black shore could so much as touch, leans toward it — the way a starving man leans toward a smell that is not food but is at least the memory of food. The way I once leaned, in the dark of the hold, toward the warmth of the body chained next to mine, though that body never once turned its face to me and was gone one cold morning and hauled up the ladder and I never knew its name.

I have a name. I hold it now, behind my teeth, in the gap where the three are gone — Rook, my mother's word, the one thing the slavers never found because they never thought to look under a child's tongue for the only thing he owned. They had their own sound for me. A low thing made behind their teeth, *come-here, shut-your-mouth, you-are-ours*, the felt-shape of a hand closing on the back of my neck. That sound is not my name. That sound I left in the sea. This one I kept. I say it now in the firelight, not out loud — never out loud, the shore is listening — but behind the teeth, against the warm of the flame, *Rook, Rook,* my mother's voice in it somewhere far down, the way her hands are in it, the way the word was the last warm thing before the cold world, before the hold, before the cold.

The fire does not answer to the name. Nothing here does. But it leaps and falls and is not the rock, and for the first time since the sea woke me I am not alone with only the cold and the two hungers and the stone that gives heat back but never asks me anything.

---

I lie back in its warmth, and the body thinks the way a body thinks when it has won a thing it did not think it could win: *I made warmth. I made it out of nothing and my own hands. If I can make warmth, maybe there is something else on this shore I can make. Maybe the second mouth is not a thing I only carry. Maybe it is a thing I can someday feed.*

And under the warmth, as if the thought rang a bell low down in me, the second mouth stirs. Not toward the fire now. Toward nothing I can see. It opens a little wider than the fire can fill and waits there, patient, the way the sea waits, and the warmth lies on top of it and does not reach the bottom of it. I do not look at it straight. I feed the fire instead.

In the deep of the night I wake once and the flame has sunk to coals, red eyes breathing in the black, and I feed it half-asleep, the body doing the work without me. And as I lay the wood I see the other wrong thing, the cold one, ringing me in the dark the way it always does where I sleep — a fur of frost crept out across the black stone in a wide circle, glittering where the firelight finds it, white and still and waiting at the edge of the warm. It rings the fire. It rings me. It does not come where my body has lain, and it does not come where the fire burns, the heat-wrong and the cold-wrong holding each other off across the little ground I have made for myself in the dark. I do not understand it. I put it on the shelf with the others — the breath that won't smoke, the stone that gives heat back, the wounds that close too soon — the small dark shelf of facts about this body that the body never agreed to.

Not now. Now I have a fire. The near mouth is quiet, the cold is held off at the frost-line, and I am not all the way alone.

I sit close, fed, warm, lit — and somewhere out past the ring of light, far off on the night water, a low sound comes back to me across the cold that is not the wind and not the sea.

I stop chewing. I listen.

It comes again.
