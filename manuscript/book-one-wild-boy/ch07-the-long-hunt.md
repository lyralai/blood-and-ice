# Chapter 7: The Long Hunt

The shore is too small for both our blood, and the cold keeps count for me.

Days. I scratch them into the soft black inside the crack, a line for each low-light, a tooth-mark below for each time I see the pale shape and live. There are many lines now. The seep runs its finger-thin thread down the wall. I drink. I sharpen. I wait. The war goes on the way the tide goes on, in and out, neither of us drowning, neither of us dry.

---

It will not walk under the rocks anymore.

That was the first thing it took from me. The spear in the shoulder taught it the high places have teeth, and now it walks its road wide of the slabs, out where there is nothing above it but sky. I lie up there one whole dark and it never comes within a spear-throw, and the cold in my bones lies patient and still and does not mind the waiting, but the heat under my ribs minds. The heat wants the blood. The heat remembers how the dark blood steamed.

So I move my teeth. I find the place where the road must narrow, where the tide-channel pinches it against a wall of black rock, and there is no wide for it there, no sky-side to keep to. There it must pass close. I learn the channel the way I learn my own hands — when it fills, when it bares, the hour the water leaves the stone slick and shining.

I make a thing then I have not made before. A pit-trap is too much rock to move. But I find the slick stone above the pinch, and I think: it favors the left fore. It will set the strong side down first, the right, where the rock is dry. So I wet the dry side. Bucket after bucket of channel-water in a seal-stomach, up the rock in the dark, poured thin so it skins to ice in the cold before the cat ever comes. Glass over black stone, right where the strong foot goes.

This is not a clever boy's plan. This is a hungry one's.

---

It does not fall.

It comes along the pinch in the least light, slow, favoring the left. It sets the right fore down on my glass. The foot skids. For one breath the great body lurches and I have it, sliding, its bad shoulder dropping toward me, and I am up out of the rocks already with the spear cocked back behind my ear —

— and it catches itself. Claws into the dry. A thing that big should not move that fast off a skidding foot. It does. The spear leaves my hand into the place where its throat was and finds only the snow beyond. Then it is gone, screaming, wide of me, out across the flats where I cannot follow.

I stand on the pinch with my hands empty and shaking, not from cold. I am laughing. It comes up out of me wrong and cracked and I cannot stop it, hunched over with my arms across my belly, laughing at the black stone, because I made the island into a snare and the island's own throat slid out of it.

The heat under my ribs lifts and goes warm and pleased, like a hand laid flat on a full belly. It is glad the cat lived. It wants the big one, the worthy one — a small thing would be no fun to kill. I should be sick at that. I am not. I file the not-being-sick where I cannot look at it, and I go back up the pinch to break my own glass off the stone before the light comes.

---

It takes a pup from my flats the next low-light. Takes it slow, where I can see, and lies down to eat it where I can see, watching the rocks where I watch from. I read it. Your larder. My mouth.

I go to bed hungry, and the hunger that keeps me awake is not the one in my gut. The one in my gut I could fill. I lie on the warm stone — and it is warm, still, under my back in a cold that should have leached it grey hours ago, and I have stopped asking the stone why — and I hold the name behind my teeth, Rook, in the gap where three are gone. There is one other thing on this whole black shore that knows I am here. That marks me. That carries my scent in its nose and will carry it till one of us is meat. I made a war because a war is a kind of company, and now the company is winning.

I lie awake watching the dark where the rocks are, where it watches from, and I do not sleep, and I tell myself it is to guard the larder. The body knows the lie. The body wants to be watched back.

I get up, because the body knows this work even when the rest of me has gone strange, and I go to make fire.

---

Here is the last day, and I know it is the last day because I have made it so.

I do not wait on its road. I go to the pinch by the channel where it must pass close, and this time I bring everything. The spear, re-toothed, the flake bound new and dried hard. A second tooth — a short stabbing-bone for the close work, lashed to my forearm so it cannot leave my hand. And fire. Three days I have nursed a coal in a fist of dry kelp and old fat, fed it spit-careful in the lee of the crack, and now I have a brand of standing-dead soaked in seal-grease that will not light easy and will not die easy either.

It comes in the least light, where its eyes are best and mine are worst, and that is the cost I choose to pay, because in the least light it does not expect the fire.

I let it come to the pinch. I let it come close enough that I smell the meat-on-the-breath, the wet fur, the old dark blood crusted on the left shoulder where my tooth went before. I breathe out hard in that killing cold and there is no plume of me on the air, no smoke of breath the way there should be, and the frost beads white on the cat's whiskers and rings clear of my own skin — but there is no time to hate the wrongness now. I stand up out of the dark with the brand blazing in its bad-light eyes.

It rears back. Half a breath. Light is the one thing I have it cannot read.

Half a breath is enough. I put the spear into the bad shoulder, into the old wound, the soft spoiled meat of it, and I lean my whole weight down the shaft the way I leaned it off the slab the first time, and it screams the island's-throat scream and the channel throws it back doubled. The shaft snaps. Then it is on me.

It is on me and the world is claws and breath-heat and the reek, and it opens my arm, opens my chest, a white blaze of hurt down to the bone — and the hurt does not stop me. That is the wrong thing that saves my life. The body takes the wounds and does not fold around them the way a body should. I get the forearm-tooth up under the jaw, into the throat, and I drive it, and I feel the heat come out over my hand, the real heat, the steaming, and the great weight bears me down into the black stone and the brand is between us burning fur, and I hold the tooth in and I do not let go, I do not let go, I hold the name behind my teeth and I do not let go.

It dies slow. It dies heavy. It dies trying to crawl back toward its road.

---

I lie under it a long time before I can move it off.

When I crawl out I am red to the waist, mine and its, and I cannot tell where one ends. The wounds are already closing — wrong, fast, days where it should be weeks, the cold creeping in at the edges to seal them — and I sit on the slick black stone in the least light with my hands trembling, and I look at what I have done.

It is bigger dead than alive. It fills the pinch. The green has gone out of the eyes. I put my hand flat on the great ribs, on the wet fur, where the breath used to be, and there is no breath, and the thing that comes up in me is not the laughing thing and not the heat. It is quiet. It is heavy as the body is heavy. I have taken the oldest, hardest thing on this shore, the thing that owned the road before I washed up, and I made it gone, and some still cold part of me bows its head to it the way the cold bows to the dark.

The shape of a thanks is in me. My hand starts a thing it does not finish — opens over the wet ribs, lifts, stalls in the air. Only the bow, and the long quiet over the body, and the knowing it had of me — it knew I was there, to the last. That was the gift. That was the worth of it.

I stand up. The shore is quiet all the way to the water. No pads on the road but mine now. The flats are mine, the channel, the high rocks, the seep, the warm stone, the crack that is my own — all of it one mouth's, and the mouth is mine. I am the longest tooth on the black shore. Nothing left walks that I need fear.

And the second mouth, the hunger no meat answers, that I thought killing this would quiet —

it opens wider.

Wider than it has ever been. Because there is no one left to know I am here. I have won the whole shore and the whole shore is empty, and the war that was my company is a cooling weight in the pinch, and I stand over it the most dangerous thing on the island and the most alone, and the hunger under my ribs lifts its head into all that silence and howls for a thing the seals cannot give and the cat could not give and nothing on this black rock has ever been able to give.

I hold the name behind my teeth.

It is not enough. It was never enough.

I start counting again, because I do not know what else to do with my hands.
