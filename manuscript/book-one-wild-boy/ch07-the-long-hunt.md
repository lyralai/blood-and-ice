# Chapter 7: The Long Hunt

The seal feeds me for nine days, and on the tenth the den is bare again, and the shore has moved the law one notch further out. It does not give back. It moves the line. I learn this the way I learn everything here — late, in the body first, then the head.

I wake on the tenth morning to the smell of nothing. That is the first knowing. For nine mornings I woke to the rank fat smell of the kept meat in the cold ring, the iron under it, the held warmth of the body I dragged up the rock — and the smell was a thing I could lean on, a thing waiting on the cold side of the den whether the near mouth was loud or quiet. Now there is only stone-smell, salt-smell, old smoke. I crawl to the cold side and put my hand on the bare slab where the cut pieces lay in their white frost, and the slab is empty and the frost is gone, and my hand comes away with nothing on it but the cold. The last of the seal went into me last night. I knew it and I did not let myself count it as the last, because counting it would have made the night long. So the body counted instead, quiet, while I slept, and woke me to the empty.

The near mouth is not loud yet. That is the worst of it. The seal-fat is still in me, banked low under the ribs the way the coals are banked under the ash, and the body is still spending it, still warm with it, still thick — and the near mouth knows there is fat to spend and so it waits, easy, the fist down there resting open. But I have learned the shape of the days now. I know what comes. The fat will spend down. The thickness will go off the ribs and into the cold and the stone, the way it went before. And the near mouth will wake, and it will not wake gentle, it will wake grinding on nothing, a fist closing and closing — and by then the shore will have nothing to put against it, because I have eaten the shore down to the bare law of it, and the law says go further.

---

I go to the flat stones first, because the flat stones are where the seals were.

They are not there. I knew before I came over the high rock and looked down that they would not be there — the body knew, the way it knows the wind has changed before the face feels it — but the eye has to be shown. The low flat stones lie bare and wet in the gray, weed strung across them, the gulls walking the edge where the sea takes them back, and no dark fat shape lying with its face to the weak sun. The place where I killed is still marked. There is a stain on the black rock, brown now, gone into the stone the way the iron goes into the back of my mouth and stays, a dark patch the tide has washed and washed and not lifted, the shape of all that warmth I spilled. I stand over it and the far mouth stirs at the sight of it, leans, the old lean, toward a spilling nine days gone and long since cold — leans at the memory of the warmth running, not the warmth, the memory, which is the worse hunger because there is nothing under it to kill, nothing to fill, only the wanting standing up bare in me. I look off the stain. I read the empty flat. The seals hauled here once. They will not haul here while the killing-smell is in the stone. The shore gives once.

So I climb. I have been low on this rock since the sea gave me to it — the shore, the tide-line, the pools, the flat stones, all of it down at the sea's lip where the body washed up and stayed, because down is where the easy law was, the eel and the crab and the shell, the giving that asked little. I have never gone up. The land behind the shore is a thing I have turned my back on, a gray rise going up into wind and stone and I-do-not-know, and the not-knowing held me at the water the way the cage held me, a wall I did not test because I had not yet been made hungry enough to test it. I am hungry enough now. Or I will be, and the body knows the difference between now and will-be is the difference between hunting fed and hunting starved, and only one of those is a hunt you walk back from. So I go up while the fat is still in me. I spend the seal's strength climbing toward the next strength. That is the trade I have learned: you do not hunt with the last of yourself. You hunt with the second-to-last, and you keep the last for the dragging home.

---

The land goes up in steps of black stone, each step a ledge the wind has scoured bare, and between the ledges the going is loose — broken rock that shifts and rings under the foot, talking, telling everything alive that I am coming. I learn to read it with the soles, slow, each step set into the still places between the loose, and the climb that should take a morning takes the whole of it. The wind is harder up here. Down at the water the den-wall taught me the wind has a lee, a place it does not reach; up here there is no lee, the wind comes off the gray and over the bare ledges and does not stop, and it pulls at the heat in me the way it always pulls, and my breath goes out in front of me into the cold and does not smoke. I watch it not smoke. Other things would smoke up here. The cold is hard enough that a breath should hang white in it, and mine goes out clear and the cold takes it and shows nothing. I put it back on the shelf. The shelf comes with me up the rock. The shelf goes everywhere I go.

Near the top of the rise the wind brings me something that is not wind. I stop with one foot still lifted and let it lay the smell against my face: faint, threading through the salt — green, and wet, and rank with a rankness that is not the sea's. Living rankness. Bodies that are not seals, smaller, warmer, droppings and old nests and crowded breathing, a smell the body reads before the head can — many, and warm, and near. The near mouth, banked and easy, lifts its head. And the far mouth lifts a half-beat behind it, the old stir, leaning toward the warm crowded smell. I bite down on the name and hold it tight behind the gap where the three teeth are gone — my mother's name, the one thing those hands never found because they never knew to reach for it, kept in the dark behind my teeth every waking and every sleeping, the stone I bite down on when the far mouth opens too wide. I go up the last of the rise low and slow, into the wind, with the green rank smell coming to me and my own smell going back behind me to the empty.

---

It is a colony of birds.

They are nested all down the seaward face of the high rock, where the stone breaks off and falls sheer to the gray water far below — hundreds of them, more than I can hold in the eye at once, white and gray bodies packed onto every ledge and crack of the cliff, and the noise of them comes up at me over the wind, a great crowded clamor of calling and answering, harsh and endless, the sound of many made loud. The smell is thick here, droppings and fish and warm crowded bodies, and the near mouth grinds at it — here is meat, meat past counting, more bodies than the seal had in it ten times over — and the far mouth leans at the clamor, at the warmth and the noise and the press of all those living throats, and for a breath I am only the leaning, pointed at the many with nothing of me left over.

I haul back. I crouch at the cliff's lip in the wind and I learn them, because I will not go down that sheer face knowing nothing and fall for it the way I have bled for everything else. They nest on the ledges. The ledges go down the cliff in steps, some wide, some no wider than a hand, and on every one the birds sit packed, and the cliff drops away below them to the gray water that would take me as it took me before and not give me back a second time. The grown birds fly. That is the wall. I cannot take a thing that flies — it lifts off the ledge and is gone into the wind and the leaning closes on nothing. But the birds are nesting, and a nesting bird sits a nest, and a nest holds eggs, and eggs do not fly. And here and there on the wide ledges there are young ones, fat gray things not yet feathered for the wind, that cannot lift off and away — fat the way the seal was fat, made of all the warmth the cold has eaten out of me, sitting there on the rock unable to flee. The near mouth fixes on the fat young. The far mouth leans at the whole clamoring cliff at once.

So I read it the way the hands taught me to read a wall I had to get over. The grown birds fly and I cannot have them. The eggs and the fat young cannot fly and I can — if I can reach the ledges, and come back off them, without the cliff taking me. The wide ledges are below the lip, a body-length down, two, three, where the stone steps inward. I lie down on the cold rock at the edge with my head out over the drop and look down the sheer of it to the gray water working at the foot, and the height pulls at the belly the way the deep pool pulled cold up the arm, a falling-feeling with no falling in it yet. I learn the steps. Where the stone holds, where it crumbles, marking the line down — that ledge, then that crack, then the wide step where the fat young sit, then back up the same line with the warmth in my hands. It is a wall. I have got over walls. The hands beat that into me, over and over, in a place I keep behind the gap with the name: how to read a thing too high and find the line up it, how to set the weight where the stone holds, how to climb slow with the body that wants to climb fast and fall. The one good thing out of all that taking. I will use it on them now.

---

I go down the cliff in the gray, into the wind, the way I have learned to go down on everything.

The first ledge takes my weight. I lower onto it with the hands gripped white in the cracks above and the toes reading the stone below, slow, the cage-patience turned to climbing, and the rock holds, and I breathe, and the breath goes out clear into the cold and does not smoke and I do not look at it. The birds nearest me lift off screaming, a white blur into the wind and gone, and the clamor breaks and spreads down the cliff away from me, throat after throat taking it up, and the leaning rises in me at the sound of all those throats and I bite down on the name and go on. The eggs are there where the grown birds rose off them — pale, warm, two and three to a nest of weed and droppings, warm the way the seal-meat was warm before the cold kept it, and I take them, careful, the gap in my teeth careful of the name as I have been careful of it through every taking. I drink the first one off the broken shell on the ledge, cold weed and droppings under my knees and the wind off the gray pulling at me, and the inside of it is rich and fat and slides down warm and the near mouth feels it go, feels it the way it did not feel the one shell long ago, a real giving, the fat of it sitting in me the way the seal's fat sat. I take the rest into the front of my skin where the old smoke and the salt are, against the body, and I work down the line to the wide step where the fat young sit.

They cannot flee. That is the thing the body fixes on and the far mouth leans hardest at — the not-fleeing. The grown birds rose and went and these stayed, fat and gray and helpless on the wide ledge, turning their heads, calling, and unable to lift off into the wind that would carry them away from me. The near mouth wants the fat. The far mouth wants the helplessness, the way it wanted the scream — I feel it lean at the open throats and the staying, and I know, low down where I know everything, that the leaning in me points at the helplessness in them, and I do it anyway, because the near mouth must eat and the body must thicken and the shore gives once and has stopped giving down below. I take the nearest. It is warm in my hands, warm the whole way through, a body of held heat fighting in my grip the way the seal fought, smaller, weaker, a thing I am stronger than for once — and that is its own wrongness, to be the strong one, after the hold, after the hands, to be the closing hand for once instead of the neck the hand closes on. I do the thing. The warmth comes out over my fingers, less than the seal's flood but the same iron, the same bright taste rising to the back of my mouth where the name is, the same far mouth opening wide over the spilling and leaning and not closing. The young one goes still. The cliff screams all around me, white wings off every ledge, the clamor broken open into terror, and I crouch on the wide step in the middle of all that noise with the warm dead thing in my hands and the far mouth open over it, fed nothing, leaning at the noise, at the spilling, at the many throats crying — and I make myself stop. One. I take one, and the eggs, and no more, because the body cannot carry more up the cliff and back to the den and because the far mouth would have me take and take and take while the throats cry, and that is the thing I will not let it have. Not because it is wrong. Because I do not know what I become if I feed it.

---

The climb back up costs more than the climb down, the way the dragging always costs more than the going.

I have the eggs against my skin, the young one slung in a twist of weed at my side, both hands for the cliff, and the cliff has got harder, the way a wall always gets harder when you climb it carrying a thing — the line I read going down reads different going up, the holds smaller above me than they looked below, the wind pulling harder at the heat in me the higher I go. The body the seal-fat carried up here is spending the last of that fat now on the climb, and the near mouth is waking under the spending, grinding low, and I climb against the grind, hand over hand, toe-hold to crack to ledge, slow, the cage-patience the only thing between me and the gray water working at the foot of the drop far below. A hold crumbles under my left hand near the top — the stone going to dust and dropping away, ringing down the cliff, and for a breath I am hung on three points over the long fall: eggs against my skin, dead warmth at my side, the whole weight of me on a failing grip — and the body does the thing the body does, drops low and finds the next hold blind with the toes while the head watches, and holds, and breathes the clear breath that will not smoke, and goes on. I come over the lip onto the flat top in the high wind, shaking, the rock peeling skin off my forearms where I dragged over it, the dark blood welling warm and bright in the gray, the one warm thing, and I lie on the bare scoured stone with the eggs unbroken against me and the young one at my side and the cliff still screaming below, and I do not have it in me yet to move. I lie there and bleed and the cold comes down on the blood and I watch it, and by the count of the body I knew before this rock the welling should run a long while, and it slows under my eye and stops and the splits draw together while I watch, fast, too fast, closing in their own time toward the three days that is no time at all. I do not look at the knowing. I put it on the shelf. I get up off the rock.

---

I go down the long rise in the dark, and it is the dragging-home all over again, only the weight is the climb itself, the spent body the heaviest thing I carry.

The wind drops as I come down off the high ledges toward the water, the lee of the lower rock taking it off me, and the dark comes on, and I read the loose stone with the soles by feel alone, slow, each step set in the still places between the ringing rock, because to turn an ankle out here in the dark with the den a rise away is to lie down on the cold stone and not get up, and the far mouth would like that, the far mouth has its own quiet voice that says lie down, the cage-lie come up the rock with me the way the shelf came. I do not lie down. I come down off the rise with the eggs against my skin, the young bird at my side, the iron drying on my forearms, and the smell of the den comes to me before I see it — old smoke, and seal-fat gone into the stone, and the warm dry smell of the one place on this whole rock that holds the heat in. The lee mouth. The wall I built. I crawl in out of the wind and the wind shuts off behind me the way it always shuts off, and the warm inner stone is there waiting, giving its heat up into the dark the way it gives it every night and never asks anything back, and I put my hand on it and lean my spent weight on it and for a breath the body is only the leaning, the way it leans on the one thing here that does not take.

I wake the coals. The fire stands up gold and does not duck, the wall holding the wind off, and I cook the young bird the way I cooked the seal, the fat running and hissing into the flame, the rank good smell crawling up the slab roof and filling the walled dark, and I crack the eggs onto the hot stone at the fire's edge where they set firm and gold. I eat slow, chewing on the side where the teeth still are, careful of the gap, careful of the name. The near mouth feeds. Fills. The grind goes out of the belly, the fist down there opening and staying open, the fat sitting in me warm and staying warm, thickening me against the cold the way the seal did — and it costs me a whole day's climbing and a sheer cliff and the skin off both arms to feed it now, where the shore once fed it from a pool at my feet. That is the law, then, learned the whole way through: the giving moves up and away, and the body must move up and away after it, further each time, climb higher, spill more, bleed more, to put back the same warmth the cold eats out of me sitting still. I keep the eggs I did not eat on the cold side, and in the night the frost comes out in its wide white ring across the black floor, glittering where the firelight finds it, holding off from the heat of me by a hand's width all the way around, never where my body is — and where it reaches the eggs it keeps them, sits down on them and keeps them, the way it kept the seal, the killing held in them unchanged at the edge of the heat.

The shelf is crowded. Four wrong things standing on it, and tonight no new one, only the old four come up the rock with me, met again in a new place and as wrong here as below — the breath that will not smoke in the high cold, the wounds that drew shut on the cliff-top in their own short time, the warm stone giving up its heat in the dark, the frost that keeps the eggs and rings the floor and holds off from my body alone. I do not take any of them down and turn it over. They do not come apart in the hands. I keep my eyes on the fire.

And the far mouth stays open over all of it — the full belly, the kept eggs, the steady gold flame — leaning still, leaning at the memory of the cliff screaming, at the warmth that came out over my fingers, at the throats crying all around me when I took the one and stopped. The near mouth is shut and easy and forgetting. The far one does not forget. It was up there on the cliff with me, leaning at the many, and it came down the rock with me, and it sits open in me now over the warm dark, fed nothing, wanting the spilling more than the meat, the way it has wanted since the seal, the way I am beginning to understand it will want for as long as I am alive to carry it. I do not look at it straight. I have learned not to look at that one straight.

I climbed higher than I have ever climbed, and spilled where the throats could all hear it, and the cliff will scream a long time before the birds nest easy on it again. The shore gives once. So does the cliff. And the second mouth leaned at all of it and was not fed, and somewhere above me in the dark the high rock goes up further still, and the law says I will have to go there too.
