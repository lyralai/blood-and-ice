# Chapter 15: The Long Hunt

You do not hunt the thing that eats you with the last of yourself. You hunt it with the second-to-last, and keep the last back for the dying you do not mean to do.

That is the first law I lay down, and it is not a brave law. The brave ones go strength to strength against the Hesh and are rolled and held and not given back. I am the smallest thing in the green. I do not go up against the Hesh. I go up against the *ground* it stands on, and I keep the last of myself banked low under the ribs, for the dying I will spend it on if the reading is wrong.

So I go down. Down off the clean shelf in the thick fever-light before the sun, into the steaming dark under the drowned giants, into the rot-thicket where the long body drags its weight. There I begin the hunt that has no blow in it. The reading-hunt. The only one the weak thing has.

---

For three lows I do not throw a thing or sharpen a thing. I only learn the Hesh. I will not go up against the apex of my green knowing nothing and bleed for it blind, the way I have bled for everything else.

I lie still in the warm soup at the channel-edge, downwind, a stone that does not move. The make-yourself-nothing the hands beat into me in the dark, turned now to the watching. The Hesh does not find me. The high eyes pass over the place where the nothing is, because a thing that has never had to hurry from anything has never learned to look for the still small thing in the rot. That not-looking is my whole standing against it.

I read where it lies up in the heat of the low, sunk to the eyes in the rot-pool. I read the firmer mat it drags its weight along, because even a thing that fears nothing chooses ground that holds. And I read the one thing the body will not let go: the Hesh is built for the water and the soft edge. It does not climb. It stopped where the firm ground began and lifted its head after the old male and did not follow, because the long weight that makes it the strongest thing in the green is the one thing the high stone has no use for. The strength is its death on the rock. The old male's strength was his death in the rot. Strong is only ever right ground and wrong ground, and the wrong ground is where I will stand it.

Every strong thing in the green is roped to the one ground its strength can stand on, and the rope is what I will pull.

On the third low I find the place. There is a run the Hesh drags itself along between the rot-pool and the deep throat of the thicket, the one line of held ground through a soup that would take a heavy thing down. And a body's-length off it, the rot keeps its hidden throat — the dark vein of soup under root and leaf that took the old male to the chest, that reads weight and thrashing and pulls and does not give back.

The Hesh keeps off that throat. It reads the ground as I read it; it is the apex because nothing has ever taught it not to be. It will not set the long weight down where the ground is a lie.

So I will move the lie a foot-width over. The lie where the truth was, the truth where the lie was — and the apex will trust the ground it has always trusted, and it will eat him.

---

I do not have a word for the thing I am building. I have all the weather of it, and the weather is the noose.

The loop is for the neck of a thing that runs, and the Hesh does not run. But a noose is only the small shape of a larger law: a weight over a high seat, a long pull, and the thing too heavy to lift comes up. I made the green the arm when I brought the old male out of the rot's throat. I will make it the arm again — only this low it will not pull the strong out of the killing ground. It will pull the killing ground out from under the strong.

And this low I am not alone on the firm run. Marrow comes down off the high shelf at my back, rocking on the crooked gait, the high shoulder riding, and squats in the soup beside me, the curled hand tucked against his chest, the quick eyes already working the bank before I have shown him a thing. He was made wrong where I was made wrong, and the wrong-made learn early to read what the strong never have to. I give him the work in gesture — the run, the throat, the moved line — and he gives it back faster than I gave it, his good hand sketching the weight coming and the floor going and the small thing light off the floor, *yes, this, and then this.* A thing turns over in me that has no place on the shelf with the wrong things. It is good. It is only good. The smallest two things in the green, laying a death for the largest, and one of them grinning his broken grin at me sidelong in the rot-light. I am grinning it back before I know I have a face for it.

We work it three lows more, in the heat when the Hesh lies sunk and the eyes are down. We crawl the firm run downwind, beside the thing that names the bank its own, and I keep my name behind the gap where the three teeth are gone, biting it when the body wants to bolt. Marrow does not bite a name. He hums, low and tuneless under the breath, *I am here, the small one is still here,* and I learn to hunt to the sound of it.

We cut the woody runners thick as my arm and weave them across the deep throat, root to root, and cover the weave with held root peeled off the firm run, so the lie reads firm. Along the true run we work the held root loose at its under-veins, the green skin whole on top so the truth reads like the lie. A foot-width over. Where the ground held, we make it a throat. Where the throat was, a green floor that will take a long weight one breath and then let it through.

It is slow work and wet work and it costs. A spined leaf in the rot-mat drives its fire up under the skin of my forearm. The arm swells and goes hot and the fire climbs to the shoulder, and I lie still and wait for it to drop me as it would drop the old male. It does not drop me. The swelling goes down under my own eye. The poison goes out as rot goes out of a cut, fast and leaving no mark, and I am clear-headed in fever-thick air that should bring the shaking-sickness and does not. Marrow watches the arm un-swell. The quick eyes go to it and stay; the curled hand works once against his chest; he does not hum for a breath. Then he looks at the work again. He is the only one who has seen it whole, and he does not turn it over either. He files it with whatever the runt files the world's wrong things in and goes back to the weave, and I love him a little for the not-turning.

I want to know what I am. I press at it like you press a swelling to find the bone, and there is no bone, only the buzz — the wounds that draw shut, the swarm that rings off my skin, the venom that slides where it should knot and kill. I reach for the why and it has no edges to take hold of. It comes apart in the hands before I can lift it. I have no word for it, and no one to ask, and the fever-air is loud, and the Hesh is on its run. I set the wanting down because the work will not wait, not because I am told not to want it. But I want it. I want it the whole time.

---

A trap is only the half of it. The other half is the bringing. We must make the Hesh come the moved line, and come it fast, in the rush of its own strength, because a thing reads the ground slow and forgets to read it in the rush.

So I must be the thing it rushes. The bait. The moving thing that wakes the high eyes. There is a way that is not strength, and the weak thing has it. I tell Marrow with the good hand and the gap-toothed sounds — *up the leaning giant, hold the high seat, when I am off the floor you pull and you do not stop* — and the high shoulder drops and he will not look at me, and I read off the dropped shoulder the thing he will not make a sound for: he would rather stand the floor himself than hold the rope. The small one does not like the big one being meat. I make him go up the giant anyway. One of us light on the floor, one holding the line. The law of two small things against one large one.

---

I do it in the thick light before the sun of the next low, when the Hesh comes off its pool, the high eyes up, the long body sliding its weight along the firm run toward the deep throat.

I show myself. I stand up out of the not-looking on the green floor over the moved line, the small warm moving thing where the high eyes can find it at last — and they find it. The broad flat head comes round, the eyes fix, and the long body that has never once had cause to hurry hurries, because here is meat on the bank the Hesh has named its own. It comes off the firm run onto the line I moved, fast, the rows of teeth already wide.

Then the green opens wrong.

Voss-Marr is on the bank. He has trailed the wrong-blood down off the high stone to see the small thing fail — and he is on the *old* firm run, the line that was true three lows ago and is a lie now. His weight comes down where I worked the under-veins loose. He reads the ground as the Hesh reads it, by the green skin whole on top, and the skin holds him one breath — one — and then his foot goes through and he is down to the thigh in a thing that reads weight and pulls. And the long body is coming.

There is no deciding in it. The body decides before the head can say it, older than fear and lower down. I am light, and I am close, and I go to him instead of off the floor. I do not pull him up; the soup has read him, and a pull only gives it the thrashing to read deeper. I go flat on the held root at the floor's edge — the true root, the one held line in all that worked ground — and put it under his hand and make the sound that is not a word, the *stop,* the still-sound the Kala makes over me. *Be still. The still is the holding.* And the proud heir, the strongest thing of my season, goes still on the wrong-blood's say-so with the apex one body-length off and closing, because the body knows true ground when a smaller thing puts it under his hand. The soup loses the thrashing to read. He holds the root and does not go down, and the Hesh comes on.

It comes on, onto the line *I* moved, because the meat is still on the floor — I am flat on its edge with my hand on Voss-Marr's wrist. Now I go. I roll off the green floor the breath before the weight comes down, foot on the true root and gone, a thing too light to pull. The Hesh sets the heavy body down where I stood, in the rush, and the skin holds it one breath — one — and then the rot reads the weight and pulls, and the lie opens under the long body and lets it through into the soup that does not give back what it swallows.

---

It goes down fighting, the way the strong always fight, the fight that takes them deeper. The great body beats the soup to froth, the teeth snapping on rot and on nothing, and the more the strength fights the more the rot reads it and pulls. A law strength makes worse. Up on the leaning giant Marrow takes his turn of the laced vine over the high stub and pulls, the curled hand and the good hand both, the whole small wrong body hung on the rope. He does not pull to lift the Hesh out. He pulls the other way, holding the green skin closed hard over the throat, so the long body cannot find firm root to drag itself up. I am the eye. Marrow is the arm. The rot is the mouth. We stand the apex where the three meet.

Voss-Marr drags himself out onto true ground a body-length off and does not run. He stands streaming and watches the apex die, watches the two smallest things in the green hold it under, and the air around him changes. It is not contempt this low. It is the thing that comes before respect, near enough to it that the body cannot tell them apart yet — a strong thing seeing a strength it has no name for, and a hand that reached for him in the soup when the easy thing was to let him go down and be rid of the wrong-blood. He looks at me and does not look away. For one breath we are only two small things and one large thing dying, on the same side of the dying, and the heir bows the great young head the bare degree the strong bow to the strong. I have a brother for a breath. I will remember that it was only a breath.

It is a long killing, and we hold the weave, and the line does not break. The far one comes back at last out of its struck silence as the froth slows and the high eyes still and the long body goes to the deep stillness. I know that stillness. I have been it, on a deck, in a hold, with the hand closed on the back of the neck. I feel it come off the apex into me, and the thing low under the ribs stands all the way up, fed nothing, leaning at the strength gone still. I make myself stop the leaning short. I do not know what I become if I feed it.

---

I do not carry the whole of it. It is longer than the old male and me laid end to end, and the soup keeps the most of it. But I take the broad flat head. I cut it free with the dead expedition's knife and sling it in the laced vine. Marrow takes a corner of the vine on the high shoulder, and we begin the dragging-home, which costs more than the hunt. Voss-Marr does not take a corner. He is the heir, not made to carry. But he climbs ahead of us slow and does not climb out of sight, and that is its own kind of carrying from a thing like him.

We come up over the broken lip in the dark, shaking, the rot drying stiff and pale on us, and the tribe is crowded frightened on the high stone, the gladness gone out of it, the small ones pressed close.

And I lay the head of the Hesh down on the clean stone in the middle of them.

The whole tribe reads it. The broad flat head, the rows of teeth, the high eyes that named the bank their own — laid down dead by the smallest, softest, wrongest thing among them. The pressed-down fear breaks open. The old male with the torn ear comes and stands over the dead head and over me and makes the sound a strong thing makes to the thing that stood between it and the squared dead — low, slow, the great head bowed. The half-grown ones circle the head and the small scarred thing that carried it. The bold small one rides my shoulder at last, the keeping-place become the thing that kept. Marrow stands at the edge on his crooked gait and lets them have me, the broken grin gone careful, because the runt reads what the tribe is reading before the tribe does.

The Kala folds down beside me, the great gray-silver bulk, and lays the keeping arm over me, and makes the still-sound whole this low — *be still, you are held, nothing comes.* And the *nothing comes* does not die half-made in the great chest as it died when the Hesh had come, because the thing that came has gone into the soup the small wrong thing she chose laid the green down on. I make the sound back, low and torn, scraping over the gap where the three teeth were gone.

Because the king is on his broken lip. Gull-Marr stands turned in now, not out into the dark where there is no longer a thing to turn toward — turned to the dead head of the apex, to the small scarred thing the tribe circles. The flat eyes do not warm. They go narrow, the weighing look, no letting-stand, the *could* and not the *is*. The strength-king met the apex, found his strength too short, turned away, and called it the law. The weak thing went up into the dark and brought the apex down. Every eye on the high stone has read which of them did it.

And lower down, at the king's lip but not on it, Voss-Marr stands streaming and silent, and the air around him is going wrong again. The breath of brother in the soup is curdling in the high clean light, here where the whole tribe can see whose head lies on the stone and whose hand it was not. He bowed to me in the dark when it was only the two of us and the dying. Up here it is the crown — and the crown was poured into the world for him, and the wrong-blood pulled him out of the soup and then laid the apex's head down where the king must read it. The grudging-toward-respect is going back to a thing with teeth. He cannot forgive the hand that saved him, because it is the hand taking what was his. I have a brother for a breath and a rival for the rest of the lows there are.

---

I lie in the warm in the dark, the small one's quick heart at my chest and the Kala's slow vast tide under me, and I lay the new law down behind my teeth, in the gap where the name lies:

*A thing too strong to be fought is not too strong to be read, and the law of the green will bend to the thing that reads the ground the strength stands on.*

Not because I am strong. Because I am light, and I read the ground, and I made the killing ground itself the thing that kills. The high stones are easy in the dark for the first time since the green went quiet, the fear gone off them. Marrow sleeps near, the rocking gait gone slack, the curled hand open.

But the high stone has only one thing on it now stronger than the thing I killed, and a second that bowed to me in the soup and will not bow again. The king stands on the broken lip, the narrow eyes turned in on me. Below him the heir stands with the rot drying on him and the breath of brother already gone sour. I have made the both of them at once — the one by being light, the other by being there with a true root when he went down.

The seer comes across the crowded rock before the light, the ash-pale head slow, and folds down at the lip where I lie. The pale finger touches the Eye-and-Thorn bound at my chest and makes the long meaning-sound, the *means.* Then it lifts and turns — not out into the empty dark this low, but up, toward the broken lip where the king stands — and makes the sound again. *This one. And that one. And the two are one telling, and the telling is not done.*

I do not sleep. I lie in the warm with the near one easy and the dead head cooling and the king weighing, and over all of it I lay the next going-up, the one the seer pointed at. The body keeps it. It has learned to keep a thing either way it goes.
