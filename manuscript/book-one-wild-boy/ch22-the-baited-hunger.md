# Chapter 22: The Baited Hunger

The keeper stops hunting the dark. He starts feeding it.

---

I read it the low after the watcher, by the camp doing the thing it did not do before.

I have lain four lows now in the high drowned roots over the bone-spit, and I have learned the leaning of the camp the way I learned the spit and the seep — slow, by watching what changes. The first low after the owned girl drew my shape at the owning-fire, the camp leaned up the spit at the dark with the weighing-look on it, the iron-and-net look. I read it for what it was. A thing that means to cut the wrongness out of the green. So I lay flat and gave it nothing. I did not move a frond. I laid the want down under the beast and dug my pads in the mud and waited for the iron.

The iron does not come.

This is the new thing, and the body reads it long before the head can shape why. The keeper does not send the net up the spit. He does not loose the owned line into my green with hooks to drag the dark. A man made of the slow sure coming-on, the one who walks toward a thing as the green walks toward the Hesh — and he does not come. He does the other thing. He turns the camp around. Where it leaned up the spit with the cutting-look, now it leans with an open hand.

I do not trust the open hand. I have only ever seen one close.

They set a thing at my line.

I watch them do it at the bare of the low, when the steam comes up thick off the cooling mud and the gray goes soft. Two of the owned carry it down the slick black spit, bent under it the way they go bent under everything, and an owning walks behind with his hands on his hips. They set it on the dry mud just short of the wet, just short of the first of my mangrove. A thing on a flat of bark. Meat. Cooked meat, the grease of it coming up the down-wind into the roots where I lie, and beside it bright water in a gourd. Not the rot-water. The kept-water, the water they drink and the owned do not. They set it down as a hand sets a thing in the open palm. And they go back up the spit. They do not stay to watch from the stumps. They leave it, and the green is left alone with the warm meat steaming at its edge.

I lie in the roots and I do not move for it.

---

It is bait. I know it in the gut before the head, the same as I know the rot-pool from the drink.

I have set bait my whole life in the green. The noose under the leaf-fall. The staked thing that screams to bring the bigger thing. The kill left open on the trail to draw the dispute up out of the rot-thickets where the Skell wait. Bait is a thing of yours set out as a thing of theirs, where the wanting will reach, and the law of bait is the oldest law I keep: *the thing you reach for is the thing with the hook in it.* I made myself a king of the wild things on that law. I read it on the owned girl the low the watcher walked her down — the soft live thing set on the line to draw the dark up where the iron can cut. I read it now on the steaming bark. The keeper has stopped trying to cut me out of the green. He is trying to draw me out of it. He has set the meat where the wanting will reach, and gone back up the spit to wait for the reach.

So I do not reach. I lie above it through the dark. The grease-smell turns the belly over slow — the easy fat I did not have to run down and break in the mud — but the belly is not what the bait is set for, and I know it, and I hold. The bait is set for the deep hunger. The keeper does not know my belly. He has read my chest. He knows there is a thing in the green that wears his mark, a thing that does not run, a thing the owned girl walked twice down the killing spit to stand at the wall of — and a man who reads that does not bait the belly. He baits the hollow under the breastbone. He is not setting out meat. He is setting out *welcome*, and the welcome is the hook, and the far mouth is the throat he means it for.

So I file it, low and hard, and I hold, and the want under my breastbone pulls tight in the dark.

---

Marrow does not lie easy in the roots beside me.

He has come down off the high stones with me each low, my shadow on the killing spit since the smoke first stood on the water, and he reads the open hand before I do and faster. The first low the meat goes down he goes still in the way that is not rest. The high shoulder, the one that rides up wrong, drops. The curled hand that never rests works against the root, open, shut, open. He does not look at the bark on the mud. He looks at me, and then he will not look at the meat at all, and the whole crooked length of him is turned a little back up the spit toward the green, toward the high stones, toward home — every line of him leaning the way I am not.

I know what the body says. I have learned to read him as I learned to read the camp. *Not yours,* the still says. *The open hand is theirs and the hand that opens is the hand that shuts. Come up. Come away.* He does not have the words for it and he does not need them; the warning is in the dropped shoulder and the working hand and the eyes that go to me and slide off the meat. He is telling me the thing my own legs will not tell me. Your own kind is not your kin. The upright men below are not the road back to the man-shape I lost. They are the snare, and the snare is shaped like welcome.

I read it whole. And I file his stillness with the meat and the bright water and do not turn it over, because to look at it straight is to know he is right.

---

The owned girl does not carry the next one.

This is a thing the keeper has read, and it is hard to read, and I lay it on him as a thing to fear: he has seen that the dark moves for her and does not move for him, and so he keeps her out of it. He read the road between us — the careful falling sound, the hand against the wet mud — and a man who reads a road takes it for himself or shuts it. He shuts it. The next low the meat is set at my line by two of the owned with an owning behind, and she is not among them, and she is not at the wall when they come. I read her up the spit at the owning-fire, kept close, the big one's shape between her and the dark.

He has cut the road. He sets the welcome out himself, in the camp's own hand, and keeps the one who could carry it back behind his fires. He does not want the dark drawn to *her*. He wants it drawn to *him*. The bait is the same, the meat and the bright water, but the hand that sets it is the keeper's now, and the keeper's hand is the one that closes.

And the deep want does the thing it should not do — leans toward the meat anyway. Not the belly-lean. The other one. Because no thing has ever set warm meat at the edge of my green and walked away and left it for me. The green does not do it. The green sets the thing that screams and waits behind it with teeth. The tribe gave me the high stones and the meaning-sound and the Kala's vast slow arm, and the tribe never once set a kill at my feet in the open palm and stepped back. Even in the home, the meat was earned, run down and broken and dragged in. Belonging in the green is a thing you bleed for and hold by force. It is never *given*.

The keeper gives. He sets the warm thing at my line and opens the hand and goes. And the far mouth, the one no meat or water answers, does not know bait from gift. It knows only the open hand. And it leans, while Marrow goes still at my flank and will not look.

---

I go down to the meat on the fifth low.

I tell the head it is to read the bait. That is the head's road, and the head lays it plain: a thing the keeper sets out is a thing the keeper is reading, and to read the reader I must touch the thing he set, learn the trap by walking its edge as I walked the seep and the spit. Marrow does not come down with me. He stops at the wet line and the curled hand works and the high shoulder will not lift, and that is its own kind of word, and I go down past it anyway. I come to my own line where the dry goes to wet, and the meat is there on the bark, fresh-set, steaming faint in the warm dark.

I do not eat it. I crouch over it and read it. The bark for the cut of their tools. The meat for the beast it came off. The bright water for the keeper's hand on the gourd — and there, low under the grease, is the thing I came down to find. The thing the gut already knew. A second smell. Faint. Laid into the bright water and into the meat both. Not the rot-poison the bad water carries. Not the venom-smell. Not the spoiled under-smell of a kill gone over. A made smell. A thing they put in. The dull-smell. I have the shape of it off the lashed Mure in its cage — the way the tide-thing went heavy and slow and stopped fighting the rope. They feed it the dull-thing in its water, and it goes quiet, and its eyes go flat. The keeper has laid the dull-smell into the welcome. The warm thing at my line is the open hand, and folded inside the open hand is the closing one. Eat, and go heavy. Drink, and go slow. Lean into the welcome and let it lay you down in the mud where the net is easy.

I should leave it. The law says it. The legs say it. The oldest thing I keep says it: *the thing you reach for is the thing with the hook in it* — and now I have the hook in my hand, smelled it out, named its shape. Leave it. Go up to the high stones where Marrow's body has been pointing since the first low. Never lay my throat where the dull-water waits.

I take the meat.

I eat it there at my own line in the dark, the warm fat and the dull-smell folded through it, and I drink the bright kept-water down with the made thing in it, and the body does the thing the body does. The wrong thing. The dull-thing comes up in me slow, a heaviness reaching for the back of my eyes, a hand laid soft against the back of the head the way the keeper means it — and it does not land. It slides. As the fever slid, as the rot-water slid, as the venom went out of me fast and left no mark while every other body knotted and dropped. The dull-thing goes through me and out and leaves me clear. My eyes wide in the dark. My pads steady. The meat in my belly warm and only warm. The keeper has fed me the thing that lays the Mure down flat in its cage, and I crouch at my line clear-headed, light, the heaviness pouring off me as fast as it comes.

His hook does not hold me. He does not know that yet. He thinks he is laying me down a low at a time, drawing me heavy and slow toward the open palm. And I am eating his bait out of his open hand and walking back up into the green untouched, and only I know it.

---

But the want is not the belly, and the dull-thing does not slide off the want.

This is what I lie awake on in the high roots after, the meat warm in me and the made thing gone, and it is the thing I cannot eat my way clear of. The keeper has read me right. Not the body — he cannot read the body, no man can, it is the one book that takes no eye but mine. He has read the want. He set warm meat at my line and opened his hand and left it mine, low after low, and the body slid the hook every time. But the deep hunger took the open hand in and held it, and the open hand does not slide off. It lodges. It is the only open hand the world has shown me since the Kala's arm, and the hunger does not know the dull-smell is folded inside it. It knows only that a thing set warm food at the edge of my green and stepped back and called it mine, in the road of the upright kind. My own kind. The kind I came down off my crown to lean at.

Marrow knew before I touched it. He has known the shape of this since the first low, in his body, with no word for it — and I have the words, all of them, and the words do not matter. The head is not the part the bait was set for. The hunger wants the open hand. It wants the meat set down and left, the welcome the tribe never gave because the green does not give. And it does not care that the palm is closing. It has wanted the open hand so long that it will lean into the closing one to feel the open one a breath longer.

That is the bait. Not the meat. The bait is the welcome itself, the open hand of my own kind, and there is no part of me that throws *that* off. The keeper found it without reading the body at all. He baited the one hunger no green and no crown could fill, and I am leaning into it with the trap drawn whole and my eyes wide open, while the one true thing on this island goes still at my side and will not look.

---

Up home, the tribe leans away from me the way Marrow leans toward the green.

I read it the lows I climb back to the high stones at the gray. The Pack holds — Grist at the fire, the Torn One on the high stone with her spear across her knees, Stub broken and faithful at my heel. They hold. But past them the air has gone wrong. Voss-Marr stands at the far edge of the seer's stones with the prime-blood at his back, and he does not come to the fire, and he does not look away from me either, and the not-looking-away is a weather I have read off him before. He blames the smoke on the water on the wrong-blood human who drew it. The slavers came up the spit for *me*. The mark is mine. He stands there in the gray with the strongest of the young men gathered loose around his shoulders and lets me feel it: a king who walks down to the killing camp low after low and comes back smelling of their meat is no king of theirs. The set of his body says the thing he will not waste breath to say. *You are theirs now. You were always going to be theirs.* The young men hang back behind him, and the fire I sit at is a little emptier each gray.

I have the crown still. I do not have all of them under it. And I am climbing down the killing spit each low to lean at an open hand while the heart of my own people leans away from me, and I see both leanings whole, and I cannot stop my legs.

---

I go down again the next low. And the next.

Marrow comes to the wet line each time and stops, and each time the curled hand works and the high shoulder drops, and each time I go past him into the dark. I tell the head the head's reasons and the head lays them plain and true — to read the keeper, to learn the trap, to know whose mark is mine, to be near the man-shaped road I lost — and the reasons are true, and they are not why my legs carry me down a low at a time toward the open hand. I will not lie about it. I go down because the welcome is set out warm at my line, and the deep hunger fills a little at the open hand, and it has been empty so long that a little is a flood.

I see the keeper learn it. I read him reading the bark gone clean each low, the meat taken, the gourd drained, the dark come down to his open hand and gone back up — and the weighing-look settles deeper into him, the look of a man whose bait has been taken. He knows now the thing in the green has a hunger he can feed, and a thing with a hunger you can feed is a thing already half in the hold. He does not know the dull-water slides. He thinks I am going heavy. He is wrong about the body and right about the want, and the want is the door, and the want is the door he is walking the brand through low after low with warm meat in his open hand.

But the camp is learning a thing too, and it is not learning it from me.

I read it the same lows I read the keeper. The owned drag slower down the spit. One of the owning ones does not come down at all the third low, and the next he comes down gray-faced and damp and leaning on the line, and the low after that he is gone from the spit entirely. The green has gotten into them. The fever that slid out of me, the rot-water that left no mark on me, the venom the body throws — it is in their water and their meat and their breath, and it does not slide off them. It pools. They came up the spit to take the wrongness out of the green and the green has begun, quiet, low by low, to take them. The keeper bends over the warm meat with his open hand and does not smell the thing already in his own camp. I smell it on the down-wind every low. The hold is full of dying men who do not know they are dying, and they are still winning.

And the law comes up plain behind my teeth: *the green never gives; only the trap gives.* The tribe never gave because the tribe was true. The keeper gives because the keeper is a snare. I have it whole — the open hand and the closing one folded inside, the fever pooling in them and gone clear out of me, Voss at the far edge of my own fire and Marrow at the wet edge of theirs — and the deep hunger leans into the open hand anyway, low after low, because the want is older than the law and was here, hollow under the breastbone, before I had a single word to set against it.

I do not eat the bait because I am fooled. Marrow's body has named the trap for me each low, and Voss's body has named what it will cost, and I read both true. I eat it because I am hungry in the place no meat answers, and the keeper found that place, and there is warm welcome set at the edge of my green in the open palm of my own kind, and I have wanted that palm my whole life.

The shape I named to no one has a hunter now. He has stopped chasing the dark. He has started feeding it. The body throws off the dull-water and the venom and the fever and the rot — every poison the green ever poured. There is one it will not throw, and the keeper poured it, and it is the open hand. *The poison you can name is not always the poison you can spit.* And Marrow goes still at the line, and will not follow, and will not look. And I go down anyway, with my eyes wide open in the dark.
